The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 12

Round the Table

A joint inquest was held on Monday.

I don't plan to give a detailed account of the proceedings. Doing so would only be repeating the same information. By arrangement with the police, very little was revealed. I testified about the cause of Ackroyd's death and the approximate time. The coroner mentioned Ralph Paton's absence but didn't emphasize it.

Afterward, Poirot and I spoke briefly with Inspector Raglan. The inspector seemed very serious.

"It doesn't look good, M. Poirot," he said. "I'm trying to be fair. I'm a local man, and I've seen Captain Paton many times in Cranchester. I don't want him to be guilty, but it looks bad no matter how you look at it.

If he's innocent, why doesn't he come forward? We have evidence against him, but it might be explained away. So why doesn't he offer an explanation?" There was more to the inspector's words than I realized at the time. Ralph's description had been sent to every port and train station in England. The police were on alert everywhere. His rooms in town were watched, as well as any houses he was known to visit.

With such a tight net, it seemed impossible for Ralph to avoid being caught. He had no luggage and, as far as anyone knew, no money.

"I can't find anyone who saw him at the station that night," continued the inspector. "And yet he's well known here, and you'd think someone would have noticed him. There's no news from Liverpool either." "You think he went to Liverpool?" Poirot asked. "Well, it's possible. That telephone message from the station, just three minutes before the Liverpool express left, suggests something." "Unless it was deliberately meant to mislead you. That could be the purpose of the telephone message." "That's an idea," said the inspector eagerly. "Do you really think that's the explanation for the telephone call?" "My friend," said Poirot gravely, "I don't know. But I will tell you this: I believe that when we find the explanation for that telephone call, we will find the explanation for the murder." "You said something like that before, I remember," I observed, looking at him curiously.

Poirot nodded.

‘I always come back to it,’ he said seriously.

‘It seems completely unimportant to me,’ I said.

‘I wouldn’t say that,’ the inspector replied. ‘But I must admit I think Mr Poirot focuses on it a little too much.

We’ve better clues than that. The fingerprints on the dagger, for instance.’ Poirot suddenly seemed very foreign, as he often did when excited about something.

‘M. Inspector,’ he said, ‘beware of the blind - the blind how do you say? - the little street that has no end.’ Inspector Raglan stared, but I understood faster.

‘You mean a dead end?’ I said.

‘That’s it - the blind street that leads nowhere. It may be the same with those fingerprints - they may lead you nowhere.’ ‘I don’t see how that could be,’ said the police officer. ‘I suppose you’re suggesting they’re fake? I’ve read about such things, though I can’t say I’ve ever seen it myself. But fake or real - they’re bound to lead somewhere.’ Poirot simply shrugged, throwing his arms out wide.

The inspector then showed us several enlarged photographs of the fingerprints and started to explain the technical details of loops and whorls.

‘Come now,’ he said finally, annoyed by Poirot’s distant attitude, ‘you have to admit that those prints were made by someone who was in the house that night?’ ‘Of course,’ said Poirot, nodding.

‘Well, I’ve taken the prints of every member of the household, everyone, mind you, from the old lady down to the kitchen maid.’ I don’t think Mrs Ackroyd would like being called the old lady. She must spend a lot on cosmetics.

‘Everyone’s,’ the inspector repeated, sounding annoyed.

‘Including mine,’ I said dryly.

‘Very well. None of them match. That leaves us two possibilities. Ralph Paton, or the mysterious stranger the doctor mentioned. When we find those two ‘ ‘Much valuable time may have been wasted,’ Poirot interrupted.

‘I don’t understand, Mr Poirot.’ ‘You said you took fingerprints from everyone in the house,’ Poirot murmured. ‘Are you being completely truthful, M. l’Inspecteur?’ ‘Certainly.’ ‘Without missing anyone?’ ‘Without missing anyone.’ ‘The living or the dead?’ For a moment, the inspector looked puzzled by what he thought was a religious comment. Then he slowly understood.

‘You mean - ?’ ‘The dead, M. l’Inspecteur.’ The inspector still took a few minutes to grasp it.

‘I’m suggesting,’ Poirot said calmly, ‘that the fingerprints on the dagger are Mr Ackroyd’s own. It’s easy to check. His body is still here.’ ‘But why? What would be the point? Surely you’re not suggesting suicide, Mr Poirot?’ Ah! no. My idea is that the murderer wore gloves or wrapped something around their hand. After stabbing him, they picked up the victim’s hand and closed it around the dagger.’ ‘But why?’ Poirot shrugged again.

‘To make a confusing case even more confusing.’ ‘Well,’ said the inspector. ‘I’ll look into it. What gave you the idea in the first place?’ ‘When you kindly showed me the dagger and pointed out the fingerprints. I know very little about loops and swirls - I freely admit it. But it seemed to me that the position of the prints was a bit awkward. That's not how I would hold a dagger to stab someone. It would have been difficult to get it in exactly the right position with the right hand brought up over the shoulder backwards.’ Inspector Raglan stared at the little man. Poirot casually brushed a speck of dust from his coat sleeve.

‘Well,’ said the inspector. ‘It’s an idea. I’ll look into it, but don’t be disappointed if it leads nowhere.’ He tried to sound kind and superior.

Poirot watched him leave. Then he turned to me with a twinkle in his eyes.

‘Next time,’ he remarked, ‘I must be more careful of his pride. And now that we’re alone, what do you think, my friend, about a little family gathering?’ The ‘little gathering,’ as Poirot called it, took place about half an hour later. We sat around the table in the dining room at Fernly. Poirot was at the head of the table, like the chairman of some dreadful board meeting. The servants were not there, so there were six of us in total. Mrs Ackroyd, Flora, Major Blunt, young Raymond, Poirot, and myself.

When everyone was gathered, Poirot stood up and bowed.

‘Ladies and gentlemen, I have brought you together for a reason.’ He paused. ‘First, I want to make a special request to Miss Flora.’ ‘To me?’ said Flora.

‘Miss, you are engaged to Captain Ralph Paton. If anyone knows his secrets, it is you. I ask you, please, if you know where he is, to convince him to come forward. Just a moment’ - as Flora looked up to speak - ‘say nothing until you have thought carefully.

Miss, his situation gets more dangerous every day. If he had come forward right away, no matter how bad the facts seemed, he might have had a chance to explain them.

But this silence - this running away - what can it mean? Surely only one thing: he knows he is guilty. Miss, if you truly believe he is innocent, persuade him to come forward before it is too late.’ Flora’s face turned very pale.

‘Too late!’ she repeated quietly.

Poirot leaned forward, looking at her.

‘Listen, Miss,’ he said gently, ‘it is Papa Poirot who is asking you this. Old Papa Poirot, who knows a lot and has seen a lot. I am not trying to trick you. Won't you trust me and tell me where Ralph Paton is hiding?’ The girl stood up and faced him.

‘M. Poirot,’ she said clearly, ‘I swear to you - I solemnly swear - that I have no idea where Ralph is, and that I have not seen or heard from him since the day of - of the murder.’ She sat down again. Poirot looked at her silently for a minute or two, then he slapped his hand on the table.

‘Good. That is settled,’ he said. His face became stern. ‘Now I ask the rest of you sitting around this table: Mrs. Ackroyd, Major Blunt, Dr. Sheppard, Mr. Raymond. You are all friends with the missing man. If you know where Ralph Paton is hiding, speak up.’ There was a long silence. Poirot looked at each of them.

‘I beg you,’ he said softly, ‘speak out.’ But still there was silence, finally broken by Mrs Ackroyd.

‘I must say,’ she said in a sad voice, ‘that Ralph’s absence is very strange - very strange indeed. Not to come forward at a time like this. It seems like there's something hidden. I can't help thinking, Flora dear, that it was lucky your engagement was never officially announced.’ ‘Mother!’ cried Flora angrily.

‘Providence,’ declared Mrs Ackroyd. ‘I strongly believe in Providence - a power that guides us, as Shakespeare beautifully wrote.’ ‘Surely you don’t blame God for thick ankles, Mrs Ackroyd, do you?’ asked Geoffrey Raymond, his carefree laugh echoing.

I think he wanted to ease the tension, but Mrs Ackroyd gave him a disapproving look and took out her handkerchief.

‘Flora has been saved from a lot of attention and trouble. Not that I think dear Ralph had anything to do with Roger’s death. I don’t think so. But I have a trusting heart - I always have, since I was a child. I hate to think the worst of anyone. But, of course, we must remember that Ralph was in several air raids as a young boy. The effects can show up much later, they say. People aren’t always in control of their actions.

They lose control, you know, without meaning to.’ ‘Mother,’ cried Flora, ‘you don’t think Ralph did it?’ ‘Come now, Mrs Ackroyd,’ said Blunt.

‘I don’t know what to think,’ said Mrs Ackroyd tearfully.

‘It’s all very upsetting. What would happen to the estate, I wonder, if Ralph were found guilty?’ Raymond pushed his chair back from the table forcefully.

Major Blunt remained very quiet, looking thoughtfully at her.

‘Like shell-shock, you know,’ said Mrs Ackroyd stubbornly, ‘and I’m sure Roger didn’t give him much money - with good intentions, of course. I can see you all disagree with me, but I do think it’s very odd that Ralph hasn’t come forward, and I’m thankful Flora’s engagement was never officially announced.’ ‘It will be tomorrow,’ said Flora clearly.

‘Flora!’ her mother cried, shocked.

Flora had turned to the desk.

‘Please send an announcement to the Morning Post and The Times, Mr Raymond.’ ‘If you’re sure it’s wise, Miss Ackroyd,’ he replied seriously.

She turned quickly to Blunt.

‘You understand,’ she said. ‘What else can I do? As things are, I have to support Ralph. Don’t you see that I must?’ She looked at him intently, and after a long pause, he nodded sharply.

Mrs. Ackroyd began to protest loudly. Flora didn't react. Then Raymond spoke.

‘I understand your reasons, Miss Ackroyd. But don’t you think you’re acting too quickly? Wait a day or two.’ ‘Tomorrow,’ Flora said clearly. ‘It’s no use, Mother, going on like this. Whatever else I am, I’m not disloyal to my friends.’ ‘M. Poirot,’ Mrs. Ackroyd pleaded tearfully. ‘Can’t you say anything at all?’ ‘Nothing to be said,’ Blunt interrupted. ‘She’s doing the right thing. I’ll support her no matter what.’ Flora held out her hand to him.

‘Thank you, Major Blunt,’ she said.

‘Mademoiselle,’ said Poirot, ‘will you let an old man congratulate you on your courage and loyalty? And please don’t misunderstand me if I ask you - ask you very seriously - to delay the announcement you mentioned for at least two more days?’ Flora hesitated.

‘I ask it as much for Ralph Paton’s sake as for yours, mademoiselle. You frown. You don’t see how that can be.

But I assure you that it is true. No jokes. You put the case into my hands - you must not stop me now.’ Flora paused a few minutes before replying.

‘I do not like it,’ she said at last, ‘but I will do what you say.’ She sat down again at the table.

‘And now, ladies and gentlemen,’ said Poirot quickly ‘I will continue with what I was about to say. Understand this I mean to find the truth. The truth, however ugly it may be, is always interesting and beautiful to the one who seeks it. I am much older, my abilities may not be what they once were.’ Here he clearly wanted someone to disagree. ‘This is probably the last case I will ever investigate. But Hercule Poirot does not end with a failure. Ladies and gentlemen, I tell you, I intend to know. And I shall know - in spite of you all.’ He spoke the last words in a challenging way, throwing them in our face, so to speak. I think we all pulled back a little, except for Geoffrey Raymond, who remained good-humoured and calm as usual.

‘What do you mean - in spite of us all?’ he asked, with slightly raised eyebrows.

‘But - exactly that, sir. Every one of you in this room is hiding something from me.’ He raised his hand as a quiet murmur of protest began. ‘Yes, yes, I know what I am saying. It may be something unimportant - small - that is thought to have no effect on the case, but it is there.

Each one of you has something to hide. Now, am I right?’ His glance, challenging and accusing, moved around the table. And every pair of eyes looked down. Yes, mine as well.

‘I have my answer,’ said Poirot, with a strange laugh. He got up from his seat. ‘I ask all of you. Tell me the truth - the whole truth.’ There was silence. ‘Will no one speak?’ He gave the same short laugh again.

‘It is a pity,’ he said, and went out.
