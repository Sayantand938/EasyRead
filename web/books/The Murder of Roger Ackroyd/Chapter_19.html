The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 19

Flora Ackroyd

The next morning, as I was coming back from my usual visits, Inspector Raglan called out to me. I stopped, and he climbed onto the step of my car.

‘Good morning, Dr Sheppard,’ he said. ‘Well, that alibi checks out.’ ‘Charles Kent’s?’ ‘Charles Kent’s. The barmaid at the Dog and Whistle, Sally Jones, remembers him clearly. She picked his photo out from five others. It was about a quarter to ten when he came into the bar, and the Dog and Whistle is more than a mile from Fernly Park. The girl says he had a lot of money - she saw him take a handful of notes out of his pocket. It surprised her, seeing the kind of guy he was, with boots falling apart. That’s where that forty pounds went.’ ‘He still won’t say why he went to Fernly?’ ‘Stubborn as a mule. I spoke to Hayes in Liverpool on the phone this morning.’ ‘Hercule Poirot says he knows why the man went there that night,’ I said.

‘Does he?’ the inspector asked, excited.

‘Yes,’ I said, wanting to annoy him. ‘He says it’s because he was born in Kent.’ It felt good to share my own embarrassment.

Raglan stared at me, not understanding.

Then he grinned, and tapped his head.

‘A bit mad,’ he said. ‘I’ve thought so for a while.

Poor guy, so that’s why he had to quit and come here. Runs in the family, maybe. He’s got a nephew who’s crazy.’ ‘Poirot has?’ I asked, surprised.

‘Yes. Hasn’t he ever mentioned him to you? Quite harmless, I believe, and all that, but quite mad, poor lad.’ ‘Who told you that?’ Again a grin showed itself on Inspector Raglan’s face.

‘Your sister. Miss Sheppard, she told me all about it.’ Really, Caroline is amazing. She never rests until she knows every detail of everyone’s family secrets. Unfortunately, I have never been able to teach her the decency of keeping them to herself.

‘Jump in, Inspector,’ I said, opening the door of the car.

‘We’ll go up to The Larches together and tell our Belgian friend the latest news.’ ‘Might as well, I suppose. After all, even if he is a bit crazy, it was a useful bit of info he gave me about those fingerprints. He’s obsessed with the man Kent, but who knows - there may be something useful behind it.’ Poirot greeted us with his usual smiling courtesy.

He listened to the information we had brought him, nodding his head now and then.

‘Seems quite OK, doesn’t it?’ said the inspector rather gloomily. ‘A chap can’t be murdering someone in one place when he’s drinking in the bar in another place a mile away.’ ‘Are you going to release him?’ ‘Don’t see what else we can do. We can’t very well hold him for getting money by lying. Can’t prove a thing.’ The inspector tossed a match into the grate in a disgruntled fashion. Poirot retrieved it and put it neatly in a little receptacle designed for the purpose. His action was purely mechanical. I could see that his thoughts were on something very different.

‘If I were you,’ he said at last, ‘I should not release the man Charles Kent yet.’ ‘What do you mean?’ Raglan stared at him.

‘What I say. I should not release him yet.’ ‘You don’t think he can have had anything to do with the murder, do you?’ ‘I think probably not - but one cannot be certain yet.’ ‘But haven’t I just told you - ?’ Poirot raised a hand protestingly.

‘Mais oui, mais oui. I heard. I am not deaf - or stupid, thank the good God! But you see, you approach the matter from the wrong - the wrong - starting point, is not that the word?’ The inspector stared at him heavily.

‘I don’t see how you make that out. Look here, we know Mr Ackroyd was alive at a quarter to ten. You admit that, don’t you?’ Poirot looked at him for a moment, then shook his head with a quick smile.

‘I admit only what is proven.’

‘Well, we have enough proof of that. We have Miss Flora Ackroyd’s evidence.’ ‘That she said goodnight to her uncle? But me - I do not always believe what a young lady tells me - no, not even when she is charming and beautiful.’ ‘But hang it all, man, Parker saw her coming out of the door.’ ‘No.’ Poirot’s voice rang out with sudden sharpness.

‘That is exactly what he did not see. I made sure of that with a small test the other day - you remember, doctor? Parker saw her outside the door, with her hand on the handle. He did not see her come out of the room.’ ‘But - where else could she have been?’ ‘Perhaps on the stairs.’ ‘The stairs?’ ‘That is my little idea - yes.’ ‘But those stairs only lead to Mr Ackroyd’s bedroom.’ ‘Precisely.’ And still the inspector stared.

‘You think she’d been up to her uncle’s bedroom? Well, why not? Why should she lie about it?’ ‘Ah! that is the question. It depends on what she was doing there, doesn’t it?’ ‘You mean - the money? Hang it all, you don’t suggest that Miss Ackroyd took that forty pounds?’ ‘I suggest nothing,’ said Poirot. ‘But I will remind you of this. Life was not easy for that mother and daughter. There were bills - there was constant trouble over small sums of money. Roger Ackroyd was difficult about money. The girl might be desperate for a small sum. Imagine what happens. She has taken the money, she goes down the little staircase. Halfway down, she hears the chink of glass from the hall. She knows what it is - Parker coming to the study. She must not be found on the stairs - Parker will not forget it, he will think it strange. If the money is missed, Parker will remember seeing her come down those stairs. She rushes to the study door - with her hand on the handle to show that she has just come out, when Parker appears in the doorway. She says the first thing that comes to her head, a repetition of Roger Ackroyd’s orders earlier in the evening, and then goes upstairs to her own room.’ ‘Yes, but later,’ persisted the inspector, ‘she must have realized the importance of telling the truth? The whole case depends on it!’ ‘Afterwards,’ said Poirot drily, ‘it was difficult for Mademoiselle Flora. She is told that the police are here and that there has been a robbery. She assumes that the theft of the money has been discovered. Her only thought is to stick to her story. When she learns that her uncle is dead she is terrified. Young women do not faint nowadays without a good reason. So, there it is. She has to stick to her story, or confess everything. And a young and pretty girl does not like to admit that she is a thief- especially before those whose respect she wants to keep.’ Raglan slammed his fist on the table.

‘I won’t believe it,’ he said. ‘It’s not believable. And you - you’ve known this all along?’ ‘The possibility has been in my mind from the start,’ admitted Poirot. ‘I was sure that Mademoiselle Flora was hiding something from us. To be sure, I did the small test I told you about. Dr Sheppard came with me.’ ‘A test for Parker, you said it was,’ I said bitterly.

‘My friend,’ said Poirot apologetically, ‘as I told you then, one has to say something.’ The inspector rose.

‘There’s only one thing to do,’ he declared. ‘We must speak to the young lady right away. Will you come up to Fernly with me, M. Poirot?’ ‘Certainly. Dr Sheppard will drive us up in his car.’ ‘I agreed willingly.

When we asked for Miss Ackroyd, we were shown into the billiard room. Flora and Major Hector Blunt were sitting on the long window seat.

‘Good-morning, Miss Ackroyd,’ said the inspector. ‘Can we have a private word with you?’ Blunt immediately got up and moved to the door.

‘What is it?’ asked Flora nervously. ‘Don’t go, Major Blunt. He can stay, can’t he?’ she asked, turning to the inspector.

‘That’s up to you,’ said the inspector dryly. ‘I need to ask you a few questions, miss, but I’d rather do it privately, and I expect you would too.’ Flora looked closely at him. I saw her face turn paler.

Then she turned and spoke to Blunt.

‘I want you to stay - please - yes, I mean it. Whatever the inspector has to say, I’d rather you heard it.’ Raglan shrugged his shoulders.

‘Well, if that’s what you want, so be it. Now, Miss Ackroyd, M. Poirot here has made a suggestion. He suggests that you weren’t in the study at all last Friday night, that you didn't say goodnight to Mr Ackroyd, and that instead of being in the study you were on the stairs coming down from your uncle’s bedroom when you heard Parker crossing the hall.’ Flora looked at Poirot. He nodded back at her.

‘Mademoiselle, the other day, when we sat round the table, I begged you to be honest with me. What you don't tell Papa Poirot, he finds out. That's what happened, isn't it? See, I will make it easy for you. You took the money, didn't you?’ ‘The money?’ said Blunt sharply.

There was a silence that lasted for at least a minute.

Then Flora straightened up and spoke.

‘M. Poirot is right. I took that money. I stole it. I am a thiefyes, a common, petty little thief. Now you know! I’m glad it’s out in the open. It’s been a nightmare these last few days!’ She sat down suddenly and buried her face in her hands. She spoke hoarsely through her fingers. ‘You don’t know what my life has been since I came here. Wanting things, planning for them, lying, cheating, running up bills, promising to pay oh!

I hate myself when I think about it! That's what brought us together, Ralph and I. We were both weak! I understood him, and I felt sorry - because I'm the same deep down.

We're not strong enough to be alone, either of us. We're weak, miserable, awful people.’ She looked at Blunt and suddenly stomped her foot.

‘Why do you look at me like that - as if you can't believe it? I may be a thief - but at least I'm being real now. I'm not lying anymore. I'm not pretending to be the kind of girl you like, young and innocent and simple. I don't care if you never want to see me again. I hate myself, I despise myself - but you have to believe one thing, if telling the truth would have helped Ralph, I would have spoken up. But I've known all along that it wouldn't help Ralph - it makes things even worse for him. I wasn't hurting him by sticking to my lie.’ ‘Ralph,’ said Blunt. ‘I see - it's always about Ralph.’ ‘You don't understand,’ said Flora hopelessly. ‘You never will.’ She turned to the inspector.

‘I admit everything; I was desperate for money. I never saw my uncle that evening after he left the dinner table.

As for the money, you can do what you want.

Nothing could be worse than this!’ Suddenly she broke down again, hid her face in her hands, and rushed from the room.

‘Well,’ said the inspector in a flat tone, ‘that's that.’ He seemed unsure of what to do next.

Blunt came forward.

‘Inspector Raglan,’ he said quietly, ‘that money was given to me by Mr. Ackroyd for a specific reason. Miss Ackroyd never touched it. When she says she did, she's lying to protect Captain Paton. The truth is what I said, and I'm ready to go to court and swear to it.’ He gave a sort of quick bow, then turned quickly and left the room.

Poirot was after him in a flash. He caught up with him in the hall.

‘Monsieur - a moment, please, if you would be so kind.’ ‘Yes?’ Blunt was clearly impatient. He frowned down at Poirot.

‘It’s this,’ said Poirot quickly: ‘I’m not fooled by your little story. No, not at all. It was really Miss Flora who took the money. All the same, it’s well thought out - it pleases me. It’s very good what you’ve done there. You think and act fast.’ ‘I don’t care about your opinion, thank you,’ said Blunt coldly.

He started to walk away again, but Poirot, not at all bothered, put a hand on his arm.

‘Ah! but you must listen to me. I have more to say. The other day I spoke of secrets. Very well, I’ve seen all along what you’re hiding. You love Mademoiselle Flora with all your heart. From the moment you first saw her, isn’t that right? Oh! let’s not be afraid to say these things - why do people in England think love is something shameful to hide? You love Mademoiselle Flora. You’re trying to hide that from everyone. That’s fine - that’s how it should be. But listen to Hercule Poirot - don’t hide it from mademoiselle herself.’ Blunt had seemed restless while Poirot was talking, but the last words seemed to grab his attention.

‘What do you mean by that?’ he said sharply.

‘You think she loves Captain Ralph Paton - but I, Hercule Poirot, tell you that’s not true. Mademoiselle Flora agreed to marry Captain Paton to please her uncle, and because she saw it as a way to escape her life here, which was becoming unbearable for her. She liked him, and they understood each other well.

But love - no! Mademoiselle Flora doesn’t love Captain Paton.’ ‘What the hell do you mean?’ asked Blunt.

I saw the dark red flush under his tan.

‘You’ve been blind, monsieur. Blind! She’s loyal, the little one. Ralph Paton is in trouble, and she feels she must stand by him.’ I felt it was time to say something to help.

‘My sister told me the other night,’ I said encouragingly, ‘that Flora has never cared at all for Ralph Paton, and never will. My sister is always right about these things.’ Blunt ignored my attempt to help. He spoke to Poirot.

‘Do you really think -’ he began, and stopped.

He is one of those men who can't express themselves well and have trouble putting things into words.

Poirot has no such problem.

‘If you doubt me, ask her yourself, monsieur. But maybe you don't care to anymore - because of the money?’ Blunt made a sound like an angry laugh.

‘Think I’d hold that against her? Roger was always strange about money. She got into trouble and didn’t dare tell him.

Poor kid. Poor lonely kid.’ Poirot looked thoughtfully at the side door.

‘Mademoiselle Flora went into the garden, I think,’ he murmured.

‘I’ve been a complete fool,’ said Blunt suddenly.

‘Strange conversation we’ve been having. Like one of those Danish plays. But you’re a good fellow, M. Poirot. Thank you.’ He took Poirot’s hand and squeezed it hard, making the other wince in pain. Then he walked to the side door and went out into the garden.

‘Not a complete fool,’ murmured Poirot, gently holding his injured hand. ‘Only one kind - a fool in love.’
