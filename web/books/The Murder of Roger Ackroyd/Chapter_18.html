The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 18

Charles Kent

Half an hour later, Poirot, Inspector Raglan, and I were on the train to Liverpool. The inspector was clearly very excited.

‘We might get a lead on the blackmail part of this, if nothing else,’ he said happily. ‘From what I heard on the phone, this guy's tough. He's also a drug user. It should be easy to get information out of him. If there was a hint of a motive, he's likely the one who killed Mr. Ackroyd. But then, why is young Paton hiding? This whole thing's a mess. By the way, M. Poirot, you were right about those fingerprints. They were Mr. Ackroyd’s. I thought so too, but dismissed it as unlikely.’ I smiled. Inspector Raglan was clearly trying to save face.

‘About this man,’ said Poirot, ‘he is not yet arrested, no?’ ‘No, just held for questioning.’ ‘And what does he say about himself?’ ‘Not much,’ said the inspector, grinning. ‘He’s cautious. A lot of insults, but little else.’ When we arrived in Liverpool, I was surprised to see Poirot greeted so warmly. Superintendent Hayes, who met us, had worked with Poirot on a case long ago and clearly thought highly of him.

‘Now that we have M. Poirot, it won't take long,’ he said cheerfully. ‘I thought you had retired, monsieur?’ ‘I had, my good Hayes, I had. But retirement is so boring! Day after day is so monotonous.’ ‘Maybe. So, you’ve come to see our suspect? Is this Dr. Sheppard? Do you think you can identify him, sir?’ ‘I’m not sure,’ I said hesitantly.

‘How did you catch him?’ Poirot asked.

‘The description was sent out, as you know. In the press and privately. Not much to go on, I admit. This guy has an American accent, and he admits he was near King’s Abbot that night. He just asks what business it is of ours and tells us to go to hell before he answers questions.’ ‘May I see him as well?’ asked Poirot.

The superintendent winked.

‘Glad to have you, sir. You can do whatever you want. Inspector Japp from Scotland Yard was asking about you the other day. Said he heard you were involved in this case unofficially. Do you know where Captain Paton is hiding, sir?’ ‘I doubt that would be wise right now,’ said Poirot formally, and I bit my lip to keep from smiling.

The little man did it very well indeed.

After talking a bit more, we were taken to meet the prisoner.

He was a young man, I'd guess about twenty-two or three. Tall and thin, with hands that shook a little, and signs that he used to be quite strong. His hair was dark, but his eyes were blue and restless, rarely looking anyone in the eye. I had always thought that there was something familiar about the person I had met that night, but if this was him, I was totally wrong. He didn't remind me of anyone I knew at all.

'Now then, Kent,' said the superintendent. 'Stand up. Here are some visitors to see you. Do you know any of them?' Kent glared at us, looking angry, but didn't answer. I saw his eyes move over the three of us, and then stop on me.

'Well, sir,' the superintendent said to me, 'what do you think?' 'He's the same height,' I said, 'and he looks like he could be the man. But that's all I can say.' 'What the hell is this all about?' Kent asked. 'What have you got against me? Come on, tell me! What am I supposed to have done?' I nodded.

'It's the man,' I said. 'I know his voice.' 'You know my voice, do you? Where do you think you've heard it before?' 'Last Friday evening, outside the gates of Fernly Park. You asked me how to get there.' 'I did, did I?' 'Do you admit it?' asked the inspector.

‘I’m not saying anything. Not until I know what you know about me.’ ‘Have you not read the newspapers lately?’ asked Poirot, speaking for the first time.

The man’s eyes narrowed.

‘So that’s what this is about? I read an old man had died at Pernly. You’re trying to say I did it, are you?’ ‘You were there that night,’ said Poirot quietly.

‘How do you know that, mister?’ ‘Because of this.’ Poirot took something from his pocket and held it out.

It was the goose quill we had found in the summer house.

The man’s face changed when he saw it. He almost reached out his hand.

‘Snow,’ said Poirot thoughtfully. ‘No, my friend, it is empty. It was lying where you dropped it in the summer house that night.’ Charles Kent looked at him uncertainly.

‘You seem to know a lot about everything, you little foreign so-and-so. Maybe you also remember this: the papers say that the old man died between a quarter to ten and ten o’clock?’ ‘That is correct,’ agreed Poirot.

‘Yes, but is it really correct? That’s my point.’ ‘This gentleman will tell you,’ said Poirot.

He pointed to Inspector Raglan. Raglan hesitated, looked at Superintendent Hayes, then at Poirot, and finally, as if given permission, said: ‘That’s right. Between a quarter to ten and ten o’clock.’ ‘Then you have no reason to hold me here,’ said Kent. ‘I left Fernly Park by twenty-five minutes past nine. You can ask at the Dog and Whistle. It’s a bar about a mile outside Fernly on the road to Cranchester. I made a bit of a scene there, I remember. It was almost a quarter to ten. What do you say to that?’ Inspector Raglan wrote something in his notebook.

‘Well?’ demanded Kent.

‘We’ll look into it,’ said the inspector. ‘If you’re telling the truth, you’ve nothing to worry about. What were you doing at Fernly Park anyway?’ ‘Went there to meet someone.’ ‘Who?’ ‘That’s none of your business.’ ‘Watch your mouth,’ the superintendent warned him.

‘To hell with watching my mouth. I went there on my own business, and that’s all there is to it. If I was gone before the murder happened, that’s all that concerns the cops.’ ‘Your name is Charles Kent,’ said Poirot. ‘Where were you born?’ The man stared at him, then he grinned.

‘I’m British, through and through,’ he said.

‘Yes,’ said Poirot thoughtfully. ‘I think you are. I imagine you were born in Kent.’ The man stared.

‘Why? Because of my name? What’s that got to do with it? Does a man named Kent have to be born in that county?’ ‘In certain cases, I can imagine he might be,’ said Poirot very carefully. ‘In certain cases, you understand.’ There was so much meaning in his voice that it surprised the two police officers. As for Charles Kent, he went bright red, and for a moment I thought he was going to attack Poirot. He changed his mind, however, and turned away with a kind of laugh.

Poirot nodded as though satisfied, and went out through the door. The two officers joined him soon after.

‘We’ll check that statement,’ remarked Raglan. ‘I don’t think he’s lying, though. But he’s got to give us a statement about what he was doing at Fernly. It looks to me as though we’ve got our blackmailer. On the other hand, if his story’s true, he couldn’t have had anything to do with the actual murder. He had ten pounds on him when he was arrested - quite a lot of money. I think that forty pounds went to him - the numbers on the notes didn’t match, but of course he’d have changed them right away. Mr Ackroyd must have given him the money, and he ran off with it as fast as possible. What was that about Kent being his birthplace? What’s that got to do with it?’ ‘Nothing at all,’ said Poirot mildly. ‘Just a little idea of mine, that was all. I am known for my little ideas.’ ‘Are you really?’ said Raglan, looking at him with a puzzled expression.

The superintendent burst out laughing.

‘I’ve heard Inspector Japp say that many times. M.

Poirot and his little ideas! Too fanciful for me, he’d say, but there was always something in them.’ ‘You make fun of me,’ said Poirot, smiling; ‘but never mind. The old ones sometimes have the last laugh, when the young, clever ones don’t laugh at all.’ And nodding his head at them in a wise way he walked out into the street.

He and I had lunch together at a hotel. I know now that the whole thing was clear to him. He had found the last piece he needed to find the truth.

But at the time I didn't suspect this. I thought he was generally very confident, and I assumed that the things that confused me must be equally confusing to him.

My main question was what Charles Kent could have been doing at Fernly. I kept asking myself this, but I couldn't find a good answer. Finally, I carefully asked Poirot about it. He answered right away.

‘My friend, I don’t think, I know.’ ‘Really?’ I said, not believing him.

‘Yes, indeed. I suppose it wouldn’t make sense to you if I said that he went to Fernly that night because he was born in Kent?’ I stared at him.

‘It certainly doesn’t make sense to me,’ I said dryly.

‘Ah!’ said Poirot, feeling sorry for me. ‘Well, no matter. I still have my little idea.’
