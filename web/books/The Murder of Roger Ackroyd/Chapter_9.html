The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 9

The Goldfish Pond

We walked back to the house together. The inspector was nowhere to be seen. Poirot stopped on the terrace and, with his back to the house, slowly turned his head from side to side.

‘A fine estate,’ he said at last, admiringly. ‘Who gets it?’ His words almost shocked me. It’s strange, but until then, I hadn’t thought about who would inherit it. Poirot watched me closely.

‘It’s a new thought for you,’ he said finally. ‘You hadn’t considered it before, eh?’ ‘No,’ I said honestly. ‘I wish I had.’ He looked at me curiously again.

‘I wonder what you mean by that,’ he said thoughtfully. ‘Oh! no,’ he added as I was about to speak. ‘It’s no use. You wouldn’t tell me what you really think.’ ‘Everyone has something to hide,’ I quoted, smiling.

‘Exactly.’ ‘You still believe that?’ ‘More than ever, my friend. But it’s not easy to hide things from Hercule Poirot. He has a talent for finding things out.’ He went down the steps into the Dutch garden as he spoke.

‘Let’s take a walk,’ he said, looking back. ‘The air is nice today.’ I followed him. He led me down a path to the left, surrounded by yew hedges. A path ran down the middle, with flower beds on each side, and at the end was a round, paved area with a seat and a goldfish pond.

Instead of following the path to the end, Poirot took another one that wound up the side of a wooded hill. In one spot, the trees had been cleared, and a seat had been placed.

Sitting there, you had a wonderful view of the countryside, and you could look straight down on the paved area and the goldfish pond.

‘England is very beautiful,’ said Poirot, looking out at the view. Then he smiled. ‘And so are English girls,’ he said quietly. ‘Hush, my friend, and look at the pretty scene below us.’ That's when I saw Flora. She was walking along the path we had just left, humming a little tune. She seemed to dance more than walk, and even in her black dress, she seemed full of joy. She suddenly spun around on her toes, making her black dress swirl out. At the same time, she threw her head back and laughed loudly.

As she did, a man came out from the trees. It was Hector Blunt.

The girl jumped. Her face changed slightly.

‘You startled me - I didn’t see you.’ Blunt didn't say anything, but just looked at her silently for a minute or two.

‘What I like about you,’ said Flora, a bit meanly, ‘is how cheerful you are to talk to.’ I think Blunt blushed under his tan. His voice sounded different when he spoke - almost humble.

‘Never was much of a talker. Not even when I was young.’ ‘That was a very long time ago, I guess,’ said Flora seriously.

I heard the hidden laughter in her voice, but I don’t think Blunt did.

‘Yes,’ he said simply, ‘it was.’ ‘How does it feel to be as old as Methuselah?’ asked Flora.

This time the laughter was clearer, but Blunt was thinking about something else.

‘Remember the guy who sold his soul to the devil? To become young again? There’s an opera about it.’ ‘Faust, you mean?’ ‘That’s him. Strange story. Some of us would do it if we could.’ ‘Anyone would think you were falling apart, listening to you talk,’ cried Flora, annoyed and amused at the same time.

Blunt said nothing for a minute or two. Then he looked away from Flora into the distance and remarked to a nearby tree that it was about time he went back to Africa.

‘Are you going on another trip - shooting animals?’ ‘Guess so. Usually do, you know - shoot animals, I mean.’ ‘You shot that head in the hall, didn’t you?’ Blunt nodded. Then he blurted out, turning rather red: ‘Want some good skins sometime? If you do, I could get them for you.’ ‘Oh! please do,’ cried Flora. ‘Will you really? You won’t forget?’ ‘I won’t forget,’ said Hector Blunt.

He added, in a sudden moment of openness: ‘Time I left. I’m no good at this kind of life. Don’t have the manners for it. I’m a rough guy, no good in society. Never remember what I’m supposed to say. Yes, time I left.’ ‘But you’re not going right away,’ cried Flora. ‘No - not while we’re in all this trouble. Oh! please. If you go -’ She turned away a little.

‘You want me to stay?’ asked Blunt.

He spoke carefully but very plainly.

‘We all-’ ‘I meant you specifically,’ said Blunt, directly.

Flora turned slowly back and looked him in the eye.

‘I want you to stay,’ she said, ‘if - if that matters at all.’ ‘It matters a lot,’ said Blunt.

There was a moment’s silence. They sat down on the stone bench by the goldfish pond. It seemed like neither of them knew what to say next.

‘It - it’s such a lovely morning,’ said Flora at last. ‘You know, I can’t help feeling happy, in spite - in spite of everything. That’s awful, I suppose?’ ‘Quite natural,’ said Blunt. ‘Never saw your uncle until two years ago, did you? Can’t be expected to grieve very much. Much better to have no pretense about it.’ ‘There’s something awfully comforting about you,’ said Flora. ‘You make things seem so simple.’ ‘Things are simple as a rule,’ said the big-game hunter.

‘Not always,’ said Flora.

Her voice had lowered, and I saw Blunt turn and look at her, bringing his eyes back from (apparently) the coast of Africa to do so. He clearly understood her change of tone, for he said, after a minute or two, in rather an abrupt manner: ‘I say, you know, you mustn’t worry. About that young chap, I mean. Inspector’s an idiot. Everybody knows it’s utterly absurd to think he could have done it. Man from outside. Burglar. That’s the only possible answer.’ Flora turned to look at him.

‘You really think so?’ ‘Don’t you?’ said Blunt quickly.

‘I - oh, yes, of course.’ Another silence, and then Flora burst out: ‘I’m - I’ll tell you why I felt so happy this morning. However heartless you think me, I’d rather tell you. It’s because the lawyer has been - Mr Hammond. He told us about the will. Uncle Roger has left me twenty thousand pounds. Think of it - twenty thousand beautiful pounds.’ Blunt looked surprised.

‘Does it mean so much to you?’ ‘Mean much to me? Why, it’s everything. Freedom - life - no more scheming and scraping and lying ‘ ‘Lying?’ said Blunt, sharply interrupting.

Flora seemed taken aback for a minute.

‘You know what I mean,’ she said uncertainly. ‘Pretending to be thankful for all the nasty cast-off things rich relations give you. Last year’s coat and skirts and hats.’ ‘Don’t know much about ladies’ clothes; should have said you were always very well dressed.’ ‘It cost me something, though,’ said Flora in a low voice.

‘Don’t let’s talk of horrid things. I’m so happy. I’m free.

Free to do what I like. Free not to—’ She stopped suddenly.

‘Not to what?’ asked Blunt quickly.

‘I forget now. Nothing important.’ Blunt had a stick in his hand, and he poked it into the pond, stirring something.

‘What are you doing, Major Blunt?’ ‘There’s something shiny down there. I wondered what it was - looks like a gold brooch. Now I’ve stirred up the mud, and it’s gone.’ ‘Perhaps it’s a crown,’ suggested Flora. ‘Like the one Melisande saw in the water.’ ‘Melisande,’ said Blunt thoughtfully - ‘she’s in an opera, isn’t she?’ ‘Yes, you seem to know a lot about operas.’ ‘People take me sometimes,’ said Blunt sadly. ‘Strange idea of fun - worse noise than the locals make with their drums.’ Flora laughed.

‘I remember Melisande,’ continued Blunt, ‘married an old man old enough to be her father.’ He threw a small piece of flint into the goldfish pond.

Then, changing his tone, he turned to Flora.

‘Miss Ackroyd, can I help with anything? About Paton, I mean.

I know how worried you must be.’ ‘Thank you,’ said Flora in a cold voice. ‘There’s really nothing to be done. Ralph will be all right. I’ve hired the best detective in the world, and he’s going to find out everything.’ For some time, I had felt uneasy about where we were. We weren't exactly eavesdropping, since the two in the garden below only had to look up to see us. Still, I should have made our presence known sooner, but my companion had warned me to be quiet.

Clearly, he wanted me to stay silent. Now, however, he moved quickly.

He rose to his feet, clearing his throat.

‘I must beg your pardon,’ he cried. ‘I can't let mademoiselle praise me so highly without acknowledging I'm here. They say you never hear good about yourself when you're eavesdropping, but that's not true this time.

To save me from embarrassment, I must join you and say sorry.’ He hurried down the path with me right behind him, and joined the others by the pond.

‘This is M. Hercule Poirot,’ said Flora. ‘I'm sure you've heard of him.’ Poirot bowed.

‘I know Major Blunt by reputation,’ he said politely. ‘I'm glad to have met you, monsieur. I need some information that you can give me.’ Blunt looked at him questioningly.

‘When did you last see M. Ackroyd alive?’ ‘At dinner.’ ‘And you didn't see or hear anything from him after that?’ ‘Didn't see him. Heard his voice.’ ‘How was that?’ ‘I walked out on the terrace ‘ ‘Excuse me, what time was that?’ ‘About half-past nine. I was walking back and forth smoking in front of the drawing-room window. I heard Ackroyd talking in his study -’ Poirot stopped and pulled off a tiny weed.

‘Surely you couldn't hear voices in the study from that part of the terrace,’ he murmured.

He wasn't looking at Blunt, but I was, and to my surprise, I saw him blush.

‘Went as far as the corner,’ he explained reluctantly.

‘Ah! indeed?’ said Poirot.

In the gentlest way, he suggested that he wanted more information.

'Thought I saw - a woman disappearing into the bushes.

Just a flash of white, you know. Must have been wrong.

I was standing at the corner of the terrace when I heard Ackroyd's voice talking to his secretary.’ ‘Talking to Mr. Geoffrey Raymond?’ ‘Yes - that's what I thought then. Seems I was wrong.’ ‘Mr. Ackroyd didn’t say his name?’ ‘Oh, no.’ ‘Then, if I may ask, why did you think - ?’ Blunt explained carefully.

‘I assumed it was Raymond, because he said just before I came out that he was taking some papers to Ackroyd. I didn’t think it could be anyone else.’ ‘Can you remember what you heard them say?’ ‘I'm afraid I can’t. Something quite normal and unimportant.

I only caught a bit of it. I was thinking about something else then.’ ‘It doesn't matter,’ murmured Poirot. ‘Did you move a chair back against the wall when you went into the study after the body was found?’ ‘Chair? No, why would I?’ Poirot shrugged, but didn’t answer. He turned to Flora.

‘There’s something I want to know from you, mademoiselle. When you were looking at the things on the silver table with Dr. Sheppard, was the dagger there, or not?’ Flora lifted her chin.

‘Inspector Raglan’s been asking me that,’ she said, annoyed. ‘I’ve told him, and I’ll tell you. I’m sure the dagger wasn’t there. He thinks it was, and that Ralph took it later that night. And - and he doesn’t believe me. He thinks I'm saying it to protect Ralph.’ ‘And aren’t you?’ I asked seriously.

Flora stamped her foot.

‘You, too, Dr. Sheppard! Oh! This is awful.’ Poirot changed the subject smoothly.

‘It’s true what I heard you say, Major Blunt. There’s something shiny in this pond. Let's see if I can get it.’ He knelt by the pond, rolling up his sleeve to his elbow, and slowly put his arm in, careful not to stir up the bottom of the pond. But even with his care, the mud swirled around, and he had to pull his arm out with nothing in it.

He gazed sadly at the mud on his arm. I offered him my handkerchief, which he took with many thanks. Blunt looked at his watch.

‘Nearly lunch time,’ he said. ‘We’d better get back to the house.’ ‘Will you have lunch with us, M. Poirot?’ asked Flora. ‘I’d like you to meet my mother. She is - very fond of Ralph.’ The little man bowed.

‘I would be delighted, mademoiselle.’ ‘And you’ll stay too, won’t you, Dr Sheppard?’ I hesitated.

‘Oh, do!’ I wanted to, so I accepted the invitation right away.

We started towards the house, Flora and Blunt walking ahead.

‘Such hair,’ said Poirot to me quietly, nodding towards Flora. ‘Real gold! They will make a nice couple. She and the dark, handsome Captain Paton. Won't they?’ I looked at him curiously, but he started to fuss about a few tiny drops of water on his coat sleeve. The man reminded me of a cat in some ways, with his green eyes and his fussy habits.

‘And all for nothing, too,’ I said kindly. ‘I wonder what was in the pond?’ ‘Would you like to see?’ asked Poirot.

I stared at him. He nodded.

‘My good friend,’ he said gently and with a hint of blame, ‘Hercule Poirot doesn’t risk messing up his clothes without being sure of getting what he wants. It would be silly and pointless. I am never silly.’ ‘But you brought your hand out empty,’ I said.

‘Sometimes it's important to be careful.’

Do you tell your patients everything - but everything, doctor? I think not. Nor do you tell your excellent sister everything either, isn’t that right? Before showing my empty hand, I moved what was in it to my other hand. You shall see what it was.’ He held out his left hand, palm open. On it was a small gold ring. A woman’s wedding ring.

I took it from him.

‘Look inside,’ ordered Poirot.

I did. Inside was a message in neat writing: From R., March 13th.

I looked at Poirot, but he was busy checking his appearance in a small pocket mirror. He focused on his moustaches, and not at all on me. I saw that he didn’t plan to talk.
