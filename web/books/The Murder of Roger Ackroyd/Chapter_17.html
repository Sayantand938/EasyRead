The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 17

Parker

It struck me the next morning that, feeling so good from Tin-ho or the Perfect Winning, I might have talked too much. It's true, Poirot hadn't told me to keep the ring a secret. But he hadn't mentioned it at Fernly, and I thought I was the only one who knew it had been found. I felt really bad. The news was now all over King’s Abbot. I expected Poirot to tell me off any minute.

The funeral for Mrs. Ferrars and Roger Ackroyd was set for eleven o’clock. It was a sad and grand event. Everyone from Fernly was there.

After it ended, Poirot, who was also there, took my arm and asked me to come back to The Larches with him. He looked very serious, and I worried he'd heard about my loose talk the night before. But I soon realized he was thinking about something completely different.

‘Listen,’ he said. ‘We must do something. With your help, I want to question someone. We'll scare him so much that the truth will come out.’ ‘Who are you talking about?’ I asked, very surprised.

‘Parker!’ said Poirot. ‘I asked him to come to my house at twelve o’clock this morning. He should be waiting for us there right now.’ ‘What do you think?’ I asked, looking at his face.

‘I know this - I'm not satisfied.’ ‘You think he blackmailed Mrs. Ferrars?’ ‘Either that, or ‘ ‘Well?’ I said, after a few minutes.

‘My friend, I'll tell you this - I hope it was him.’ His serious manner, and something else I couldn't quite describe, made me quiet.

When we got to The Larches, we were told that Parker was already there waiting for us. As we walked into the room, the butler stood up politely.

‘Good morning, Parker,’ said Poirot pleasantly. ‘Just a moment, if you please.’ He took off his overcoat and gloves.

‘But I never hurt a hair on Mr Ackroyd’s head,’ he moaned.

‘Honest to God, sir, I didn’t. I’ve been afraid of this coming all the time. And I tell you I didn’t - I didn’t kill him.’ His voice rose almost to a scream.

And when I did sneak along once or twice to the study it was no use. Parker returned his gaze earnestly.

‘I hope you believe me, sir. I’ve been afraid all along the police would dig up that old business with Major Ellerby and suspect me because of it.’ ‘Eh bien,’ said Poirot at last. ‘I am inclined to believe you.

Poirot got straight to the point.

‘Sir, I’d like some information from you, if you’d be so kind as to give it to me. You represented the late Mrs. Ferrars of King’s Paddock, I believe?’ I noticed a quick flash of surprise in the lawyer’s eyes, before his professional composure settled back like a mask over his face.

‘Indeed. All her business matters were handled by us.’ ‘Very good. Now, before I ask you to tell me anything, I’d like you to listen to the story Dr. Sheppard will tell you. Hammond listened very attentively.

But Caroline rarely stays quiet for long. She boldly told Poirot that even though James made fun of her, she was a strict vegetarian. She raved about how wonderful nut cutlets were (which I’m sure she’s never tried) and ate a Welsh rarebit with gusto, often commenting on how dangerous ‘meat’ was.
