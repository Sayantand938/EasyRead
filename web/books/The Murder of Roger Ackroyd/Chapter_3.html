Chapter 3

The Man Who Grew Vegetable Marrows

I told Caroline at lunch that I was going to Fernly for dinner. She didn't mind - quite the opposite.

‘Excellent,’ she said. ‘You’ll hear all about it. By the way, what's wrong with Ralph?’ ^ ‘With Ralph?’ I asked, surprised; ‘there isn’t anything.’ ‘Then why is he staying at the Three Boars instead of at Fernly Park?’ I didn't doubt for a second that Ralph Paton was staying at the local inn. If Caroline said so, it was true.

‘Ackroyd told me he was in London,’ I said. In my surprise, I broke my own rule of never giving away information.

‘Oh!’ said Caroline. I could see her nose twitching as she thought about this.

‘He arrived at the Three Boars yesterday morning,’ she said. ‘And he’s still there. Last night he was out with a girl.’ That didn't surprise me at all. Ralph, I should say, is out with a girl most nights. But I did wonder why he was doing it in King’s Abbot instead of in London.

‘One of the barmaids?’ I asked.

‘No. That’s the thing. He went out to meet her. I don’t know who she is.’ (It was hard for Caroline to admit that.) ‘But I can guess,’ my tireless sister continued.

I waited patiently.

‘His cousin.’ ‘Flora Ackroyd?’ I asked, surprised.

Flora Ackroyd isn't really related to Ralph Paton at all. But Ralph has been seen as almost Ackroyd's own son for so long, that everyone just assumes they're cousins.

‘Flora Ackroyd,’ said my sister.

‘But why not go to Fernly if he wanted to see her?’ ‘Secretly engaged,’ said Caroline, clearly enjoying the gossip.

‘Old Ackroyd won’t allow it, so they have to meet like this.’ I saw problems with Caroline’s idea, but I didn't bother mentioning them. A simple comment about our new neighbour changed the subject.

The house next door, The Larches, has recently been rented by a stranger. Caroline is very annoyed that she hasn't been able to learn anything about him, except that he's a foreigner. Her usual sources have failed her. The man probably gets milk, vegetables, meat, and fish just like everyone else, but none of the people who deliver these things seem to have found out any information.

His name, it seems, is Mr Porrott – a name that feels strangely unreal. The only thing we know about him is that he likes growing vegetable marrows.

But that's not the kind of information Caroline wants. She wants to know where he's from, what he does, if he's married, what his wife was (or is) like, if he has children, what his mother's maiden name was – and so on. I think someone like Caroline must have created the questions on passports.

‘My dear Caroline,’ I said. ‘There’s no question about what the man used to do. He’s a retired hairdresser. Look at his moustache.’ Caroline disagreed. She said that if the man was a hairdresser, he would have wavy hair, not straight hair. All hairdressers did.

I mentioned several hairdressers I knew who had straight hair, but Caroline refused to believe me.

‘I can’t understand him at all,’ she said, sounding annoyed. ‘I borrowed some garden tools the other day, and he was very polite, but I couldn’t get any information out of him. I asked him directly if he was French, and he said he wasn’t - and, for some reason, I didn’t want to ask him anything else.’ I started to become more interested in our mysterious neighbour. A man who can silence Caroline and send her away unsatisfied, like the Queen of Sheba, must be quite someone.

‘I think,’ said Caroline, ‘that he’s got one of those new vacuum cleaners.’ I saw her planning to borrow it and ask more questions. I saw my chance to escape into the garden. I quite like gardening. I was busy pulling up dandelion roots when a shout of warning came from nearby, and something heavy flew past my head and landed at my feet with a disgusting squelch. It was a vegetable marrow!

I looked up angrily. Over the wall, to my left, a face appeared. An egg-shaped head, with hair that looked suspiciously black, huge moustaches, and watchful eyes. It was our mysterious neighbour, Mr Porrott.

He immediately started apologizing. ‘Please forgive me a thousand times, sir. I have no excuse. I have been growing marrows for some months now. This morning, I suddenly became very angry with these marrows. I imagined them going for a walk - and then, unfortunately, I made it real. I grabbed the biggest one. I threw it over the wall. Sir, I am ashamed. I beg your pardon.’ With such sincere apologies, my anger had to disappear. After all, the vegetable hadn’t hit me. But I really hoped that throwing large vegetables over walls wasn’t our new friend’s hobby. Such a habit wouldn’t make him a very good neighbour.

The strange little man seemed to read my thoughts.

‘Ah! no,’ he exclaimed. ‘Don’t worry. I don’t usually do this. But you can imagine, sir, that a man might work towards a certain goal, he might struggle and work hard to achieve a certain kind of rest and activity, and then find that, after all, he misses the old busy days, and the old jobs that he was so happy to leave behind?’ ‘Yes,’ I said slowly. ‘I think that happens quite often. I’m probably an example myself. A year ago, I inherited some money - enough to make my dream come true. I’ve always wanted to travel, to see the world. Well, that was a year ago, as I said, and - I’m still here.’ My little neighbour nodded.

‘The chains of habit. We work to achieve something, and once we have it, we realize that what we miss is the daily work. And remember, sir, my work was interesting work. The most interesting work in the world.’ ‘Yes?’ I said encouragingly. For the moment, I was feeling like Caroline.

‘The study of human nature, sir!’ ‘Indeed,’ I said kindly.

Clearly a retired hairdresser. Who knows human nature better than a hairdresser?

‘Also, I had a friend - a friend who was always by my side for many years. Sometimes he was so stupid it was frightening, but I loved him very much. Imagine that I even miss his stupidity. His innocence, his honest way of seeing things, the joy of impressing and surprising him with my superior abilities - I miss all of this more than I can say.’ ‘He died?’ I asked sympathetically.

‘Not so. He’s alive and doing well - but on the other side of the world. He’s now in Argentina.’ ‘In Argentina,’ I said, feeling envious.

I’ve always wanted to go to South America. I sighed, and then looked up to see Mr. Porrott watching me with sympathy. He seemed like a kind, understanding man.

‘Will you go there, yes?’ he asked.

I shook my head with a sigh.

‘I could have gone,’ I said. ‘A year ago. But I was foolish, and worse than foolish - greedy. I risked what I had for something I didn’t.’ ‘I understand,’ said Mr. Porrott. ‘You invested?’ I nodded sadly, but I couldn’t help feeling secretly amused. This funny little man was so seriously important.

‘Not the Porcupine Oilfields?’ he asked suddenly.

I stared.

‘I thought about them, actually, but in the end, I chose a gold mine in Western Australia.’ My neighbor was looking at me with a strange expression I couldn’t understand.

‘It is Fate,’ he said at last.

‘What is Fate?’ I asked, annoyed.

‘To think I live next to a man who seriously considers Porcupine Oilfields, and also West Australian Gold Mines.

Tell me, do you also like auburn hair?’ I stared at him, speechless, and he laughed.

‘No, no, I'm not crazy. Don't worry. That was a silly question. You see, my friend was a young man who thought all women good, and most of them beautiful. But you are middle-aged, a doctor, someone who knows life's follies and vanities. Well, well, we are neighbours. Please give your excellent sister my best marrow.’ He bent down and grandly presented a huge one, which I accepted in the same spirit.

‘Indeed,’ said the little man happily, ‘this morning hasn't been wasted. I've met someone who reminds me of my friend. By the way, who is the young man with the dark hair and eyes, and the handsome face? He walks with his head back and a smile?’ I knew who he meant.

‘That must be Captain Ralph Paton,’ I said slowly.

‘I haven't seen him around before?’ ‘No, he's been away. He's the adopted son of Mr. Ackroyd of Fernly Park.’ My neighbour seemed a little impatient.

‘Of course, I should have guessed. Mr. Ackroyd mentioned him often.’ ‘You know Mr. Ackroyd?’ I asked, surprised.

‘Mr. Ackroyd knew me in London when I worked there. I asked him not to mention my job here.’ ‘I see,’ I said, amused by his snobbery.

But the little man went on with a grand smile.

‘One prefers to be unknown. I don't want fame. I haven't even bothered to correct how people say my name.’ ‘Indeed,’ I said, unsure what to say.

‘Captain Ralph Paton,’ Mr Porrott mused. ‘So he’s engaged to Mr Ackroyd’s niece, the charming Miss Flora.’ ‘Who told you that?’ I asked, very surprised.

‘Mr Ackroyd. About a week ago. He’s very happy about it - has wanted it for a long time, or so I understood. I even think he pushed the young man a bit. That’s never wise. A young man should marry who he wants - not to please a stepfather he hopes to get money from.’ My ideas were completely confused. I couldn't imagine Ackroyd confiding in a hairdresser and discussing his niece and stepson's marriage. Ackroyd is kindly to those he sees as beneath him, but he’s very aware of his own importance. I began to think Porrott wasn’t a hairdresser after all.

To hide my confusion, I said the first thing I thought of.

‘What made you notice Ralph Paton? His good looks?’ ‘No, not just that - though he is very good-looking for an Englishman - what your lady novelists would call a Greek God. No, there was something about that young man I didn't understand.’ He said the last sentence thoughtfully, which made a strange impression on me. It was as if he was judging the boy using some secret knowledge I didn’t have. That impression stayed with me, because just then my sister called me from the house.

I went in. Caroline had her hat on and had clearly just come from the village. She started right away.

‘I stopped him, of course, but he seemed in a big hurry and wanted to leave.’ I’m sure that was true. He felt about Caroline much like he felt about Miss Gannett earlier - maybe even more. Caroline is harder to get rid of.

Mistaken!’ ‘Ridiculous,’ I said. ‘He should know you better.’ ‘Then he told me that Ralph and Flora are engaged.’ ‘I knew that,’ I interrupted, with modest pride.

‘Who told you?’ ‘Our new neighbour.’ Caroline hesitated for a moment, like a roulette ball deciding between two numbers.

Then she ignored the tempting distraction.

‘I told Mr. Ackroyd that Ralph was staying at the Three Boars.’ ‘Caroline,’ I said, ‘do you never think that you might cause trouble by repeating everything you hear?’ ‘Nonsense,’ said my sister. ‘People should know things. I think it’s my duty to tell them. Mr. Ackroyd was very grateful to me.’ ‘Well,’ I said, expecting more to come.

Caroline blushed.

‘It was such a lovely day,’ she said. ‘I thought I’d take a short walk. The woods with their autumn colors are so beautiful this time of year.’ Caroline doesn't care about woods at any time of year. Usually, she thinks of them as places where you get your feet wet, and where unpleasant things might fall on your head. No, it was a strong, natural instinct that took her to our local wood. It’s the only place near King’s Abbot where you can talk to a young woman without the whole village seeing you.

It’s next to Fernly Park.

‘Well,’ I said, ‘go on.’ ‘As I said, I was walking back through the wood when I heard voices.’ Caroline stopped.

‘Yes?’ ‘One was Ralph Paton’s - I knew it right away. The other was a girl’s. Of course, I didn’t mean to listen ‘ ‘Of course not,’ I said, with obvious sarcasm that Caroline didn’t notice.

‘But I couldn’t help overhearing. The girl said something - I didn’t quite hear what it was, and Ralph answered. He sounded very angry. “My dear girl,” he said.

“Don’t you realize that it’s possible the old man will leave me almost nothing? He’s been pretty annoyed with me for the last few years. A little more and he will. And we need the money, my dear. ‘But if it wasn’t Flora, who could it have been?’ My sister quickly listed the young women living nearby, with many reasons for and against each one.

When she stopped to breathe, I mumbled something about a patient and slipped out.

I decided to go to the Three Boars. It seemed likely that Ralph Paton would be back there by now.

I knew Ralph very well – perhaps better than anyone else in King’s Abbot, because I had known his mother before him. Because of this, I understood things about him that confused others. He was, in some ways, a victim of his family history.

He hadn't inherited his mother’s weakness for drink, but he still had a kind of weakness in him.

As my new friend from this morning had said, he was very handsome. Just under six feet tall, perfectly built, with the easy grace of an athlete, he was dark, like his mother, with a handsome, tanned face that was always ready to smile. Ralph Paton was one of those people who could charm others easily and without trying. He was self-indulgent and spent too much money, and he didn't respect anything, but he was still lovable, and his friends were all loyal to him.

Could I help the boy in any way? I thought I could.

When I asked at the Three Boars, I learned that Captain Paton had just arrived. I went up to his room and walked in without knocking.

For a moment, remembering what I had heard and seen, I wasn't sure how he would react, but I didn't need to worry.

'Why, it’s Sheppard! Good to see you.' He came forward to greet me, his hand outstretched, a bright smile lighting up his face.

'The only person I'm happy to see in this awful place.' I raised my eyebrows.

‘How's the place been?’ He gave a frustrated laugh.

‘It’s a long story. Things haven’t been good for me, doctor. But have a drink, won’t you?’ ‘Thanks,’ I said, ‘I will.’ He pressed the bell, then coming back threw himself into a chair.

‘To be blunt,’ he said gloomily, ‘I’m in a terrible mess. In fact, I haven’t the slightest idea what to do next.’ ‘What’s the matter?’ I asked kindly.

‘It’s my blasted stepfather.’ ‘What has he done?’ ‘It isn’t what he’s done yet, but what he’s likely to do.’ The bell was answered, and Ralph ordered the drinks.

When the man had gone again, he sat hunched in the armchair, frowning to himself.

‘Is it really - serious?’ I asked.

He nodded.

‘I’m really up against it this time,’ he said seriously.

The unusual seriousness in his voice told me that he spoke the truth. It took a lot to make Ralph serious.

‘In fact,’ he continued, ‘I can’t see what to do... I’m damned if I can.’ ‘If I could help -’ I suggested hesitantly.

But he shook his head firmly.

‘Kind of you, doctor. But I can’t tell you about this. I have to do this alone.’ He was quiet for a minute and then said again in a slightly different way: ‘Yes - I have to do this alone...’?
