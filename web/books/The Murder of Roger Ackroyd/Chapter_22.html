The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 22

Ursula’s Story

For a moment or two the girl looked silently at Poirot. Then, her composure collapsing completely, she nodded once, and burst into sobs.

Caroline pushed past me, and putting her arm round the girl, patted her on the shoulder.

‘There, there, my dear,’ she said gently, ‘it will be all right. You’ll see - everything will be all right.’ Hidden under curiosity and gossip there is a lot of kindness in Caroline. For the moment, even the interest of Poirot’s news was lost in the sight of the girl’s distress.

Presently Ursula sat up and wiped her eyes.

‘This is very weak and silly of me,’ she said.

‘No, no, my child,’ said Poirot kindly. ‘We can all understand the stress of this last week.’ ‘It must have been a terrible experience,’ I said.

‘And then to find that you knew,’ continued Ursula.

‘How did you know? Was it Ralph who told you?’ Poirot shook his head.

‘You know why I came to you tonight,’ the girl continued. ‘This-’ She held out a crumpled newspaper, and I recognized the article Poirot had put in.

‘It says Ralph has been arrested. So it’s all pointless. I don’t need to pretend anymore.’ ‘Newspaper articles aren't always true, mademoiselle,’ Poirot murmured, looking somewhat ashamed. ‘Still, I think you should tell the truth. That's what we need now.’ The girl hesitated, looking at him doubtfully.

‘You don’t trust me,’ Poirot said gently. ‘Yet you came here to find me, didn't you? Why?’ ‘Because I don’t believe Ralph did it,’ the girl said softly. ‘And I think you're clever and will find out the truth. And also ‘ ‘Yes?’ ‘I think you're kind.’ Poirot nodded several times.

‘That is very good - yes, very good. Listen, I truly believe your husband is innocent - but things look bad. If I am to save him, I must know everything - even if it makes him look even more guilty.’ ‘You understand so well,’ said Ursula.

‘So you will tell me the whole story, yes? From the beginning.’ ‘You're not going to send me away, I hope,’ said Caroline, settling into an armchair. ‘What I want to know,’ she continued, ‘is why this girl was pretending to be a parlourmaid?’ ‘Pretending?’ I asked.

‘Yes, pretending. Why did you do it, child? Was it a bet?’ ‘For a living,’ said Ursula dryly.

And encouraged, she began the story which I will tell in my own words.

Ursula Bourne, it seemed, came from a family of seven poor Irish gentry. When her father died, most of the girls had to go out and earn their own living. Ursula’s eldest sister was married to Captain Folliott. She was the one I had seen that Sunday, and her embarrassment was now clear. Determined to earn her living and not wanting to be a nursery governess - the only job available to an untrained girl, Ursula preferred being a parlourmaid. She refused to call herself a ‘lady parlourmaid.’ She would be the real thing, her sister providing a reference. At Fernly, despite her aloofness, which caused some comment, she was good at her job - quick, competent, and thorough.

‘I enjoyed the work,’ she explained. ‘And I had plenty of time to myself.’ Then she met Ralph Paton, and they had a love affair that led to a secret marriage. Ralph had persuaded her, somewhat against her wishes. He said his stepfather wouldn't approve of him marrying a penniless girl. It was better to marry in secret and tell him later when the time was right.

So they did, and Ursula Bourne became Ursula Paton. Ralph said he would pay off his debts, find a job, and then, when he could support her and was independent of his stepfather, they would tell him.

But for people like Ralph Paton, starting fresh is easier said than done. He hoped that his stepfather, still unaware of the marriage, might be convinced to pay his debts and help him get back on his feet.

But the extent of Ralph’s debts only angered Roger Ackroyd, and he refused to do anything. Some months passed, and then Ralph was invited back to Fernly. Roger Ackroyd got straight to the point. He wanted Ralph to marry Flora, and he made his wishes clear to the young man.

And here, Ralph Paton's inherent weakness surfaced. As always, he chose the easy, immediate solution. As far as I could tell, neither Flora nor Ralph pretended to be in love. It was a business deal for both of them. Roger Ackroyd stated his wishes, and they agreed. Flora saw it as a chance for freedom, money, and a broader life. Ralph, of course, had different motives. But he was in a very difficult financial situation. He jumped at the opportunity. His debts would be paid. He could start over with a clean slate. He didn't tend to think about the future, but I think he vaguely imagined breaking off the engagement with Flora after a suitable amount of time had passed. Both Flora and he insisted that it be kept secret for now. He wanted to hide it from Ursula. He instinctively felt that her strong, resolute nature, with its dislike for deceit, would not approve of such a plan.

Then came the critical moment when Roger Ackroyd, always bossy, decided to announce the engagement.

He said nothing of his intention to Ralph—only to Flora, and Flora, indifferent, didn't object. The news hit Ursula hard. Summoned by her, Ralph hurried down from town. They met in the woods, where my sister overheard part of their conversation. Ralph begged her to remain silent for a while longer. Ursula was equally determined to end the secrecy.

She would tell Mr. Ackroyd the truth without delay. Husband and wife parted bitterly.

Ursula, firm in her resolve, sought an interview with Roger Ackroyd that very afternoon and revealed the truth to him. Their interview was stormy—it might have been even more so if Roger Ackroyd hadn't already been preoccupied with his own problems. It was bad enough, however. Ackroyd wasn't the kind of man to forgive the deception. His anger was mainly directed at Ralph, but Ursula also received her share, since he saw her as a girl who had deliberately tried to 'trap' the adopted son of a very rich man. Unforgivable things were said by both.

That same evening, Ursula met Ralph by appointment in the small summerhouse, sneaking out of the house through the side door. Their interview consisted of mutual reproaches. Ralph accused Ursula of having ruined his prospects beyond repair by her poorly timed revelation.

Ursula reproached Ralph for his deceit.

They parted at last. A little over half an hour later came the discovery of Roger Ackroyd’s body. Since that night, Ursula had neither seen nor heard from Ralph.

As the story went on, I saw more and more how serious the facts were. If Ackroyd were alive, he would have changed his will - I knew him well enough to know that would be the first thing he'd do. His death happened just in time for Ralph and Ursula Paton. It was no wonder the girl had kept quiet and played her part so well.

My thoughts were interrupted. It was Poirot speaking, and I knew from his serious tone that he also understood the importance of the situation.

‘Mademoiselle, I must ask you one question, and you must answer it honestly, because everything depends on it: What time did you leave Captain Ralph Paton in the summer-house? Now, take a moment to be very precise with your answer.’ The girl gave a laugh that sounded very bitter.

‘Do you think I haven’t thought about that over and over? It was exactly half-past nine when I went out to meet him. Major Blunt was walking on the terrace, so I had to go around through the bushes to avoid him. It must have been about twenty-seven minutes to ten when I got to the summer-house. Ralph was waiting for me. I was with him ten minutes - no longer, because it was exactly a quarter to ten when I got back to the house.’ I now understood why she had been so insistent the other day. She wanted Ackroyd to have been killed before a quarter to ten, not after.

I saw that thought reflected in Poirot’s next question.

‘Who left the summer-house first?’ ‘I did.’ ‘Leaving Ralph Paton in the summer-house?’ ‘Yes - but you don’t think ‘ ‘Mademoiselle, it doesn't matter what I think. What did you do when you got back to the house?’ ‘I went up to my room.’ ‘And stayed there until when?’ ‘Until about ten o’clock.’ ‘Can anyone prove that?’ ‘Prove? That I was in my room, you mean? Oh! no. But surely - oh! I see, they might think - they might think ‘ I saw the horror dawn in her eyes.

Poirot finished the sentence for her.

‘That you were the one who entered through the window and stabbed Mr Ackroyd as he sat in his chair? Yes, they might think that.’ ‘Only a fool would think such a thing,’ said Caroline angrily.

She patted Ursula on the shoulder.

The girl had her face hidden in her hands.

‘Horrible,’ she was murmuring. ‘Horrible.’ Caroline gave her a friendly shake.

‘Don’t worry, my dear,’ she said. ‘M. Poirot doesn’t think that really. As for that husband of yours, I don’t think much of him, and I’m telling you straight. Running away and leaving you to face the music.’ But Ursula shook her head strongly.

‘Oh, no,’ she cried. ‘It wasn’t like that at all. Ralph wouldn’t run away for himself. I see now. If he heard of his stepfather’s murder, he might think I had done it.’ ‘He wouldn’t think any such thing,’ said Caroline.

‘I was so cruel to him that night - so hard and bitter. I wouldn’t listen to what he was trying to say - wouldn’t believe that he really cared. I just stood there telling him what I thought of him, and saying the coldest, cruellest things that came into my mind - trying my best to hurt him.’ ‘Don’t worry about him,’ said Caroline. ‘Never worry about what you say to a man. They’re so full of themselves that they never believe you mean it if it’s unflattering.’ Ursula went on nervously twisting and untwisting her hands.

‘When the murder was discovered and he didn’t come forward, I was terribly upset. Just for a moment I wondered - but then I knew he couldn’t - he couldn’t... But I wished he would come forward and say openly that he’d had nothing to do with it. I knew that he was fond of Dr Sheppard, and I thought that perhaps Dr Sheppard might know where he was hiding.’ She turned to me.

‘That’s why I said what I did to you that day. I thought, if you knew where he was, you might pass on the message to him.’ ‘I?’ I exclaimed.

‘Why should James know where he was?’ demanded Caroline sharply.

‘It was very unlikely, I know,’ admitted Ursula, ‘but Ralph had often spoken of Dr Sheppard, and I knew that he would probably think of him as his best friend in King’s Abbot.’ ‘My dear child,’ I said, ‘I have no idea where Ralph Paton is right now.’ ‘That is true enough,’ said Poirot.

‘But -’ Ursula held out the newspaper cutting in a puzzled way.

‘Ah! that,’ said Poirot, slightly embarrassed; ‘a trifle, mademoiselle. Nothing at all. I don’t believe for a moment that Ralph Paton has been arrested.’ ‘But then -’ began the girl slowly.

Poirot continued quickly: ‘There is one thing I’d like to know - did Captain Paton wear shoes or boots that night?’ Ursula shook her head.

‘I can’t remember.’ ‘A pity! But how would you? Now, madame,’ he smiled at her, his head tilted, his finger waving meaningfully, ‘no questions. And don’t worry yourself. Be brave, and trust in Hercule Poirot.’
