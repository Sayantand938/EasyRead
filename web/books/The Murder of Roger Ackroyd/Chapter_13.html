The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 13

The Goose Quill

That evening, because Poirot asked, I went to his house after dinner. Caroline watched me leave, clearly unwilling.

I think she would have liked to come with me.

Poirot welcomed me warmly. He had put a bottle of Irish whiskey (which I hate) on a small table, with soda water and a glass. He was making hot chocolate. I learned later it was one of his favourite drinks.

He asked politely about my sister, saying she was a very interesting woman.

‘I think you’ve made her conceited,’ I said dryly. ‘What about Sunday afternoon?’ He laughed and smiled knowingly.

‘I like to use the expert,’ he said mysteriously, but he wouldn't explain.

‘You got all the local gossip anyway,’ I said. ‘True, and untrue.’ ‘And a lot of useful information,’ he added quietly.

‘Such as-’ He shook his head.

‘Why didn't you just tell me the truth?’ he replied. ‘In a place like this, everyone knew what Ralph Paton was up to. If your sister hadn't walked through the woods that day, someone else would have.’ ‘I guess so,’ I said, annoyed. ‘What's with your interest in my patients?’ He twinkled again.

‘Only one of them, doctor. Only one.’ ‘The last one?’ I guessed.

He held out the little quill to me. I looked at it, curious.

Then I remembered something I had read.

Poirot, who had been watching my face, nodded.

‘Yes, heroin, “snow.” Drug users carry it like this and sniff it up their nose.’ ‘Diamorphine hydrochloride,’ I murmured, without thinking.

‘This way of taking the drug is very common overseas. Another piece of evidence, if we needed it, that the man came from Canada or the States.’ ‘What made you notice that summerhouse in the first place?’ I asked, curious.

‘My friend the inspector assumed that anyone using that path was taking a shortcut to the house. But as soon as I saw the summerhouse, I realized that anyone using it as a meeting place would take the same path. Now, it seems pretty clear that the stranger didn't come to the front or back door. So, did someone from the house go out to meet him? If so, what could be a better place than that little summerhouse?

I searched it, hoping to find a clue inside. I found two things: the scrap of fabric and the quill.’ ‘And the scrap of fabric?’ I asked, curious. ‘What about it?’ Poirot raised his eyebrows.

‘You're not using your little grey cells,’ he said dryly.

‘The bit of starched cloth should be obvious.’ ‘Not very obvious to me.’ I changed the subject. ‘Anyway,’ I said, ‘this man went to the summer-house to meet somebody. Who was that somebody?’ ‘Exactly the question,’ said Poirot. ‘You will remember that Mrs Ackroyd and her daughter came over from Canada to live here?’ ‘Is that what you meant today when you accused them of hiding the truth?’ ‘Perhaps. Now another point. What did you think of the parlourmaid’s story?’ ‘What story?’ ‘The story of her dismissal. Does it take half an hour to dismiss a servant? Was the story of those important papers a likely one? And remember, though she says she was in her bedroom from nine-thirty until ten o’clock, there is no one to confirm her statement.’ ‘You confuse me,’ I said.

‘To me it gets clearer. But tell me now your own ideas and theories.’ I drew a piece of paper from my pocket.

‘I just wrote down a few ideas,’ I said apologetically.

‘But excellent - you have method. Let us hear them.’ I read out in a somewhat embarrassed voice.

‘To begin with, one must look at the thing logically ‘ ‘Just what my poor Hastings used to say,’ interrupted Poirot, ‘but alas! he never did so.’ ‘Point No. 1. - Mr Ackroyd was heard talking to someone at half-past nine.

‘Point No. 2. - At some time during the evening Ralph Paton must have come in through the window, as shown by the prints of his shoes.

‘Point No. 3. - Mr Ackroyd was nervous that evening, and would only have let in someone he knew.

‘Point No. 4. - The person with Mr Ackroyd at nine-thirty was asking for money. We know Ralph Paton was in trouble.

‘ These four points suggest that the person with Mr Ackroyd at nine-thirty was Ralph Paton. But we know that Mr Ackroyd was alive at a quarter to ten, therefore it was not Ralph who killed him. Ralph left the window open. Afterwards the murderer came in that way.’ ‘And who was the murderer?’ inquired Poirot.

‘The American stranger. He may have been working with Parker, and possibly Parker is the man who blackmailed Mrs Ferrars. If so, Parker may have heard enough to realize the game was up, have told his accomplice so, and the latter did the crime with the dagger which Parker gave him.’ ‘It is a theory, that,’ admitted Poirot. ‘You definitely have a certain skill. But it leaves a good deal unexplained.’ ‘Such as ‘ ‘The telephone call, the pushed-out chair ‘ ‘Do you really think that last important?’ I interrupted.

‘Maybe not,’ my friend admitted. ‘It could have been pulled out by accident, and Raymond or Blunt might have pushed it back without realizing it, because they were upset. Also, there’s the missing forty pounds.’ ‘Given by Ackroyd to Ralph,’ I suggested. ‘He might have changed his mind about refusing it.’ ‘That still leaves something unexplained.’ ‘What?’ ‘Why was Blunt so sure that Raymond was with Mr Ackroyd at nine-thirty?’ ‘He explained that,’ I said.

‘Do you think so? I won’t argue. Tell me, instead, why did Ralph Paton disappear?’ ‘That’s harder to explain,’ I said slowly. ‘I have to speak as a doctor. Ralph must have been extremely nervous. If he suddenly found out that his uncle had been murdered shortly after he left – maybe after a heated discussion – he might panic and run away. People have done that – acted like they’re guilty when they’re completely innocent.’ ‘Yes, that’s true,’ said Poirot. ‘But we can’t forget one thing.’ ‘I know what you’re going to say,’ I said: ‘motive.’

Ralph Paton inherits a lot of money when his uncle dies.’ ‘That’s one motive,’ Poirot agreed.

‘One?’ ‘Yes. Do you realize that there are three separate motives right in front of us? Someone definitely stole the blue envelope and what was inside. That’s one motive.

Blackmail! Ralph Paton might have been the one blackmailing Mrs. Ferrars. Remember, Hammond didn’t know that Ralph Paton had asked his uncle for help recently. That suggests he was getting money from somewhere else. Also, he was in some kind of – what do you call it – mess? – that he was afraid his uncle would find out about. And finally, there’s the one you just mentioned.’ ‘Goodness,’ I said, surprised. ‘The evidence really seems to be against him.’ ‘Does it?’ Poirot said. ‘That’s where we disagree. Three motives – it’s almost too much. I’m starting to think that Ralph Paton is innocent after all.’
