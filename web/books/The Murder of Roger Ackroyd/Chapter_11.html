The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 11

Poirot Pays A Call

I felt a little nervous as I rang the bell at Marby Grange the next afternoon. I really wondered what Poirot hoped to learn. He had given me this task. Why? Was it because, like when he questioned Major Blunt, he wanted to stay out of sight? That wish made sense before, but here it seemed pointless.

My thoughts were broken by the arrival of a neat housemaid.

Yes, Mrs Folliott was home. I was led into a large drawing-room and looked around curiously as I waited for the lady of the house. It was a big, empty room with some nice old china, beautiful etchings, and worn covers and curtains. It was clearly a lady's room.

I turned away from looking at a Bartolozzi print on the wall as Mrs Folliott entered. She was a tall woman with messy brown hair and a very charming smile.

'Dr Sheppard,' she said uncertainly.

'That's right,' I replied. 'I'm sorry for visiting you like this, but I needed some information about a former housemaid of yours, Ursula Bourne.' As soon as I said the name, her smile disappeared, and her friendly manner vanished. She looked uneasy and uncomfortable.

'Ursula Bourne?' she said hesitantly.

'Yes,' I said. 'Perhaps you don't recall the name?' 'Oh, yes, of course. I - I remember perfectly.' 'She left you a little over a year ago, I believe?' 'Yes. Yes, she did. That's correct.' 'And were you happy with her work while she was here?

How long was she with you, by the way?’ ‘Oh! a year or two - I can’t remember exactly how long.

She - she is very good. I’m sure you will find her quite satisfactory. I didn’t know she was leaving Fernly. I had no idea.’ ‘Can you tell me anything about her?’ I asked.

‘Anything about her?’ ‘Yes, where she comes from, who her family is - that sort of thing?’ Mrs Folliott’s face looked even more frozen than before.

‘I don’t know at all.’ ‘Who was she with before she came to you?’ ‘I’m afraid I don’t remember.’ There was a hint of anger now beneath her nervousness. She tossed her head in a way that seemed familiar.

‘Is it really necessary to ask all these questions?’ ‘Not at all,’ I said, sounding surprised and a little apologetic. ‘I had no idea you would mind answering them. I am very sorry.’ Her anger faded and she became confused again.

‘Oh! I don’t mind answering them. I promise you I don’t.

Why should I? It - it just seemed a little strange, you know.

That’s all. A little strange.’ One good thing about being a doctor is that you can usually tell when people are lying. I should have known from Mrs Folliott’s manner, if nothing else, that she did mind answering my questions - minded a lot.

She was very uncomfortable and upset, and there was clearly some mystery in the background. I thought she was a woman not used to lying, and so very uneasy when she had to. A child could have seen through her.

But it was also clear that she had no intention of telling me anything more. Whatever the mystery about Ursula Bourne was, I was not going to learn it from Mrs Folliott.

Feeling defeated, I apologized again for bothering her, grabbed my hat, and left.

I visited a couple of patients and got home around six. Caroline was sitting next to the messy tea set.

She had that look of barely hidden excitement that I know so well. It always means she's either gotten or shared some news. I wondered which it was this time.

"I had a very interesting afternoon," Caroline began, as I sank into my favorite armchair and stretched my feet towards the welcoming fire.

"Oh?" I said. "Did Miss Gannett come by for tea?" Miss Gannett is one of our town's biggest gossips.

"Guess again," Caroline said, looking very pleased with herself.

I guessed several times, naming all the members of Caroline's group of informants. My sister shook her head triumphantly at each guess. Finally, she told me herself.

"M. Poirot!" she said. "What do you think of that?" I thought a lot of things, but I kept them to myself.

"Why did he come?" I asked.

"To see me, of course. He said that, knowing my brother so well, he hoped he could meet his charming sister - your charming sister, I got mixed up - but you know what I mean." "What did he talk about?" I asked.

‘He told me a lot about himself and his cases. You know that Prince Paul of Mauretania - the one who’s just married a dancer?’ ‘Yes?’ ‘I saw a really interesting bit about her in Society Snippets the other day, hinting that she was really a Russian Grand Duchess - one of the Czar’s daughters who escaped from the Bolsheviks. Well, it seems that M. Poirot solved a tricky murder mystery that almost involved them both. Prince Paul was so grateful.’ ‘Did he give him an emerald tie pin the size of a plover’s egg?’ I asked sarcastically.

‘He didn’t mention it. Why?’ ‘Nothing,’ I said. ‘I thought it was the usual thing. It is in detective stories anyway. The great detective always has his rooms full of rubies and pearls and emeralds from grateful Royal clients.’ ‘It’s very interesting to hear about these things from someone who knows,’ said my sister smugly.

It would be - to Caroline. I had to admire M. Hercule Poirot's cleverness. He had picked the one case that would most interest an older woman living in a small village.

‘Did he tell you if the dancer was really a Grand Duchess?’ I asked.

‘He wasn’t allowed to say,’ said Caroline importantly.

I wondered how much Poirot had exaggerated when talking to Caroline - probably not at all. He had made his suggestions with his eyebrows and his shoulders.

‘And after all this,’ I said, ‘I suppose you were ready to do anything for him?’ ‘Don’t be rude, James. I don’t know where you learn these crude expressions.’ ‘Probably from my only link with the outside world - my patients. Unfortunately, I don’t treat Royal princes and interesting Russian emigres.’ Caroline pushed her glasses up and looked at me.

‘You seem very bad-tempered, James. It must be your liver. A blue pill, I think, tonight.’ Seeing me at home, you would never guess I was a doctor. Caroline prescribes for both herself and me.

‘Damn my liver,’ I said, annoyed. ‘Did you talk about the murder at all?’ ‘Well, of course, James. What else is there to talk about locally? I was able to correct M. Poirot on several points. He was very grateful to me. He said I had the makings of a natural detective in me - and a wonderful understanding of people.’ Caroline was like a cat that has had too much rich cream. She was practically purring.

‘He talked a lot about the little grey cells of the brain, and what they do. He says his own are excellent.’ ‘He would say that,’ I said bitterly. ‘He’s certainly not modest.’ ‘I wish you wouldn’t be so horribly American, James. He thought it very important that Ralph should be found as soon as possible, and persuaded to come forward and explain himself. He says that his disappearance will create a very bad impression at the inquest.’ ‘And what did you say to that?’ ‘I agreed with him,’ said Caroline importantly. ‘And I was able to tell him what people were already saying about it.’ ‘Caroline,’ I said sharply, ‘did you tell M. Poirot what you overheard in the woods that day?’ ‘I did,’ said Caroline smugly.

I got up and started to walk around.

‘You know what you’re doing, right?’ I blurted out.

‘You’re putting a noose around Ralph Paton’s neck as surely as you’re sitting in that chair.’ ‘Not at all,’ said Caroline, completely calm. ‘I was surprised you hadn’t told him.’ ‘I made sure not to,’ I said. ‘I like that boy.’ ‘So do I. That’s why I say you’re talking nonsense. I don’t believe Ralph did it, so the truth can’t hurt him, and we should give M. Poirot all the help we can. Think, Ralph might have been out with that same girl on the night of the murder, and if so, he has a perfect alibi.’ ‘If he has a perfect alibi,’ I replied, ‘why doesn’t he come forward and say so?’ ‘Might get the girl into trouble,’ said Caroline wisely.

‘But if M. Poirot finds her, and explains that it’s her duty, she’ll come forward and clear Ralph.’ ‘You seem to have made up your own romantic story,’ I said. ‘You read too many silly novels, Caroline.

I’ve always told you so.’ I sat down in my chair again.

‘Did Poirot ask you any other questions?’ I asked.

‘Only about the patients you had that morning.’ ‘The patients?’ I asked, not believing it.

‘Yes, your surgery patients. How many and who they were.’ ‘Are you saying you could tell him that?’ I asked.

Caroline is truly amazing.

‘Why not?’ asked my sister triumphantly. ‘I can see the path up to the surgery door perfectly from this window.

And I’ve got an excellent memory, James. Much better than yours, let me tell you.’ ‘I’m sure you have,’ I murmured automatically.

My sister continued, counting the names on her fingers.

‘There was old Mrs Bennett, and that boy from the farm with the infected finger. Dolly Grice to have a needle taken out of her finger; that American steward from the ship. Let me see - that’s four. Yes, and old George Evans with his ulcer. And lastly -' She paused importantly.

‘Well?’ Caroline revealed her big moment triumphantly. She whispered it dramatically - helped by the number of s’s in the name.

‘Miss Russell!’ She leaned back in her chair and looked at me meaningfully, and when Caroline looks at you meaningfully, it is impossible to miss it.

‘I don’t know what you mean,’ I said, quite dishonestly.

‘Why shouldn’t Miss Russell see me about her bad knee?’ ‘Bad knee,’ said Caroline. “Nonsense! Her knee is as good as yours and mine. She wanted something else.’ ‘What?’ I asked.

Caroline had to admit that she didn’t know.

‘But you can be sure, that was what he was trying to find out - M. Poirot, I mean. There’s something suspicious about that woman, and he knows it.’ ‘Exactly what Mrs Ackroyd said to me yesterday,’ I said. ‘That there was something suspicious about Miss Russell.’ ‘Ah!’ said Caroline darkly, ‘Mrs Ackroyd! There’s another!’ ‘Another what?’ Caroline wouldn't explain what she meant. She just nodded her head a few times, rolled up her knitting, and went upstairs to put on the bright purple silk blouse and the gold locket which she calls dressing for dinner.

I stayed there staring into the fire and thinking about Caroline’s words. Had Poirot really come to get information about Miss Russell, or was it just Caroline’s complicated mind that twisted everything to fit her own ideas?

Miss Russell hadn't acted suspiciously that morning. I did recall her going on and on about drug use - and from there, she'd steered the talk to poisons and how to use them. But that didn't mean anything.

Ackroyd hadn't been poisoned. Even so, it was strange...

I heard Caroline's voice, sharp as could be, calling from the top of the stairs.

'James, you'll be late for dinner.' I added some coal to the fire and went upstairs like a good boy.

Peace at home is worth almost any cost.
