The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 2

Who’s Who in King’s Abbot

Before I go on with what I said to Caroline and what Caroline said to me, I should probably explain our local area.

Our village, King’s Abbot, is like any other village, I suppose. Our nearest big town is Cranchester, nine miles away.

We have a large railway station, a small post office, and two rival 'General Stores.' Young men tend to leave to find work, but we have plenty of unmarried women and retired army officers. Our main hobby is gossip. There are only two important houses in King’s Abbot. One is King’s Paddock, which Mrs Ferrars inherited from her husband. The other, Fernly Park, belongs to Roger Ackroyd. Ackroyd has always struck me as being more like a country squire than any real country squire could be. He reminds me of the red-faced men who always appeared at the start of an old musical comedy, on the village green. They usually sang a song about going to London.

Now we have revues, and the country squire is no longer fashionable in musicals.

Of course, Ackroyd isn't really a country squire. He's a very successful manufacturer of (I think) wagon wheels. He's nearly fifty, with a red face and a friendly manner. He's very close to the vicar, donates generously to the church (though people say he's very tight-fisted with his own money), and supports cricket, youth clubs, and veterans' associations. He's the life and soul of our quiet village.

When Roger Ackroyd was twenty-one, he fell in love with and married a beautiful woman who was about five or six years older than him. Her name was Paton, and she was a widow with a child. The marriage was short and unhappy. To be blunt, Mrs Ackroyd was an alcoholic. She drank herself to death four years after they married.

After that, Ackroyd didn't seem interested in marrying again. His wife's son from her first marriage was only seven when his mother died. He's now twenty-five. Ackroyd has always treated him like his own son, but he's been a wild young man and a constant worry to his stepfather. Still, we all like Ralph Paton in King’s Abbot. He's a very handsome young man, for one thing.

As I said, we love to gossip in our village. Everyone noticed that Ackroyd and Mrs Ferrars got on very well. After her husband died, they became even closer. They were always seen together, and people thought that after her mourning period, Mrs Ferrars would marry Roger Ackroyd. It seemed like a good match. Roger Ackroyd's wife had died of drink, and Ashley Ferrars had been a drunkard for years before his death. It seemed right that these two victims of alcoholism should find happiness together after what they'd been through.

The Ferrars moved here a little over a year ago, but Ackroyd has been the subject of gossip for many years. While Ralph Paton grew up, several lady housekeepers managed Ackroyd’s household, each viewed with suspicion by Caroline and her friends. It’s fair to say that for at least fifteen years, the village expected Ackroyd to marry one of them. The latest, a formidable lady named Miss Russell, has been in charge for five years, twice as long as any before her. People felt that without Mrs. Ferrars' arrival, Ackroyd would have been trapped. That - and another thing - the unexpected arrival of a widowed sister-in-law from Canada with her daughter. Mrs. Cecil Ackroyd, widow of Ackroyd’s good-for-nothing younger brother, has moved to Fernley Park and, according to Caroline, has put Miss Russell in her place.

I don’t know what "her place" means - it sounds cold and unpleasant - but Miss Russell now has pinched lips and an acid smile. She expresses great sympathy for "poor Mrs. Ackroyd - dependent on her husband’s brother." She says charity is bitter and she would be miserable if she didn’t work. I don’t know what Mrs. Cecil Ackroyd thought of the Ferrars affair when it came up. It was clearly in her interest for Ackroyd to stay single. She was always charming - even gushing - to Mrs. Ferrars when they met. Caroline says that doesn't mean anything.

These have been our concerns in King’s Abbot for years. We've discussed Ackroyd and his affairs from every angle. Mrs. Ferrars fit into the picture.

Now everything has changed.

From light talk of wedding gifts, we've been thrown into tragedy.

Thinking about these things, I went on my rounds. I had no urgent cases, which was good, because my thoughts kept returning to Mrs. Ferrars’s death. Did she kill herself? If so, wouldn’t she have left a note explaining her actions? In my experience, women who decide to commit suicide usually want to reveal their state of mind. They want attention.

When did I last see her? Not for over a week. She seemed normal enough, considering everything.

Then I remembered seeing her, though not speaking to her, just yesterday. She was walking with Ralph Paton, and I was surprised because I didn’t think he was in King’s Abbot. I thought he had permanently argued with his stepfather.

He hadn’t been seen here for nearly six months. They were walking closely together, heads near each other, and she was talking intently.

I can say that at that moment, I first felt a sense of foreboding. Nothing concrete yet - just a feeling of how things were developing. That serious talk between Ralph Paton and Mrs. Ferrars the day before bothered me.

I was still thinking about it when I met Roger Ackroyd.

‘Sheppard!’ he said. ‘Just the person I wanted to find. This is awful.’ ‘You’ve heard?’ He nodded. He was deeply affected, I could tell. His big red cheeks looked sunken, and he was a shadow of his usual cheerful, healthy self.

‘It’s worse than you know,’ he said softly. ‘Listen, Sheppard, I need to talk to you. Can you come back with me now?’ ‘I can’t. I still have three patients to visit, and I must be back by twelve for my surgery patients.’ ‘Then this afternoon - no, better yet, have dinner tonight. At 7.30. Can you do that?’ ‘Yes, that works for me. What’s wrong? Is it Ralph?’ I wasn't sure why I asked that - except, maybe, it was often Ralph.

Ackroyd stared at me blankly, as if he didn't understand.

I began to realize that something must be very wrong. I'm sure Miss Russell knows more about high society than I do. I didn’t try to argue with her.

‘Just tell me this, doctor,’ said Miss Russell. ‘If you are really addicted to drugs, is there a cure?’ You can't answer a question like that quickly. I gave her a brief explanation, and she listened carefully. I still thought she was looking for information about Mrs Ferrars.

‘Now, veronal, for example -’ I continued.

But, strangely, she didn’t seem interested in veronal. Instead, she changed the topic and asked if it was true that some poisons were so rare they couldn't be detected.

‘Ah!’ I said. ‘You’ve been reading detective stories.’ She admitted it.

‘The key to a detective story,’ I said, ‘is to have a rare poison - ideally something from South America that no one has ever heard of- something a little-known tribe of savages uses to poison their arrows. Death is instant, and Western science can’t detect it.

‘Is that what you mean?’ ‘Yes. Does such a thing really exist?’ I shook my head sadly.

‘I’m afraid not. There’s curare, of course.’ I told her a lot about curare, but she seemed to lose interest again. She asked if I had any in my poison cabinet, and when I said no, I think she thought less of me.

She said she had to go back, and I saw her out at the surgery door just as the lunch bell rang.

I never would have thought Miss Russell liked detective stories. It makes me happy to imagine her leaving the housekeeper’s room to scold a naughty housemaid, and then going back to enjoy reading The Mystery of the Seventh Death, or something like that.
