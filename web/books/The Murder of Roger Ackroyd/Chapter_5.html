The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 5

Crime

I got out of the car quickly and drove fast to Fernly. I jumped out and rang the bell impatiently. It took a while for someone to answer, so I rang again.

Then I heard the chain jangle, and Parker, his face as calm as ever, stood in the doorway.

I pushed past him into the hall.

‘What’s wrong with you, Parker? If, like you said, your master has been murdered…’ Parker gasped.

‘The master? Murdered? That can’t be, sir!’ Now it was my turn to stare.

‘Didn’t you call me, just five minutes ago, and tell me Mr. Ackroyd had been found murdered?’ ‘Me, sir? Oh! No, sir. I would never do that.’ ‘Are you saying it’s a joke? ‘A very bad joke to play, sir,’ he said at last, in a shocked tone. ‘Imagine saying something like that.’ ‘Where is Mr. Ackroyd?’ I asked suddenly.

‘Still in the study, I think, sir. The ladies have gone to bed, and Major Blunt and Mr. Raymond are in the billiard room.’ ‘I think I’ll just look in and see him for a minute,’ I said.

‘I know he didn’t want to be bothered again, but this strange joke has made me worried. I’d just like to make sure he’s all right.’ ‘Quite so, sir. It makes me feel quite worried myself. There was no answer. I turned the handle, but the door was locked.

‘Allow me, sir,’ said Parker.

Very quickly, for a man of his size, he dropped to one knee and looked through the keyhole.

‘Key is in the lock, all right, sir,’ he said, standing up. ‘On the inside. Mr. Ackroyd must have locked himself in and maybe just fallen asleep.’ I bent down and checked Parker’s statement.

‘It seems fine,’ I said, ‘but still, Parker, I’m going to wake your master up. I wouldn’t be happy going home without hearing from him that he’s quite all right.’ Saying this, I rattled the handle and called out, ‘Ackroyd, Ackroyd, just a minute.’ But still, there was no answer. I glanced over my shoulder.

‘I don’t want to scare everyone,’ I said hesitantly.

Parker went over and shut the door from the big hall we had come through.

‘I think that will be all right now, sir. The billiard room is on the other side of the house, and so are the kitchen areas and the ladies’ bedrooms.’ I nodded, understanding. Then I banged frantically on the door again, and bending down, shouted through the keyhole: ‘Ackroyd, Ackroyd! It’s Sheppard. Let me in.’ But still - silence. Not a sign of life from inside the locked room. Parker and I looked at each other.

‘Look here, Parker,’ I said, ‘I’m going to break this door down - or rather, we are. I’ll take the blame.’ ‘If you say so, sir,’ said Parker, sounding unsure.

‘I do say so. I’m really worried about Mr Ackroyd.’ I looked around the small lobby and picked up a heavy oak chair. Parker and I held it together and went towards the door. Once, twice, and three times we hit it against the lock. On the third hit, it broke, and we stumbled into the room.

Ackroyd was sitting as I had left him in the armchair by the fire. His head had fallen to the side, and easily seen, just below his collar, was a shiny piece of twisted metal.

Parker and I moved closer until we were standing over him. I heard the butler gasp.

‘Stabbed from behind,’ he whispered. ‘Awful!’ He wiped his sweaty forehead with his handkerchief, then cautiously reached out towards the dagger’s handle.

‘Don’t touch that,’ I said quickly. ‘Go to the telephone right away and call the police station. Tell them what has happened. Then tell Mr Raymond and Major Blunt.’ ‘Very good, sir.’ Parker hurried off, still wiping his sweaty forehead.

I did what little I could. I was careful not to move the body, and not to touch the dagger at all. There was no point in moving it.

Ackroyd had clearly been dead for a while.

Then I heard young Raymond’s voice outside, full of horror and disbelief.

‘What are you saying? Oh! That can’t be! Where’s the doctor?’ He rushed into the doorway, then stopped, his face very pale. A hand pushed him aside, and Hector Blunt came into the room.

‘My God!’ said Raymond from behind him; ‘it’s true, then.’ Blunt walked straight to the chair. He bent over the body, and I thought he would grab the dagger handle like Parker. I pulled him back with one hand.

‘Nothing must be touched,’ I explained. ‘The police need to see him exactly as he is now.’ Blunt nodded, understanding immediately. His face showed nothing, but I thought I saw emotion beneath his calm appearance. Geoffrey Raymond had joined us and was peering at the body over Blunt’s shoulder.

‘This is terrible,’ he said quietly.

He was calm again, but as he took off and cleaned the glasses he usually wore, I noticed his hand was shaking.

‘Robbery, I guess,’ he said. ‘How did he get in?

Through the window? Is anything missing?’ He went towards the desk.

‘You think it’s burglary?’ I asked slowly.

‘What else could it be? Surely it’s not suicide?’ ‘No one could stab themselves like that,’ I said confidently.

‘It’s definitely murder. But what was the reason?’ ‘Roger didn’t have an enemy,’ said Blunt quietly.

‘Must have been burglars. But what was the thief after?

Nothing seems to be disturbed?’ He looked around the room. Raymond was still sorting the papers on the desk.

‘Nothing seems to be missing, and none of the drawers look like they've been touched,’ the secretary said finally. ‘It’s very strange.’ Blunt nodded slightly.

‘There are some letters on the floor here,’ he said.

I looked down. Three or four letters were still lying where Ackroyd had dropped them earlier.

But the blue envelope with Mrs Ferrars’s letter was gone. I almost spoke, but just then a bell rang through the house. There was a confused sound of voices in the hall, and then Parker appeared with our local inspector and a police officer.

‘Good evening, gentlemen,’ said the inspector. ‘I’m very sorry about this! A good, kind man like Mr Ackroyd. The butler says it’s murder. No chance of accident or suicide, doctor?’ ‘None at all,’ I said.

‘Ah! A bad situation.’ He came and stood over the body.

‘Has it been moved?’ he asked quickly.

‘Besides making sure he was dead - which was easy - I haven't touched the body at all.’ ‘Ah! And it looks like the killer got away clean - for now, anyway. Now then, tell me everything. Who found the body?’ I carefully explained what happened.

‘A phone call, you say? From the butler?’ ‘A call I never made,’ Parker said firmly.

‘I haven’t been near the phone all evening. The others can tell you that I haven’t.’ ‘Very strange. Did it sound like Parker’s voice, doctor?’ ‘Well - I can’t say I noticed. I just assumed it was, you see.’ ‘Of course. So, you came up here, broke down the door, and found Mr. Ackroyd like this. How long do you think he had been dead, doctor?’ ‘At least half an hour - maybe longer,’ I said.

‘The door was locked from the inside, you say? What about the window?’ ‘I closed and locked it myself earlier this evening, as Mr. Ackroyd asked.’ The inspector walked over to it and pulled back the curtains.

‘Well, it’s open now,’ he said. Sure enough, the window was open, with the bottom part raised all the way up.

The inspector took out a small flashlight and shined it along the outside of the window.

‘This is how he got in and out,’ he said, ‘Look here.’ In the bright light, several clear footprints could be seen. They looked like they were made by shoes with rubber studs on the bottom. One very clear print faced inward, and another, slightly on top of it, faced outward.

‘Clear as day,’ said the inspector. ‘Are any valuables missing?’ Geoffrey Raymond shook his head.

‘Not that we’ve found yet. Mr. Ackroyd didn’t keep anything really valuable in this room.’ ‘H’m,’ said the inspector. ‘Man found an open window, climbed in, saw Mr. Ackroyd sitting there - maybe he’d fallen asleep. Man stabbed him from behind, then got scared and ran off. But he left his tracks pretty clearly. We should be able to catch him without too much trouble. Have you seen any suspicious strangers around?’ ‘Oh!’ I said suddenly.

‘What is it, doctor?’ ‘I met a man this evening - just as I was leaving the gate. He asked me how to get to Fernly Park.’ ‘What time was that?’ ‘Right at nine o’clock. I heard the clock strike the hour as I was leaving the gate.’ ‘Can you describe him?’ I did my best to describe him.

The inspector turned to the butler.

‘Did anyone who looked like that come to the front door?’ ‘No, sir. No one has come to the house at all this evening.’ ‘What about the back?’ ‘I don’t think so, sir, but I’ll ask around.’ He started towards the door, but the inspector held up a hand.

‘No, thanks. I’ll ask myself. But first I want to get the times a little clearer. When was Mr Ackroyd last seen alive?’ ‘Probably by me,’ I said, ‘when I left at - let me see - about ten minutes before nine. He told me he didn’t want to be bothered, and I told Parker the same.’ ‘Yes, sir,’ Parker said politely.

‘Mr Ackroyd was definitely alive at half-past nine,’ Raymond said, ‘because I heard him talking in here.’ ‘Who was he talking to?’ ‘I don’t know. I just assumed it was Dr Sheppard. I wanted to ask him a question about some papers I was working on, but when I heard the voices, I remembered he had said he wanted to talk to Dr Sheppard alone, so I left. But now it seems the doctor had already gone?’ I nodded.

‘I was home by a quarter past nine,’ I said. ‘I didn’t go out again until I got the phone call.’ ‘Who could have been with him at half-past nine?’ the inspector asked. ‘It wasn’t you, Mr - er ‘ ‘Major Blunt,’ I said.

‘Major Hector Blunt?’ asked the inspector, his tone becoming respectful.

Blunt just nodded.

‘I think we’ve seen you here before, sir,’ said the inspector. ‘I didn’t recognize you at first, but you were staying with Mr Ackroyd last May a year ago.’ ‘June,’ Blunt corrected.

‘Right, June. Now, like I was saying, it wasn’t you with Mr Ackroyd at nine-thirty this evening?’ Blunt shook his head.

‘Never saw him after dinner,’ he offered.

The inspector turned again to Raymond.

‘You didn’t happen to overhear any of the conversation, did you, sir?’ ‘I did catch a little bit,’ said the secretary, ‘and, since I thought it was Dr Sheppard with Mr Ackroyd, that bit seemed very strange to me. As far as I can recall, Mr Ackroyd was speaking. He said, “The demands on my money have been so frequent lately – so frequent, that I don’t think I can agree to your request…” I left right away, so I didn’t hear any more. But I was a little curious because Dr Sheppard ‘ ‘ – Doesn’t ask for loans for himself or subscriptions for others,’ I finished.

‘A request for money,’ said the inspector thoughtfully. ‘This might be a very important clue.’ He turned to the butler. ‘You say, Parker, that no one came in through the front door this evening?’ ‘That’s right, sir.’ ‘Then it seems almost certain that Mr Ackroyd himself must have let this stranger in. But I don’t quite see ‘ The inspector seemed to drift off into his thoughts for a few minutes.

‘One thing is clear,’ he said finally, snapping out of his thoughts, ‘Mr Ackroyd was alive and well at nine-thirty.

That’s the last time we know he was alive.’ Parker coughed slightly, as if to apologize, and the inspector looked at him immediately.

‘Yes?’ he said sharply.

‘Excuse me, sir. Miss Flora saw him after that.’ ‘Miss Flora?’ ‘Yes, sir. About a quarter to ten. It was after that she told me Mr Ackroyd wasn’t to be disturbed again tonight.’ ‘Did he send her to you with that message?’ ‘Not exactly, sir. I was bringing in a tray with soda and whisky when Miss Flora, who was just coming out of this room, stopped me and said her uncle didn’t want to be disturbed.’ The inspector looked at the butler more closely than he had before.

‘You’d already been told that Mr Ackroyd didn’t want to be disturbed, had you?’ Parker started to stammer. His hands trembled.

‘Yes, sir. Yes, sir. That’s right, sir.’ ‘And yet you were going to disturb him?’ ‘I’d forgotten, sir. I mean, I always bring the whisky and soda around that time, sir, and ask if he needs anything else, and I just – well, I was doing it without thinking.’ It was at this point that I began to realize that Parker was acting very suspiciously nervous. The man was shaking and twitching all over.

‘H’m,’ said the inspector. ‘I need to speak with Miss Ackroyd right away.

For now, we'll leave this room just as it is. I can come back here after I’ve spoken with Miss Ackroyd. I'll just lock and bolt the window to be safe.’ After doing this, he went into the hall, and we followed. He paused, looking up at the small staircase, then spoke to the constable.

‘Jones, stay here. Don't let anyone go into that room.’ Parker added politely,

‘Excuse me, sir. If you locked the door to the main hall, no one could get to this part of the house. That staircase only leads to Mr. Ackroyd’s bedroom and bathroom. He wanted his area to be completely private.’ To make it easier to understand, I've included a simple drawing of the right wing of the house.

The small staircase leads to a large bedroom made by combining two rooms, as Parker said, and a bathroom and toilet.

The inspector understood the layout quickly. We went into the large hall, and he locked the door behind him, putting the key in his pocket. Then he quietly gave the constable some instructions, and the constable got ready to leave.
