The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 20

Miss Russell

Inspector Raglan was badly shaken. He wasn't fooled by Blunt's brave lie any more than we were.

Our walk back to the village was filled with his complaints.

'This changes everything. I don't know if you realize that, Monsieur Poirot?' 'I think so, yes, I think so,' said Poirot. 'You see, I have been thinking about this for some time.' Inspector Raglan, who had only heard the idea a short half-hour ago, looked at Poirot sadly, and continued with his thoughts.

'Those alibis are now useless! Absolutely useless. We have to start again. Find out what everyone was doing from nine-thirty onwards. Nine-thirty - that's the time we have to focus on. You were right about the man Kent; we don't release him yet. Let me see now - nine forty-five at the Dog and Whistle. He could have gotten there in fifteen minutes if he ran. It's possible that it was his voice Mr. Raymond heard talking to Mr. Ackroyd, asking for money, which Mr. Ackroyd refused. But one thing is clear - it wasn't him who sent the telephone message.

The station is half a mile in the other direction - over a mile and a half from the Dog and Whistle, and he was at the Dog and Whistle until about ten minutes past ten. Damn that telephone call! We always come back to it.' 'We do indeed,' agreed Poirot. 'It is strange.' 'It's possible that if Captain Paton climbed into his uncle's room and found him murdered, he might have sent it. Got scared, thought he'd be accused, and ran away. That's possible, isn't it?' 'Why would he have telephoned?' I 185 'Maybe he had doubts if the old man was really dead.

Thought he'd get the doctor there as soon as possible, but didn't want to give himself away. Yes, I say, how's that for a theory? There's something to that, I'd say.' The inspector puffed out his chest importantly. He was so clearly pleased with himself that anything we said would have been unnecessary.

We arrived back at my house at that moment, and I hurried in to see my patients, who had all been waiting a long time, leaving Poirot to walk to the police station with the inspector.

Having seen the last patient, I walked into the small room at the back of the house that I call my workshop. I am quite proud of the homemade wireless set I made.

Caroline dislikes my workroom. I keep my tools there, and Annie isn't allowed to make a mess with the dustpan and brush. I was fixing the inside of an alarm clock that the family had said was completely unreliable when the door opened and Caroline stuck her head in.

‘Oh! there you are, James,’ she said, with clear disapproval.

‘M. Poirot wants to see you.’ ‘Well,’ I said, a bit annoyed, because her sudden appearance had startled me and I had dropped a small part. ‘If he wants to see me, he can come in here.’ ‘In here?’ said Caroline.

‘That’s what I said - in here.’ Caroline sniffed disapprovingly and left. She came back a moment later, bringing Poirot in, and then left again, slamming the door.

‘Aha! my friend,’ said Poirot, stepping forward and rubbing his hands. ‘You haven't gotten rid of me so easily, you see!’ ‘Finished with the inspector?’ I asked.

‘For now, yes. And you, have you seen all the patients?’ ‘Yes.’ Poirot sat down and looked at me, tilting his egg-shaped head to one side, like he was enjoying a very good joke.

‘You are mistaken,’ he said finally. ‘You still have one patient to see.’ ‘Not you?’ I asked, surprised.

‘Ah, not me, of course. Me, I am in excellent health. No, to tell you the truth, it's a little scheme of mine. There is someone I want to see, you understand - and it's better if the whole village doesn't get curious about it - which would happen if the lady was seen coming to my house, because it is a lady. But she has already come to you as a patient before.’ ‘Miss Russell!’ I exclaimed.

‘Exactly. I really want to talk to her, so I sent her a little note and made the appointment in your office. You aren't angry with me?’ ‘On the contrary,’ I said. ‘That is, if I can be there during the interview?’ ‘But of course! In your own office!’ ‘You know,’ I said, dropping the pliers I was holding, ‘the whole thing is incredibly intriguing.

Every new thing that happens is like shaking a kaleidoscope - the view changes completely.

'Now, why are you so eager to see Miss Russell?' Poirot raised his eyebrows.

'Surely it's clear?' he murmured.

'There you go again,' I grumbled. 'According to you, everything is clear. But you leave me completely confused.' Poirot shook his head kindly at me.

'You make fun of me. Think about Mademoiselle Flora. The inspector was surprised - but you - you weren't.' 'I never imagined she was the thief,' I protested.

'That - perhaps not. But I watched your face, and you weren't - like Inspector Raglan - shocked and disbelieving.' I thought for a minute or two.

'Perhaps you're right,' I said finally. 'All along, I've felt that Flora was hiding something - so the truth, when it came, was expected without me realizing it. It upset Inspector Raglan a lot, poor man.' 'Ah! The poor man must rethink everything. I used his confusion to get him to do me a little favor.' 'What was it?' Poirot took a piece of notepaper from his pocket. Some words were written on it, and he read them aloud.

'The police have been looking for Captain Ralph Paton, the nephew of Mr. Ackroyd of Fernly Park, for several days. Mr. Ackroyd died in tragic circumstances last Friday. Captain Paton has been found in Liverpool, where he was about to sail for America.' He folded the paper again.

'That, my friend, will be in the newspapers tomorrow morning.' I stared at him, speechless.

'But - but it isn't true! He's not in Liverpool!' Poirot smiled at me.

‘You’re very quick-witted! No, he hasn’t been found in Liverpool. Inspector Raglan was very unwilling to let me send this to the newspapers, especially since I couldn’t confide in him. But I assured him most solemnly that very interesting things would happen if it appeared in print, so he agreed, after insisting that he wouldn't be held responsible.’ I stared at Poirot. He smiled back at me.

‘I don’t understand,’ I said finally, ‘what you expect to gain from that.’ ‘You should use your little grey cells,’ said Poirot seriously.

He got up and walked over to the bench.

‘It’s clear you really love how things work,’ he said, after looking at the remains of my work.

Everyone has a hobby. I immediately showed Poirot my homemade radio. Finding him interested, I showed him a couple of my own little inventions - small things, but useful around the house.

‘Definitely,’ said Poirot, ‘you should be an inventor, not a doctor. But I hear the bell - that's your patient.

Let's go into the surgery.’ I had noticed the traces of beauty in the housekeeper’s face before. This morning, I noticed it again.

Simply dressed in black, tall, upright, and independent as always, with her big dark eyes and an unusual blush on her usually pale cheeks, I realized that she must have been strikingly beautiful as a girl.

‘Good morning, mademoiselle,’ said Poirot. ‘Please, sit down. Dr. Sheppard is kind enough to let me use his surgery for a little conversation I'm eager to have with you.’ Miss Russell sat down with her usual calmness. If she felt any inner turmoil, she didn't show it.

‘It seems like a strange way of doing things, if you don’t mind me saying so,’ she said.

‘Miss Russell - I have news for you.’ ‘Indeed!’ ‘Charles Kent has been arrested in Liverpool.’ Not a muscle of her face moved. She simply opened her eyes a little wider, and asked, with a hint of defiance: ‘Well, what about it?’ But at that moment it struck me - the resemblance that had bothered me all along, something familiar in Charles Kent’s defiant manner. The two voices, one rough and coarse, the other painfully ladylike - were strangely alike in tone. It was Miss Russell who I had been reminded of that night outside the gates of Fernly Park.

I looked at Poirot, full of my discovery, and he gave me a slight nod.

In answer to Miss Russell’s question, he threw out his hands in a very French way.

‘I thought you might be interested, that’s all,’ he said gently.

‘Well I’m not especially,’ said Miss Russell. ‘Who is this Charles Kent anyway?’ ‘He is a man, mademoiselle, who was at Fernly on the night of the murder.’ ‘Really?’ ‘Luckily for him, he has an alibi. At a quarter to ten he was at a pub a mile from here.’ ‘Lucky for him,’ commented Miss Russell.

‘But we still don’t know what he was doing at Fernly, who he went to meet, for example.’ ‘I’m afraid I can’t help you at all,’ said the housekeeper politely. ‘I didn’t hear anything. If that’s all ‘ She made a slight movement as if to rise. Poirot stopped her.

‘It isn’t quite all,’ he said smoothly. ‘This morning new information has come to light. It seems now that Mr Ackroyd was murdered, not at a quarter to ten, but earlier. Between ten minutes to nine, when Dr Sheppard left, and a quarter to ten.’ I saw the colour leave the housekeeper’s face, turning it deathly white. She leaned forward, swaying.

‘But Miss Ackroyd said - Miss Ackroyd said ‘ ‘Miss Ackroyd has admitted that she was lying. She was never in the study at all that evening.’ ‘Then-’ ‘Then it seems that in this Charles Kent we have the man we are looking for. He came to Fernly, can’t explain what he was doing there ‘ ‘I can tell you what he was doing there. He never touched a hair on old Ackroyd’s head - he never went near the study. He didn’t do it, I tell you.’ She was leaning forward. That strong self-control was finally broken. Terror and desperation were on her face.

‘M. Poirot! M. Poirot! Oh, please believe me.’ Poirot got up and came to her. He patted her reassuringly on the shoulder.

‘But yes - but yes, I will believe. I had to make you talk, you know.’ For an instant suspicion flashed in her eyes.

‘Is what you said true?’ ‘That Charles Kent is suspected of the crime? Yes, that is true. You alone can save him, by telling me why he was at Fernly.’ ‘He came to see me.’ She spoke softly and quickly. ‘I went out to meet him ‘ ‘In the summer-house, yes, I know.’ ‘How do you know?’ ‘Mademoiselle, it is Hercule Poirot’s job to know things. I know that you went out earlier in the evening, that you left a message in the summer-house saying what time you would be there.’ ‘Yes, I did. I had heard from him - saying he was coming. I couldn't let him come to the house. I wrote to the address he gave me and said I would meet him in the summerhouse, and described it to him so he could find it.

Then I was afraid he might not wait there, and I ran out and left a note saying I would be there about ten minutes past nine. I didn’t want the servants to see me, so I slipped out through the drawing-room window. When I came back, I met Dr Sheppard, and I thought he would find it strange. I was out of breath, because I had been running. I didn't know he was expected to dinner that night.’ She paused.

‘Go on,’ said Poirot. ‘You went out to meet him at ten minutes past nine. What did you say to each other?’ ‘It’s difficult. You see-’ ‘Mademoiselle,’ said Poirot, interrupting her, ‘in this matter I must have the whole truth. What you tell us will stay within these four walls. Dr Sheppard will be discreet, and so will I. Let me help you. This Charles Kent, he is your son, isn't he?’ She nodded. Her cheeks flushed.

‘No one has ever known. It was long ago - long ago - in Kent. I was not married...’ ‘So you gave him the county name as a last name. I understand.’ ‘I got work. I managed to pay for his food and lodging. I never told him I was his mother. But he turned out badly, he drank, then took drugs. I managed to pay for his trip to Canada. I didn’t hear from him for a year or two.

Then, somehow, he found out I was his mother.

He wrote asking me for money. Finally, I heard from him again in this country. He was coming to see me at Fernly, he said. I couldn't let him come to the house. I have always been considered so - so respectable. If anyone found out - I would lose my job as housekeeper. So I wrote to him as I told you.’ ‘And in the morning you came to see Dr Sheppard?’ ‘Yes. I wondered if something could be done. He was not a bad boy - before he took drugs.’ ‘I see,’ said Poirot. ‘Now let's continue the story. He came that night to the summerhouse?’ ‘Yes, he was waiting for me when I got there. He was very harsh and rude. I had brought all the money I had, and I gave it to him. We talked a little, and then he left.’ ‘What time was that?’ ‘It must have been between twenty and twenty-five minutes past nine. It was not yet half-past when I got back to the house.’ ‘Which way did he go?’ ‘Straight out the way he came, by the path that joined the drive just inside the lodge gates.’ Poirot nodded.

‘And you, what did you do?’ ‘I went back to the house. Major Blunt was walking up and down the terrace smoking, so I went around to the side door. It was just then half-past nine, as I told you.’ Poirot nodded again. He made a note or two in a tiny notebook.

‘I think that is all,’ he said thoughtfully.

‘Should I -?’ she hesitated. ‘Should I tell all this to Inspector Raglan?’ ‘Maybe. But let's not rush. Let's proceed slowly and carefully. Charles Kent is not yet officially accused of murder. Things may happen that make your story unnecessary.’ Miss Russell stood up.

‘Thank you very much, M. Poirot,’ she said. ‘You have been very kind - very kind indeed. You - you believe me, don’t you? That Charles had nothing to do with this awful murder!’ ‘It seems certain that the man who spoke to Mr Ackroyd in the library at nine-thirty could not have been your son. Take heart, mademoiselle. All will be well.’ Miss Russell left. Poirot and I were alone.

‘So that’s that,’ I said. ‘We always come back to Ralph Paton. How did you figure out Miss Russell was who Charles Kent came to meet? Did you see the resemblance?’ ‘I linked her to the unknown man long before we met him. As soon as we found that quill. The quill suggested drugs, and I recalled your story of Miss Russell’s visit. Then I found the cocaine article in the paper. It seemed clear. She had heard from someone that morning - someone addicted to drugs, she read the article, and she asked you some cautious questions. She mentioned cocaine, since the article was about cocaine. Then, when you seemed too interested, she quickly changed to detective stories and untraceable poisons. I suspected a son or brother, or some other bad male relative. Ah! but I must go. It is lunchtime.’ ‘Stay and have lunch with us,’ I said.

Poirot shook his head. A slight twinkle came into his eye.

‘Not again today. I don’t want to make Mademoiselle Caroline eat vegetarian two days in a row.’ It struck me that Hercule Poirot missed very little.
