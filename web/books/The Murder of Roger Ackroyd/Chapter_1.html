The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 1

Dr Sheppard at the Breakfast Table

Mrs. Ferrars died on the night of September 16th–17th—a Thursday. I was called to her house at eight o’clock on the morning of Friday the 17th. There was nothing I could do. She had been dead for several hours.

It was just after nine when I got back home. I unlocked the front door and paused in the hall to hang up my hat and the light coat I thought was a good idea because of the cool morning air. To be honest, I was quite upset and anxious. I won’t pretend I knew what would happen in the next few weeks. I certainly did not.

But I had a feeling that interesting times were coming.

From the dining-room on my left, I heard tea-cups clinking and my sister Caroline’s short, dry cough.

‘Is that you, James?’ she asked.

A pointless question, since who else could it be? Actually, my sister Caroline was why I had delayed for a few minutes. Mr. Kipling tells us that the mongoose family motto is: ‘Go and find out.’ If Caroline ever had a crest, I would suggest a mongoose standing up on its hind legs. We could leave out the first part of the motto. Caroline can find out a lot just by sitting quietly at home. I don’t know how she does it, but she does. I think the servants and shopkeepers are her spies. When she goes out, it’s not to gather information but to spread it. She’s amazingly good at that, too.

It was really this last trait of hers that was making me hesitate. Anything I told Caroline now about Mrs. Ferrars’ death would be known throughout the village within an hour and a half. As a doctor, I naturally try to be discreet. So, I’ve made it a habit to keep as much information from my sister as possible.

She usually finds out anyway, but I feel better knowing I’m not to blame.

Mrs Ferrars' husband passed away a little over a year ago, and Caroline has constantly claimed, without any proof whatsoever, that his wife poisoned him.

She dismisses my constant reply that Mr Ferrars died of severe stomach inflammation, worsened by his regular excessive drinking. I agree that the signs of stomach inflammation and arsenic poisoning are somewhat similar, but Caroline's accusation is based on completely different reasons.

'You just have to look at her,' I've heard her say.

Mrs Ferrars, while no longer young, was a very attractive woman, and her clothes, though simple, always seemed to suit her perfectly, but still, many women buy their clothes in Paris, and that doesn't mean they've necessarily poisoned their husbands.

As I stood hesitating in the hall, with all this running through my head, Caroline's voice came again, with a sharper tone.

'What on earth are you doing out there, James? Why don't you come and eat your breakfast?' 'Coming, dear,' I said quickly. 'I was hanging up my coat.' 'You could have hung up half a dozen coats in that time.' She was quite right. I could have.

I walked into the dining room, gave Caroline the usual peck on the cheek, and sat down to eggs and bacon. The bacon was rather cold.

'You've had an early visitor,' Caroline remarked.

'Yes,' I said. 'King's Paddock. Mrs Ferrars.' 'I know,' said my sister.

'How did you know?' 'Annie told me.' Annie is the housemaid. A nice girl, but she can't stop talking.

There was a pause. I kept eating eggs and bacon. My sister’s nose, long and thin, twitched a little at the tip, as it always does when she's interested or excited.

‘Well?’ she asked.

‘A sad thing. Nothing to be done. Must have died in her sleep.’ ‘I know,’ said my sister again.

This time I was annoyed.

‘You can’t know,’ I snapped. ‘I didn’t know myself until I got there, and haven’t told anyone yet. If Annie knows, she must be psychic.’ ‘Annie didn’t tell me. The milkman did. He heard it from the Ferrarses’ cook.’ As I said, Caroline doesn’t need to go out to get information. She sits at home and it comes to her.

My sister went on: ‘What did she die of? Heart failure?’ ‘Didn’t the milkman tell you that?’ I asked sarcastically.

Sarcasm is lost on Caroline. She takes it seriously and answers that way.

‘He didn’t know,’ she explained.

Anyway, Caroline was going to hear sooner or later. She might as well hear from me.

‘She died of a veronal overdose. She’s been taking it for sleeplessness. Must have taken too much.’ ‘Nonsense,’ Caroline said right away. ‘She did it on purpose. Don’t tell me!’ It's strange, when you have a secret belief you don't want to admit, someone else saying it makes you angry and deny it. I immediately burst out, indignant.

‘There you go again,’ I said. ‘Rushing along without any reason. Why on earth would Mrs Ferrars want to commit suicide? A widow, still fairly young, very rich, healthy, and with nothing to do but enjoy life. It’s absurd.’ ‘Not at all. Even you must have noticed how different she’s looked lately. It’s been going on for the last six months. She’s looked really haunted. And you just admitted that she hasn’t been able to sleep.’ ‘What's your diagnosis?’ I asked coldly. ‘An unfortunate love affair, I suppose?’ My sister shook her head.

‘‘Guilt,’ she said, with great relish.

‘Guilt?’ ‘Yes. You never believed me when I told you she poisoned her husband. I’m even more sure of it now.’ ‘I don’t think you’re very logical,’ I argued. ‘Surely if a woman committed a crime like murder, she’d be cold-blooded enough to enjoy the benefits without weak emotions like regret.’ Caroline shook her head.

‘There probably are women like that - but Mrs Ferrars wasn’t one of them. She was a bundle of nerves. An overwhelming urge made her get rid of her husband because she's the kind of person who simply can’t stand any suffering, and there’s no doubt that the wife of a man like Ashley Ferrars must have suffered a lot.’ I nodded.

‘And ever since, she’s been haunted by what she did. I can’t help feeling sorry for her.’ I don’t think Caroline ever felt sorry for Mrs Ferrars when she was alive. Now that she has gone where (presumably) Paris dresses can no longer be worn, Caroline is willing to feel softer emotions like pity and understanding.

I told her firmly that her whole idea was nonsense. I was even more firm because I secretly agreed with at least some of what she had said. But it's wrong that Caroline should arrive at the truth simply by inspired guesswork. I wasn’t going to encourage that sort of thing. She will go around the village sharing her views, and everyone will think she’s doing so based on medical information I gave her. Life is very trying.

‘Nonsense,’ said Caroline, in response to my scolding.

‘You’ll see. I bet she’s left a letter confessing everything.’

‘She didn’t leave a letter of any kind,’ I said sharply, not realizing where the admission was going to lead me.

‘Oh!’ said Caroline. ‘So you did ask about that, did you? I think, James, that deep down, you think just like I do. You’re a precious old fake.’ ‘One always has to consider the possibility of suicide,’ I said importantly.

‘Will there be an inquest?’ ‘Maybe. It depends. If I can say for sure the overdose was an accident, we might not need one.’ ‘And are you sure?’ my sister asked, sharp and quick.

I didn't answer, but stood up from the table.
