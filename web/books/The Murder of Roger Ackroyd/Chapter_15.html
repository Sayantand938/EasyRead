The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 15

Geoffrey Raymond

That day, I would see more proof that Poirot's methods worked. His challenge had been a clever move, based on his understanding of people. Fear and guilt had forced Mrs. Ackroyd to tell the truth. She was the first to break.

That afternoon, when I got back from seeing patients, Caroline told me Geoffrey Raymond had just left.

'Did he want to see me?' I asked, hanging up my coat.

Caroline was close by.

'He wanted to see M. Poirot,' she said. 'He had just come from the Larches. Mr. Poirot was out. Mr. Raymond thought he might be here, or that you might know where he was.' 'I have no idea.' 'I tried to get him to wait,' Caroline said, 'but he said he would go back to The Larches in half an hour and left. It's a shame because M. Poirot came in almost right after he left.' 'Came in here?' 'No, to his own house.' 'How do you know?' 'The side window,' Caroline said simply.

I thought we were done talking about it.

Caroline didn't.

'Aren't you going over?' 'Over where?' 'To The Larches, of course.' 'Caroline,' I said, 'why?' 'Mr. Raymond really wanted to see him,' Caroline said. 'You could find out what it's about.' I raised my eyebrows.

‘Curiosity isn't my weakness,’ I said coolly. ‘I'm fine not knowing what my neighbors are up to.’ ‘Nonsense, James,’ said my sister. ‘You're just as curious as I am. You're just not as honest.’

‘You always have to pretend.’ ‘Really, Caroline,’ I said, and went into my office.

Ten minutes later, Caroline knocked and came in.

She was holding what looked like a jar of jam.

‘James,’ she said, ‘would you mind taking this jar of medlar jelly to M. Poirot? I promised it to him.

He's never had homemade medlar jelly before.’ ‘Why can't Annie go?’ I asked coldly.

‘She's doing some sewing. I need her here.’ Caroline and I looked at each other.

‘Fine,’ I said, standing up. ‘But if I take the awful thing, I'll just leave it at the door. Got it?’ My sister raised her eyebrows.

‘Of course,’ she said. ‘Who said you should do anything else?’ Caroline had won that round.

‘If you happen to see M. Poirot,’ she said, as I opened the door, ‘you could tell him about the boots.’ It was a clever parting remark. I really wanted to understand the mystery of the boots. When the old woman with the Breton cap opened the door, I found myself asking if M. Poirot was in, without even thinking.

Poirot jumped up to greet me, looking very pleased.

‘Sit down, my good friend,’ he said. ‘The big chair? This small one? The room isn't too hot, no?’ I thought it was stifling, but didn't say so. The windows were closed, and a large fire burned in the fireplace.

‘The English people, they are obsessed with fresh air,’ declared Poirot. ‘The big air is all very well outside, where it belongs. Why let it in the house? But let's not talk about such boring things. You have something for me, yes?’ ‘Two things,’ I said. ‘First - this - from my sister.’ I handed over the pot of medlar jelly.

‘How kind of Mademoiselle Caroline. She remembered her promise. And the second thing?’ ‘Information - of a sort.’ And I told him about my talk with Mrs Ackroyd. He listened with interest, but not much excitement.

‘It helps clarify things,’ he said thoughtfully. ‘And it's somewhat valuable because it confirms what the housekeeper said.

She said, you remember, that she found the silver table lid open and closed it as she passed by.’ ‘What about her saying she went into the drawing-room to see if the flowers were fresh?’ ‘Ah! We never really believed that, did we, my friend? It was obviously an excuse, made up quickly by a woman who felt she needed to explain why she was there - which, by the way, you probably wouldn't have questioned. I thought her nervousness might be because she had been messing with the silver table, but now I think we need to look for another reason.’ ‘Yes,’ I said. ‘Who did she go to meet? And why?’ ‘You think she went to meet someone?’ ‘I do.’ Poirot nodded.

‘So do I,’ he said thoughtfully.

There was a pause.

‘By the way,’ I said, ‘I have a message for you from my sister. Ralph Paton’s boots were black, not brown.’ I watched him closely as I gave the message, and I thought I saw him look slightly disturbed for a moment. If I did, it disappeared almost right away.

‘She's completely sure they're not brown?’ ‘Completely.’ ‘Ah!’ said Poirot sadly. ‘That's too bad.’ And he seemed quite disappointed.

He didn't explain anything, but immediately changed the subject.

‘The housekeeper. Miss Russell, who came to see you that Friday morning - is it wrong to ask what you talked about - besides the medical stuff, I mean?’ ‘Not at all,’ I said. ‘When we were done talking about her health, we spoke for a few minutes about poisons, how easy or hard they are to find, and about people who take drugs.’ ‘Especially cocaine?’ Poirot asked.

‘How did you know?’ I asked, a little surprised.

Instead of answering, he got up and went to where the newspapers were kept. He brought me a copy of the Daily Budget, from Friday, September 16th, and showed me an article about smuggling cocaine. It was a pretty sensational article, written to be dramatic.

‘That's what made her think about cocaine, my friend,’ he said.

I wanted to ask him more, because I didn't quite understand what he meant, but just then the door opened and Geoffrey Raymond was announced.

He came in looking as cheerful and stylish as always, and greeted us both.

‘How are you, doctor? M. Poirot, this is the second time I’ve been here today. I really wanted to see you.’ ‘Maybe I should leave,’ I said, feeling a little uncomfortable.

‘Not because of me, doctor. No, it’s just this,’ he continued, sitting down when Poirot gestured for him to do so, ‘I have to admit something.’ ‘Really?’ Poirot said, sounding politely interested.

‘Oh, it’s not a big deal, really. But, to be honest, I've felt guilty since yesterday afternoon. You said we were all hiding something, M.

Poirot. I admit it. I had a secret.’ ‘And what is it, M. Raymond?’ ‘Like I said, it's not a big deal - just this. I was in debt - deep in it, and that inheritance came just in time. Five hundred pounds gets me back on my feet with a bit extra.’ He smiled at us both with that charming honesty that made him so likeable.

‘You know how it is. Cops looking suspicious - don’t want to admit you were broke - think it'll look bad. But I was a fool, really, because Blunt and I were in the billiard room from a quarter to ten onwards, so I’ve got a solid alibi and nothing to worry about. Still, when you boomed out all that about hiding things, I felt a pang of guilt, and I thought I’d like to confess.’ He got up again and stood smiling at us.

‘You are a very wise young man,’ said Poirot, nodding approvingly. ‘You see, when I know someone is hiding things from me, I suspect the hidden thing might be very bad indeed. You have done well.’ ‘I’m glad I’m cleared of suspicion,’ laughed Raymond. ‘I’ll be off now.’ ‘So that’s that,’ I remarked, as the door closed behind the young secretary.

‘Yes,’ agreed Poirot. ‘A small thing - but if he hadn't been in the billiard room - who knows? After all, many crimes have been committed for less than five hundred pounds. It all depends on what amount is enough to push a man over the edge.

It’s all relative, isn’t it? Have you considered, my friend, that many people in that house stood to gain from Mr. Ackroyd’s death? Mrs. Ackroyd, Miss Flora, young Mr. Raymond, the housekeeper, Miss Russell. Only one, in fact, doesn’t. Major Blunt.’ His tone was so odd when he said that name that I looked up, puzzled.

‘I don’t understand you,’ I said.

‘Two of the people I accused have told me the truth.’ ‘You think Major Blunt is also hiding something?’ ‘As for that,’ remarked Poirot casually, ‘there’s a saying, isn't there, that Englishmen only hide their feelings? And Major Blunt, I'd say, isn't good at hiding things.’ ‘Sometimes,’ I said, ‘I wonder if we’ve jumped to conclusions on one point.’ ‘What is that?’ ‘We’ve assumed that Mrs. Ferrars’ blackmailer is necessarily Mr. Ackroyd’s murderer. Could we be wrong?’ Poirot nodded eagerly.

‘Very good. Very good indeed. I wondered if you'd think of that. Of course, it's possible. But we must remember one thing. The letter vanished. Still, that, as you say, might not mean the murderer took it. When you first found the body, Parker might have swiped the letter without you noticing.’ ‘Parker?’ ‘Yes, Parker. I always come back to Parker - not as the murderer - no, he didn't commit the murder; but who's more likely to be the mysterious villain who terrorized Mrs. Ferrars? He might have learned about Mr. Ferrars’ death from one of the King’s Paddock servants. At least, he's more likely to have found out about it than a casual guest like Blunt, for example.’ ‘Parker could have taken the letter,’ I admitted. ‘I didn’t notice it was gone until later.’ ‘How much later? After Blunt and Raymond were in the room, or before?’ ‘I can’t remember,’ I said slowly. ‘I think it was before - no, after. Yes, I’m almost sure it was after.’ ‘That widens the field to three,’ said Poirot thoughtfully.

‘But Parker is the most likely. I'm thinking of trying a little experiment with Parker. What do you say, my friend, will you come with me to Fernly?’ I agreed, and we left at once. Poirot asked to see Miss Ackroyd, and soon Flora came to us.

‘Mademoiselle Flora,’ said Poirot, ‘I have to tell you a little secret. I’m not convinced Parker is innocent. I want to do a little experiment with your help. I want to recreate some of his actions that night. But we need to think of something to tell him - ah! I have it. I want to know if voices in the small lobby could have been heard outside on the terrace.

'Now, please ring for Parker.' I did, and soon the butler came, as smooth as always.

'You rang, sir?'

'Yes, Parker. I'm planning a small test.

I've put Major Blunt on the terrace outside the study window. I want to see if anyone there could have heard Miss Ackroyd and you talking in the lobby that night.

I want to recreate that scene. Could you fetch the tray or whatever you were carrying?' Parker disappeared, and we went to the lobby outside the study door. Soon we heard a sound in the hall, and Parker appeared with a tray holding a siphon, a whisky decanter, and two glasses.

'One moment,' cried Poirot, raising his hand and looking very excited. 'We must get everything right.

Exactly as it happened. It's a little trick of mine.'

'A foreign idea, sir,' said Parker. 'They call it reconstructing the crime, don't they?' He remained perfectly calm as he stood there, politely waiting for Poirot's instructions.

'Ah! Parker knows something,' cried Poirot.

'He's read about these things. Now, please, let's be exact. You came from the hall - like this. Mademoiselle was - where?'

'Here,' said Flora, standing just outside the study door.

'Quite right, sir,' said Parker.

'I had just closed the door,' Flora continued.

‘Yes, miss,’ Parker agreed. ‘Your hand was still on the handle as it is now.’ ‘Then let’s go,’ said Poirot. ‘Play me the little scene.’ Flora stood with her hand on the door handle, and Parker came through the door from the hall, carrying the tray.

He stopped just inside the door. Flora spoke.

‘Oh! Parker. Mr Ackroyd doesn’t want to be disturbed again tonight.’ ‘Right?’ she added quietly.

‘As far as I remember, Miss Flora,’ said Parker, ‘you said evening instead of night.’ Then, raising his voice in a theatrical way: ‘Very good, miss. Shall I lock up as usual?’ ‘Yes, please.’ Parker left through the door. Flora followed him and started to go up the main staircase.

‘Is that enough?’ she asked, looking back.

‘Perfect,’ declared the little man, rubbing his hands.

‘By the way, Parker, are you sure there were two glasses on the tray that night? Who was the second one for?’ ‘I always bring two glasses, sir,’ said Parker. ‘Anything else?’ ‘Nothing. Thank you.’ Parker left, still dignified.

Poirot stood in the middle of the hall, frowning. Flora came down and joined us.

‘Did your experiment work?’ she asked. ‘I don’t really understand -’ Poirot smiled at her with admiration.

‘You don’t need to,’ he said. ‘But tell me, were there really two glasses on Parker’s tray that night?’ Flora frowned for a moment.

‘I really can’t remember,’ she said. ‘I think there were. Is - is that what you’re trying to find out?’ Poirot took her hand and patted it.

‘Let’s put it this way,’ he said. ‘I always like to see if people will tell the truth.’ ‘And did Parker tell the truth?’ ‘I think he did,’ said Poirot thoughtfully.

A few minutes later, we were walking back to the village.

‘Why did you ask about the glasses?’ I asked, curious.

Poirot shrugged.

‘You have to say something,’ he said. ‘That question was as good as any.’ I stared at him.

‘Anyway, my friend,’ he said seriously, ‘I now know something I wanted to know. Let’s just leave it at that.’
