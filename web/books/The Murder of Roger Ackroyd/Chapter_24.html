The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 24

Ralph Paton’s Story

That was a very awkward moment for me. I barely registered what happened next, but there were shouts and cries of surprise! When I had finally calmed down enough to understand what was happening, Ralph Paton was standing next to his wife, holding her hand, and smiling at me from across the room.

Poirot was also smiling, while shaking a scolding finger at me.

‘Have I not told you at least thirty-six times that it is pointless to hide things from Hercule Poirot?’ he asked.

‘That he always finds out?’ He turned to the others.

‘One day, you remember, we had a little seance around a table - just the six of us. I accused the other five people there of hiding something from me. Four of them revealed their secret. Dr. Sheppard did not. But I have suspected all along. Dr. Sheppard went to the Three Boars that night hoping to find Ralph. He didn't find him there; but what if, I thought, he met him in the street on his way home? Dr. Sheppard was a friend of Captain Paton’s, and he had just come from the crime scene. He must know that things looked very bad for him. Perhaps he knew more than everyone else.’ ‘I did,’ I said sadly. ‘I guess I should just confess everything now. I went to see Ralph that afternoon.

At first, he didn't want to tell me anything, but later he told me about his marriage and the trouble he was in.

As soon as the murder was discovered, I knew that once the truth came out, Ralph would be suspected - or, if not him, the girl he loved. That night I explained the situation clearly to him. The thought of possibly having to give evidence that might incriminate his wife made him decide to - to’ I paused, and Ralph finished my sentence.

‘To run away,’ he said bluntly. ‘You see, Ursula left me to go back to the house. I thought she might have tried to talk to my stepfather again. He had already been very rude to her that afternoon. It seemed possible that he might have insulted her so badly - in such an unacceptable way - that without realizing what she was doing...’ He stopped. Ursula let go of his hand and stepped back.

‘You thought that, Ralph! You actually thought that I might have done it?’ ‘Let us return to Dr Sheppard’s actions,’ said Poirot dryly. ‘Dr Sheppard agreed to help him. He succeeded in hiding Captain Paton from the police.’ ‘Where?’ asked Raymond. ‘In his own house?’ ‘Ah, no, indeed,’ said Poirot. ‘You should ask yourself the question that I did. If the good doctor is hiding the young man, where would he choose? It must be somewhere nearby. I think of Cranchester. A hotel? No.

Lodgings? Even more emphatically, no. Where, then? Ah! I have it. A nursing home. A home for the mentally ill. I test my idea. I invent a nephew with mental problems. I ask Mademoiselle Sheppard about suitable homes. She gives me the names of two near Cranchester where her brother has sent patients. I make inquiries. Yes, at one of them a patient was brought there by the doctor himself early on Saturday morning.

That patient, though known by another name, I easily identified as Captain Paton. After some necessary paperwork, I was allowed to take him away. He arrived at my house early yesterday morning.’ I looked at him sadly.

‘Caroline’s Home Office expert,’ I murmured. ‘And to think I never guessed!’ ‘You see now why I pointed out the limited information in your writing,’ murmured Poirot. ‘It was completely truthful as far as it went - but it didn’t go very far, eh, my friend?’ I was too ashamed to argue.

‘Dr Sheppard has been very loyal,’ said Ralph. ‘He has stood by me through everything. He did what he thought was best. I see now, from what M. Poirot has told me, that it wasn't really the best thing. I should have come forward and faced the consequences. You see, in the home, we never saw a newspaper. I knew nothing of what was going on.’ ‘Dr Sheppard has been a model of discretion,’ said Poirot dryly. ‘But me, I discover all the little secrets. It is my job.’ ‘Now we can have your story of what happened that night,’ said Raymond impatiently.

‘You know it already,’ said Ralph. ‘There’s very little for me to add. I left the summer-house about nine forty-five, and walked around the lanes, trying to decide what to do next - what approach to take. I have to admit that I have no alibi at all, but I give you my solemn word that I never went to the study, that I never saw my stepfather alive - or dead. Whatever the world thinks, I’d like all of you to believe me.’ ‘No alibi,’ murmured Raymond. ‘That’s bad. I believe you, of course, but - it’s a bad situation.’ ‘It makes things very simple, though,’ said Poirot, in a cheerful voice. ‘Very simple indeed.’ We all stared at him.

‘You see what I mean? No? Just this - to save Captain Paton the real criminal must confess.’ He beamed at us all.

‘But yes - I mean what I say. See now, I did not invite Inspector Raglan to be present. That was for a reason. I didn’t want to tell him all that I knew - at least I didn’t want to tell him tonight.’ He leaned forward, and suddenly his voice and his whole personality changed. He suddenly became dangerous.

‘I who speak to you - I know the murderer of Mr Ackroyd is in this room now. It is to the murderer I speak.

Tomorrow the truth goes to Inspector Raglan. You understand?’

There was a tense silence. Into the middle of it came the old Breton woman with a telegram on a tray. Poirot tore it open.

Blunt’s voice rose suddenly, loud and clear.

‘The murderer is among us, you say? You know who?’

Poirot had read the message. He crumpled it up in his hand.

‘I know - now.’ He tapped the crumpled ball of paper.

‘What is that?’ said Raymond sharply.

‘A wireless message - from a steamer now on its way to the United States.’ There was a dead silence. Poirot rose to his feet, bowing.

‘Messieurs et Mesdames, this gathering of mine is over. Remember - the truth goes to Inspector Raglan in the morning.’
