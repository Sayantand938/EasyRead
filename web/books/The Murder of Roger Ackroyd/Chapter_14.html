The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 14

Mrs Ackroyd

After the talk that evening, the case seemed to me to change. The whole thing can be split into two clear parts. Part I covers from Ackroyd’s death on Friday evening to the following Monday night. It's the simple story of what happened, as told to Hercule Poirot. I was always with Poirot. I saw what he saw. I tried to guess what he was thinking, but I now know I failed. Poirot showed me all his findings - like the gold wedding-ring - but he kept back the important impressions he formed. I later learned this secrecy was typical of him. He would hint at things, but nothing more.

As I said, up till Monday evening, my story could have been Poirot’s. I was his Watson to his Sherlock. But after Monday, we went our separate ways. Poirot was busy on his own. I heard about what he was doing, because in King’s Abbot, you hear everything, but he didn’t tell me in advance. And I had my own concerns, too.

Looking back, I’m struck by how fragmented this period was. Everyone helped to solve the mystery. It was like a jigsaw puzzle where everyone added their own bit of knowledge. But that's where their job ended. Only Poirot deserves the credit for putting the pieces in the right place.

Some things seemed unimportant and meaningless at the time. Like the black boots, for example. But that comes later... To keep things in order, I must start with Mrs Ackroyd's summons.

She sent for me early on Tuesday morning. It sounded urgent, so I hurried over, expecting to find her very ill.

She was in bed, as she should be in that situation. She gave me her bony hand and pointed to a chair by the bed.

‘Well, Mrs Ackroyd,’ I said, ‘what’s wrong?’ I spoke with the fake cheerfulness that doctors seem to need.

‘I’m worn out,’ she said weakly. ‘Absolutely worn out. It’s the shock of poor Roger’s death.

They say you often don’t feel these things when they happen.

It’s the reaction later.’ It’s a shame a doctor can’t always say what they really think.

I’d have given anything to say ‘Nonsense!’ Instead, I suggested a tonic. Mrs. Ackroyd took the tonic. One move in the game seemed done.

I didn’t think for a second I was called because of the shock of Ackroyd’s death. But Mrs. Ackroyd can’t be straightforward about anything. She always gets to her point in a twisted way. I wondered why she had sent for me.

‘And then that scene - yesterday,’ my patient went on.

She paused as if waiting for me to say something.

‘What scene?’ ‘Doctor, how can you? Have you forgotten? That awful little Frenchman - or Belgian - or whatever he is.

Bossing us all around. It’s really upset me. On top of Roger’s death.’ ‘I’m very sorry, Mrs. Ackroyd,’ I said.

‘I don’t know what he meant - shouting at us like that. I hope I know my duty too well to hide anything. I’ve given the police all the help I can.’ Mrs. Ackroyd paused, and I said, ‘Quite so.’ I was starting to see what the trouble was about.

‘No one can say I haven’t done my duty,’ Mrs. Ackroyd continued. ‘I’m sure Inspector Raglan is happy. Why is this foreign nobody making a fuss? He looks ridiculous too - like a funny Frenchman in a show. I don’t know why Flora brought him into it. She didn’t say a word to me. Just did it herself.

Flora is too independent. I am a worldly woman and her mother. She should have asked me for advice first.’ I listened to all this in silence.

‘What does he think? That’s what I want to know. Does he really think I’m hiding something? He - he - practically accused me yesterday.’ I shrugged my shoulders.

‘It’s surely not important, Mrs Ackroyd,’ I said.

‘If you’re not hiding anything, his remarks don’t apply to you.’ Mrs Ackroyd changed the subject, as she usually did.

‘Servants are so annoying,’ she said. ‘They gossip and talk to each other. And then it spreads - and it’s probably nothing at all.’ ‘Have the servants been talking?’ I asked. ‘About what?’ Mrs Ackroyd looked at me very closely. It made me uncomfortable.

‘I was sure you’d know, doctor, if anyone did. You were with M. Poirot the whole time, weren’t you?’ ‘I was.’ ‘Then of course you know. It was that girl, Ursula Bourne, wasn’t it? Of course - she’s leaving. She’d want to cause as much trouble as she could. They’re all spiteful. Now, since you were there, doctor, you must know exactly what she said? I really want to make sure no wrong idea gets around. After all, you don’t tell the police every little thing, do you? There are family matters sometimes - nothing to do with the murder. But if the girl was spiteful, she might have made things up.’ I realized that she was very worried. Poirot had been right. Of the six people at the table yesterday, Mrs Ackroyd at least was hiding something. I had to find out what it was.

‘If I were you, Mrs Ackroyd,’ I said bluntly, ‘I’d tell the truth.’ She gave a little scream.

‘Oh! Doctor, how can you be so direct? It sounds as if - as if - But I can explain everything so easily.’ ‘Then why don’t you?’ I asked.

Mrs Ackroyd took out a lacy handkerchief and started to cry.

‘I thought, doctor, you could explain it to M. Poirot - because it’s hard for a foreigner to understand our point of view. And you don’t know - nobody could know - what I’ve had to deal with. A terrible ordeal - a long ordeal. That’s what my life has been. I don’t like to speak badly of the dead - but it’s true. He had to check every little bill - as if Roger had only a few hundred pounds a year instead of being (as Mr Hammond told me yesterday) one of the richest men around here.’ Mrs Ackroyd stopped to dab her eyes with the lacy handkerchief.

‘Yes,’ I said encouragingly. ‘You were talking about bills?’ ‘Those dreadful bills. And some I didn’t want Roger to see at all. They were things a man wouldn’t understand.

He would have said the things weren’t necessary. And of course they added up, you know, and they kept coming!’ She looked at me pleadingly, as though asking me to sympathize with her on this strange problem.

‘It’s something they do,’ I agreed.

And her tone changed - became quite sharp. ‘I assure you, doctor, I was becoming a nervous wreck. I couldn’t sleep at nights. And a dreadful fluttering around my heart.

And then I got a letter from a Scottish gentleman - actually, there were two letters - both Scottish gentlemen. Mr Bruce MacPherson was one, and the other was Colin MacDonald. Quite a coincidence.’ ‘Not really,’ I said dryly. ‘They are usually Scottish gentlemen, but I suspect they have Jewish ancestors.’ ‘Ten pounds to ten thousand on just a signed note,’ murmured Mrs Ackroyd thoughtfully. ‘I wrote to one of them, but there seemed to be problems.’ She paused.

I guessed that we were getting to a sensitive topic. I have never known anyone more difficult to get to the point.

‘You see,’ murmured Mrs Ackroyd, ‘it’s all about what you expect to get,’ isn’t it? What you expect in a will. ‘And though, of course, I expected that Roger would take care of me, I didn’t know. I thought that if only I could look at a copy of his will - not to be nosy - but just so that I could make my own plans.’ She glanced sideways at me. The situation was now very tricky indeed. Fortunately, clever words can hide unpleasant truths.

‘I could only tell this to you, dear Doctor Sheppard,’ said Mrs Ackroyd quickly. ‘I trust you not to judge me badly, and to explain the matter in the right way to M. Poirot. It was on Friday afternoon ‘ She stopped and swallowed nervously.

‘Yes,’ I repeated encouragingly. ‘On Friday afternoon.

Well?’ ‘Everyone was out, or so I thought. And I went into Roger’s study - I had a good reason for going there - I mean, it wasn’t sneaky. And when I saw all the papers piled on the desk, it just occurred to me, like a flash: “I wonder if Roger keeps his will in one of the desk drawers.” I’m so impulsive, always have been, since I was a child. I do things without thinking. He’d left his keys - very careless of him - in the top drawer lock.’ ‘I see,’ I said helpfully. ‘So you searched the desk. Did you find the will?’ Mrs Ackroyd gave a little scream, and I realized that I had not been gentle enough.

‘How dreadful it sounds. But it wasn’t at all like that really.’ ‘Of course it wasn’t,’ I said quickly. ‘You must forgive my clumsy way of saying things.’ ‘Of course, men are so strange. If I were Roger, I wouldn’t have minded showing my will. But men are so secretive. You have to use little tricks to protect yourself.’ ‘And what happened with the little trick?’ I asked.

‘That’s what I’m telling you. Just as I got to the bottom drawer, Bourne came in. Very awkward. Of course, I shut the drawer and stood up, and pointed out some dust on the surface. But I didn’t like the way she looked - polite, but with a nasty look in her eyes. Almost like she was looking down on me, if you know what I mean. I’ve never liked that girl much. She’s a good servant, and she says Ma’am, and doesn’t mind wearing caps and aprons (which a lot of them do these days, I tell you), and she can say “Not at home” without feeling bad if she has to answer the door instead of Parker, and she doesn’t make those weird gurgling sounds that so many parlourmaids seem to make when they serve at the table. Let me see, where was I?’ ‘You were saying that, despite her good qualities, you’ve never liked Bourne.’ ‘No, I don’t. She’s - strange. There’s something different about her. Too educated, in my opinion. You can’t tell who’s a lady and who isn’t these days.’ ‘And what happened next?’ I asked.

‘Nothing. Well, Roger came in. I thought he was out for a walk. He said: “What’s all this?” and I said “Nothing. I just came in to get Punch.” And I took Punch and left. Bourne stayed behind. I heard her ask Roger if she could talk to him for a minute. I went straight up to my room to lie down. I was very upset.’ There was a pause.

‘You will explain to M. Poirot, won’t you? You can see how unimportant it all was. But, of course, when he was so serious about hiding things, I thought of this right away. Bourne might have made up some crazy story, but you can explain, can’t you?’ ‘Is that all?’ I said. ‘Have you told me everything?’ ‘Ye-es,’ said Mrs Ackroyd. ‘Oh! yes,’ she added firmly.

But I had noticed her hesitate for a moment, and I knew she was still hiding something. It was a stroke of genius that made me ask the question I did.

‘Mrs Ackroyd,’ I said, ‘was it you who left the silver table open?’ Her guilty blush, which even makeup couldn’t hide, gave me my answer.

‘How did you know?’ she whispered.

‘So it was you?’ ‘Yes - I - you see - there were a few pieces of old silver - very interesting. I had been reading about them, and there was a picture of a small piece that had sold for a huge price at Christy’s. It looked just like one in the silver table. I thought I’d take it to London with me when I went - and - and have it appraised. If it turned out to be valuable, just think how wonderful it would have been for Roger.’ I didn’t comment, accepting Mrs Ackroyd’s story as it was. I didn’t even ask why she had to take what she wanted in such a sneaky way.

‘Why did you leave the lid open?’ I asked. ‘Did you forget?’ ‘I was startled,’ said Mrs Ackroyd. ‘I heard footsteps on the terrace outside. I rushed out of the room and got upstairs before Parker opened the front door for you.’ ‘That must have been Miss Russell,’ I said thoughtfully.

Mrs Ackroyd had told me something very interesting. I didn’t know or care if her plans for Ackroyd’s silver were honest. What interested me was that Miss Russell must have come into the drawing-room through the window, and I had been right to think she was out of breath from running. Where had she been? I thought of the summer-house and the scrap of fabric.

‘I wonder if Miss Russell has had her handkerchiefs ironed!’ I said without thinking.

Mrs. Ackroyd's surprise reminded me of myself, and I stood up.

‘Do you think you can explain to M. Poirot?’ she asked, worried.

‘Oh, certainly. Absolutely.’ I finally left, after having to listen to more reasons for her behavior.

The maid was in the hall, and she helped me with my coat. I looked at her more closely than before. It was obvious she had been crying.

‘Why,’ I asked, ‘did you tell us Mr. Ackroyd called you to his study on Friday? I now hear you asked to speak to him.’ The girl looked down for a moment.

Then she spoke.

‘I was going to leave anyway,’ she said, sounding unsure.

I didn't say anything else. She opened the front door for me. Just as I was leaving, she said quietly: ‘Excuse me, sir, is there any news of Captain Paton?’ I shook my head, looking at her questioningly.

‘He should come back,’ she said. ‘He really should come back.’ She looked at me with pleading eyes.

‘Does no one know where he is?’ she asked.

‘Do you?’ I said sharply.

She shook her head.

‘No, really. I know nothing. But any friend of his would tell him this: he should come back.’ I waited, thinking she might say more.

Her next question surprised me.

‘When do they think the murder happened? Just before ten o’clock?’ ‘That's the idea,’ I said. ‘Between a quarter to ten and ten.’ ‘Not earlier? Not before a quarter to ten?’ I looked at her closely. She clearly wanted me to say yes.

‘That’s impossible,’ I said. ‘Miss Ackroyd saw her uncle alive at a quarter to ten.’ She turned away, looking defeated.

‘A beautiful girl,’ I said to myself as I drove off. ‘A very beautiful girl.’ Caroline was home. Poirot had visited, and she was thrilled and full of herself.

‘I am helping him with the case,’ she explained.

I felt uneasy. Caroline is bad enough already. How will she be with her detective skills encouraged?

‘Are you going around the neighbourhood looking for Ralph Paton’s mysterious girl?’ I asked.

‘I might do that for myself,’ said Caroline. ‘No, M. Poirot wants me to find out something specific.’ ‘What is it?’ I asked.

‘He wants to know if Ralph Paton’s boots were black or brown,’ said Caroline with great seriousness.

I stared at her. I see now that I was incredibly clueless about these boots. I completely missed the point.

‘They were brown shoes,’ I said. ‘I saw them.’ ‘Not shoes, James, boots. M. Poirot wants to know if a pair of boots Ralph had with him at the hotel were brown or black. It’s very important.’ Call me slow if you like. I didn’t get it.

‘And how are you going to find out?’ I asked.

Caroline said it would be easy. Our Annie’s best friend was Miss Gannett’s maid, Clara. And Clara was dating the Boots at the Three Boars.

It was very simple, and with Miss Gannett’s help, who quickly agreed to let Clara leave, the matter was resolved very quickly.

It was when we were sitting down to lunch that Caroline said casually: ‘About those boots of Ralph Paton’s.’ ‘Well,’ I said, ‘what about them?’ ‘M. Poirot thought they were probably brown. He was wrong. They’re black.’ And Caroline nodded her head several times. She clearly felt she had beaten Poirot.

I did not answer. I was wondering what the colour of a pair of Ralph Paton’s boots had to do with the case.
