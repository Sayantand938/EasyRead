The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 27

Apologia

Five a.m. I'm very tired, but I've finished my work. My arm hurts from writing.

A strange ending to my manuscript. I intended it to be published someday as the story of one of Poirot's failures!

It's funny how things turn out.

I've had a feeling of something bad coming from the start, from when I saw Ralph Paton and Mrs. Ferrars whispering together. I thought she was telling him something important. I was wrong, but the idea stayed with me even after I went into the study with Ackroyd that night, until he told me the truth.

Poor old Ackroyd. I'm glad I gave him a chance. I urged him to read that letter before it was too late.

Or, let me be honest, didn't I kind of know that with a stubborn guy like him, it was the best way to make sure he didn't read it? His nervousness that night was interesting. He knew danger was near, but he never suspected me.

The dagger was a last-minute idea. I had brought a useful little weapon of my own, but when I saw the dagger on the silver table, I immediately realized it would be much better to use a weapon that couldn't be traced back to me.

I guess I must have planned to kill him all along. As soon as I heard about Mrs. Ferrars's death, I was sure she would have told him everything before she died. When I met him and he seemed so worried, I thought maybe he knew the truth but couldn't believe it and was going to give me a chance to deny it.

So I went home and took precautions. If the trouble was just about Ralph, no harm done. The dictaphone he'd given me two days ago needed fixing. Something was off, so I offered to fix it instead of sending it back. I did what I needed to and took it with me that evening.

I'm quite proud of my writing. What's better than this: ‘The letters came at eight forty. I left him just before nine, the letter still unopened.’

I paused at the door, wondering if I'd forgotten something. All true. But what if I'd put stars after the first sentence? Would someone wonder what happened in those ten minutes?

Looking around from the door, I was satisfied. Nothing was left undone. The dictaphone was on the table by the window, set for nine-thirty (it worked like an alarm clock), and the armchair hid it from the door.

I was shocked to see Parker outside. I've written that down.

Later, when they found the body, I sent Parker to call the police, saying: ‘I did what little I could!’ It was just putting the dictaphone in my bag and putting the chair back. I never thought Parker would notice the chair. He should have been too focused on the body. But I forgot about the trained servant's eye.

I wish I knew Flora would say she saw her uncle alive at nine forty-five.

That confused me greatly. Throughout this case, things have baffled me.

Everyone seems involved.

I've been most afraid of Caroline. I thought she might guess. It's strange how she mentioned my ‘weakness.’ She'll never know. Like Poirot said, there's a way out...

I can trust him. He and Inspector Raglan will handle it together. I don’t want Caroline to know. She cares about me, and she's proud too... My death will sadden her, but sadness fades...

When I'm done writing, I'll put this entire manuscript in an envelope and address it to Poirot.

And then - what should it be? Veronal? There's a kind of fitting irony. Not that I'm to blame for Mrs. Ferrars’s death. It was her own fault. I don’t feel sorry for her.

I don’t feel sorry for myself either.

So, veronal it is.

But I wish Hercule Poirot had never retired and come here to grow vegetable marrows.
