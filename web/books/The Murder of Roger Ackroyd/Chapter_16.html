The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 16

An Evening at Mah Jong

That night we had a small Mah Jong party. This simple amusement is quite popular in King’s Abbot. The guests arrive in their overshoes and raincoats after dinner. They have coffee and later cake, sandwiches, and tea.

Tonight, our guests were Miss Gannett and Colonel Carter, who lives near the church. A lot of gossip is shared at these evenings, sometimes really getting in the way of the game. We used to play bridge - very chatty bridge, the worst kind. We find Mah Jong much more peaceful. The annoyed question of why your partner didn't play a certain card is completely gone, and even though we still say what we think, there isn't the same angry feeling.

‘Very cold evening, eh, Sheppard?’ said Colonel Carter, standing with his back to the fire. Caroline had taken Miss Gannett to her room, and was helping her take off her many layers of clothing. ‘Reminds me of the Afghan passes.’ ‘Indeed?’ I said politely.

‘Very strange business, this, about poor Ackroyd,’ continued the colonel, taking a cup of coffee. ‘A lot more to it than we know - that’s what I think. Between you and me, Sheppard, I’ve heard someone mention blackmail!’ The colonel gave me a look that seemed to say, ‘we both know how the world works.’ ‘A woman’s involved, for sure,’ he said. ‘You can bet on it, a woman’s involved.’ Caroline and Miss Gannett came back to us at that moment. Miss Gannett drank coffee while Caroline got out the Mah Jong box and spread the tiles on the table.

‘Washing the tiles,’ said the colonel jokingly. ‘That’s right - washing the tiles, as we used to say at the Shanghai Club.’ Caroline and I both think that Colonel Carter has never been to the Shanghai Club in his life. We also think he’s never been farther east than India, where he played around with cans of corned beef and jam during the Great War. But the colonel really wants to seem military, and in King’s Abbot we let people enjoy their little quirks.

‘Shall we start?’ said Caroline.

We sat around the table. For about five minutes, it was completely silent because we're all secretly trying to be the fastest at building our wall.

‘Go on, James,’ said Caroline at last. ‘You’re East Wind.’ I threw away a tile. We played a few rounds, interrupted by the usual comments of ‘Three Bamboos,’ ‘Two Circles,’ ‘Pung,’ and often from Miss Gannett ‘Unpung,’ because she often claimed tiles too quickly when she didn't have the right to.

‘I saw Flora Ackroyd this morning,’ said Miss Gannett.

‘Pung - no - Unpung. I made a mistake.’ ‘Four Circles,’ said Caroline. ‘Where did you see her?’ ‘She didn’t see me,’ said Miss Gannett, with that tremendous importance only found in small villages.

‘Ah!’ said Caroline interestedly. ‘Chow.’ ‘I believe,’ said Miss Gannett, briefly distracted, ‘that it’s now proper to say “Chee” not “Chow.”‘ ‘Nonsense,’ said Caroline. ‘I have always said “Chow.”‘ ‘In the Shanghai Club,’ said Colonel Carter, ‘they say “Chow;” Miss Gannett retreated, defeated.

‘What were you saying about Flora Ackroyd?’ asked Caroline, after a moment or two spent on the game. ‘Was she with anyone?’ ‘Very much so,’ said Miss Gannett.

The eyes of the two ladies met, and seemed to exchange information.

‘Really,’ said Caroline interestedly. ‘Is that so? Well, it doesn’t surprise me in the least.’ ‘We’re waiting for you to discard, Miss Caroline,’ said the colonel. He sometimes pretends to be the straightforward man, focused on the game and uninterested in gossip. But nobody is fooled.

‘If you ask me,’ said Miss Gannett. (‘Was that a Bamboo you discarded, dear? Oh! no, I see now - it was a Circle.) As I was saying, if you ask me, Flora’s been extremely lucky.

Extremely lucky she’s been.’ ‘How so, Miss Gannett?’ asked the colonel. ‘I’ll Pung that Green Dragon. How do you figure that Miss Flora’s been lucky? Very charming girl and all that, I know.’ ‘I may not know much about crime,’ said Miss Gannett, with the air of someone who knows everything, ‘but I can tell you one thing. The first question that’s always asked is “Who last saw the deceased alive?” And the person who did is regarded with suspicion. Now, Flora Ackroyd last saw her uncle alive. It might have looked very bad for her - very bad indeed. It’s my opinion - and I offer it for what it’s worth, that Ralph Paton is staying away because of her, to take suspicion away from her.’ ‘Come, now,’ I protested gently, ‘you surely can’t suggest that a young girl like Flora Ackroyd is capable of stabbing her uncle in cold blood?’ ‘Well, I don’t know,’ said Miss Gannett. ‘I’ve just been reading a book from the library about the criminal world of Paris, and it says that some of the worst women criminals are young girls with the faces of angels.’ ‘That’s in France,’ said Caroline immediately.

‘Just so,’ said the colonel. ‘Now, I’ll tell you a very curious thing - a story that was going around the markets in India...’ The colonel’s story was incredibly long, and of remarkably little interest. A thing that happened in India many years ago cannot compare at all with an event that took place in King’s Abbot the day before yesterday.

It was Caroline who brought the colonel’s story to an end by fortunately winning Mah Jong. After the slight awkwardness always caused by my corrections of Caroline’s somewhat flawed calculations, we started a new hand.

‘East Wind passes,’ said Caroline. ‘I’ve got an idea of my own about Ralph Paton. Three Characters. But I’m keeping it to myself for now.’ ‘Are you, dear?’ said Miss Gannett. ‘Chow - I mean Pung.’ ‘Yes,’ said Caroline firmly.

‘Was everything all right with the boots?’ asked Miss Gannett.

‘Their being black, I mean?’ ‘Quite all right,’ said Caroline.

‘What was the point, do you think?’ asked Miss Gannett.

Caroline pursed her lips and shook her head, acting like she knew everything about it.

‘Pung,’ said Miss Gannett. ‘No - Unpung. I suppose that now the doctor's working with M. Poirot, he knows all the secrets?’ ‘Far from it,’ I said.

‘James is so modest,’ said Caroline. ‘Ah! A concealed Kong.’ The colonel whistled. For the moment, gossip was forgotten.

‘Your own wind, too,’ he said. ‘And you’ve got two Pungs of Dragons. We must be careful. Miss Caroline’s aiming for a big hand.’ We played for some minutes without any other talk.

‘This M. Poirot now,’ said Colonel Carter, ‘is he really such a great detective?’ ‘The greatest the world has ever known,’ said Caroline solemnly. ‘He has to come here secretly to avoid attention.’ ‘Chow,’ said Miss Gannett. ‘Quite wonderful for our little village, I’m sure. By the way, Clara - my maid, you know is great friends with Elsie, the housemaid at Fernly, and guess what Elsie told her? That a lot of money’s been stolen, and Elsie thinks the parlourmaid had something to do with it. She’s leaving at the end of the month, and she’s crying a lot at night. If you ask me, the girl is probably working with a gang. She’s always been a strange girl - she’s not friends with any of the girls around here. She goes off by herself on her days out - very strange, I think, and very suspicious. I asked her once to come to our Friendly Girls’ Evenings, but she refused, and then I asked her some questions about her home and her family - all that sort of thing, and I have to say I thought she was very rude. She seemed very respectful, but she shut me down very obviously.’ Miss Gannett stopped to breathe, and the colonel, who didn’t care at all about the servant, said that fast play was normal at the Shanghai Club.

We had a round of fast play.

‘That Miss Russell,’ said Caroline. ‘She came here pretending to ask James for advice on Friday morning. I think she wanted to see where the poisons were kept. Five Characters.’ ‘Chow,’ said Miss Gannett. ‘What an odd idea! I wonder if you could be right.’ ‘Speaking of poisons,’ said the colonel. ‘Eh - what? Haven’t I thrown that away? Oh! Eight Bamboos.’ ‘Mah Jong!’ said Miss Gannett.

Caroline was very annoyed.

‘One Red Dragon,’ she said sadly, ‘and I would have had a hand of three doubles.’ ‘I’ve had two Red Dragons the whole time,’ I mentioned.

‘That’s just like you, James,’ said Caroline, disapprovingly. ‘You don’t understand the game at all.’ I thought I had played quite well. I would have had to pay Caroline a lot if she had gone Mah Jong. Miss Gannett’s Mah Jong was very bad, as Caroline made sure to tell her.

East Wind passed, and we started a new hand quietly.

‘What I wanted to tell you was this,’ said Caroline.

‘Yes?’ said Miss Gannett encouragingly.

‘My idea about Ralph Paton, I mean.’ ‘Yes, dear,’ said Miss Gannett, even more encouragingly. ‘Chow!’ ‘It’s a sign of weakness to Chow so early,’ said Caroline sternly. ‘You should try for a big hand.’ ‘I know,’ said Miss Gannett. ‘You were saying - about Ralph Paton, you know?’ ‘Yes. Well, I’ve a pretty good idea where he is.’ We all stopped to stare at her.

‘This is very interesting. Miss Caroline,’ said Colonel Carter. ‘All your own idea, eh?’ ‘Well, not exactly. I’ll tell you about it. You know that big map of the county we have in the hall?’ We all said Yes.

‘As M. Poirot was leaving the other day, he stopped and looked at it, and he made some remark - I can’t remember exactly what it was. Something about Cranchester being the only big town anywhere near us which is true, of course. But after he had gone - it suddenly came to me.’ ‘What came to you?’ ‘What he meant. Of course Ralph is in Cranchester.’ It was at that moment that I knocked down the rack holding my pieces. My sister immediately scolded me for clumsiness, but not really. She was focused on her theory.

‘Cranchester, Miss Caroline?’ said Colonel Carter. ‘Surely not Cranchester! It’s so near.’ ‘That’s exactly it,’ cried Caroline triumphantly. ‘It seems quite clear now that he didn’t leave here by tram. He must have simply walked into Cranchester. And I believe he’s still there. No one would think of him being so close.’ I pointed out several problems with the theory, but once Caroline has an idea in her head, nothing changes it.

‘And you think M. Poirot has the same idea,’ said Miss Gannett thoughtfully. ‘It’s a strange coincidence, but I was out for a walk this afternoon on the Cranchester road, and he passed me in a car coming from that direction.’ We all looked at each other.

‘Why, dear me,’ said Miss Gannett suddenly, ‘I’ve had Mah Jong all this time, and I never noticed it.’ Caroline’s attention was taken away from her own thinking. She pointed out to Miss Gannett that a hand made up of different suits and too many Chows was not worth calling Mah Jong. Miss Gannett listened calmly and collected her counters.

‘Yes, dear, I know what you mean,’ she said. ‘But it depends on what kind of hand you start with, doesn’t it?’ ‘You’ll never get the big hands if you don’t try for them,’ urged Caroline.

‘Well, we must all play our own way, mustn’t we?’ said Miss Gannett. She looked down at her counters. ‘After all, I’m winning, so far.’ Caroline, who was losing a lot, said nothing.

East Wind passed, and we started again. Annie brought in the tea things. Caroline and Miss Gannett were both a little annoyed, as often happens during these fun evenings.

‘If you would only play a little faster, dear,’ said Caroline, as Miss Gannett hesitated before discarding a tile. ‘The Chinese put down the tiles so quickly it sounds like little birds chirping.’ For some minutes we played like the Chinese.

‘You haven’t shared much information, Sheppard,’ said Colonel Carter kindly. ‘You’re a sly dog. Working closely with the great detective, and not a hint about what’s happening.’ ‘James is an unusual person,’ said Caroline. ‘He can’t bring himself to share information.’ She looked at me disapprovingly.

‘I assure you,’ I said, ‘that I don’t know anything. Poirot keeps his thoughts to himself.’ ‘Wise man,’ said the colonel with a chuckle. ‘He doesn’t give himself away. But they’re amazing, these foreign detectives. They're full of tricks, I think.’ ‘Pung,’ said Miss Gannett, in a quietly pleased tone.

‘And Mah Jong.’ The situation became more tense. Annoyed that Miss Gannett was winning at Mah Jong for the third time, Caroline said to me as we built a new wall: ‘You are so annoying, James. You sit there like a dummy, and say nothing at all!’ ‘But, my dear,’ I protested, ‘I really have nothing to say - at least, not what you mean.’ ‘Nonsense,’ said Caroline, as she sorted her tiles. ‘You must know something interesting.’ I didn't answer right away. I was stunned and thrilled. I had heard of someone getting The Perfect Winning - winning Mah Jong with their starting hand. I never thought I'd get that hand myself.

With hidden joy, I laid my hand face up on the table.

‘As they say in the Shanghai Club,’ I remarked - ‘Tin-ho - the Perfect Winning!’ The colonel’s eyes almost popped out of his head.

‘Good heavens,’ he said, ‘what an amazing thing. I've never seen that before!’ It was then that I went on, pushed by Caroline’s taunts, and made bold by my victory.

‘And as for something interesting,’ I said. ‘How about a gold wedding ring with a date and “From R.” inside.’ I'll skip the scene that followed. I was forced to say exactly where this treasure was found. I was made to reveal the date.

‘March 13th,’ said Caroline. ‘Just six months ago. Ah!’ From the jumble of excited ideas and guesses, three theories came out: 1. Colonel Carter’s idea: that Ralph was secretly married to Flora. The first and simplest idea.

2. Miss Gannett’s idea: that Roger Ackroyd had been secretly married to Mrs. Ferrars.

3. My sister’s idea: that Roger Ackroyd had married his housekeeper, Miss Russell.

A fourth, even grander, theory was suggested by Caroline later as we went up to bed.

‘Mark my words,’ she said suddenly, ‘I wouldn’t be surprised if Geoffrey Raymond and Flora got married.’ ‘Surely it would be “From G,” not “From R” then,’ I suggested.

‘You never know. Some girls call men by their surnames.

And you heard what Miss Gannett said this evening - about Flora’s behavior.’ Strictly speaking, I hadn’t heard Miss Gannett say anything like that, but I respected Caroline’s understanding of hints.

‘How about Hector Blunt?’ I suggested. ‘If it’s anyone ‘ ‘Nonsense,’ said Caroline. ‘I expect he admires her, maybe even loves her. But believe me, a girl won’t fall for a man old enough to be her father when there’s a handsome secretary around. She might encourage Major Blunt as a cover. Girls are very clever.

But I’ll tell you one thing, James Sheppard. Flora Ackroyd doesn’t care at all for Ralph Paton, and never has. You can believe me.’ I believed her without question.
