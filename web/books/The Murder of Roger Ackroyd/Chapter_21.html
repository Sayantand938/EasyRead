The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 21

The Paragraph in the Paper

Caroline, of course, had seen Miss Russell come to the surgery door. I had expected this, and had prepared a detailed explanation of the lady’s bad knee. But Caroline wasn’t in an interrogative mood. She believed she knew why Miss Russell had really come and that I didn't.

‘Pumping you, James,’ said Caroline. ‘Pumping you shamelessly, I’m sure. Don’t bother interrupting. I bet you had no idea she was doing it. Men are so naive. She knows you are close to M. Poirot, and she wants to find things out. Do you know what I think, James?’ ‘I can’t even guess. You think so many strange things.’ ‘There’s no point in being sarcastic. I think Miss Russell knows more about Mr. Ackroyd’s death than she’s letting on.’ Caroline leaned back triumphantly in her chair.

‘Do you really think so?’ I said distractedly.

‘You’re very boring today, James. No energy. It’s your liver.’ Our conversation then turned to personal matters.

The paragraph inspired by Poirot appeared in our daily paper the next morning. I didn’t know its purpose, but its effect on Caroline was huge.

She started by saying, quite falsely, that she had said so all along. I raised my eyebrows, but didn't argue.

Caroline, however, must have felt a pang of guilt, for she went on: ‘I might not have actually said Liverpool, but I knew he’d try to escape to America. That’s what Crippen did.’ ‘Not very successfully,’ I reminded her.

‘Poor boy, and they’ve caught him. I think, James, it’s your duty to make sure he isn't hanged.’ ‘What do you expect me to do?’ ‘Well, you’re a doctor, aren’t you? You’ve known him since he was a boy. He's not mentally stable. That’s the approach to take, clearly. I read recently that people are very happy in Broadmoor - it’s like a fancy club.’ But Caroline’s words reminded me of something.

‘I didn't know Poirot had a simple-minded nephew?’ I asked, curious.

‘Didn’t you? Oh, he told me all about it. Poor boy. It’s a great sadness for the family. They’ve kept him at home so far, but it’s getting so bad that they’re worried he’ll have to go into an institution.’ ‘I suppose you know almost everything about Poirot’s family by now,’ I said, annoyed.

‘Pretty much,’ said Caroline, pleased. ‘It helps people to tell someone all their troubles.’ ‘It might,’ I said, ‘if they were ever allowed to do so willingly. Whether they enjoy having secrets forced out of them is another matter.’ Caroline just looked at me like a Christian martyr enjoying being martyred.

‘You are so private, James,’ she said. ‘You hate speaking openly or sharing any information, and you think everyone else must be the same. I hope I never force secrets out of anyone. For example, if M. Poirot comes this afternoon, as he said he might, I won’t ask him who arrived at his house early this morning.’ ‘Early this morning?’ I asked.

‘Very early,’ said Caroline. ‘Before the milkman. I just happened to be looking out the window - the blind was loose. It was a man. He came in a closed car, and he was all covered up. I couldn’t see his face. But I’ll tell you my idea, and you’ll see I’m right.’ ‘What’s your idea?’ Caroline lowered her voice mysteriously.

‘A government expert,’ she whispered.

‘A government expert,’ I said, surprised. ‘My dear Caroline!’ ‘Remember my words, James, you’ll see I’m right. That Russell woman was here that morning after your poisons.

Roger Ackroyd could easily have been poisoned in his food that night." I laughed out loud.

"Nonsense," I cried. "He was stabbed in the neck. You know that as well as I do." "After death, James," said Caroline, "to make a false clue." "My good woman," I said, "I examined the body, and I know what I'm talking about. That wound wasn't inflicted after death - it was the cause of death, and you can be sure of that." Caroline merely continued to look all-knowing, which so annoyed me that I went on: "Perhaps you will tell me, Caroline, whether I have a medical degree or not?" "You have the medical degree, I suppose, James - at least, I mean I know you have. But you've no imagination whatsoever."

"Having given you a triple portion, there was none left for me," I said dryly.

I was amused to see Caroline’s tricks that afternoon when Poirot duly arrived. My sister, without asking a direct question, danced around the subject of the mysterious guest in every way imaginable. By the twinkle in Poirot’s eyes, I saw that he realized her aim. He remained calmly unaffected, and blocked her efforts so successfully that she herself was at a loss how to proceed.

Having, I suspect, enjoyed the little game, he rose to his feet and suggested a walk.

"It is that I need to reduce the figure a little," he explained. "You will come with me, doctor? And perhaps later Miss Caroline will give us some tea." "Delighted," said Caroline. "Won't your - er - guest come in also?" "You are too kind," said Poirot. "But no, my friend is resting. Soon you must meet him." "Quite an old friend of yours, so somebody told me," said Caroline, making one last valiant effort.

"Did they?" murmured Poirot. "Well, we must start." Our walk took us in the direction of Fernly. I had guessed beforehand that it might. I was beginning to understand Poirot’s methods. Every little detail had a bearing upon the whole.

"I have a request for you, my friend," he said at last.

"Tonight, at my house. I want to have a little conference.

You will attend, won't you?" "Certainly," I said.

‘Good. I also need those in the house - that is: Mrs Ackroyd, Mademoiselle Flora, Major Blunt, Mr Raymond. I want you to be my messenger. This little meeting is set for nine o’clock. You will ask them - yes?’ ‘With pleasure; but why not ask them yourself?’ ‘Because they will then ask questions: Why? What for? They will want to know my idea. And, as you know, my friend, I really don’t like explaining my ideas until the time is right.’ I smiled a little.

‘My friend Hastings, the one I told you about, used to say I was like a clam. But he was wrong. I keep no facts to myself. But everyone has their own way of seeing them.’ ‘When do you want me to do this?’ ‘Now, if you can. We’re close to the house.’ ‘Aren’t you coming in?’ ‘No, I will walk around the grounds. I’ll meet you by the lodge gates in fifteen minutes.’ I nodded and started my task. The only one at home was Mrs Ackroyd, drinking an early cup of tea. She greeted me very kindly.

‘So grateful to you, doctor,’ she said softly, ‘for clearing up that little thing with M. Poirot. But life is one problem after another. You’ve heard about Flora, of course?’ ‘What exactly?’ I asked carefully.

‘This new engagement. Flora and Hector Blunt. Of course, not as good as Ralph would have been.

But happiness comes first. Dear Flora needs an older man - someone steady and reliable, and Hector is quite a distinguished man in his own way. You saw the news about Ralph’s arrest in the paper this morning?’ ‘Yes,’ I said, ‘I did.’ ‘Horrible.’ Mrs Ackroyd closed her eyes and shivered.

‘Geoffrey Raymond was very upset. He called Liverpool. But they wouldn’t tell him anything at the police station. They said they hadn’t arrested Ralph at all. Mr Raymond says it’s all a mistake - a ~ what do they call it? - a newspaper rumour. I’ve told everyone not to mention it in front of the servants. Such a terrible shame.

Imagine if Flora had married him.’ Mrs Ackroyd shut her eyes in pain. I wondered when I could give Poirot’s invitation.

Before I could speak, Mrs Ackroyd started again.

‘You were here yesterday with that awful Inspector Raglan, weren’t you? A terrible man - he scared Flora into saying she took the money from Roger’s room. But it was simple, really. The dear girl wanted to borrow a few pounds, didn’t want to bother her uncle since he’d told her not to. But she knew where he kept his money, so she went and took what she needed.’ ‘Is that what Flora says happened?’ I asked.

‘My dear doctor, you know what girls are like these days. So easily influenced. You know about hypnosis and that sort of thing. The inspector yells at her, says the word “steal” over and over, until the poor girl gets an inhibition - or is it a complex? - I always get those two mixed up - and actually thinks she stole the money. I knew right away what happened. But I’m thankful for the whole thing in one way - it seems to have brought Hector and Flora together. And I’ve been worried about Flora: I thought she might get involved with young Raymond. Just imagine!’ Mrs Ackroyd’s voice rose in horror. ‘A private secretary - with no money of his own.’ ‘That would have been hard on you,’ I said. ‘Now, Mrs Ackroyd, I have a message for you from M. Hercule Poirot.’ ‘For me?’ Mrs Ackroyd looked worried.

I quickly reassured her, and I explained what Poirot wanted.

‘Certainly,’ said Mrs. Ackroyd rather doubtfully. ‘I suppose we must come if M. Poirot says so. But what is it all about? I like to know beforehand.’ I truthfully told her that I didn't know any more than she did.

‘Very well,’ said Mrs. Ackroyd at last, rather reluctantly, ‘I will tell the others, and we will be there at nine o’clock.’ Then I left and met Poirot at the agreed meeting place.

‘I’m afraid I’m more than fifteen minutes late,’ I said. ‘But once that good woman starts talking, it’s almost impossible to get a word in.’ ‘It does not matter,’ said Poirot. ‘I have been well amused. This park is magnificent.’ We started for home. When we arrived, to our great surprise, Caroline, who had clearly been watching for us, opened the door herself.

She put her finger to her lips. Her face was full of importance and excitement.

‘Ursula Bourne,’ she said, ‘the parlourmaid from Fernly. She’s here! I’ve put her in the dining-room. She’s in a terrible state, poor thing. Says she must see M. Poirot!’
