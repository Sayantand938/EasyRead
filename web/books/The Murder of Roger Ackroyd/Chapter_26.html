The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 26

And Nothing But The Truth

There was a dead silence for a minute and a half.

Then I laughed.

‘You’re mad,’ I said.

‘No,’ said Poirot calmly. ‘I am not mad. It was the small difference in time that first made me suspicious of you - right at the beginning.’ ‘Difference in time?’ I asked, confused.

‘Yes. You remember that everyone agreed - you included - that it took five minutes to walk from the lodge to the house - less if you took the short cut to the terrace. But you left the house at ten minutes to nine - both according to you and Parker, and yet it was nine o’clock when you passed through the lodge gates. It was a cold night - not one for a leisurely stroll; why did it take you ten minutes to do a five-minute walk? All along, I knew that we only had your word that the study window was ever locked. Ackroyd asked you if you had locked it - he didn't check. What if the study window was unlocked? Would you have time in those ten minutes to run around the outside of the house, change your shoes, climb in through the window, kill Ackroyd, and get to the gate by nine o’clock? I decided against that idea because a nervous man like Ackroyd would probably hear you climbing in, and there would be a struggle. But what if you killed Ackroyd before you left - while standing beside his chair? Then you go out the front door, run around to the summerhouse, take Ralph Paton’s shoes out of the bag you brought that night, put them on, walk through the mud, and leave prints on the window ledge. You climb in, lock the study door from the inside, run back to the summerhouse, change back into your own shoes, and rush down to the gate. (I did something similar the other day when you were with Mrs. Ackroyd - it took exactly ten minutes.) Then home - and an alibi - since you had set the dictaphone for half-past nine.’ ‘My dear Poirot,’ I said, my voice sounding strange and strained, ‘you’ve been thinking about this case too much. What could I possibly gain by murdering Ackroyd?’ ‘Safety. You were the one who blackmailed Mrs. Ferrars. Who would know better what killed Mr. Ferrars than the doctor treating him? When you spoke to me that first day in the garden, you mentioned a legacy you received about a year ago. I have found no evidence of a legacy. You had to create a story to explain Mrs. Ferrars’s twenty thousand pounds. It didn't help you much. You lost most of it gambling - then you pushed too hard, and Mrs. Ferrars killed herself, which you didn't expect. If Ackroyd learned the truth, he would have been merciless - you would have been ruined forever.’ ‘And the telephone call?’ I asked, trying to recover. ‘You have a good explanation for that too, I suppose?’ ‘I admit that it was my biggest problem when I found that a call had actually been made to you from King’s Abbot station. At first, I thought you had simply made up the story. It was a very clever move. You needed an excuse to arrive at Fernly, find the body, and have a chance to remove the dictaphone that provided your alibi. I had a vague idea of how it worked when I visited your sister that first day and asked about your patients on Friday morning. I wasn't thinking about Miss Russell at that time. Her visit was a lucky coincidence because it distracted you from the real purpose of my questions. I found what I was looking for. Among your patients that morning was the steward of an American liner. Who better to be leaving for Liverpool on the train that evening? And then he would be far out at sea, out of reach. I noted that the Orion sailed on Saturday, and after getting the steward's name, I sent him a wireless message asking a question. The reply you saw me receive just now is his answer.’ He held out the message to me. It read: ‘Quite correct. Dr. Sheppard asked me to leave a note at a patient’s house. I was to call him from the station with the reply. Reply was “No answer.”‘ ‘It was a clever idea,’ said Poirot. ‘The call was real. Your sister saw you take it. But there was only one person's word about what was actually said - yours!’ I yawned.

‘All this,’ I said, ‘is very interesting - but not very practical.’ ‘You think not? Remember what I said - the truth goes to Inspector Raglan in the morning. But, for your sister's sake, I am willing to give you another option. There might be, for example, an overdose of sleeping pills. Do you understand me? But Captain Ralph Paton must be cleared - that goes without saying. I suggest you finish that interesting manuscript you're writing - but be more open about things.’ ‘You seem to have a lot of suggestions,’ I remarked.

‘Are you sure you’ve quite finished?’ ‘Now that you mention it, there is one more thing. It would be very unwise to try to silence me as you silenced Mr. Ackroyd. That doesn't work against Hercule Poirot, you understand.’ ‘My dear Poirot,’ I said, smiling slightly, ‘whatever else I may be, I'm not a fool.’ I stood up.

‘Well, well,’ I said, yawning slightly, ‘I must go home.

"Thank you for a very interesting and helpful evening." Poirot also stood up and bowed with his usual politeness as I left the room.
