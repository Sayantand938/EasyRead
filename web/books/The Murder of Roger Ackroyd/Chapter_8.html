The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 8

Inspector Raglan is Confident

We looked at each other.

‘You’ll check with the station, right?’ I said.

‘Of course, but I’m not too hopeful about it.

You know how that station is.’ I did. King’s Abbot is just a village, but its station is a major hub. Most of the big express trains stop there, and trains are moved around, re-sorted, and put together. It has a few public phone booths. Around that time of night, three local trains arrive one after another, to connect with the express train to the north that comes in at 10:19 and leaves at 10:23. The place is chaotic, and the chances of anyone noticing a specific person making a call or getting on the express are very slim.

‘But why call at all?’ Melrose asked. ‘That’s what I don’t get. It makes no sense.’ Poirot carefully straightened a china figure on one of the bookshelves.

‘There was a reason, for sure,’ he said, without turning around.

‘But what reason could it be?’ ‘When we know that, we’ll know everything. This case is very strange and very interesting.’ There was something almost impossible to describe in the way he said those last words. I felt like he was looking at the case from a unique perspective, and I couldn't figure out what he saw.

He went to the window and stood there, looking out.

‘You say it was nine o’clock, Dr Sheppard, when you met this stranger outside the gate?’ He asked without turning around.

‘Yes,’ I replied. ‘I heard the church clock strike nine.’ ‘How long would it take him to get to the house - to this window, for example?’ ‘Five minutes at most. Only two or three minutes if he took the path to the right of the drive and came straight here.’ ‘But to do that he would have to know the way. How can I explain? - it would mean he had been here before - that he knew the place.’ ‘That’s true,’ replied Colonel Melrose.

‘We could probably find out if Mr Ackroyd had any visitors in the past week?’ ‘Young Raymond could tell us that,’ I said.

‘Or Parker,’ suggested Colonel Melrose.

‘Both of them,’ suggested Poirot, smiling.

Colonel Melrose went to find Raymond, and I rang the bell again for Parker.

Colonel Melrose returned quickly, with the young secretary, whom he introduced to Poirot. Geoffrey Raymond was as lively and cheerful as ever. He seemed surprised and pleased to meet Poirot.

‘I had no idea you were living among us secretly, M. Poirot,’ he said. ‘It will be a great honor to watch you work - Hello, what’s this?’ Poirot had been standing just to the left of the door. Now he stepped aside suddenly, and I saw that while I wasn’t looking, he must have quickly pulled out the armchair to where Parker said it was.

‘Do you want me to sit in the chair while you test my blood?’ asked Raymond cheerfully. ‘What’s the idea?’ ‘M. Raymond, this chair was pulled out - like this - last night when Mr Ackroyd was found dead. Someone put it back. Did you do that?’ The secretary answered immediately, without hesitation.

‘No, I didn’t. I don’t even recall it being there, but it must have been if you say so. Anyway, someone else must have put it back where it belonged.

Did they ruin a clue by doing that? Too bad!’ ‘It doesn't matter,’ said the detective. ‘Not at all. What I really want to ask you is this, M.

Raymond: Did any unknown person visit Mr. Ackroyd this past week?’ The secretary thought for a minute or two, frowning, and during the pause Parker came in after being summoned.

‘No,’ said Raymond finally. ‘I can’t think of anyone.

Can you, Parker?’ ‘I'm sorry, sir?’ ‘Any unknown person coming to see Mr. Ackroyd this week?’ The butler paused for a minute or two.

‘There was the young man who came on Wednesday, sir,’ he said eventually. ‘From Curtis and Troute, I believe.’ Raymond waved this away impatiently.

‘Oh! yes, I remember, but that's not the kind of stranger this gentleman means.’ He turned to Poirot. ‘Mr. Ackroyd was thinking about buying a dictaphone,’ he explained. ‘It would have helped us get more done faster. The company sent their representative, but nothing happened. Mr. Ackroyd didn’t decide to buy it.’ Poirot turned to the butler.

‘Can you describe this young man to me, my good Parker?’ ‘He was fair-haired, sir, and short. Very well-dressed in a blue serge suit. A very respectable young man, sir, for someone of his position.’ Poirot turned to me.

‘The man you met outside the gate, doctor, was tall, wasn’t he?’ ‘Yes,’ I said. ‘About six feet, I’d guess.’ ‘That means nothing then,’ declared the Belgian. ‘Thank you, Parker.’ The butler spoke to Raymond.

‘Mr. Hammond has just arrived, sir,’ he said. ‘He wants to know if he can help, and he’d like to speak with you.’ ‘I’ll be right there,’ said the young man. He hurried out.

Poirot looked questioningly at the chief constable.

‘The family lawyer, M. Poirot,’ said the latter.

‘It's a busy time for this young M. Raymond,’ murmured M. Poirot. ‘He seems efficient.’ ‘I believe Mr. Ackroyd thought he was a very good secretary.’ ‘He's been here - how long?’ ‘Just about two years, I think.’ ‘He does his job carefully. I'm sure of that. What does he do for fun? Is he sporty?’ ‘Private secretaries don't have much time for that,’ said Colonel Melrose, smiling. ‘Raymond plays golf, I think. And tennis in the summer.’ ‘He doesn't go to the races, does he?’ ‘Race meetings? No, I don't think he's interested in racing.’ Poirot nodded and seemed to lose interest. He glanced slowly around the study.

‘I think I've seen everything here.’ I, too, looked around.

‘If those walls could talk,’ I murmured.

Poirot shook his head.

‘A tongue isn't enough,’ he said. ‘They'd also need eyes and ears. But don't be too sure that these dead things’ - he touched the top of the bookcase as he spoke - ‘are always silent. They sometimes speak to me - chairs, tables - they have their message!’ He turned away toward the door.

‘What message?’ I cried. ‘What have they told you today?’ He looked over his shoulder and raised one eyebrow questioningly.

‘An open window,’ he said. ‘A locked door. A chair that seems to have moved on its own. I ask “Why?” about all three, and I find no answer.’ He shook his head, puffed out his chest, and blinked at us. He seemed absurdly self-important.

I wondered if he was really a good detective. Had his great reputation come from a string of lucky breaks? I think Colonel Melrose must have had the same thought, because he frowned.

‘Anything else you want to see, M. Poirot?’ he asked curtly.

‘Would you please show me the silver table where the weapon was taken from? After that, I won't bother you anymore.’ We went to the drawing-room, but on the way the constable stopped the colonel, and after a quiet conversation the colonel excused himself and left us alone. I showed Poirot the silver table, and after lifting the lid a few times and letting it drop, he pushed open the window and stepped out onto the terrace. I followed him.

Inspector Raglan had just come around the corner of the house and was walking towards us. His face looked stern and pleased.

‘So there you are, M. Poirot,’ he said. ‘Well, this won’t be a difficult case. I’m sorry, though. A nice young man who went astray.’ Poirot’s face fell, and he spoke very gently.

‘I’m afraid I won’t be of much help to you, then?’ ‘Maybe next time,’ said the inspector kindly.

‘We don’t have murders every day in this quiet little part of the world.’ Poirot looked at him with admiration.

‘You acted with amazing speed,’ he said.

‘How exactly did you proceed, if I may ask?’ ‘Certainly,’ said the inspector. ‘First of all - method.

That’s what I always say - method!’ ‘Ah!’ cried the other. ‘That’s my motto, too.

Method, order, and using your brain.’ ‘Your brain?’ said the inspector, staring.

‘The brain,’ explained the Belgian.

‘Oh, of course; well, we all use it, I suppose.’ ‘Some more than others,’ murmured Poirot. ‘And there are differences in quality. Also, there’s the psychology of a crime. One must study that.’ ‘Ah!’ said the inspector, ‘you’re into all that psycho-analysis stuff? Now, I’m a simple man -’ ‘Mrs Raglan would disagree, I’m sure,’ said Poirot, with a little bow.

Inspector Raglan, a little surprised, bowed.

‘You don’t understand,’ he said, grinning. ‘Language makes such a difference. I’m telling you how I work. First, method. Mr Ackroyd was last seen alive at a quarter to ten by his niece, Miss Flora Ackroyd.

That’s fact number one, right?’ ‘If you say so.’ ‘Well, it is. At half-past ten, the doctor here says Mr Ackroyd had been dead at least half an hour. You stand by that, doctor?’ ‘Certainly,’ I said. ‘Half an hour or more.’ ‘Very good. That gives us exactly fifteen minutes in which the crime must have been committed. I make a list of everyone in the house and note where they were and what they were doing between 9.45 and 10 p.m.’ He handed a sheet of paper to Poirot. I read it over his shoulder. It was written neatly: Major Blunt. - In billiard room with Mr Raymond. (Mr Raymond confirms.) Mr Raymond. - Billiard room. (See above.) Mrs Ackroyd. - 9.45 watching billiard match. Went to bed at 9.55. (Raymond and Blunt watched her go upstairs.) Miss Ackroyd. - Went straight from her uncle’s room upstairs.

(Confirmed by Parker, also housemaid, Elsie Dale.) Servants: Parker. - Went straight to butler’s pantry. (Confirmed by housekeeper, Miss Russell, who came to speak to him about something at 9.47 and stayed at least ten minutes.) Miss Russell. - As above. Spoke to housemaid, Elsie Dale, upstairs at 9.45.

Ursula Bourne (parlourmaid). - In her own room until 9.55.

Then in Servants’ Hall.

Mrs Cooper (cook). - In Servants’ Hall.

Gladys Jones (second housemaid). - In Servants’ Hall.

Elsie Dale. - Upstairs in bedroom. Seen there by Miss Russell and Miss Flora Ackroyd.

Mary Thripp (kitchenmaid). - Servants’ Hall.

‘The cook has been here seven years, the parlourmaid eighteen months, and Parker just over a year. The others are new. Except for something odd about Parker, they all seem quite all right.’ ‘A very thorough list,’ said Poirot, handing it back to him. ‘I am quite sure that Parker did not do the murder,’ he added seriously.

‘So is my sister,’ I chimed in. ‘And she’s usually right.’ Nobody paid any attention to my interruption.

‘That pretty much rules out the household,’ continued the inspector. ‘Now we come to a very important point.

The woman at the lodge - Mary Black - was pulling the curtains last night when she saw Ralph Paton turn in at the gate and go up towards the house.’ ‘She is sure of that?’ I asked quickly.

‘Quite sure. She knows him well by sight. He went past very quickly and turned off by the path to the right, which is a shortcut to the terrace.’ ‘And what time was that?’ asked Poirot, who had sat without moving a muscle.

‘Exactly twenty-five minutes past nine,’ said the inspector seriously.

There was a silence. Then the inspector spoke again.

‘It’s all very clear. It all fits together perfectly. At twenty-five past nine, Captain Paton is seen passing the gate; around nine-thirty, Mr. Geoffrey Raymond hears someone in here asking for money, and Mr. Ackroyd refusing. What happens next? Captain Paton leaves the same way - through the window. He walks along the terrace, angry and confused. He comes to the open drawing-room window. Let's say it's about quarter to ten. Miss Flora Ackroyd is saying goodnight to her uncle. Major Blunt, Mr. Raymond, and Mrs. Ackroyd are in the billiard room. The drawing-room is empty. He sneaks in, takes the dagger from the silver table, and returns to the study window. He slips off his shoes, climbs in, and - well, I don’t need to explain further. Then he slips out again and leaves.

He didn't dare go back to the inn. He goes to the station and calls from there.’ ‘Why?’ said Poirot softly.

I was startled by the interruption. The little man was leaning forward. His eyes shone with a strange green light.

For a moment, Inspector Raglan was surprised by the question.

‘It’s hard to say exactly why he did that,’ he said finally. ‘But murderers do strange things. You’d know that if you were in the police force. Even the smartest ones make silly mistakes sometimes. But come on, and I’ll show you those footprints.’ We followed him around the corner of the terrace to the study window. At Raglan's signal, a police officer produced the shoes that had been obtained from the local inn.

The inspector placed them over the marks.

‘They match,’ he said confidently. ‘That is, they're not the exact same pair that made these prints. He left wearing those. This is a pair just like them, but older – see how the studs are worn down?’ ‘Surely many people wear shoes with rubber studs?’ asked Poirot.

‘That’s true, of course,’ said the inspector. ‘I wouldn’t focus so much on the footprints if it wasn’t for everything else.’ ‘A very foolish young man, Captain Ralph Paton,’ said Poirot thoughtfully. ‘To leave so much evidence of his presence.’ ‘Ah! well,’ said the inspector, ‘it was a dry, clear night, you know. He left no prints on the terrace or on the gravel path. But, unfortunately for him, a spring must have recently appeared at the end of the path from the drive. Look here.’ A small gravel path joined the terrace a few feet away.

In one spot, a few yards from its end, the ground was wet and muddy. Crossing this wet area were more footprints, including those of shoes with rubber studs.

Poirot followed the path a little further, the inspector beside him.

‘You saw the women’s footprints?’ he said suddenly.

The inspector laughed.

‘Of course. But several different women have walked this way - and men too. It’s a common short cut to the house, you see. It would be impossible to tell all the footsteps apart.

After all, the ones on the window-sill are what really matter.’ Poirot nodded.

‘It’s no use going further,’ said the inspector, as we came to the drive. ‘It’s all gravel here again, and very hard.’ Again Poirot nodded, but his eyes were on a small garden house - a kind of fancy summer-house. It was a little to the left of the path ahead, and a gravel path led up to it.

Poirot waited until the inspector had gone back towards the house. Then he looked at me.

‘You must have truly been sent to me to replace my friend Hastings,’ he said, with a twinkle. ‘I see that you are staying right by my side. What do you say, Doctor Sheppard, shall we look into that summer-house? It interests me.’ He went to the door and opened it. Inside, it was almost dark. There were one or two simple seats, a croquet set, and some folded deck-chairs.

I was surprised to see what my new friend was doing. He had dropped to his hands and knees and was crawling on the floor.

Every so often he shook his head as if not pleased. Finally, he sat back on his heels.

‘Nothing,’ he murmured. ‘Well, maybe it wasn't meant to be. But it would have meant so much ‘ He stopped, becoming tense. Then he reached out to one of the plain chairs and took something off it.

‘What is it?’ I asked. ‘What have you found?’ He smiled, opening his hand to show me what was in it. A piece of stiff white cloth.

I took it, looked at it closely, and gave it back.

‘What do you think of it, eh?’ he asked, watching me closely.

‘A piece torn from a handkerchief,’ I guessed, shrugging.

He moved quickly again and picked up a small quill - a goose quill, it seemed.

‘And that?’ he said, excited. ‘What do you think of that?’ I just stared.

He put the quill in his pocket, and looked again at the bit of white cloth.

‘A piece of a handkerchief?’ he wondered. ‘Maybe you're right. But remember - a good laundry doesn't starch handkerchiefs.’ He nodded at me, pleased, then he put the scrap carefully in his wallet.
