The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 10

The Parlourmaid

We found Mrs Ackroyd in the hall. With her was a small, dried-up little man, with a strong chin and sharp grey eyes, who looked every bit the lawyer.

‘Mr Hammond is staying to lunch with us,’ said Mrs Ackroyd. ‘You know Major Blunt, Mr Hammond? And dear Doctor Sheppard - also a close friend of poor Roger’s.

‘This is M. Poirot, Mother,’ said Flora. ‘I told you about him this morning.’ ‘Oh! yes,’ said Mrs Ackroyd vaguely. ‘Of course, my dear, of course. He is to find Ralph, isn't he?’ ‘He is to find out who killed Uncle,’ said Flora.

‘Oh! my dear,’ cried her mother. ‘Please! My poor nerves. I am a wreck this morning, a positive wreck. Such a dreadful thing to happen. I saw Poirot move closer to the lawyer, and speak to him quietly. They moved aside into the window space. I joined them - then hesitated.

‘Perhaps I’m intruding,’ I said.

‘Not at all,’ cried Poirot warmly. ‘You and I, Doctor, we investigate this affair together. Without you I would be lost. I want a little information from Mr Hammond.’ ‘You are acting for Captain Ralph Paton, I understand,’ said the lawyer carefully.

Poirot shook his head.

‘Not so. I am acting to ensure justice is done. Miss Ackroyd has asked me to investigate her uncle’s death.

Mr. Hammond seemed slightly surprised.

‘I can’t believe Captain Paton is involved in this crime,’ he said, ‘no matter how much the evidence points to him. The simple fact that he needed money ‘ ‘Did he need money?’ Poirot asked quickly.

The lawyer shrugged.

‘Ralph Paton always needed money,’ he said dryly. ‘He spent it as fast as he got it. He was always asking his stepfather for help.’ ‘Had he done so recently? In the past year, for example?’

‘I don’t know. Mr. Ackroyd didn’t mention it to me.’ ‘I understand. Mr. Hammond, I assume you know what Mr. Ackroyd’s will says?’ ‘Certainly. Mr. Hammond seemed a little surprised.

‘A thousand pounds to his housekeeper, Miss Russell; fifty pounds to the cook, Emma Cooper; five hundred pounds to his secretary, Mr. Geoffrey Raymond. Then to various hospitals -’ Poirot stopped him.

‘Mr Hammond,’ Mrs Ackroyd said sadly from the fireplace.

The lawyer went to her. Poirot took my arm and pulled me to the window.

‘Look at the irises,’ he said rather loudly.

‘Beautiful, aren’t they? So straight and neat.’ At the same time I felt his hand squeeze my arm, and he added quietly: ‘Do you really want to help me? So, you will ask the questions.’ ‘What questions do you want me to ask?’ I asked nervously.

‘I want you to mention Mrs Ferrars.’ ‘Yes?’ ‘Talk about her normally. Ask him if he was here when her husband died. You understand what I mean. And while he answers, watch his face without looking like you’re watching. Understood?’ There was no time to say more, because just as Poirot had said, Blunt quickly left the others and came over to us.

I suggested walking on the terrace, and he agreed.

Poirot stayed behind.

I stopped to look at a late rose.

‘Things change so quickly,’ I said. ‘I was here last Wednesday, walking on this terrace. Ackroyd was with me - very cheerful. And now - three days later - Ackroyd’s dead, poor man. Mrs Ferrars is dead too - you knew her, didn’t you? Of course you did.’ Blunt nodded.

‘Had you seen her since you came down this time?’ ‘Went with Ackroyd to call. Last Tuesday, I think it was.

Fascinating woman - but something odd about her. Deep - you'd never know what she was planning.’ I looked into his steady grey eyes. Surely nothing was hidden there. I went on: ‘I suppose you’d met her before?’ ‘Last time I was here - she and her husband had just moved here.’ He paused a minute and then added: ‘Strange thing, she had changed a lot between then and now.’ ‘How - changed?’ I asked.

‘Looked ten years older.’ ‘Were you here when her husband died?’ I asked, trying to make the question sound as if it didn't matter.

‘No. From what I heard, it was a good thing he was gone. Maybe uncharitable, but it’s the truth.’ I agreed.

‘Ashley Ferrars was not exactly a perfect husband,’ I said carefully.

‘A scoundrel, I thought,’ said Blunt.

‘No,’ I said, ‘just a man with too much money.’ ‘Oh! money! All the problems in the world come down to money - or not having it.’ ‘Which has been your problem?’ I asked.

‘Enough for what I need. I’m one of the lucky ones.’ ‘Indeed.’ ‘I'm a bit short right now, actually. I inherited some money a year ago, and like an idiot, I let myself be talked into putting it into some crazy scheme.’ I sympathized, and told him about my own similar problem.

Then the gong rang out, and we all went in to lunch.

Poirot pulled me back a little.

Why shouldn’t he? I’d swear the man is completely honest and trustworthy.’ ‘Without a doubt, without a doubt,’ said Poirot soothingly.

‘Do not upset yourself.’ He spoke as though to a naughty child.

We all trooped into the dining-room. It seemed unreal that less than twenty-four hours had passed since I last sat at that table.

Afterwards, Mrs Ackroyd took me aside and sat down with me on a sofa.

‘I can’t help feeling a little hurt,’ she murmured, taking out a handkerchief of the kind obviously not meant for crying. ‘Hurt, I mean, by Roger’s lack of trust in me. I call it a lack of trust.’ ‘You forget, Mrs Ackroyd,’ I said, ‘Flora was Ackroyd’s own niece, a blood relation. It has been a very difficult situation for both Flora and myself. He didn’t even give the poor child an allowance. - to that woman.’ ‘What woman?’ ‘That Russell woman. Something very odd about her, and so I’ve always said. But Roger wouldn’t hear a word against her. I saw through her.’ I began to wonder if there was any chance of stopping Mrs Ackroyd’s flow of words, and getting away.

Mr Hammond provided the necessary distraction by coming up to say goodbye. I seized my chance and rose also.

‘About the inquest,’ I said. ‘Where would you prefer it to be held? Here, or at the Three Boars?’ Mrs Ackroyd stared at me, her mouth open.

‘The inquest?’ she asked, looking shocked.

‘But surely there won’t have to be an inquest?’ Mr Hammond coughed softly and said, ‘Unavoidable. Given the situation,’ in two short bursts.

‘But surely Dr Sheppard can arrange ‘ ‘My ability to arrange things has limits,’ I said dryly.

‘If his death was an accident ‘ ‘He was murdered, Mrs Ackroyd,’ I stated bluntly.

She cried out softly.

‘No accident theory holds up at all.’ Mrs Ackroyd looked upset. I was impatient with her seeming fear of unpleasantness.

‘If there’s an inquest, I - I won’t have to answer questions and things like that, will I?’ she asked.

‘I don’t know what will be necessary,’ I replied. ‘I expect Mr Raymond will handle most of it for you. He knows all the details and can formally identify the body.’ The lawyer agreed with a small bow.

‘I really don’t think you have anything to worry about, Mrs Ackroyd,’ he said. ‘You’ll be spared any unpleasantness.

Now, about money, do you have enough for now? I mean,’ he added, as she looked at him questioningly, ‘spending money. Cash, you know.

If not, I can arrange to get you whatever you need.’ ‘That should be fine,’ said Raymond, who was nearby. ‘Mr Ackroyd cashed a cheque for a hundred pounds yesterday.’ ‘A hundred pounds?’ ‘Yes. For wages and other expenses due today. It’s all still there.’ ‘Where is the money? In his desk?’ ‘No, he always kept his cash in his bedroom. In an old collar box, actually. Odd, wasn’t it?’ ‘I think,’ said the lawyer, ‘we should check the money is there before I leave.’ ‘Of course,’ agreed the secretary. ‘I’ll take you up now... Oh! I forgot. The door’s locked.’ Asking Parker, we learned that Inspector Raglan was in the housekeeper’s room asking a few more questions. A few minutes later the inspector joined us in the hall, with the key. He unlocked the door, and we went into the hallway and up the small stairs. At the top of the stairs, Ackroyd’s bedroom door was open. Inside, it was dark, the curtains were closed, and the bed was turned down as it had been last night. The inspector opened the curtains, letting in sunlight, and Geoffrey Raymond went to the top drawer of a rosewood desk.

‘He kept his money like that, in an unlocked drawer. Imagine,’ said the inspector.

The secretary blushed slightly.

‘Mr. Ackroyd completely trusted all the servants,’ he said, sounding annoyed.

‘Oh! Of course,’ said the inspector quickly.

Raymond opened the drawer, took out a round leather collar-box from the back, and opened it to reveal a thick wallet.

‘Here’s the money,’ he said, pulling out a large wad of notes.

‘You’ll find all hundred pounds there, I’m sure. Mr. Ackroyd put it in the collar-box in front of me last night when he was getting ready for dinner, and it hasn’t been touched since.’ Mr. Hammond took the wad from him and counted it. He looked up, surprised.

‘A hundred pounds, you said? But there’s only sixty here.’ Raymond stared at him.

‘Impossible,’ he exclaimed, jumping forward. Taking the notes, he counted them aloud.

Mr. Hammond was right. There was a total of sixty pounds.

‘But - I can’t understand it,’ cried the secretary, confused.

Poirot asked a question.

‘You saw Mr Ackroyd put this money away last night when he was dressing for dinner? Are you sure he hadn't already used any of it?’ ‘I’m sure he hadn’t. He even said, “I don’t want to take a hundred pounds down to dinner with me. He turned to Mrs Ackroyd. ‘Which of the servants would come in here yesterday evening?’ ‘I suppose the housemaid would turn down the bed.’ ‘Who is she? What do you know about her?’ ‘She’s not been here very long,’ said Mrs Ackroyd. ‘But she’s a nice, ordinary country girl.’ ‘I think we should sort this out,’ said the inspector.

‘If Mr Ackroyd spent that money himself, it might relate to the mystery of the crime. Then he nodded and said, ‘I think I’d better talk to Miss Russell, and I’ll see the girl Dale as well.’ Poirot and I went with him to the housekeeper’s room.

Miss Russell greeted us with her usual composure.

Elsie Dale had been at Fernly five months. A nice girl, quick at her work, and very respectable. Good references.

The last girl in the world to steal anything.

What about the parlourmaid?

‘She, too, was a very good girl. Very quiet and refined. An excellent worker.’ ‘Then why is she leaving?’ asked the inspector.

Miss Russell pursed her lips.
