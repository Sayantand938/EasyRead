The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 6

The Tunisian Dagger

I met the inspector as he came from the kitchen door.

‘How’s the young lady, doctor?’ ‘Getting better. Her mother’s with her.’ ‘That’s good. I’ve asked the servants some questions. They all say no one came to the back door tonight. Your description of that stranger was pretty vague. Can’t you give us something more to go on?’ ‘I’m afraid not,’ I said, feeling bad. ‘It was a dark night, and the man had his coat collar pulled up and his hat pulled down over his eyes.’ ‘H’m,’ said the inspector. ‘Looked like he wanted to hide his face. Sure it was no one you know?’ I said no, but not as firmly as I could have. I remembered thinking the stranger’s voice sounded familiar. I explained this to the inspector, but I hesitated.

‘It was a rough, uneducated voice, you say?’ I agreed, but I thought the roughness seemed a bit forced. If the man wanted to hide his face, he might also have tried to hide his voice.

‘Do you mind coming to the study with me again, doctor? I want to ask you a few more things.’ I agreed. Inspector Davis unlocked the lobby door, we went through, and he locked the door again.

‘We don’t want to be disturbed,’ he said sternly. ‘And no one listening in. What’s this about blackmail?’ ‘Blackmail!’ I said, surprised.

‘Is Parker making it up? Or is there something to it?’ ‘If Parker heard anything about blackmail,’ I said slowly, ‘he must have been listening at the door with his ear to the keyhole.’ Davis nodded.

‘More than likely. I’ve been asking around about what Parker did tonight. To be honest, I didn’t like his attitude. The man knows something. When I started asking questions, he got nervous and blurted out some story about blackmail.’ I made a quick decision.

‘I’m glad you brought it up,’ I said. ‘I was trying to decide whether to be honest about everything or not. I’d almost decided to tell you everything anyway, but I was going to wait for the right time. I might as well tell you now.’ And then and there, I told him everything that happened that evening, just as I’ve written it here. The inspector listened closely, asking questions now and then.

‘Most amazing story I ever heard,’ he said, when I had finished. ‘And you say that letter has completely disappeared?

It looks bad - it looks very bad indeed. It gives us what we’ve been looking for - a reason for the murder.’ I nodded.

‘I understand that.’ ‘You say that Mr Ackroyd suggested he suspected someone in his house was involved? "House" can mean a lot of people.’ ‘You don’t think that Parker himself might be the man we’re after?’ I suggested.

‘It seems likely. He was clearly listening at the door when you came out. Then Miss Ackroyd found him later trying to get into the study. Suppose he tried again when she was gone. He stabbed Ackroyd, locked the door from the inside, opened the window, and got out that way, and went around to a side door which he had left open before. How’s that?’ ‘I want to see if Mr Raymond can tell us anything about this dagger,’ he explained.

Locking the outside door behind us again, we went to the billiard room, where we found Geoffrey Raymond. The inspector held up the dagger.

‘Ever seen this before, Mr Raymond?’ ‘Yes - I think - I’m almost sure that’s a souvenir given to Mr Ackroyd by Major Blunt. It comes from Morocco - no, Tunis. So that was used for the crime? That’s amazing. It seems almost impossible, but there probably aren’t two daggers exactly alike. May I get Major Blunt?’ Without waiting for an answer, he hurried off.

‘Nice young man,’ said the inspector. ‘He seems honest and sincere.’ I agreed. In the two years that Geoffrey Raymond has been Ackroyd’s secretary, I have never seen him upset or angry. And he has been, I know, a very good secretary.

In a minute or two Raymond returned with Blunt.

‘I was right,’ said Raymond excitedly. ‘It is the Tunisian dagger.’ ‘Major Blunt hasn’t looked at it yet,’ the inspector pointed out.

‘Saw it as soon as I came into the study,’ said the quiet man.

‘You knew what it was, then?’ Blunt nodded.

‘You didn’t mention it,’ the inspector said, sounding suspicious.

‘Not the right time,’ said Blunt. ‘Saying things at the wrong time can cause problems.’ He looked back at the inspector calmly.

The inspector grunted and turned away. He took the dagger to Blunt.

‘You’re certain about this, sir? You’re sure it’s the right one?’

‘Completely. No doubt at all.’

‘Where was this - um - object usually kept? Can you tell me, sir?’ The secretary answered.

‘In the silver table in the drawing-room.’

‘What?’ I said loudly.

The others looked at me.

‘Yes, doctor?’ the inspector asked kindly.

‘It’s nothing,’ I said again, still feeling encouraged.

‘It’s so unimportant,’ I said, sounding sorry. ‘It’s just that when I got here last night for dinner, I heard the silver table being closed in the drawing-room.’ I saw the inspector looked very doubtful and a bit suspicious.

‘How did you know it was the silver table?’ I had to explain everything - a long, boring explanation that I really didn’t want to give.

The inspector listened until I finished.

‘Was the dagger there when you looked at the things on the table?’ he asked.

‘I don’t know,’ I said. ‘I don’t remember seeing it - but it might have been there.’ ‘We should talk to the housekeeper,’ the inspector said, and rang the bell.

A few minutes later, Miss Russell, who Parker had called, came into the room.

‘I don’t think I went near the silver table,’ she said, after the inspector asked his question. ‘I was checking that all the flowers were fresh. Oh! yes, I remember now.

The silver table was open - which it shouldn’t have been, so I closed it as I walked by.’ She looked at him like she was ready to fight.

‘I see,’ said the inspector. ‘Can you tell me if this dagger was there then?’ Miss Russell looked at the dagger calmly.

‘I’m not sure,’ she answered. ‘I didn’t stop to look. I knew the family would be down soon, and I wanted to leave.’ ‘Thank you,’ said the inspector.

There was just a hint of hesitation in his manner, as if he wanted to ask her more, but Miss Russell clearly took his words as a dismissal, and left the room smoothly.

‘A bit sharp, I'd say, eh?’ said the inspector, watching her go. ‘Let me see. This silver table is in front of one of the windows, I think you said, doctor?’ Raymond answered for me.

‘Yes, the left-hand window.’ ‘And the window was open?’ ‘They were both slightly open.’ ‘Well, I don’t think we need to look into it much further. Somebody - I’ll just say somebody - could have taken that dagger any time they wanted, and when they took it doesn’t really matter. I’ll be back in the morning with the chief constable, Mr Raymond. Until then, I’ll keep the key to that door. I want Colonel Melrose to see everything exactly as it is. I happen to know that he’s having dinner on the other side of the county, and, I believe, staying the night...’ We watched the inspector pick up the jar.

‘I shall have to pack this carefully,’ he said. ‘It’s going to be important evidence in more ways than one.’ A few minutes later, as I came out of the billiard room with Raymond, he chuckled quietly.

I felt his hand on my arm, and looked where he was looking. Inspector Davis seemed to be asking Parker’s opinion of a small pocket diary.

‘A little obvious,’ murmured my companion. ‘So Parker is the suspect, is he? Shall we give Inspector Davis our fingerprints too?’ He took two cards from the card tray, wiped them with his silk handkerchief, then gave one to me and kept the other. Then, with a grin, he handed them to the police inspector.

‘Souvenirs,’ he said. ‘No. 1. Dr Sheppard, No. 2, me. Major Blunt will give you one in the morning.’ Youth is very lively. Even the brutal murder of his friend and employer couldn’t dampen Geoffrey Raymond’s spirits for long. Maybe that's how it should be. I don't know. I lost the ability to bounce back a long time ago.

It was very late when I got back, and I hoped Caroline would be in bed. I should have known better.

She had hot cocoa waiting for me, and while I drank it, she got the whole story of the evening from me. I didn't say anything about the blackmail, but just told her the facts of the murder.

‘The police suspect Parker,’ I said, as I got up and got ready to go to bed. ‘There seems to be a pretty strong case against him.’ ‘Parker!’ said my sister. ‘Nonsense! That inspector must be a complete fool. Parker indeed! Don’t tell me.’ With that strange statement, we went up to bed.
