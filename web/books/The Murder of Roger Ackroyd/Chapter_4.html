The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 4

Dinner at Fernly

It was just before seven-thirty when I rang the doorbell at Fernly Park. Parker, the butler, opened the door quickly.

It was such a nice night that I had decided to walk. I entered the large square hall, and Parker took my overcoat. Just then, Ackroyd's secretary, a friendly young man named Raymond, walked through the hall toward Ackroyd's study, carrying a stack of papers.

'Good evening, doctor. Here for dinner? Or is this a work visit?' He mentioned my black bag, which I had placed on the oak chest.

I explained that I was expecting a call for a childbirth case at any moment, so I was prepared for an emergency. Raymond nodded and continued on, calling back, 'Go to the drawing-room. You know the way. The ladies will be down shortly. I need to give these papers to Mr. Ackroyd, and I'll let him know you're here.' Parker had left when Raymond appeared, so I was alone in the hall. I straightened my tie, looked in a large mirror, and walked to the door directly in front of me, which I knew led to the drawing-room.

As I turned the handle, I heard a sound from inside—a window closing, I thought. I noticed it almost without thinking, not considering it important at the time.

I opened the door and entered. I nearly bumped into Miss Russell, who was exiting. We both apologized.

For the first time, I really looked at the housekeeper and thought about how beautiful she must have been—and in fact, still was. Her dark hair had no grey in it, and when she had some colour in her face, like now, she didn't look so severe.

Without really thinking about it, I wondered if she had been outside, as she was breathing heavily, as if she had been running.

‘I’m afraid I’m a few minutes early,’ I said.

‘Oh! I don’t think so. It’s after half-past seven, Dr Sheppard.’ She paused a moment before saying, ‘I - didn’t know you were expected to dinner tonight. Mr Ackroyd didn’t mention it.’ I got the feeling that my being there for dinner bothered her, but I couldn’t think why.

‘How’s the knee?’ I asked.

‘About the same, thank you, doctor. I must go now. Mrs Ackroyd will be down in a moment. I - I only came in here to see if the flowers were all right.’ She hurried out of the room. I walked to the window, wondering why she felt the need to explain why she was in the room. As I did, I saw what I should have realized all along if I had thought about it: the windows were long French doors that opened onto the terrace. So, the sound I heard couldn't have been a window closing.

Just to pass the time and take my mind off upsetting thoughts, I tried to guess what could have made that sound.

Coals falling in the fire? No, it didn't sound like that at all.

A bureau drawer being pushed in? No, not that.

Then I noticed what I think is called a silver table, with a lid that lifts up, letting you see what's inside through the glass. I went over to it, looking at what was inside. There were a couple of old silver items, a baby shoe that belonged to King Charles the First, some Chinese jade figures, and a lot of African tools and curiosities. Wanting to get a better look at one of the jade figures, I lifted the lid. It slipped and fell.

I knew right away that was the sound I had heard. It was the sound of this same table lid being closed gently and carefully. I did it again a couple of times to be sure.

Then I lifted the lid to look at what was inside more closely.

I was still bending over the open silver table when Flora Ackroyd came into the room.

Quite a lot of people do not like Flora Ackroyd, but everyone admires her. And she can be very charming to her friends. The first thing you notice about her is how incredibly fair she is. She has truly Scandinavian pale gold hair. Her eyes are blue - as blue as a Norwegian fjord, and her skin is like cream and roses. She has square, boyish shoulders and narrow hips. And it is very refreshing for a weary doctor to see such perfect health.

A simple, honest English girl - I may be old-fashioned, but I think the real thing is hard to beat.

Flora came to me by the silver table, and doubted that King Charles I had ever worn the baby shoe.

‘And anyway,’ continued Miss Flora, ‘all this fuss about things just because someone wore or used them seems silly to me. They aren’t wearing or using them now.

That pen that George Eliot wrote The Mill on the Floss with - well, it’s just a pen. If you really like George Eliot, why not get a cheap copy of The Mill on the Floss and read it?’ ‘I suppose you never read such old-fashioned stuff, Miss Flora?’ ‘You’re wrong, Dr Sheppard. I love The Mill on the Floss.’ I was rather pleased to hear it. The things young women read nowadays and say they enjoy really frighten me.

‘You haven’t congratulated me yet, Dr Sheppard,’ said Flora. ‘Haven’t you heard?’ She held out her left hand. On her ring finger was a beautifully set single pearl.

‘I’m going to marry Ralph, you know,’ she went on.

‘Uncle is very pleased. It keeps me in the family, you see.’ I took both her hands in mine.

My dear,’ I said, ‘I hope you’ll be very happy.’ ‘We’ve been engaged for about a month,’ continued Flora in her cool voice, ‘but it was only announced yesterday.

Uncle is going to fix up Cross-stones, and give it to us to live in, and we’re going to pretend to farm. Really, we shall hunt all winter, go to town for the season, and then go yachting. I love the sea. And, of course, I shall take a great interest in the parish affairs, and attend all the Mothers’ Meetings.’ Just then Mrs Ackroyd rustled in, full of apologies for being late.

I am sorry to say I detest Mrs Ackroyd. She is all chains and teeth and bones. A most unpleasant woman. She has small pale flinty blue eyes, and however enthusiastic her words may be, those eyes always remain coldly speculative.

I went across to her, leaving Flora by the window. She gave me a handful of assorted knuckles and rings to squeeze, and began talking quickly.

Had I heard about Flora’s engagement? So suitable in every way. The dear young things had fallen in love at first sight. Such a perfect pair, he so dark and she so fair.

‘I can’t tell you, my dear Dr Sheppard, the relief to a mother’s heart.’ Mrs Ackroyd sighed - a tribute to her mother’s heart, while her eyes remained shrewdly observant of me.

‘I was wondering. You are such an old friend of dear Roger’s. We know how much he trusts your judgment.

So difficult for me - in my position as poor Cecil’s widow.

But there are so many tiresome things - settlements, you know - all that. I fully believe that Roger intends to make settlements upon dear Flora, but, as you know, he is just a little peculiar about money. Very usual, I’ve heard, among men who are captains of industry. I wondered, you know, if you could just ask him about it?

Flora is so fond of you. We feel you are quite an old friend, although we have only really known you just over two years.’ Mrs Ackroyd’s eloquence was cut short as the drawing-room door opened once more. I was pleased at the interruption. I hate interfering in other people’s affairs, and I had not the least intention of tackling Ackroyd on the subject of Flora’s settlements. In another moment I should have been forced to tell Mrs Ackroyd as much.

‘You know Major Blunt, don’t you, doctor?’ ‘Yes, indeed,’ I said.

A lot of people know Hector Blunt - at least what people say about him.

He has shot more wild animals in far-off places than anyone I know. When you mention him, people say: ‘Blunt - you mean the big game hunter, right?’ His friendship with Ackroyd has always puzzled me. The two men are so different. Hector Blunt is maybe five years younger than Ackroyd. They became friends early on, and even though they've gone different ways, they're still friends. About every two years, Blunt spends two weeks at Fernly, and a huge animal head with impressive horns that seems to stare at you as soon as you walk in the door, is a constant reminder of their friendship.

Blunt had come into the room with his own peculiar, careful, yet quiet steps. He is a man of average height, strongly and solidly built. His face is almost the colour of mahogany, and doesn't show much expression. He has grey eyes that seem to be watching something happening very far away. He doesn't talk much, and when he does, it's in short bursts, as if he's forcing the words out.

He said now: ‘How are you, Sheppard?’ in his usual abrupt way, and then stood straight in front of the fireplace looking over our heads as if he saw something interesting happening in a faraway place.

‘Major Blunt,’ said Flora, ‘I wish you’d tell me about these African things. I’m sure you know all about them.’ I’ve heard Hector Blunt called a woman hater, but I noticed he went to join Flora at the silver table quickly. They leaned over it together.

I was afraid Mrs Ackroyd would start talking about property settlements again, so I quickly said a few things about the new sweet pea. I knew about the new sweet pea because the Daily Mail had mentioned it that morning. Mrs Ackroyd doesn't know anything about gardening, but she's the kind of woman who likes to seem informed about current topics, and she also reads the Daily Mail. We were able to talk quite well until Ackroyd and his secretary joined us, and right after that, Parker announced dinner.

My place at the table was between Mrs Ackroyd and Flora.

Blunt was on Mrs Ackroyd’s other side, and Geoffrey Raymond next to him.

Dinner wasn't a happy occasion. Ackroyd was clearly worried. He looked terrible, and ate almost nothing.

Mrs. Ackroyd, Raymond, and I kept talking.

Flora seemed affected by her uncle's sadness, and Blunt became his usual quiet self.

Right after dinner, Ackroyd took my arm and led me to his study.

'Once we've had coffee, we won't be bothered again,' he said. 'I told Raymond to make sure we weren't interrupted.' I watched him carefully, without making it obvious. He was clearly very excited about something. He walked back and forth for a minute or two, then sat down in an armchair by the fire as Parker came in with the coffee.

The study was a cozy room. Bookshelves covered one wall. The chairs were large and covered in dark blue leather. A big desk stood by the window, covered with papers neatly organized. On a round table were magazines and sports papers.

'I've been having that pain after eating again lately,' Ackroyd said calmly, as he poured himself coffee. 'You need to give me more of those pills you have.' It seemed like he wanted me to think we were just having a medical discussion. I acted along.

'I thought so. I brought some with me.' 'Good. Give them to me now.' 'They're in my bag in the hall. I'll go get them.' Ackroyd stopped me.

'Don't bother. Parker will get them. Bring in the doctor's bag, Parker?' 'Yes, sir.' Parker left. As I was about to speak, Ackroyd raised his hand.

'Not yet. Wait. Can't you see I'm so nervous I can barely hold myself together?' I could see that clearly. And I was very worried. I had all sorts of bad feelings.

Ackroyd spoke again almost right away.

‘Make sure that window's closed, will you,’ he asked.

Slightly surprised, I got up and went to it. It wasn't a french window, but a regular sash window. The thick blue velvet curtains were drawn in front of it, but the window was open at the top.

Parker came back into the room with my bag while I was still at the window.

‘That’s all right,’ I said, coming back into the room.

‘You’ve put the latch across?’ ‘Yes, yes. What’s the matter with you, Ackroyd?’ The door had just closed behind Parker, or I wouldn't have asked.

Ackroyd waited a moment before answering.

‘I’m in hell,’ he said slowly, after a moment. ‘No, don’t worry about those damn tablets. I only said that for Parker. Servants are so curious. Come here and sit down. The door’s closed too, isn’t it?’ ‘Yes. Nobody can overhear; don’t worry.’ ‘Sheppard, nobody knows what I’ve gone through in the last twenty-four hours. If a house ever fell in ruins about him, mine has about me. This business of Ralph’s is the last straw. But we won’t talk about that now. It’s the other - the other - I don’t know what to do about it. And I’ve got to decide soon.’ ‘What’s the trouble?’ Ackroyd was silent for a minute or two. He seemed strangely reluctant to begin. When he did, the question he asked was a complete surprise. It was the last thing I expected.

‘Sheppard, you took care of Ashley Ferrars in his last illness, didn’t you?’ ‘Yes, I did.’ He seemed to find it even harder to form his next question.

‘Did you ever suspect - did it ever cross your mind - that he might have be-’

‘I’ll tell you the truth,’ I said. ‘At the time I had no suspicion at all, but since - well, it was just idle talk from my sister that first put the idea into my head. Since then I haven’t been able to get rid of it again. But, mind you, I’ve no reason whatsoever for that suspicion.’ ‘He was poisoned,’ said Ackroyd.

He spoke in a dull, heavy voice - “Who by?” I asked sharply. “His wife.” “How do you know that?” “She told me so herself.” “When?” “Yesterday! My God! Yesterday! It seems ten years ago.” I waited a minute, then Ackroyd went on.

“You understand, Sheppard, I’m telling you this in confidence.

It’s to go no further. I want your advice. I can’t carry the whole weight myself. As I said just now, I don’t know what to do.” “Can you tell me the whole story?” I said. “I’m still in the dark. How did Mrs. Ferrars come to make this confession to you?” “It’s like this. Three months ago I asked Mrs. Ferrars to marry me. She refused. I asked her again and she agreed, but she refused to allow me to make the engagement public until her year of mourning was up. Yesterday I called upon her, pointed out that a year and three weeks had now passed since her husband’s death, and that there could be no further objection to making the engagement public.”

“I had noticed that she had been very strange in her manner for some days. Now, suddenly, without the least warning, she broke down completely. She - she told me everything. Her hatred of her brute of a husband, her growing love for me, and the - the dreadful means she had taken. Poison! My God! It was murder in cold blood.” I saw the repulsion, the horror, in Ackroyd’s face. So Mrs. Ferrars must have seen it. Ackroyd’s is not the type of the great lover who can forgive all for love’s sake. He is fundamentally a good citizen. All that was sound and wholesome and law-abiding in him must have turned from her utterly in that moment of revelation.

“Yes,” he went on, in a low, monotonous voice, “she confessed everything. It seems that there is one person who has known all along - who has been blackmailing her for huge sums. It was the strain of that that drove her nearly mad.” “Who was the man?” Suddenly before my eyes there arose the picture of Ralph Paton and Mrs. Ferrars side by side. Their heads so close together. I felt a momentary throb of anxiety. Supposing oh! but surely that was impossible. I remembered the frankness of Ralph’s greeting that very afternoon. Absurd!

“She wouldn’t tell me his name,” said Ackroyd slowly. “As a matter of fact, she didn’t actually say that it was a man.

But of course -” “Of course,” I agreed. “It must have been a man. And you’ve no suspicion at all?” For answer Ackroyd groaned and dropped his head into his hands.

“It can’t be,” he said. “I’m mad even to think of such a thing. No, I won’t even admit to you the wild suspicion that crossed my mind. I’ll tell you this much, though. Something she said made me think that the person in question might be actually among my household - but that can’t be so. I must have misunderstood her.” “What did you say to her?” I asked.

“What could I say? She saw, of course, the awful shock it had been to me. And then there was the question, what was my duty in the matter? She had made me, you see, an accessory after the fact. She saw all that, I think, quicker than I did. I was stunned, you know. She asked me for twenty-four hours - made me promise to do nothing till the end of that time. And she steadfastly refused to give me the name of the scoundrel who had been blackmailing her. I suppose she was afraid that I might go straight off and hammer him, and then the fat would have been in the fire as far as she was concerned. She told me that I should hear from her before twenty-four hours had passed. My God! I swear to you, Sheppard, that it never entered my head what she meant to do. Suicide! And I drove her to it.” “No, no,” I said. “Don’t take an exaggerated view of things. The responsibility for her death doesn’t lie at your door.” “The question is, what am I to do now? The poor lady is dead. Why rake up past trouble?” “I rather agree with you,” I said.

“But there’s another point. How am I to get hold of that scoundrel who drove her to death as surely as if he’d killed her? He knew of the first crime, and he fastened on to it like some obscene vulture. She’s paid the penalty. Is he to go scot free?” “I see,” I said slowly. “You want to hunt him down? It will mean a lot of publicity, you know.” “Yes, I’ve thought of that. I’ve zigzagged to and fro in my mind.” “I agree with you that the villain ought to be punished, but the cost has got to be reckoned.” Ackroyd rose and walked up and down. Presently he sank into the chair again.

‘Listen, Sheppard, let’s say this. If we don’t hear from her, we’ll leave things as they are.’ ‘What do you mean by hearing from her?’ I asked, curious.

‘I feel strongly that she must have left a message for me somewhere, somehow, before she left. I can’t explain it, but that’s how it is.’ I shook my head.

‘She didn’t leave a letter or any message?’ I asked.

‘Sheppard, I’m sure she did. And I also feel that by choosing to die, she wanted the truth to be known, to get back at the man who made her desperate. I think if I could have seen her then, she would have told me his name and asked me to go after him with everything I had.’ He looked at me.

‘You don’t believe in feelings?’ ‘Yes, I do, in a way. If, as you say, we hear from her ‘ I stopped. The door opened quietly and Parker came in with a tray holding some letters.

‘The evening mail, sir,’ he said, giving the tray to Ackroyd.

Then he took the coffee cups and left.

My attention, briefly interrupted, returned to Ackroyd. He was staring, frozen, at a long blue envelope. He had dropped the other letters on the floor.

‘Her writing,’ he whispered. ‘She must have posted it last night, just before - before ‘ He tore open the envelope and took out a thick letter.

Then he looked up suddenly.

‘Are you certain you closed the window?’ he asked.

‘Quite certain,’ I replied, surprised. ‘Why?’ ‘All evening I’ve felt strange, like someone was watching, spying on me. What’s that—’ He turned quickly. So did I. We both felt like we heard the door latch move just a bit. I went to the door and opened it. No one was there.

‘Nerves,’ Ackroyd muttered to himself.

He unfolded the thick pages and read aloud in a low voice.

‘My dear, my very dear Roger,—A life requires a life. I see that—I saw it in your face this afternoon. So, I’m taking the only path left to me. I leave it to you to punish the person who has made my life a living hell for the past year. I wouldn’t tell you the name this afternoon, but I’ll write it to you now. I have no children or close relatives to protect, so don’t worry about the scandal. If you can, Roger, my very dear Roger, forgive me for the wrong I planned to do you, since when the time came, I couldn’t do it after all.’ Ackroyd, his finger on the page to turn it, stopped.

‘Sheppard, please forgive me, but I must read this alone,’ he said, sounding shaky. ‘It was meant only for me.’ He put the letter in the envelope and placed it on the table.

‘Later, when I’m alone.’ ‘No,’ I blurted out, ‘read it now.’ Ackroyd looked at me, surprised.

‘I’m sorry,’ I said, blushing. ‘I don’t mean read it aloud to me. But read it while I’m still here.’ Ackroyd shook his head.

‘No, I’d rather wait.’ But for some reason I didn’t understand, I kept pressing him.

‘At least, read the man’s name,’ I said.

Now Ackroyd is really stubborn. The more you push him to do something, the more he refuses. All my reasons were useless.

The letter arrived at twenty minutes to nine.

It was almost ten minutes to nine when I left him, the letter still unopened. I paused with my hand on the door handle, looking back and wondering if I had forgotten something. I couldn't think of anything. I shook my head, went out, and closed the door.

I was surprised to see Parker standing so close.

He seemed uneasy, and I thought he might have been listening at the door.

What a round, pleased, and greasy face the man had, and his eyes definitely looked dishonest.

'Mr. Ackroyd really doesn't want to be bothered,' I said sharply. 'He told me to tell you that.' 'Yes, sir. I - I thought I heard the bell.' This was such an obvious lie that I didn't bother to answer. Parker walked ahead of me to the hall, helped me with my coat, and I went out into the night. The moon was hidden by clouds, and everything seemed very dark and quiet.

The village church clock struck nine as I went through the gates. I turned left toward the village and almost ran into a man coming the other way.

'Is this the way to Fernly Park, mister?' the stranger asked in a rough voice.

I looked at him. He wore a hat pulled low and his coat collar turned up. I could barely see his face, but he seemed young. His voice was coarse and unrefined.

‘These are the lodge gates here,’ I said.

‘Thank you, mister.’ He paused, and then added, quite unnecessarily, ‘I’m a stranger here, you see.’ He went on, passing through the gates as I turned to watch him.

The strange thing was that his voice reminded me of someone I knew, but I couldn't place who it was.

Ten minutes later I was home again. Caroline was very curious to know why I was back so early. I had to make up a slightly untrue story about the evening to satisfy her, and I had an uneasy feeling she saw through the obvious lie.

At ten o’clock I got up, yawned, and suggested bed, and Caroline agreed.

It was Friday night, and I wind the clocks on Friday night. I did it as usual, while Caroline made sure the servants had locked up the kitchen properly.

It was a quarter past ten as we went upstairs. I had just reached the top when the telephone rang in the hall below.

‘Mrs Bates,’ said Caroline immediately.

‘I expect so,’ I said with a sigh.

I ran down the stairs and picked up the phone.

‘What?’ I said. ‘What? Of course, I’ll come right away.’ I ran upstairs, grabbed my bag, and stuffed a few extra bandages into it.

‘Parker calling,’ I shouted to Caroline, ‘from Fernly. They’ve just found Roger Ackroyd murdered.’
