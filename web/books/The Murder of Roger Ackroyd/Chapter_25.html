The Murder of Roger Ackroyd

Chapter 25

The Whole Truth

A small sign from Poirot told me to stay behind. I did as he asked, walking to the fire and poking the big logs with my boot.

I was confused. For the first time, I had no idea what Poirot meant. For a moment, I thought the scene I'd just seen was just for show - that he had been, as he put it, ‘playing the comedy’ to make himself seem interesting and important. But, I couldn't help but feel there was something real behind it. His words had real menace - a clear honesty. But I still thought he was on the wrong track.

When the door closed behind the last person, he came over to the fire.

‘Well, my friend,’ he said quietly, ‘what do you think of it all?’ ‘I don’t know what to think,’ I said honestly. ‘What was the point? Why not just tell Inspector Raglan the truth instead of giving the guilty person this big warning?’ Poirot sat down and took out his case of small Russian cigarettes. He smoked in silence for a minute or two. Then: ‘Use your little grey cells,’ he said. ‘There is always a reason for what I do.’ I paused for a moment, and then I said slowly: ‘The first idea I have is that you don't know who the guilty person is, but you're sure it's someone here tonight. So your words were meant to force a confession from the unknown murderer?’ Poirot nodded in agreement.

‘A clever idea, but not the truth.’ ‘I thought that by making him think you knew, you might force him to reveal himself - not necessarily by confessing. He might try to silence you as he silenced Mr Ackroyd - before you could act tomorrow morning.’ ‘A trap with me as the bait! Thank you, my friend, but I’m not that brave.’ ‘Then I don’t understand you. Surely you’re risking letting the murderer escape by warning him?’ Poirot shook his head.

‘He cannot escape,’ he said seriously. ‘There is only one way out - and that way doesn’t lead to freedom.’ ‘Do you really believe that one of the people here tonight committed the murder?’ I asked, not believing it.

‘Yes, my friend.’ ‘Which one?’ There was silence for a few minutes. Then Poirot threw his cigarette into the grate and began to speak in a quiet, thoughtful way.

‘I will show you how I came to my conclusion. Step by step, you will follow me, and see for yourself that all the facts clearly point to one person. Now, to begin with, there were two facts and a small difference in time that caught my attention. The first fact was the telephone call. If Ralph Paton was the murderer, the telephone call makes no sense. So, I said to myself, Ralph Paton is not the murderer.’

‘I was sure the call wasn't sent by anyone in the house, but I believed the criminal was someone present on the fatal evening. So, I thought the telephone call must have been sent by an accomplice. I wasn't completely happy with that idea, but I accepted it for the moment.

‘Next, I looked at why the call was made. That was hard. I could only understand it by looking at its result: the murder was discovered that night instead of, most likely, the next morning. Do you agree?’ ‘Ye-es,’ I said. ‘Yes. As you said, Mr Ackroyd, since you had asked not to be disturbed, nobody would have gone to the study that night.’ ‘Very good. We're making progress, aren't we? But things were still unclear. Why was it better to have the crime discovered that night instead of the next morning? The only thing I could think of was that the murderer, knowing when the crime would be found, could make sure to be there when the door was broken down - or soon after.

And now we come to the second thing: the chair pulled away from the wall. Inspector Raglan said it wasn't important. But I've always thought it was extremely important.

‘In your notes, you drew a nice little map of the study. If you had it now, you'd see that - with the chair pulled out as Parker described - it would be right between the door and the window.’ ‘The window!’ I said quickly.

‘You have the same first thought I did. I thought the chair was pulled out so something by the window wouldn't be seen by anyone entering through the door. But I quickly dismissed that idea, because even though the chair was large, it didn't hide much of the window - only the part between the frame and the floor. No, my friend - but remember that just in front of the window was a table with books and magazines on it. That table was completely hidden by the pulled-out chair - and I immediately had my first hint of the truth.

‘What if there was something on that table that wasn't meant to be seen? Something put there by the murderer? I didn't know what it was yet. But I knew some interesting things about it.

For example, it was something the murderer couldn't take with them when they committed the crime. But it was very important to remove it as soon as possible after the crime was discovered. So - the telephone message, and the chance for the murderer to be there when the body was found.

‘Now, four people were there before the police arrived: you, Parker, Major Blunt, and Mr Raymond.

I ruled out Parker right away, because no matter when the crime was discovered, he would be there. Also, he was the one who told me about the pulled-out chair. So, Parker was cleared (of the murder, anyway. I still thought he might have been blackmailing Mrs Ferrars). But Raymond and Blunt were still suspects, because if the crime had been discovered early in the morning, they might have arrived too late to prevent the object on the table from being discovered.

‘Now, what was that object? You heard my arguments tonight about the scrap of overheard conversation?

As soon as I learned that someone from a dictaphone company had called, the idea of a dictaphone stuck in my head. You heard what I said in this room less than half an hour ago? They all agreed with my idea - but one important fact seems to have been missed. Assuming Mr. Ackroyd used a dictaphone that night - why wasn't it found?’ ‘I never thought of that,’ I said.

‘We know Mr. Ackroyd received a dictaphone.

But no dictaphone was found with his things. So, if something was taken from the table - why not the dictaphone? But there were some problems. Everyone was, of course, focused on the murdered man. I think anyone could have gone to the table without being noticed. But a dictaphone is quite bulky - you can't just slip it into a pocket. There had to be something to carry it in.

‘Do you see what I'm getting at? The murderer is becoming clearer. Someone who was there right away, but might not have been if the crime had been found the next morning. Someone carrying something that could hold the dictaphone.’ I interrupted.

‘But why remove the dictaphone? What was the point?’ ‘You're like Mr. Raymond. You assume that what was heard at nine-thirty was Mr. Ackroyd's voice speaking into a dictaphone. But think about this useful invention for a moment. You speak into it, right?

And later, a secretary or typist turns it on, and the voice speaks again.’ ‘You mean - ?’ I gasped.

Poirot nodded.

‘Yes, that's what I mean. At nine-thirty Mr. Ackroyd was already dead. It was the dictaphone speaking - not the man.’ ‘And the murderer turned it on. Then they must have been in the room at that moment?’ ‘Possibly. But we can't rule out the chance of some mechanical device being used - something like a timer, or even a simple alarm clock.

But in that case, we have to add two things to our idea of the murderer. It must be someone who knew Mr. Ackroyd had bought a dictaphone, and someone with some mechanical knowledge.

‘I had gotten this far when we came to the footprints on the window ledge. Here, I saw three possibilities. (1) They might really have been made by Ralph Paton. He was at Fernly that night and could have climbed into the study and found his uncle dead. That was one idea. (2) It's possible the footprints were made by someone else who happened to have the same kind of studs in their shoes. But the people in the house had crepe rubber soles, and I didn't think it was likely someone from outside would have the same shoes as Ralph Paton. Charles Kent, as we know from the barmaid at the Dog and Whistle, had boots that were “falling apart.” (3) Those prints were made by someone deliberately trying to make Ralph Paton look guilty. To check this last idea, I needed to find out some things. The police got one pair of Ralph's shoes from the Three Boars. Ralph or anyone else couldn't have worn them that evening because they were being cleaned downstairs. According to the police, Ralph was wearing another pair of the same kind, and I found out he did have two pairs. Now, for my idea to be right, the murderer had to have worn Ralph's shoes that evening - in which case Ralph must have been wearing a third pair of shoes of some kind. I didn't think he'd bring three pairs of shoes that were all the same - the third pair was more likely to be boots. I got your sister to ask about this - focusing on the color, to hide the real reason I was asking.

‘You know what her work found. Ralph Paton had brought boots with him. The first thing I asked him when he came to my house yesterday morning was what he had on his feet on the night it happened. He said right away that he had worn boots - he was still wearing them, actually - because he didn't have anything else.

‘So, we know more about the killer - someone who could have taken Ralph Paton’s shoes from the Three Boars that day.’ He stopped, and then said, a little louder: ‘There’s one more thing. The killer must have been someone who could have stolen that dagger from the silver table. You could say anyone in the house could have, but remember that Flora Ackroyd was sure the dagger wasn't there when she looked at the silver table.’ He paused again.

‘Let's go over it again - now that everything is clear. Someone who was at the Three Boars earlier that day, someone who knew Ackroyd well enough to know he had bought a dictaphone, someone who liked to fix things, who could have taken the dagger from the silver table before Miss Flora arrived, who had something with them to hide the dictaphone in - like a black bag, and who was alone in the study for a few minutes after the crime was found while Parker was calling the police. Actually - Dr. Sheppard, you.?
