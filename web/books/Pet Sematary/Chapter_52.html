Pet Sematary

Chapter 52

At one o'clock that morning, Jud Crandall's telephone rang, its sharp sound filling the empty house and waking him up. He had been dreaming he was twenty-three again, sitting on a bench in the B & A train yard with George Chapin and René Michaud. They were sharing a bottle of strong, homemade whiskey while a powerful nor'easter wind howled outside. The wind drowned out all other sounds, even the trains of the B & A railroad. So they sat and drank near the potbellied stove, watching the red glow of the coals behind the cloudy glass. The fire cast diamond-shaped shadows on the floor as they told stories they had kept inside for years, like hidden treasures boys keep under their beds. These were stories saved for nights like this. Like the stove's glow, they were dark tales with a red-hot center, wrapped in the howling wind. He was twenty-three, and Norma was alive (though in bed, he was sure; she wouldn't expect him home on such a wild night). René Michaud was telling a story about a Jewish salesman in Bucksport who—

That was when the phone began to ring, and he jerked awake, his neck stiff. A heavy, sour feeling dropped into him like a stone—the weight of sixty years, from twenty-three to eighty-three, all at once. And then he thought: You've been sleeping, boy. You can't run things that way… not tonight.

He got up, straightening his stiff back, and went to the phone.

It was Rachel.

"Jud? Has he come home?"

"No," Jud said. "Rachel, where are you? You sound closer."

"I am closer," Rachel said. Though she did sound nearer, there was a distant hum on the line. It was the sound of the wind, somewhere between them. The wind was strong tonight—a sound that always made Jud think of dead voices, sighing together, maybe singing something too far away to understand. "I'm at the rest area in Biddeford on the Maine Turnpike."

"Biddeford!"

"I couldn't stay in Chicago. It was getting to me, too… whatever got Ellie, it was getting me too. And you feel it. It's in your voice."

"Yep." He took a Chesterfield from his pack and put it in his mouth. He lit a wooden match and watched it flicker as his hand shook. His hands hadn't shaken—not before this nightmare started anyway. Outside, he heard that dark wind blow hard. It grabbed the house and shook it.

The power's growing. I can feel it.

A faint fear in his old bones. It was like glass, thin and easily broken.

"Jud, please tell me what's going on!"

He guessed she had a right to know—a need to know. And he guessed he would tell her. He would eventually tell her the whole story. He would show her how it had all built up, step by step. Norma's heart attack, the cat's death, Louis's question—has anyone ever buried a person up there?—Gage's death… and God only knew what Louis might be doing right now. He would eventually tell her. But not on the phone.

"Rachel, why are you on the highway instead of on a plane?"

She explained how she had missed her connecting flight in Boston. "I rented a car, but I'm not making good time. I got a little lost getting from the airport to the highway, and I've only just gotten into Maine. I don't think I can get there until dawn. But Jud… please. Please tell me what's happening. I'm so scared, and I don't even know why."

"Rachel, listen to me," Jud said, "drive up to Portland and stay there, do you hear me? Check into a motel and get some—"

"Jud, I can't do th—"

"—and get some sleep. Don't worry, Rachel. Something might be happening here tonight, or it might not. If something is—if it's what I think—then you wouldn't want to be here anyway. I can handle it, I think. I better be able to handle it because what's happening is my fault. If nothing's happening, then you get here this afternoon, and that will be fine. I think Louis will be very happy to see you."

“I can’t sleep tonight, Jud.”

“Yes,” he said, thinking he had felt the same—heck, Peter probably felt the same the night Jesus was arrested. Falling asleep on guard. “Yes, you can. Rachel, if you fall asleep driving that rental car and crash and get killed, what will happen to Louis? And Ellie?”

“Tell me what’s happening! If you tell me that, Jud, maybe I’ll listen to you. But I have to know!”

“When you get to Ludlow, come here,” Jud said. “Not to your house. Come here first. I’ll tell you everything I know, Rachel. And I’m watching for Louis.”

“Tell me,” she said.

“No, ma’am. Not on the phone. I won’t. Rachel, I can’t. You go now. Drive to Portland and rest.”

There was a long pause, as she thought it over.

“All right,” she said finally. “Maybe you’re right. Jud, tell me one thing. Tell me how bad it is.”

“I can handle it,” Jud said calmly. “Things are as bad as they can get.”

Outside, a car’s headlights appeared, moving slowly. Jud stood up a little, watching it, and then sat down again when it sped past the Creed house and disappeared.

“All right,” she said. “I guess. This whole drive has felt like a weight on my head.”

“Let the weight go, my dear,” Jud said. “Please. Save yourself for tomorrow. Things here will be all right.”

“You promise you’ll tell me the whole story?”

“Yes. We’ll have a beer, and I’ll tell you everything.”

“Goodbye, then,” Rachel said, “for now.”

“For now,” Jud agreed. “I’ll see you tomorrow, Rachel.”

Before she could say anything else, Jud hung up the telephone.

He thought there were caffeine pills in the medicine cabinet, but he couldn't find them. He put the rest of the beer back in the refrigerator—not without regret—and settled for a cup of black coffee. He took it back to the bow window and sat down again, sipping and watching.

The coffee—and the conversation with Rachel—kept him awake and alert for forty-five minutes, but then he started to nod again.

No sleeping on guard duty, old man. You let it get to you; you bought something, and now you have to pay for it. So no sleeping on guard duty.

He lit a fresh cigarette, took a deep drag, and coughed a rough, old man's cough. He put the cigarette on the ashtray's edge and rubbed his eyes with both hands. Outside, a big truck roared past, its bright lights cutting through the windy, uneasy night.

He caught himself dozing off again, snapped awake, and abruptly slapped himself across the face, hard enough to make his ears ring. Now, terror woke up in his heart, a sneaky visitor who had broken into that secret place.

It’s puttin me to sleep… hypnotizin me… somethin. It doesn’t want me awake. Because he’ll be comin back pretty soon. Yeah, I feel that. And it wants me out of the way.

“No,” he said firmly. “No way at all. You hear me? I’m puttin a stop to this. This has gone far enough.”

The wind whined around the roof, and the trees across the road shook their leaves in hypnotic patterns. His mind went back to that night by the Defiant stove in the coupling shed, which used to be where the Evarts Furniture Mart was in Brewer now. They had talked all night, he and George and René Michaud, and now he was the only one left—René crushed between two boxcars on a stormy night in March of 1939, George Chapin dead of a heart attack just last year. Out of so many, he was the only one left, and old people get foolish. Sometimes the foolishness looks like kindness, and sometimes it looks like pride—a need to tell old secrets, to pass things on, to pour from the old glass to the new one, to…

So this Jewish salesman comes in and he says “I got something you never seen before. These postcards, they just look like women in bathing suits until you rub them with a wet cloth, and then—”

Jud’s head nodded. His chin settled slowly, gently, against his chest.

“—they’re as naked as the day they were born! But when they dry, the clothes, they come back on! And that ain’t all! I got—”

René telling this story in the coupling shed, leaning forward, smiling, and Jud holds the bottle—he feels the bottle and his hand closes around it in the air.

In the ashtray, the cigarette ash on the end of the cigarette grew longer. Finally, it tipped forward into the ashtray and burned out, its shape left in the neat roll of ash like a symbol.

Jud slept.

And when the taillights flashed outside and Louis turned the Honda Civic into his driveway some forty minutes later and drove it into the garage, Jud didn't hear, stir, or wake up, any more than Peter woke up when the Roman soldiers came to take a wanderer named Jesus into their custody.
