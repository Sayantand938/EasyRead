Pet Sematary

Chapter 21

The next day, Louis called the intensive care unit at the EMMC. Norma’s condition was still listed as critical; that was normal for the first twenty-four hours after a heart attack. Louis got a more positive update from Weybridge, her doctor, however. “I wouldn’t even call it a small heart attack,” he said. “No scarring. She owes you a lot, Dr. Creed.”

On impulse, Louis stopped by the hospital later that week with flowers, and found that Norma had been moved to a room with another patient downstairs—a very good sign. Jud was with her.

Norma was thrilled with the flowers and called a nurse for a vase. Then she told Jud how to arrange them until they were in water, arranged just how she wanted, and placed on the dresser in the corner.

“Mother’s feeling much better,” Jud said dryly after he had adjusted the flowers for the third time.

“Don’t be a smart aleck, Judson,” Norma said.

“No, ma’am.”

Finally, Norma looked at Louis. “I want to thank you for what you did,” she said with a shyness that was completely genuine and therefore even more touching. “Jud says I owe you my life.”

Embarrassed, Louis said, “Jud is exaggerating.”

“Not much, he isn’t,” Jud said. He squinted at Louis, almost smiling but not quite. “Didn’t your mother tell you to always accept a thank-you, Louis?”

She hadn’t mentioned it, at least not that Louis recalled, but he thought she had once said that acting humble was almost as bad as being proud.

“Norma,” he said, “I was happy to help with anything I could.”

“You’re a good man,” Norma said. “Take my husband out for a beer. I’m feeling sleepy again, and I can’t seem to get rid of him.”

Jud jumped up eagerly. “Great! I’m up for that, Louis. Let’s go before she changes her mind.”

The first snow fell a week before Thanksgiving. They got another four inches on November twenty-second, but the day before the holiday was clear, blue, and cold. Louis took his family to Bangor International Airport and watched them leave on the first part of their trip back to Chicago to see Rachel’s parents.

“It’s not right,” Rachel said, probably for the twentieth time since they started seriously talking about it a month ago. “I don’t like the thought of you being all alone in the house on Thanksgiving. It’s supposed to be a family holiday, Louis.”

Louis shifted Gage, who looked huge and wide-eyed in his first big-boy coat, to his other arm. Ellie was at one of the big windows, watching an Air Force helicopter take off.

“I’m not going to be sad,” Louis said. “Jud and Norma are having me over for turkey and all the fixings. Honestly, I’m the one who feels bad. I’ve never liked these big holiday gatherings anyway. I start drinking during some football game at three in the afternoon and fall asleep at seven, and the next day my head feels awful. I just don’t like sending you off with the two kids.”

“I’ll be fine,” she said. “Flying first class, I feel like royalty. And Gage will sleep on the flight from Logan to O’Hare.”

“You hope,” he said, and they both laughed.

The flight was called, and Ellie hurried over. “That’s us, Mommy. Come on—come on—come on. They’ll leave without us.”

“No, they won’t,” Rachel said. She held her three pink boarding cards in one hand. She was wearing her fur coat, some fake stuff that was a rich brown… maybe it was trying to look like muskrat, Louis thought. Whatever it was, it made her look beautiful.

Maybe he showed some of what he felt, because she hugged him quickly, almost squashing Gage between them. Gage looked surprised but not too upset.

“Louis Creed, I love you,” she said.

“Mom-eee,” Ellie said, now very impatient. “come on-come on-c—”

“Oh, all right,” she said. “Be good, Louis.”

“Okay,” he said, grinning, “I’ll be careful. Say hello to your folks, Rachel.”

“Oh, you,” she said and wrinkled her nose at him. Rachel wasn’t fooled; she knew why Louis was skipping this trip. “Fun-nee.”

He watched them go onto the plane… and disappear for the next week. He already felt homesick and lonely for them. He moved to the window where Ellie had been, hands in his coat pockets, watching the baggage handlers load the plane.

The truth was simple. Not only Mr. but also Mrs. Irwin Goldman of Lake Forest had disliked Louis from the start. He came from a poor background, but that was just the beginning. Even worse, he expected their daughter to support him while he went to medical school, where he would probably fail.

Louis could have handled all this, in fact had been doing so. Then something had happened which Rachel did not know about and never would… not from Louis, anyway. Irwin Goldman had offered to pay for Louis’s entire medical school. The price of this “scholarship” (Goldman’s word) was that Louis should break off his engagement with Rachel right away.

Louis Creed was not at the best time of his life to deal with such an insult, but such dramatic offers (or bribes, to be honest) are rarely made to those who are at the best time—which might be around the age of eighty-five. He was tired, for one thing. He was spending eighteen hours a week in classes, another twenty studying, another fifteen waiting tables in a pizza place near the Whitehall Hotel. He was also nervous. Mr. Goldman’s strangely cheerful manner that evening was completely different from his previous cold behavior, and Louis thought that when Goldman invited him into the study for a cigar, a look had passed from him to his wife. Later—much later, when time had given him some perspective—Louis would think that horses must feel the same vague anxiety when they smell the first smoke of a prairie fire. He began expecting Goldman to reveal at any moment that he knew Louis had been sleeping with his daughter.

When Goldman instead made his unbelievable offer—even taking his checkbook from the pocket of his smoking jacket like a prop in a silly play—Louis had exploded. He accused Goldman of trying to keep his daughter like a museum piece, of having no regard for anyone but himself, and of being a bossy, insensitive jerk. It would be a long time before he would admit to himself that part of his rage had been relief.

All of these little insights into Irwin Goldman’s character, though perhaps true, had no polite touch. Any sense of light comedy vanished; if there was humor in the rest of the conversation, it was of a much more crude kind. Goldman told him to get out and that if he ever saw Louis on his doorstep again, he would shoot him like a worthless dog. Louis told Goldman to take his checkbook and shove it up his ass. Goldman said he had seen bums in the gutter who had more potential than Louis Creed. Louis told Goldman he could also shove his goddamn BankAmericard and his American Express Gold Card right up there beside his checkbook.

None of this was a good start to building good relations with the future in-laws.

In the end Rachel had brought them around (after each man had had a chance to regret the things he had said, although neither of them had ever changed his mind about the other). There was no more drama, certainly no terribly theatrical from-this-day-forward-I-have-no-daughter scene. Goldman would have probably suffered through Rachel’s marriage to a monster before disowning her. Nevertheless the face above the collar of Irwin Goldman’s morning coat on the day Louis married Rachel looked a lot like the faces sometimes seen carved on Egyptian coffins. Their wedding present had been a six-place setting of Spode china and a microwave oven. No money. For most of Louis’s chaotic med school days, Rachel had worked as a clerk in a women’s clothing store. And from that day to this day, Rachel only knew that things had been and continued to be “tense” between her husband and her parents… particularly between Louis and her father.

Louis could have gone to Chicago with his family, although the university schedule would have meant flying back three days earlier than Rachel and the kids. That was no big deal. On the other hand, four days with his in-laws would have been.

The children had softened his in-laws a lot, as children often do. Louis suspected that he himself could have completed the reconciliation simply by pretending he had forgotten that evening in Goldman’s study. It wouldn’t even matter that Goldman knew he was pretending. But the fact was (and he at least had the guts to be honest about it with himself) that he did not quite want to reconcile. Ten years was a long time, but it was not quite long enough to take away the bad taste that had come into his mouth when, in Goldman’s study over glasses of brandy, the old man had opened one side of that ridiculous smoking jacket and removed the checkbook inside. Yes, he had felt relief that the nights—five of them in all—that he and Rachel had spent in his narrow, sagging apartment bed had not been discovered, but that surprised disgust was its own thing, and the years between then and now had not changed it.

He could have come, but he preferred to send his father-in-law his grandchildren, his daughter, and a message.

The Delta 727 pulled away from the ramp, turned… and he saw Ellie at one of the front windows, waving wildly. Louis waved back, smiling, and then someone—Ellie or Rachel—lifted Gage into the window. Louis waved, and Gage waved back—perhaps seeing him, perhaps only copying Ellie.

“Fly my people safe,” he muttered, then zipped his coat and went out to the parking lot. Here the wind howled and rushed with force enough to almost tear his hunter’s cap off his head, and he clapped a hand to it. He struggled with his keys to unlock the driver’s side door of his car and then turned as the jet rose beyond the terminal building, its nose tilted upward into the hard blue, its engines roaring.

Feeling very lonely indeed now—almost about to cry—Louis waved again.

He was still sad that evening when he recrossed Route 15 after a couple of beers with Jud and Norma—Norma had drunk a glass of wine, something Dr. Weybridge allowed, even suggested she have. They had moved into the kitchen tonight because of the season.

Jud had fired up the small Marek stove, and they had sat around it, the beer cold, the heat good, and Jud had talked about how the Micmac Indians had prevented a British landing at Machiss two hundred years ago. In those days the Micmacs had been quite scary, he said, and then added that he guessed there were still a few state and federal land lawyers who thought they were.

It should have been a good evening, but Louis was aware of the empty house waiting for him. Crossing the lawn and feeling the frost crunching under his shoes, he heard the telephone begin to ring in the house. He broke into a run, got through the front door, sprinted through the living room (knocking over a magazine stand), and then slid most of the way across the kitchen, his frosty shoes skidding over the linoleum. He grabbed the phone.

“Hello?”

“Louis?” Rachel’s voice, a little distant but absolutely fine. “We’re here. We made it. No problems.”

“Great!” he said and sat down to talk to her, thinking: I wish to God you were here.
