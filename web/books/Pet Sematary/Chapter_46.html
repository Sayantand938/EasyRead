Pet Sematary

Chapter 46

After he and Rachel finished talking, Jud put on his light coat—the day had become cloudy and the wind had started to blow—and crossed the road to Louis’s house, stopping on his side to check carefully for trucks before crossing. The trucks were the reason for all this. The damned trucks.

Except they weren’t.

He could feel the Pet Sematary pulling at him—and something more. Its voice used to be like a tempting lullaby, a voice of possible comfort and a dreamy kind of power, but now it was deeper and more than ominous—it was threatening and harsh. Stay out of this, you.

But he wouldn’t stay out of it. His duty went back too far.

He saw that Louis’s Honda Civic was gone from the garage. Only the big Ford wagon was there, looking dusty and unused. He tried the back door of the house and found it open.

“Louis?” he called, knowing Louis wouldn’t answer, but needing to break through the heavy silence of the house somehow. Oh, getting old was starting to be a pain—his limbs felt heavy and awkward most of the time, his back was a misery after only two hours in the garden, and it felt like a screw was stuck in his left hip.

He began to go through the house carefully, looking for the signs he had to look for—world’s oldest housebreaker, he thought without much humor and kept looking. He didn’t find any of the things that would have seriously upset him: boxes of toys saved back from the Salvation Army, clothes for a small boy put aside behind a door or in the closet or under a bed… or worst of all, the crib set up again in Gage’s room. There were none of the signs at all, but the house still felt unpleasantly empty, as if it were waiting to be filled with… well, something.

Maybe I should take a little trip out to Pleasantview Cemetery. See if anything’s going on there. Might even run into Louis Creed. I could buy him dinner, or something.

But the danger wasn’t at Pleasantview Cemetery in Bangor; it was here, in this house, and beyond it.

Jud left again and crossed the road to his own house. He pulled a six-pack of beer out of the kitchen fridge and took it into the living room. He sat down in front of the bay window that looked out on the Creed house, opened a beer, and lit a cigarette. The afternoon faded around him, and as it did so often these last few years, he found his mind turning back and back in a widening circle. If he had known what Rachel Creed had been thinking earlier, he could have told her that what her psych teacher said might be true, but when you got older, that fading of memory broke down little by little, the same way that everything else in your body broke down, and you found yourself recalling places and faces and events with an eerie certainty. Old memories grew bright again, the colors becoming true, the voices losing that tinny echo of time and regaining their original sound. It wasn’t a failure of information at all, Jud could have told him. The name for it was old age.

In his mind Jud again saw Lester Morgan’s bull Hanratty, his eyes rimmed with red, charging at everything in sight, everything that moved. Charging at trees when the wind moved the leaves. Before Lester gave up and stopped it, every tree in Hanratty’s fenced field was gored with his senseless anger and his horns were splintered and his head was bleeding. When Lester put Hanratty down, Lester had been sick with fear—the way Jud himself was right now.

He drank beer and smoked. Daylight faded. He did not turn on the light. Gradually the tip of his cigarette became a small red dot in the darkness. He sat and drank beer and watched Louis Creed’s driveway. He believed that when Louis came home from wherever he was, he would go over and have a little talk with him. Make sure Louis wasn’t planning to do anything he shouldn’t.

And still he felt the soft pull of whatever it was, whatever sick power it was that lived in that devil's place, reaching down from its bluff of rotted stone where all those cairns had been built.

Stay out of this, you. Stay out of it or you’re going to be very, very sorry.

Ignoring it as best he could, Jud sat and smoked and drank beer. And waited.
