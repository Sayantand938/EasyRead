Pet Sematary

Chapter 49

The trees were just blurry shapes against the cloudy sky, lit from behind by the airport lights in the distance. Louis parked the Honda on Mason Street, which bordered Pleasantview to the south. The wind there was so strong it almost pulled the car door out of his hand. He had to force it shut. The wind tugged at his jacket as he opened the Honda’s trunk and took out the tarp-covered bundle of tools.

He stood in a dark space between two streetlights, holding the canvas-wrapped bundle, carefully checking for cars before crossing to the wrought-iron fence that marked the graveyard's edge. He didn't want to be seen at all if he could avoid it, not even by someone who would forget him instantly. Next to him, the branches of an old elm creaked in the wind, making Louis think of lynchings. God, he was so scared. This wasn't just risky; it was insane.

No cars. On Mason Street, the streetlights stretched out in perfect white circles, lighting up the sidewalk where kids would ride bikes, jump rope, and play hopscotch after school, barely noticing the graveyard, except maybe at Halloween when it seemed a little spooky. Maybe they would even dare to cross the street and hang a paper skeleton on the fence, giggling at the old jokes: It’s the most popular place in town; people are dying to get in. Why is it wrong to laugh in the graveyard? Because everyone there is always in a grave mood.

“Gage,” he muttered. Gage was in there, behind that fence, unfairly trapped under the dark earth, and that was no joke. Gonna get you out, Gage, he thought. Gonna get you out, big guy, or die trying.

Louis crossed the street with the heavy bundle, stepped onto the curb, glanced both ways again, and tossed the canvas roll over the fence. It clinked softly when it landed. He dusted his hands and walked away, having memorized the spot. Even if he forgot, he could just follow the fence until he was across from his Civic, and he would find it.

But would the gate be open this late?

He walked down Mason Street to the stop sign, the wind pushing him. Moving shadows danced on the road.

He turned onto Pleasant Street, still following the fence. Car headlights flashed, and Louis stepped behind an elm tree. It wasn't a police car, he saw, just a van heading toward Hammond Street and probably the turnpike. Once it passed, Louis continued walking.

Of course it will be unlocked. It’s got to be.

He reached the gate, which looked like a cathedral made of iron, thin and elegant in the shifting shadows from the streetlights. He reached out and tried it.

Locked.

You idiot, of course it’s locked—did you really think someone would leave a cemetery unlocked in an American city after eleven at night? No one trusts that much, buddy, not anymore. So what do you do now?

Now he would have to climb and just hope no one looked away from the Carson Show long enough to see him climbing the iron fence like the world’s oldest, slowest kid.

Hey, police? I just saw the world’s oldest, slowest kid climbing into Pleasantview Cemetery. Looked like he was dying to get in. Yeah, looked like a serious matter to me. Kidding? Oh no, I’m totally serious. Maybe you ought to look into it.

Louis continued up Pleasant Street and turned right at the next corner. The tall iron fence went on and on beside him. The wind cooled and dried the sweat on his forehead and temples. His shadow grew and shrank in the streetlights. Every now and then he glanced at the fence, and then he stopped and made himself really look at it.

You’re going to climb that thing? Don’t make me laugh.

Louis Creed was a pretty tall man, a little over six-two, but the fence was easily nine feet high, each iron bar ending in a fancy, arrow-like point. Fancy, that is, until you slipped while swinging your leg over and the force of your falling two hundred pounds drove one of those arrow points into your groin, destroying your testicles. And there you would be, stuck like a pig at a barbecue, yelling until someone called the police and they came and pulled you off and took you to the hospital.

The sweat kept coming, making his shirt stick to his back. Everything was quiet except for the faint sound of late traffic on Hammond Street.

There had to be a way to get in there.

Had to be.

Come on, Louis, face the facts. You may be crazy, but you’re not that crazy. Maybe you could climb to the top of that fence, but it would take a trained gymnast to swing over those points without getting stuck. And even if you can get in, how are you going to get yourself and Gage’s body out?

He went on walking, vaguely aware that he was circling the cemetery but not doing anything useful.

All right, here’s the answer. I’ll just go home to Ludlow tonight and come back tomorrow, in the late afternoon… I’ll go in through the gate around four o’clock and find a place to hide until it’s midnight or a little later. I will, in other words, put off until tomorrow what I should have been smart enough to think of today.

Good idea, O Great Swami Louis… and in the meantime, what do I do about that big pile of stuff 1 threw over the wall? Pick, shovel, flashlight… you might as well stamp GRAVE-ROBBING EQUIPMENT on every damn piece of it.

It landed in the bushes. Who’s going to find it, for Chrissake?

On the surface that made sense. But this was no sensible thing he was doing, and his heart told him quietly and surely that he couldn’t come back tomorrow. If he didn’t do it tonight, he would never do it. He would never be able to get himself this worked up again. This was the moment, the only time he was ever going to have.

There were fewer houses up this way—an occasional square of yellow light shone on the other side of the street, and once he saw the gray-blue flicker of a TV—and looking through the fence he saw that the graves were older here, more rounded, sometimes leaning forward or backward from the freezes and thaws of many years. There was another stop sign up ahead, and another right turn would put him on a street roughly parallel to Mason Street, where he had begun. And when he got back to the beginning, what did he do? Collect two hundred dollars and go around again? Admit defeat?

Car headlights turned down the street. Louis stepped behind another tree, waiting for it to pass. This car was moving very slowly, and after a moment a white spotlight shone out from the passenger side and ran flickering along the wrought-iron fence. His heart squeezed painfully in his chest. It was a police car, checking the cemetery.

He pressed himself tight against the tree, the rough bark against his cheek, hoping desperately that it was big enough to hide him. The spotlight ran toward him. Louis put his head down, trying to hide the white blur of his face. The spotlight reached the tree, disappeared for a moment, and then reappeared on Louis’s right. He slipped around the tree a little. He had a momentary glimpse of the dark bubbles on the cruiser’s roof. He waited for the taillights to glow brighter red, for the doors to open, for the spotlight to suddenly turn back on its ball joint, hunting for him like a big white finger. Hey, you! You behind that tree! Come on out where we can see you, and we want to see both hands empty! Come out NOW!

The police car kept on going. It reached the corner, signaled politely, and turned left. Louis slumped back against the tree, breathing fast, his mouth sour and dry. He figured they would drive past his parked Honda, but that didn’t really matter. Parking from 6 P.M. to 7 A.M. was allowed on Mason Street. There were many other cars parked there. Their owners lived in the apartment buildings across the street.

Louis glanced up at the tree he was hiding behind.

Just above his head, the tree split. He thought he could—

Without thinking more about it, he reached into the split and pulled himself up, scrambling with his tennis shoes for grip, sending a little shower of bark down to the sidewalk. He got a knee up and a moment later had one foot planted firmly in the fork of the elm. If the police car came back, their spotlight would find a very strange bird in this tree. He needed to move quickly.

He pulled himself up onto a higher branch, one that hung over the very top of the fence. He felt ridiculously like the twelve-year-old he guessed he had once been. The tree was not still; it rocked easily, almost comfortably, in the steady wind. Its leaves rustled and whispered. Louis looked at the situation and then, before he could lose his nerve, he dropped off into space, holding on to the branch with his hands clasped together over it. The branch was a little thicker than a strong man’s forearm. With his sneakers dangling about eight feet over the sidewalk, he pulled himself hand over hand toward the fence. The branch dipped but didn’t seem like it would break. He was vaguely aware of his shadow following along on the sidewalk below, a shapeless black ape-shape. The wind chilled his sweaty armpits, and he shivered despite the sweat running down his face and neck. The branch dipped and swayed with his movements. The farther out he moved, the more the branch dipped. His hands and wrists were getting tired, and he was afraid his sweaty palms might slip.

He reached the fence. His tennis shoes dangled about a foot below the arrow tips. The tips didn’t look blunt at all from this angle. They looked very sharp. Sharp or not, he realized it wasn’t just his balls that were in danger here. If he fell and landed on one of those things, his weight would drive it up into his lungs. The returning cops would find a gruesome early Halloween decoration on the Pleasantview fence.

Breathing fast, almost gasping, he groped for the fence points with his feet, needing a moment’s rest. For a moment he hung there, his feet moving in the air, searching but not finding.

Light touched him and grew.

Oh Christ, that’s a car, a car is coming—!

He tried to move his hands forward, but his palms slipped. His clasped fingers were coming apart.

Still struggling to hold on, he turned his head to the left, looking under his straining arm. It was a car, but it sped through the intersection up the street without slowing. Lucky. If it had—

His hands slipped again. He felt bark fall onto his hair.

One foot found a hold, but now his other pants leg had caught on one of the arrow points. And Christ, he couldn’t hang on much longer. Desperately, Louis jerked his leg. The branch dipped. His hands slipped again. There was a sound of tearing cloth, and then he was standing on two of the arrow points. They dug into the soles of his tennis shoes, and the pressure quickly became painful, but Louis stood on them anyway. The relief in his hands and arms was greater than the pain in his feet.

What a sight I must be, Louis thought with faint and sad amusement. Holding the branch with his left hand, he wiped his right hand across his jacket. Then he wiped off the left while he held with the right.

He stood on the points for a moment longer and then moved his hands forward along the branch. It was slim enough for him to lace his fingers together comfortably now. He swung forward like Tarzan, feet leaving the arrow points. The branch dipped sharply, and he heard a worrying cracking sound. He let go, dropping blindly.

He landed badly. One knee hit against a gravestone, sending a sharp pain up his thigh. He rolled over on the grass, holding the knee, lips pulled back in a sort of grin, hoping that he hadn’t broken his kneecap. At last the pain began to fade a little, and he found that he could bend the joint. It would be all right if he kept moving and didn’t let it stiffen up on him. Maybe.

He got to his feet and started to walk along the fence back toward Mason Street and his equipment. His knee was bad at first, and he limped, but the pain smoothed out to a dull ache as he went. There was aspirin in the Honda’s first-aid kit. He should have remembered to bring that with him. Too late now. He kept an eye out for cars and moved back deeper into the cemetery when one came.

On the Mason Street side, which was likely to be busier, he kept well back from the fence until he was opposite the Civic. He was about to trot down to the fence and pull his bundle out of the bushes when he heard footsteps on the sidewalk and a woman’s soft laughter. He sat down behind a large grave marker—it hurt his knee too much to squat—and watched a couple walk up the far side of Mason Street. They were walking with their arms around each other’s waists, and something about their movement from one white patch of light to the next made Louis think of some old TV show. In a moment he had it: “The Jimmy Durante Hour.” What would they do if he rose up now, a shaky shadow in this silent city of the dead, and cried out to them: “Goodnight, Mrs. Calabash, wherever you are!”

They stopped in the patch of light just beyond his car and embraced. Watching them, Louis felt a kind of sick wonder and self-hatred. Here he was, crouched behind a tombstone like a monster in some cheap comic-book story, watching lovers. Is the line so thin, then? he wondered, and that thought also felt familiar. So thin you can simply cross it with this little fuss, mess, and bother? Climb a tree, crawl along a branch, drop into a graveyard, watch lovers…dig holes? That simple? Is it madness? I spent eight years becoming a doctor, but I’ve become a grave robber in one simple step—what I suppose people would call a ghoul.

He pressed his fists against his mouth to stop himself from making a sound and looked for that inner coldness, that sense of being cut off. It was there, and Louis drew it gratefully around him.

When the couple finally walked on, Louis watched them with simple impatience. They climbed the steps of one of the apartment buildings. The man fumbled for a key, and a moment later they were inside. The street was silent again except for the constant beat of the wind, rustling the trees and tumbling his sweaty hair over his forehead.

Louis ran down to the fence, bent low, and felt through the brush for his canvas bundle. Here it was, rough under his fingers. He picked it up, listening to the muffled clank from inside. He carried it over to the broad graveled drive that led in through the gates and paused to get his bearings. Straight up here, go left at the fork. No problem.

He walked along the edge of the drive, wanting to stay in the shadow of the elms in case there was a full-time caretaker and he happened to be out.

He bore left at the fork, approaching Gage’s grave now, and suddenly, terribly, realized he could not remember what his son had looked like. He paused, staring off into the rows of graves, the frowning fronts of the monuments, and tried to picture him. He remembered individual features—his blond hair, still so fine and light, his slanting eyes, his small, white teeth, the little twist of scar on his chin from when he had fallen down the back steps of their place in Chicago. He could see these things but could not put them together. He saw Gage running toward the road, running toward his death by the Orinco truck, but Gage’s face was turned away. He tried to picture Gage as he had been in his crib on the night of the kite-flying day and could see only darkness in his mind.

Gage, where are you?

Have you ever thought, Louis, that you may be doing your son a disservice? Perhaps he’s happy where he is… maybe all of that isn’t the nonsense you always thought it was. Maybe he’s with the angels or maybe he’s just sleeping. And if he’s sleeping, do you really know what you might wake up?

Oh Gage, where are you? I want you home with us.

But was he really in control of himself? Why couldn’t he picture Gage’s face, and why was he ignoring everyone’s warning—Jud’s, the dream of Pascow, the fear in his own troubled heart?

He thought of the grave markers in the Pet Sematary, those crude circles, spiraling down into the unknown, and then the coldness came over him again. Why was he standing here, trying to picture Gage’s face anyway?

He would be seeing it soon enough.

The headstone was there now; it read simply GAGE WILLIAM CREED, followed by the two dates. Someone had come today to pay respects, he saw; there were fresh flowers. Who could it have been? Missy Dandridge?

His heart beat heavily but slowly in his chest. This was it then; if he was going to do it, he better start. There was only so much night left, and then the day would come.

Louis looked into his heart one last time and saw that yes, he meant to go through with this. He nodded almost without moving and reached for his pocketknife. He had tied his bundle with Scotch tape, and now he cut it. He unrolled the tarp at the foot of Gage’s grave like a bedroll and then arranged things exactly as he would arrange tools to stitch a cut or do a small office procedure.

Here was the flashlight with its lens covered in felt as the store clerk had said. The felt was also held with tape. He had made a small circle in the middle by putting a penny on the felt and cutting around it with a scalpel. Here was the short pick, which he shouldn’t need—he had brought it just in case. He wouldn't have a sealed lid to deal with, and he shouldn’t find any rocks in a new grave. Here was the shovel, the spade, the rope, the work gloves. He put the gloves on, grabbed the spade, and started.

The ground was soft, the digging easy. The grave’s shape was clear, the dirt he was throwing out softer than the earth at the edge. His mind automatically compared how easy this was to the rocky, hard ground where, if everything worked out, he would be reburying his son later tonight. Up there, he would need the pick. Then he tried to stop thinking altogether. It only got in the way.

He threw the dirt on the ground to the left of the grave, working at a steady pace that became harder to keep up as the hole got deeper. He stepped into the grave, smelling the damp smell of fresh dirt, a smell he remembered from summers with Uncle Carl.

Digger, he thought and stopped to wipe sweat from his face. Uncle Carl had told him that was the nickname for every graveyard worker in America. Their friends called them Digger.

He started again.

He stopped only once more, to check his watch. It was twenty after twelve. He felt time slipping away like something greasy.

Forty minutes later, the spade scraped across something, and Louis bit his lip hard enough to draw blood. He got the flashlight and shined it down. Here was more dirt, and across it, a grayish-silver line. It was the top of the grave liner. Louis got most of the dirt off, but he didn’t want to make too much noise, and nothing was louder than a shovel scraping concrete in the middle of the night.

He climbed out of the grave and got the rope. He put it through the iron rings on one half of the grave-liner top. He got out of the grave again, spread out the tarp, lay down on it, and grabbed the ends of the rope.

Louis, I think this is it. Your last chance.

You’re right. It’s my last chance and I’m taking it.

He wrapped the ends of the rope around his hands and pulled. The square of concrete came up easily, scraping on the turning end. It stood upright over a square of blackness, now a vertical tombstone instead of a horizontal grave cover.

Louis pulled the rope out of the rings and tossed it aside. He wouldn’t need it for the other half; he could stand on the sides of the grave liner and pull it up.

He got down into the grave again, moving carefully, not wanting to knock over the cement slab he had already pulled up and crush his toes or break the thing, which was quite thin. Pebbles rattled down into the hole, and he heard some of them hit Gage’s coffin.

Bending, he grabbed the other half of the grave-liner top and pulled upward. As he did so, he felt something squish coldly under his fingers. When he had this second half of the top standing on end, he looked down at his hand and saw a fat earthworm wriggling weakly there. With a choked cry of disgust, Louis wiped it off on the dirt wall of his son’s grave.

Then he shone his flashlight downward.

Here was the coffin he had last seen resting on chrome runners over the grave at the funeral service, surrounded by that awful green Astroturf. This was the safety-deposit box in which he was supposed to bury all his hopes for his son. Fury, clean and white hot, the opposite of his former coldness, rose up in him. Idiotic! The answer was no!

Louis groped for the spade and found it. He raised it over his shoulder and brought it down on the coffin’s latch once, twice, a third time, a fourth. His lips were drawn back in a furious grimace.

Going to get you out, Gage, you just wait and see!

The latch had splintered on the first hit, and he probably didn't need to hit it anymore, but he kept going, not just wanting to open the coffin, but to damage it. He finally regained some sense and stopped with the shovel raised for another hit.

The blade was bent and scratched. He threw it aside and climbed out of the grave on legs that felt weak and like rubber. He felt nauseous, and the anger had disappeared as fast as it had arrived. The coldness returned in its place, and he had never felt so alone and cut off; he felt like an astronaut who had floated away from their ship during a spacewalk and was now drifting in a vast darkness, breathing on borrowed time. Did Bill Baterman feel like this? he wondered.

He lay on the ground, on his back this time, waiting to see if he was calm and ready to go on. When the rubbery feeling had left his legs, he sat up and slipped back down into the grave. He shone the flashlight on the latch and saw it wasn't just broken, but destroyed. He had swung the shovel in a blind rage, but every hit had landed right there, bull’s eye, as if guided. The wood around it had splintered.

Louis slipped the flashlight under his arm. He squatted down a little. His hands reached out, like the hands of a catcher in a circus act, waiting to do his part in a dangerous catch.

He found the groove in the lid and slipped his fingers into it. He paused for a moment—you couldn't really call it a hesitation—and then he opened his son’s coffin.
