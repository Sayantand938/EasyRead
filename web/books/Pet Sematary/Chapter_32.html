Pet Sematary

Chapter 32

It wasn't a heart attack. It was a stroke, sudden and likely painless. When Louis called Steve Masterton that afternoon and told him what happened, Steve said he wouldn't mind going that way himself.

"Sometimes God messes around," Steve said, "and sometimes He just picks you and tells you your time is up."

Rachel didn't want to talk about it at all and wouldn't let Louis talk to her about it.

Ellie wasn't so much upset as she was surprised and curious—which Louis thought was a perfectly healthy reaction for a six-year-old. She wanted to know if Mrs. Crandall had died with her eyes shut or open. Louis said he didn't know.

Jud was holding up as well as could be expected, considering he'd shared his life with her for almost sixty years. Louis found the old man—and he looked very much like an old man of eighty-three that day—sitting alone at the kitchen table, smoking a Chesterfield, drinking a beer, and staring blankly into the living room.

He looked up when Louis came in and said, "Well, she's gone, Louis." He said this so clearly and matter-of-factly that Louis thought it hadn't really sunk in yet—hadn't hit him where it hurt. Then Jud's mouth started to quiver, and he covered his eyes with one arm. Louis went to him and put an arm around him. Jud broke down and wept. It had sunk in, all right. Jud understood perfectly. His wife had died.

"That's good," Louis said. "That's good, Jud, she would want you to cry a little, I think. Probably be mad if you didn't." He had started to cry a little himself. Jud hugged him tightly, and Louis hugged him back.

Jud cried for ten minutes or so, and then the storm passed. Louis listened carefully to what Jud said then—he listened as a doctor and as a friend. He listened for any rambling in Jud's conversation; he listened to see if Jud knew what time it was (no need to ask him where; that wouldn't prove anything because for Jud Crandall, where had always been Ludlow, Maine); he listened most of all for any use of Norma's name as if she were still alive. He found little or no sign that Jud was losing it. Louis knew that it wasn't uncommon for two old married people to die close together, a month, a week, even a day apart. The shock, he supposed, or maybe even some deep inner urge to join the one who had died (that was a thought he wouldn't have had before Church; he found that many of his thoughts about spiritual and supernatural things had changed quietly but deeply). He concluded that Jud was grieving hard but was still in his right mind. Louis sensed none of the obvious weakness in Jud that Norma had seemed to have on New Year's Eve, when the four of them had sat in the Creed's living room, drinking eggnog.

Jud brought him a beer from the fridge, his face still red and blotchy from crying.

“A bit early in the day,” he said, “but the sun’s over the yardarm somewhere in the world, and considering everything…”

“Say no more,” Louis told him and opened the beer. He looked at Jud. “Shall we drink to her?”

“I guess we better,” Jud said. “You should have seen her when she was sixteen, Louis, coming back from church with her jacket unbuttoned… you’d have been amazed. She could have made the devil swear off drinking. Thank Christ she never asked me to do it.”

Louis nodded and raised his beer a little. “To Norma,” he said.

Jud clinked his bottle against Louis’s. He was crying again but he was also smiling. He nodded. “May she have peace, and let there be no damn arthritis wherever she is.”

“Amen,” Louis said, and they drank.

It was the only time Louis saw Jud get past being a little tipsy, and even then he didn't get drunk. He reminisced; a constant stream of warm memories and stories, colorful and clear and sometimes striking, flowed from him. Yet between the stories of the past, Jud dealt with the present in a way Louis could only admire; if it had been Rachel who had simply dropped dead after her grapefruit and morning cereal, he wondered if he could have done half as well.

Jud called the Brookings-Smith Mortuary in Bangor and made as many of the arrangements as he could by telephone; he made an appointment to come in the following day and make the rest. Yes, he would have her embalmed; he wanted her in a dress, which he would provide; yes, he would pick out underwear; no, he did not want the mortuary to supply the special shoes which laced up the back. Would they have someone wash her hair? he asked. She washed it last on Monday night, and so it had been dirty when she died. He listened, and Louis, whose uncle had been in what those in the business called “the quiet trade,” knew the undertaker was telling Jud that a final wash and set was part of the service given. Jud nodded and thanked the man he was talking to, then listened again. Yes, he said, he would have her made up, but it was to be a light layer. “She’s dead and people know it,” he said, lighting a Chesterfield. “No need to doll her up.” The coffin would be closed during the funeral, he told the director calmly, but open during the visiting hours the day before. She was to be buried in Mount Hope Cemetery, where they had bought plots in 1951. He had the papers in hand and gave the mortician the plot number so that preparations could begin out there: H-101. He himself had H-102, he told Louis later on.

He hung up, looked at Louis, and said, “Prettiest cemetery in the world is right there in Bangor, as far as I’m concerned. Get yourself another beer, if you want, Louis. All of this is going to take awhile.”

Louis was about to refuse—he was feeling a little tipsy—when a strange image popped up suddenly in his mind: Jud pulling Norma’s corpse on a crude carrier through the woods. Toward the Micmac burying ground beyond the Pet Sematary.

It hit him hard. Without a word, he got up and grabbed another beer from the fridge. Jud nodded and dialed the telephone again. By three that afternoon, when Louis went home for a sandwich and soup, Jud had made a lot of progress organizing his wife’s funeral; he moved from one thing to the next like a man planning a very important dinner party. He called the North Ludlow Methodist Church, where the funeral would be held, and the Cemetery Administration Office at Mount Hope; the undertaker at Brookings-Smith would be calling them too, but Jud called first as a courtesy. It was something few people grieving ever thought of… or if they did, they could rarely bring themselves to do it. Louis admired Jud even more for it. Later he called Norma’s few living relatives and his own, flipping through an old and worn address book with a leather cover to find the numbers. And between calls, he drank beer and remembered the past.

Louis felt great admiration for him… and love?

Yes, his heart confirmed. And love.

When Ellie came down that night in her pajamas to be kissed, she asked Louis if Mrs. Crandall would go to heaven. She almost whispered the question to Louis, as if she knew it would be better if they weren’t overheard. Rachel was in the kitchen making a chicken pie, which she planned to take to Jud the next day.

Across the street, all the lights were on in the Crandall house. Cars were parked in Jud’s driveway and along the side of the road on that side for a hundred feet in both directions. The official viewing hours would be tomorrow, at the funeral home, but tonight people had come to comfort Jud as best they could, and to help him remember, and to celebrate Norma’s passing—what Jud had called “the foregoing” that afternoon. Between that house and this, a frigid February wind blew. The road was patched with black ice. The coldest part of the Maine winter was now upon them.

“Well, I don’t really know, honey,” Louis said, taking Ellie on his lap. On the TV, a gunfight was happening. A man spun and fell, unnoticed by either of them. Louis was aware—uncomfortably so—that Ellie probably knew a lot more about Ronald McDonald and Spiderman and the Burger King than she did about Moses, Jesus, and St. Paul. She was the daughter of a woman who didn’t practice Judaism and a man who no longer went to Methodist church, and he figured her ideas about the whole spirit world were very vague—not myths, not dreams, but dreams of dreams. It’s late for that, he thought randomly. She’s only five, but it’s late for that. Jesus Christ, it gets late so fast.

But Ellie was looking at him, and he should say something.

“People believe all sorts of things about what happens to us when we die,” he said. “Some people think we go to heaven or hell. Some people believe we’re born again as little children—”

“Sure, reincarnation. Like what happened to Audrey Rose in that movie on TV.”

“You never saw that!” Rachel, he thought, would have a heart attack if she thought Ellie had seen Audrey Rose.

“Marie told me at school,” Ellie said. Marie was Ellie’s self-declared best friend, a thin, grubby little girl who always looked like she might catch impetigo, ringworm, or even scurvy. Both Louis and Rachel encouraged the friendship as best they could, but Rachel had once admitted to Louis that after Marie left, she always felt like checking Ellie’s head for bugs and lice. Louis had laughed and agreed.

“Marie’s mommy lets her watch all the shows.” There was a hint of disapproval in this that Louis chose to ignore.

“Well, it’s reincarnation, but you get the idea. Catholics believe in heaven and hell, but also in limbo and purgatory. Hindus and Buddhists believe in Nirvana—”

There was a shadow on the dining room wall. Rachel. Listening.

Louis went on more slowly.

“There are probably lots more too. But the thing is, Ellie, no one really knows. People say they do, but they mean they believe it because of faith. Do you know what faith is?”

“Well…”

“Here we are, sitting in my chair,” Louis said. “Do you think my chair will still be here tomorrow?”

“Yeah, sure.”

“Then you have faith it will be here. I do, too. Faith is believing something will be, or is. Get it?”

“Yes.” Ellie nodded.

“But we don’t know it’ll be here. Someone could break in and steal it, right?”

Ellie giggled. Louis smiled.

“We just have to believe it won’t happen. Faith is great, and some religious people think faith and knowing are the same, but I don’t think so. There are too many different ideas about it. What we know is this: When we die, one of two things happens. Either our souls and thoughts live on somehow, or they don’t. If they do, anything is possible. If they don’t, it’s just nothing. The end.”

“Like going to sleep?”

He thought about it and said, “More like being put to sleep with ether, I think.”

“What do you believe, Daddy?”

The shadow on the wall moved and stopped again.

For most of his life, since college, he had believed that death was the end. He had been at many deathbeds and never felt a soul fly past him on its way to… wherever. Hadn’t he thought this when Victor Pascow died? He agreed with his Psychology teacher that the life-after-death stories in books and newspapers were probably just the mind’s last fight against dying—the human mind, making up a vision of life after death to avoid going crazy. He also agreed with a friend from college who said, one night when they stayed up talking, that the Bible had too many miracles that stopped happening when people started being rational. (“Stopped completely,” he had said at first, but others said there were still weird things happening, little mysteries in a world that was mostly clear and simple—like the Shroud of Turin, which no one could prove was fake.) “So Jesus brought Lazarus back from the dead,” this friend—who became a respected doctor—had said. “Okay, fine. If I have to believe it, I will. I mean, I had to accept that one twin can sometimes swallow the other in the womb, like a baby cannibal, and then show up with teeth in his testicles or lungs years later, and if I can believe that, I can believe anything. But I want to see the death certificate. I want proof. I’m like Thomas, who wouldn’t believe Jesus had risen until he could see the nail holes and touch his side. He was the real doctor, not Luke.”

No, he had never really believed in life after death. At least, not until Church.

“I think we continue,” he told his daughter slowly. “But I don’t know what it’s like. Maybe it’s different for everyone. Maybe you get what you believed in your life. But I think we continue, and Mrs. Crandall is probably someplace where she can be happy.”

“You believe that,” Ellie said. It wasn’t a question. She sounded amazed.

Louis smiled, a little pleased and a little embarrassed. “I guess so. And it’s time for you to go to bed. Like ten minutes ago.”

He kissed her twice, once on the lips and once on the nose.

“Do you think animals continue?”

“Yes,” he said, without thinking, and for a moment he almost added, Especially cats. The words almost came out, and his skin felt gray and cold.

“Okay,” she said and slid down. “I have to go kiss Mommy.”

“Right.”

He watched her go. At the dining room doorway, she turned back and said, “I was really dumb about Church that day, wasn’t I? Crying like that.”

“No, honey,” he said. “I don’t think you were dumb.”

“If he died now, I could handle it,” she said, then seemed to think about what she'd just said, as if a little surprised. Then she said, as if agreeing with herself: “Yes, I could.” And went to find Rachel.

Later, in bed, Rachel said, “I heard you talking with her.”

“And you don’t like it?” Louis asked. He thought maybe it was best to discuss it, if that’s what Rachel wanted.

“No,” Rachel said, with a hesitation unusual for her. “No, Louis, it’s not that. I just get… scared. And you know me. When I get scared, I get defensive.”

Louis couldn’t remember Rachel ever speaking with such difficulty, and suddenly he felt more careful than he had with Ellie earlier. He felt like he was in a minefield.

“Scared of what? Dying?”

“Not me,” she said. “I hardly even think about that… anymore. But when I was a kid, I thought about it a lot. Lost sleep. Dreamed of monsters eating me in bed, and they all looked like my sister Zelda.”

Yes, Louis thought. Here it is; finally, after all this time, here it is.

“You don’t talk about her much,” he said.

Rachel smiled and touched his face. “You’re sweet, Louis. I never talk about her. I try not to think about her.”

“I always thought you had your reasons.”

“I did. I do.”

She paused, thinking.

“I know she died… spinal meningitis…”

“Spinal meningitis,” she repeated. “There aren’t any pictures of her in the house anymore.”

“There’s a picture of a young girl in your father’s—”

“In his study. Yes, I forgot that one. And my mother still carries one in her wallet, I think. She was two years older than I was. She caught it… and she was in the back bedroom… she was in the back bedroom like a dirty secret, Louis, she was dying in there, my sister died in the back bedroom and that’s what she was, a dirty secret—she was always a dirty secret!”

Rachel suddenly broke down completely, and in the loud, rising sound of her sobs, Louis sensed she was becoming hysterical and became alarmed. He reached for her and caught a shoulder, which she pulled away from him as soon as he touched it. He could feel the whisper of her nightdress under his fingertips.

“Rachel—babe—don’t—”

“Don’t tell me don’t,” she said. “Don’t stop me, Louis. I only have the strength to tell this once, and then I don’t ever want to talk about it again. I probably won’t sleep tonight anyway.”

“Was it that horrible?” he asked, already knowing. It explained so much. Things he'd never connected or only vaguely suspected now clicked into place. He realized she'd never gone to a funeral with him—not even Al Locke's, a med student killed in a motorcycle accident with a bus. Al often visited their apartment, and Rachel liked him. Yet she skipped his funeral.

She was sick that day, Louis suddenly recalled. The flu or something. She looked bad. But she was fine the next day.

She was okay again after the funeral, he corrected himself. He remembered thinking then her sickness might be all in her head.

“It was horrible, all right. Worse than you can imagine. Louis, we watched her get worse every day, and no one could do anything. She was always in pain. Her body seemed to shrink… curl up… her shoulders hunched and her face drooping, like a mask. Her hands were like bird claws. I had to feed her sometimes. I hated it, but I did it and never complained. When the pain got too bad, they gave her drugs—weak ones at first, then ones that would have made her an addict if she'd lived. But everyone knew she wouldn't. I guess that’s why she’s such a… secret to us all. Because we wanted her to die, Louis, we wished she would die, and not just to end her pain, but to end ours. She was starting to look like a monster, and act like one… oh Christ, I know that sounds awful…”

She covered her face.

Louis touched her gently. “Rachel, it doesn’t sound awful at all.”

“It does!” she cried. “It does!”

“It just sounds true,” he said. “People with long illnesses often become demanding, unpleasant monsters. The idea of the saintly, suffering patient is a romantic lie. Once bedsores appear, they start to lash out and spread misery. They can’t help it, but that doesn’t help those around them.”

She looked at him, amazed… almost hopeful. Then distrust returned to her face. “You’re making that up.”

He smiled grimly. “Want me to show you the books? What about suicide rates? Want to see those? In families who nurse a dying patient at home, suicides skyrocket in the six months after the patient dies.”

“Suicide!”

“They swallow pills, or sniff a pipe, or blow their brains out. Their hate… their weariness… their disgust… their sorrow…” He shrugged and gently touched his closed fists together. “The people left behind start feeling like they’re guilty of murder. So they step out.”

A crazy, hurt kind of relief had come into Rachel’s puffy face. “She was demanding… hateful. Sometimes she’d pee in her bed on purpose. My mother would ask if she needed help getting to the bathroom… and later, when she couldn’t get up, if she wanted the bedpan… and Zelda would say no… and then she’d pee the bed so my mother or my mother and I would have to change the sheets… and she’d say it was an accident, but you could see the smile in her eyes, Louis. You could see it. The room always smelled of pee and her drugs… she had bottles of some dope that smelled like Smith Brothers’ Wild Cherry cough drops, and that smell was always there… some nights I wake up… even now I wake up and I think I can smell Wild Cherry cough drops… and I think… if I’m not really awake… I think ‘Is Zelda dead yet? Is she?’ … I think...”

Rachel caught her breath. Louis took her hand, and she squeezed his fingers with fierce, bright tightness.

“When we changed her, you could see how her back was twisting and knotting. Near the end, Louis, near the end it seemed like her… like her butt had somehow gotten all the way up to the middle of her back.”

Now Rachel’s wet eyes had the glassy, horrified look of a child remembering the same terrible nightmare again and again.

“And sometimes she’d touch me with her… her hands… her birdy hands… and sometimes I’d almost scream and ask her not to, and once I spilled some of her soup on my arm when she touched my face and I burned myself, and that time I did scream… and I cried and I could see the smile in her eyes then, too.

“Near the end, the drugs stopped working. She was the one who would scream then, and none of us could remember what she was like before, not even my mother. She was just this gross, hateful, screaming thing in the back bedroom… our dirty secret.”

Rachel swallowed. Her throat clicked.

“My parents were gone when she finally… when she… you know, when she…”

With terrible, wrenching effort, Rachel brought it out.

“When she died, my parents were away. They were away but I was with her. It was Passover, and they went to see friends for a bit. Just for a few minutes. I was reading a magazine in the kitchen. Well, looking at it, anyway. I was waiting—for the time to give her more medicine because she was screaming. She’d been screaming since my folks left, almost. I couldn’t read with her screaming like that. And then… see, what happened was… well… Zelda stopped screaming. Louis, I was eight… bad dreams every night… I thought she hated me because my back was straight, because I didn’t hurt all the time, because I could walk, because I was going to live… I started to think she wanted to kill me. Only, even now, Louis, I don’t really think it was all my imagination. I think she hated me. I don’t think she would have killed me, but if she could have taken over my body somehow… turned me out of it like in a fairy tale… I think she would have done that. But when she stopped screaming, I went to see if she was okay… to see if she had fallen over or slipped off her pillows. I went in and I looked at her and I thought she must have swallowed her own tongue and was choking to death. Louis”—Rachel’s voice rose, teary and frighteningly childish, as if she were going back, reliving it—”Louis, I didn’t know what to do! I was eight!”

“No, of course you didn’t,” Louis said. He hugged her, and Rachel gripped him with the desperate strength of a swimmer whose boat has flipped over in a lake. “Did someone actually give you a hard time about it?”

“No,” she said, “no one blamed me. But no one could make it better. No one could change it. No one could undo it, Louis. She hadn’t swallowed her tongue. She made a sound, a kind of, I don’t know—gaaaaaa—like that—”

In her distressed, total memory of that day she did a very good imitation of how her sister Zelda must have sounded, and Louis thought of Victor Pascow. He held his wife tighter.

“—and there was spit, spit coming down her chin—”

“Rachel, that’s enough,” he said, not quite steadily. “I know the symptoms.”

“I’m explaining,” she said stubbornly. “I’m explaining why I can’t go to poor Norma’s funeral, for one thing, and why we had that stupid fight that day—”

“Shh—that’s forgotten.”

“Not by me,” she said. “I remember it well, Louis. I remember it as well as I remember my sister Zelda choking to death in her bed on April 14, 1965.”

For a long moment there was silence in the room.

“I turned her over on her belly and thumped her back,” Rachel went on at last. “It’s all I knew to do. Her feet were going up and down… and her twisted legs… and I remember there was a sound like farting… I thought she was farting or I was, but it wasn’t farts, it was the seams under both arms of my blouse ripping out when I turned her over. She started to… to shake… and I saw that her face was turned sideways, turned into the pillows, and I thought, oh, she’s choking, Zelda’s choking, and they’ll come home and say I murdered her by choking, they’ll say you hated her, Rachel, and that was true, and they’ll say you wanted her to be dead, and that was true too. Because, Louis, see, the first thought that went through my mind when she started to go up and down in the bed like that, I remember it, my first thought was Oh good, finally, Zelda’s choking and this is going to be over. So I turned her over again and her face had gone black, Louis, and her eyes were bulging and her neck was swelled up. Then she died. I backed across the room. I guess I wanted to back out the door, but I hit the wall and a picture fell down—it was a picture from one of the Oz books that Zelda liked before she got sick with the meningitis, when she was well, it was a picture of Oz the Great and Terrible, only Zelda always called him Oz the Gweat and Tewwible because she couldn’t make that sound, and so she sounded like Elmer Fudd. My mother got that picture framed because… because Zelda liked it most of all… Oz the Gweat and Tewwible… and it fell down and hit the floor and the glass in the frame shattered and I started to scream because I knew she was dead and I thought… I guess I thought it was her ghost, coming back to get me, and I knew that her ghost would hate me like she did, but her ghost wouldn’t be stuck in bed, so I screamed… I screamed and I ran out of the house screaming ‘Zelda’s dead! Zelda’s dead! Zelda’s dead!’ And the neighbors… they came and they looked… they saw me running down the street with my blouse all ripped out under the arms… I was yelling ‘Zelda’s dead!’ Louis, and I guess maybe they thought I was crying but I think… I think maybe I was laughing, Louis. I think maybe that’s what I was doing.”

“If you were, I admire you for it,” Louis said.

“You don’t mean that, though,” Rachel said with the complete certainty of someone who has gone over a point again and again. He let it go. He thought she might eventually get rid of this awful, rotten memory that had haunted her for so long—most of it, anyway—but never this part. Never completely. Louis Creed was no psychiatrist, but he knew that there are rusty, half-buried things in every life and that people seem forced to go back to these things and pull at them, even though they hurt. Tonight Rachel had pulled almost all of it out, like some terrible and stinking rotten tooth, its crown black, its nerves infected, its roots foul. It was out. Let that last harmful cell remain; if God was good it would stay quiet except in her deepest dreams. That she had been able to remove as much as she had was almost unbelievable—it didn't just show her courage; it shouted it. Louis was in awe of her. He felt like cheering.

He sat up now and turned on the light. “Yes,” he said, “I admire you for it. And if I needed another reason to… to really hate your mother and father, I’ve got it now. You never should have been left alone with her, Rachel. Never.”

Like a child—the child of eight she had been when this awful, unbelievable thing had happened—she corrected him, “Lou, it was Passover season—”

“I don’t care if it was judgment day,” Louis said with a sudden low and rough anger that caused her to pull back a little. He was remembering the student nurses, those two candy-stripers who had the bad luck to be there the morning Pascow had been brought in dying. One of them, a tough little lady named Carla Shavers, had returned the next day and had worked out so well that even Charlton was impressed. The other they had never seen again. Louis was not surprised and did not blame her.

Where was the nurse? There should have been a nurse in attendance… they went out, they actually went out and left an eight-year-old kid in charge of her dying sister, who was probably completely insane by then. Why? Because it was Passover season. And because elegant Dory Goldman couldn’t stand the stink that particular morning and had to get away from it for just a little while. So Rachel got the duty. Right? Rachel got the duty. Eight years old, pigtails, middy blouse. Rachel got the duty. Rachel could stay and put up with the stink. What did they send her to Camp Sunset in Vermont for six weeks every year for, if not to put up with the stink of her dying, insane sister? Ten new shirt-and-jumper combinations for Gage and six new dresses for Ellie and I’ll pay your way through medical school if you’ll stay away from my daughter… but where was the overflowing checkbook when your daughter was dying of spinal meningitis and your other daughter was alone with her, you bastard? Where was the nurse?

Louis sat up, got out of bed.

“Where are you going?” Rachel asked, alarmed.

“To get you a Valium.”

“You know I don’t—”

“Tonight you do,” he said.

She took the pill and then told him the rest. Her voice stayed calm. The tranquilizer was working.

The next-door neighbor had found eight-year-old Rachel behind a tree, crouching and screaming “Zelda’s dead!” over and over. Rachel’s nose was bleeding. She had blood all over her. The same neighbor had called the ambulance and then her parents. After stopping Rachel’s nosebleed and calming her with hot tea and two aspirins, she got her parents' location—they were visiting Mr. and Mrs. Cabron across town; Peter Cabron was an accountant in her father’s business.

By that evening, big changes had happened in the Goldman household. Zelda was gone. Her room had been cleaned and aired out. All the furniture was gone. The room was empty. Later—much later—it became Dory Goldman’s sewing room.

The first nightmare came to Rachel that night. When she woke up at two in the morning, screaming for her mother, she was horrified that she could barely get out of bed. Her back was in pain. She had hurt it moving Zelda. In her burst of strength, she had lifted Zelda hard enough to tear her own blouse.

That she had strained herself trying to keep Zelda from choking was simple and obvious. To everyone, that is, except Rachel herself. Rachel was sure it was Zelda’s revenge from beyond the grave. Zelda knew Rachel was glad she was dead. Zelda knew that when Rachel ran from the house shouting Zelda’s dead at the top of her voice, she had been laughing, not screaming. Zelda knew she had been murdered and had given Rachel spinal meningitis, and soon Rachel’s back would twist and change, and she too would have to lie in bed, slowly turning into a monster, her hands turning into claws.

Soon she would scream with the pain, as Zelda had, and then she would wet the bed, and finally choke to death on her own tongue. It was Zelda’s revenge.

No one could change Rachel’s mind—not her mother, her father, or Dr. Murray, who said it was a mild back strain and told Rachel sharply (cruelly, some—Louis, for instance—would have said) to stop acting so badly. She should remember her sister had just died, Dr. Murray told her. Her parents were deep in grief, and this was not the time for Rachel to seek attention. Only the fading pain convinced her she was not a victim of Zelda’s revenge or God’s punishment. For months (or so she told Louis; it was actually years, eight of them) afterward, she would wake from nightmares in which her sister died again and again, and in the dark Rachel’s hands would fly to her back to check it was all right. In the fearful aftermath of these dreams, she often thought the closet door would bang open and Zelda would stumble out, blue and twisted, her eyes rolled back, her black tongue sticking out, her hands hooked into claws to murder the murderer cowering in her bed with her hands pressed into her back…

She hadn’t gone to Zelda’s funeral, or any funeral since.

“If you’d told me this before,” Louis said, “it would explain so much.”

“Lou, I couldn’t,” she said simply. She sounded very sleepy now. “Since then, I’ve been… I guess, a little afraid of it.”

Just a little afraid, Louis thought. Yeah, right.

“I can’t… seem to stop it. I know you’re right, that death is perfectly natural—good, even—but what I know and what happens… inside me…”

“Yeah,” he said.

“That day I blew up at you,” she said, “I knew Ellie was just crying about the idea… a way of getting used to it… but I couldn’t help it. I’m sorry, Louis.”

“No need to apologize,” he said, stroking her hair. “But what the hell, I accept it anyway, if it’ll make you feel better.”

She smiled. “It does, you know. And I feel better. I feel like I just threw up something that’s poisoned me for years.”

“Maybe you have.”

Rachel’s eyes closed and then opened again… slowly. “And don’t blame it all on my father, Louis. Please. That was a terrible time for them. The bills—Zelda’s bills—were huge. My dad had missed his chance to grow into the suburbs, and the sales in the downtown store were down. On top of that, my mother was a bit crazy herself.

“Well, it all worked out. It was as if Zelda’s death was the start of good times coming back. There had been a recession, but then the money came easier and Daddy got his loan, and since then he’s never looked back. But that’s why they’ve always been protective of me, I think. It’s not just because I’m the only one left—”

“It’s guilt,” Louis said.

“Yes, I suppose. And you won’t be mad at me if I’m sick when they bury Norma?”

“No, honey, I won’t be mad.” He paused and then took her hand. “Can I take Ellie?”

Her hand tightened in his. “Oh, Louis, I don’t know,” she said. “She’s so young—”

“She’s known where babies come from for a year or more,” he reminded her again.

She was quiet for a long time, looking up at the ceiling and biting her lips. “If you think it’s best,” she said finally. “If you think it won’t… won’t hurt her.”

“Come over here, Rachel,” he said, and that night they slept back-to-back in Louis’s bed, and when she woke up shaking in the middle of the night, the Valium worn off, he calmed her with his hands and whispered in her ear that everything was okay, and she slept again.
