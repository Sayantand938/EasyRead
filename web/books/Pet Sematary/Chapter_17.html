Pet Sematary

Chapter 17

It takes the average person seven minutes to fall asleep, but according to Hand’s Human Physiology, it takes the same person fifteen to twenty minutes to wake up. It is as if sleep is a pool; getting out is harder than getting in. When someone wakes, they come up slowly, from deep sleep to light sleep to what is sometimes called “waking sleep,” a state where they can hear sounds and even answer questions without remembering it later… except maybe as bits of a dream.

Louis heard the click and rattle of bones, but slowly the sound became sharper, more metallic. There was a bang. A yell. More metallic sounds… something rolling? Sure, his wandering mind agreed. Roll dem bones.

He heard his daughter calling “Get it, Gage! Go get it!”

Then Gage crowed with joy, the sound that made Louis open his eyes and see the ceiling of his own bedroom.

He stayed perfectly still, waiting for the reality, the good reality, the blessed reality, to fully return.

All a dream. No matter how awful, how real, it had all been a dream. Just a fossil in the mind under his mind.

The metallic sound came again. It was one of Gage’s toy cars being rolled along the upstairs hall.

“Get it, Gage!”

“Get it!” Gage yelled. “Get it-get it-get it!”

Thumpa-thumpa-thumpa. Gage’s little bare feet pounded along the hallway runner. He and Ellie were giggling.

Louis looked to his right. Rachel’s side of the bed was empty, the covers thrown back. The sun was high up. He glanced at his watch and saw it was almost eight o’clock. Rachel had let him sleep in… probably on purpose.

Usually this would have bothered him, but not this morning. He took a deep breath and let it out, happy for now to lie here with a stripe of sunlight coming in through the window, feeling the clear reality of the real world. Dust danced in the sunlight.

Rachel called upstairs. “Come down for your snack and the bus, El!”

“Okay!” The louder tap-tap of her feet. “Here’s your car, Gage. I have to go to school.”

Gage began to yell, upset. Although it was unclear—the only clear words being Gage, car, get it, and Ellie-bus, his meaning was clear: Ellie should stay. School could wait for the day.

Rachel’s voice again, “Give your dad a shake before you come down, El.”

Ellie came in, her hair in a ponytail, wearing her red dress.

“I’m awake, babe,” he said. “Go get your bus.”

“Okay, Daddy.” She came over, kissed his slightly rough cheek, and ran for the stairs.

The dream was starting to blur, to make less sense. A very good thing, too.

“Gage!” he yelled. “Come give your dad a kiss!”

Gage didn't listen. He was hurrying after Ellie downstairs, shouting as loud as he could, “Get it! Get-it-get-it-GET-IT!” Louis just saw his strong little boy’s body, wearing only diapers and rubber pants.

Rachel called up again, “Louis, was that you? Are you awake?”

“Yeah,” he said, sitting up.

“I told you it was him!” Ellie called. “I’m leaving. Bye!” The front door slammed, and Gage’s angry yell finished the sentence.

“One egg or two?” Rachel called.

Louis pushed the blankets back and put his feet on the rough rug, ready to say he didn’t want eggs, just cereal, and he’d run… but he couldn't say the words.

His feet were covered in dirt and pine needles.

His heart jumped in his chest like a wild toy. Quickly, eyes wide, biting his tongue without feeling it, he kicked the covers off. The bottom of the bed was full of needles. The sheets were stained and dirty.

“Louis?”

He saw some stray pine needles on his knees, and then he looked at his right arm. There was a scratch there on his bicep, a new scratch, exactly where the dead branch had poked him… in the dream.

I’m going to scream. I can feel it.

And he could too; it was rising from inside, a big cold bullet of fear. Reality seemed to waver. Reality—the real reality, he thought—was those needles, the dirt on the sheets, the bloody scratch on his bare arm.

I’m going to scream and then I’ll go crazy and I won’t have to worry about it anymore—

“Louis?” Rachel was coming up the stairs. “Louis, did you go back to sleep?”

He fought to regain control in those few seconds; he fought hard, like he had in those moments of confusion after Pascow had been brought into the Medical Center, dying in a blanket. He won. The thing that helped him was knowing she couldn't see him like this, his feet muddy and covered with needles, the blankets on the floor showing the dirty ground sheet.

“I’m awake,” he called out, sounding cheerful. His tongue was bleeding from where he'd bitten it without meaning to. His mind raced, and deep down, away from everything else, he wondered if he had always been close to such madness; if everyone was.

“One egg or two?” She had stopped on the second or third step. Thank God.

“Two,” he said, hardly knowing what he was saying. “Scrambled.”

“Good for you,” she said, and went back downstairs again.

He closed his eyes briefly in relief, but in the darkness he saw Pascow’s silver eyes. His eyes flew open again. Louis began to move quickly, putting off any further thought. He jerked the bedclothes off the bed. The blankets were okay. He separated the two sheets, balled them up, took them into the hallway, and dumped them down the laundry chute.

Almost running, he entered the bathroom, jerked the shower handle on, and stepped under water so hot it was almost scalding, not caring. He washed the dirt from his feet and legs.

He began to feel better, more in control. Drying off, it struck him that this was how murderers must feel when they believe they have gotten rid of all the evidence. He began to laugh. He went on drying himself, but he also went on laughing. He couldn’t seem to stop.

“Hey, up there!” Rachel called. “What’s so funny?”

“Private joke,” Louis called back, still laughing. He was frightened, but the fright didn’t stop the laughter. The laughter came, rising from a belly that was as hard as stones stuck into a wall. It occurred to him that shoving the sheets down the laundry chute was absolutely the best thing he could have done. Missy Dandridge came in five days a week to vacuum, clean… and do the laundry. Rachel would never see those sheets at all until she put them back on the bed… clean. He supposed it was possible that Missy would mention it to Rachel, but he didn’t think so. She would probably whisper to her husband that the Creeds were playing some strange sex game that involved mud and pine needles instead of body paints.

This thought made Louis laugh all the harder.

The last of the giggles and chuckles dried up as he was dressing, and he realized that he felt a little better. How that could be he didn’t know, but he did. The room looked normal now except for the stripped bed. He had gotten rid of the poison. Maybe “evidence” was actually the word he was looking for, but in his mind it felt like poison.

Perhaps this is what people do with the inexplicable, he thought. This is what they do with the irrational that refuses to be broken down into the normal causes and effects that run the Western world. Maybe this was how your mind coped with the flying saucer you saw hovering silently over your back field one morning, casting its own tight little pool of shadow; the rain of frogs; the hand from under the bed that stroked your bare foot in the dead of night. There was a giggling fit or a crying fit… and since it was its own untouchable self and would not break down, you simply passed terror intact, like a kidney stone.

Gage was in his chair, eating Cocoa Bears and decorating the table with them. He was decorating the plastic mat under his high chair with Cocoa Bears and apparently shampooing with them.

Rachel came out of the kitchen with his eggs and a cup of coffee. “What was so funny, Lou? You were laughing like crazy up there. You scared me a little.”

Louis opened his mouth without knowing what he was going to say, and what came out was a joke he had heard the week before at the corner market down the road—something about a Jewish tailor who bought a parrot whose only line was “Ariel Sharon jerks off.”

By the time he finished, Rachel was laughing too—so was Gage for that matter.

Fine. Our hero has taken care of all the evidence—meaning: the muddy sheets and the crazy laughter in the bathroom. Our hero will now read the morning paper—or at least look at it—making the morning seem normal.

So thinking, Louis opened the paper.

That’s what you do, all right, he thought with great relief. You forget it like a stone, and that’s the end of it… unless there comes a campfire some night with friends when the wind is strong and the talk turns to strange events. Because on campfire nights when the wind is strong, talk is easy.

He ate his eggs. He kissed Rachel and Gage. He glanced at the square, white-painted laundry cabinet at the bottom of the chute only as he left. Everything was okay. It was another great morning. Late summer seemed like it would just go on forever, and everything was okay. He glanced at the path as he backed the car out of the garage, but that was okay too. Didn’t think twice. You forgot it like a stone.

Everything was okay until he had gotten ten miles down the road, and then the shakes hit him so hard that he had to pull off Route 2 and into the empty morning parking lot of Sing’s, the Chinese restaurant not far from the Eastern Maine Medical Center… where Pascow’s body would have been taken. The EMMC, that is, not Sing’s. Vic Pascow was never going to eat another helping of moo goo gai pan, ha-ha.

The shakes twisted his body, ripped at it, controlled it. Louis felt helpless and terrified—not terrified of anything supernatural, not in this bright sunshine, but simply terrified of the possibility that he might be losing his mind. It felt as if a long, invisible wire was being twisted through his head.

“No more,” he said. “Please, no more.”

He reached for the radio and found Joan Baez singing about diamonds and rust. Her clear, calm voice calmed him, and by the time she was done, Louis felt ready to drive on.

When he arrived at the Medical Center, he greeted Charlton and then slipped into the bathroom, thinking he must look awful. But he didn't. He was a little tired around the eyes, but even Rachel hadn't seen it. He splashed cold water on his face, dried it, combed his hair, and went to his office.

Steve Masterton and the Indian doctor, Surrendra Hardu, were inside, drinking coffee and still reviewing the first file.

“Morning, Lou,” Steve said.

“Morning.”

“Let’s hope it’s better than yesterday,” Hardu said.

“That’s right, you missed all the action.”

“Surrendra had his own excitement last night,” Masterton said, grinning. “Tell him, Surrendra.”

Hardu cleaned his glasses, smiling. “Two boys bring in their girlfriend around one in the morning,” he said. “She is very drunk and happy, celebrating being back at school. She has a bad cut on her thigh, and I tell her it will need at least four stitches, but won’t scar. Stitch away, she tells me, and so I do, bending over like this—”

Hardu showed them, bowing over an invisible thigh. Louis started to grin, guessing what was coming.

“And as I am stitching, she vomits on my head.”

Masterton laughed loudly. So did Louis. Hardu smiled calmly, as if this had happened to him countless times.

“Surrendra, how long have you been working?” Louis asked, when the laughter stopped.

“Since midnight,” Hardu said. “I’m just leaving. But I wanted to stay long enough to say hello again.”

“Well, hello,” Louis said, shaking his small, brown hand. “Now go home and sleep.”

“We’re almost done with the front cases,” Masterton said. “Say hallelujah, Surrendra.”

“I won’t,” Hardu said, smiling. “I’m not a Christian.”

“Then sing the chorus of ‘Instant Karma’ or something.”

“May you both shine on,” Hardu said, still smiling, and walked out the door.

Louis and Steve Masterton watched him go for a moment, quiet, and then looked at each other. They started laughing. To Louis, no laugh had ever felt so good… so normal.

“Good thing we finished the file,” Steve said. “Today’s the day the drug sellers start showing up.”

Louis nodded… The first of the drug salesmen would arrive at ten. As Steve liked to joke, Wednesday might be Prince Spaghetti Day, but at UMO every Tuesday was D-day. The D stood for Darvon, the most popular drug.

“Some advice, Boss,” Steve said. “I don’t know what these guys were like in Chicago, but around here they’ll do anything, from free hunting trips to the Allagash in November to free bowling at Family Fun Lanes in Bangor. One guy tried to give me an inflatable doll. Me! And I’m just a P.A.! If they can’t sell you drugs, they’ll make you need them.”

“You should have taken the doll.”

“Nah, she was a redhead. Not my type.”

“Well, I agree with Surrendra,” Louis said. “As long as it’s not like yesterday.”
