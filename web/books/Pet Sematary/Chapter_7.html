Pet Sematary

Chapter 7

The next two weeks were busy for the family. Little by little, Louis began to settle into his new job. He wondered what it would be like when ten thousand students arrived, many using drugs and alcohol, some with diseases, some worried about grades or homesick, a dozen or so—mostly girls—with eating disorders. He knew that when they all arrived on campus, it would be a different story. As Louis started to understand his job as head of University Medical Services, Rachel began to understand the house.

Gage was busy falling and bumping as he got used to his new surroundings. His sleep schedule was off for a while, but by the middle of their second week in Ludlow, he was sleeping through the night again. Only Ellie, about to start kindergarten in a new place, seemed constantly excited and on edge. She would suddenly giggle for a long time, become almost depressed, or throw tantrums over nothing. Rachel said Ellie would be fine once she realized school wasn't as scary as she imagined. Louis agreed. Most of the time, Ellie was her usual sweet self.

Louis started having a beer or two with Jud Crandall in the evenings. Around the time Gage began sleeping through the night, Louis began bringing over a six-pack every other night or so. He met Norma Crandall, a kind woman with rheumatoid arthritis—a terrible disease that ruins the later years of otherwise healthy people. But she had a positive attitude and refused to give in to the pain. Louis thought she might have another five to seven good years ahead of her, even if they weren't very comfortable.

Against his usual habits, he examined her himself and reviewed the prescriptions her doctor had given her, finding them to be correct. He was disappointed that he couldn't do or suggest anything else for her. Her doctor had things under control as much as possible, unless there was a sudden breakthrough, which was unlikely. He knew you had to accept things, or you would end up losing your mind.

Rachel liked Norma, and they became friends by sharing recipes, like boys trading baseball cards. Norma started with her apple pie recipe, and Rachel shared her beef stroganoff recipe. Norma liked both of the Creed children, especially Ellie, who she said would be "a real beauty." Later that night, Louis told Rachel that at least Norma hadn't said Ellie was going to grow into a troublemaker. Rachel laughed so hard she farted, and then they both laughed so long and loud that they woke up Gage in the next room.

The first day of kindergarten arrived. Louis, feeling confident in his job at the infirmary and the medical facilities, took the day off. (Besides, the infirmary was empty; the last patient, a summer student who had broken her leg, had been discharged a week before.) He stood on the lawn with Rachel and Gage as the school bus turned onto their street and stopped in front of their house. The doors opened, and the noise of children filled the air.

Ellie looked back at them, as if wondering if she could still avoid going. Perhaps seeing their expressions, she realized it was too late and that everything after this day was inevitable, like Norma Crandall's worsening arthritis. She turned and got on the bus. The doors closed, and the bus drove away. Rachel started crying.

"Don't," Louis said. He wasn't crying, but he was close. "It's only half a day."

"Half a day is bad enough," Rachel said, scolding him as she cried harder. Louis held her, and Gage put his arms around both of them. When Rachel cried, Gage usually cried too. But not this time. He has us to himself, Louis thought, and he knows it.

They waited with some worry for Ellie to return, drinking too much coffee and guessing how it was going for her. Louis went into the back room that would be his study and fiddled around, moving papers without really doing anything. Rachel started lunch way too early.

When the phone rang at ten fifteen, Rachel rushed to it and answered with a quick “Hello?” before it rang again. Louis stood in the doorway between his office and the kitchen, convinced it was Ellie’s teacher saying she couldn’t handle it; the school system had found her too difficult and was sending her back. But it was just Norma Crandall, saying Jud had picked the last of the corn and they could have a dozen ears if they wanted. Louis went over with a shopping bag and told Jud he should have let him help pick it.

“Most of it ain’t worth much anyway,” Jud said.

“Please don’t talk like that while I’m here,” Norma said. She came out on the porch with iced tea on an old Coca-Cola tray.

“Sorry, my Love.”

“He’s not sorry at all,” Norma said to Louis and sat down with a wince.

“Saw Ellie get on the bus,” Jud said, lighting a Chesterfield.

“She’ll be fine,” Norma said. “They usually are.”

Almost, Louis thought sadly.

But Ellie was fine. She came home at noon, smiling and happy, her blue first-day-of-school dress swaying around her scraped legs (with a new scrape on one knee to see), holding a picture of what looked like two kids or maybe two tall cranes, one shoe untied, one ribbon gone from her hair, shouting, “We sang ‘Old MacDonald’! Mommy! Daddy! We sang ‘Old MacDonald’! The same one as at Carstairs Street School!”

Rachel glanced over at Louis, who was sitting in the window seat with Gage on his lap. The baby was almost asleep. There was something sad in Rachel’s glance, and although she looked away quickly, Louis felt a moment of terrible panic. We’re really going to get old, he thought. It’s really true. No one’s going to make an exception for us. She’s on her way… and so are we.

Ellie ran over to him, trying to show him her picture, her new scrape, and tell him about “Old MacDonald” and Mrs. Berryman all at the same time. Church was twining in and out between her legs, purring loudly, and Ellie was somehow, almost miraculously, not tripping over him.

“Shh,” Louis said and kissed her. Gage had gone to sleep, unmindful of all the excitement. “Just let me put the baby to bed and then I’ll listen to everything.”

He took Gage up the stairs, walking through hot slanting September sunshine, and as he reached the landing, such a premonition of horror and darkness struck him that he stopped—stopped cold—and looked around in surprise, wondering what could possibly have come over him. He held the baby tighter, almost clutching him, and Gage stirred uncomfortably. Louis’s arms and back had broken out in great rashes of gooseflesh.

What’s wrong? he wondered, confused and frightened. His heart was racing; his scalp felt cool and suddenly too small to cover his skull; he could feel the surge of adrenaline behind his eyes. Human eyes really did bug out when fear was extreme, he knew; they did not just widen but actually bulged as blood pressure climbed and the fluid pressure in the head increased. What the hell is it? Ghosts? Christ, it really feels as if something just brushed by me in this hallway, something I almost saw.

Downstairs the screen door whacked against its frame.

Louis Creed jumped, almost screamed, and then laughed. It was simply one of those psychological cold pockets people Sometimes passed through—no more, no less. A momentary lapse. They happened; that was all. What had Scrooge said to the ghost of Jacob Marley? You may be no more than an underdone bit of potato. There’s more gravy than grave to you. And that was more correct—physically and mentally—than Charles Dickens had probably known. There were no ghosts, at least not in his experience. He had pronounced two dozen people dead in his career and had never once felt the passage of a soul.

He took Gage into his room and laid him in his crib. As he pulled the blanket up over his son, though, a shudder twisted up his back, and he thought suddenly of his Uncle Carl’s “showroom.” No new cars there, no televisions with all the modern features, no dishwashers with glass fronts so you could watch the magical sudsing action. Only boxes with their lids up, a carefully hidden spotlight over each. His father’s brother was an undertaker.

Good God, what gave you the horrors? Let it go! Dump it!

He kissed his son and went down to listen to Ellie tell about her first day at the big kid’s school.
