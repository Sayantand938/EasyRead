Pet Sematary

Chapter 27

Louis didn’t really know he was drunk until he was back in his own garage.

Outside there was starlight and a thin slice of moon. Not enough light to make a shadow, but enough to see by. Once he got in the garage, he was blind. There was a light switch somewhere, but he couldn’t remember where it was. He felt his way along slowly, shuffling his feet, his head spinning, expecting to painfully crack his knee or stumble over a toy, scaring himself with its crash, maybe even falling. Ellie’s little Schwinn with its red training wheels. Gage’s Crawly-Gator.

Where was the cat? Had he left him inside?

Somehow he got lost and ran into the wall. A splinter stuck in his palm and he cried out “Shit!” to the darkness, realizing it sounded more scared than angry. The whole garage seemed to have secretly turned. Now it wasn’t just the light switch; now he didn’t know where anything was, including the door into the kitchen.

He began walking again, moving slowly, his palm stinging. This is what it would be like to be blind, he thought, and that made him think of a Stevie Wonder concert he and Rachel had gone to—when? Six years ago? It seemed impossible, but it had to be. She had been pregnant with Ellie then. Two guys had led Wonder to his synthesizer, guiding him over the cables on the stage so he wouldn’t trip. And later, when he had gotten up to dance with one of the singers, she had carefully led him to an open space on the floor. He had danced well, Louis remembered thinking. He had danced well, but he needed a hand to lead him to the space where he could do it.

How about a hand right now to lead me to my kitchen door? he thought… and suddenly shivered.

If a hand came out of the darkness now to lead him, he would scream—scream and scream and scream.

He stood still, heart pounding. Come on, he told himself. Stop this, come on, come on—

Where was that damn cat?

Then he slammed into something, the station wagon's rear bumper, and pain shot up his body from his scraped shin, bringing tears to his eyes. He grabbed his leg and rubbed it, standing on one leg like a heron, but at least he knew where he was now, the garage's layout clear in his mind again, and besides, his night vision was returning, good old visual purple. He had left the cat inside, he remembered now, hadn’t really wanted to touch it, to pick it up and put it out and—

And that was when Church’s hot, furry body rubbed against his ankle like a small swirl of water, followed by its nasty tail, curling against his calf like a clutching snake, and then Louis screamed; he opened his mouth wide and screamed.
