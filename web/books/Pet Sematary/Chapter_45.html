Pet Sematary

Chapter 45

By the time United Airlines flight 419 landed at O’Hare Airport and let its passengers off at ten minutes past three, central standard time, Ellie Creed was close to panic, and Rachel was very scared.

If you lightly touched Ellie on the shoulder, she jumped and looked around at you with wide eyes, and her whole body shook constantly. It was as if she was charged with electricity. The nightmare on the airplane had been bad enough, but this… Rachel simply didn’t know what to do.

Going into the terminal, Ellie tripped and fell. She didn’t get up but just lay there on the carpet with people walking around her (or looking down at her with that slightly sympathetic but distant look of people who are traveling and don’t want to be bothered) until Rachel picked her up.

“Ellie, what’s wrong?” Rachel asked.

But Ellie wouldn’t answer. They walked across the lobby toward the luggage carousels, and Rachel saw her mother and father waiting there. She waved at them with her free hand, and they came over.

“They said we shouldn’t go to the gate and wait,” Dory said, “so we thought… Rachel? How’s Eileen?”

“Not good.”

“Is there a bathroom, Mommy? I’m going to throw up.”

“Oh, God,” Rachel said in despair and took her hand. There was a bathroom across the lobby, and she quickly led Ellie toward it.

“Rachel, should I come?” Dory called.

“No, get the luggage, you know what it looks like. We’re okay.”

Thankfully the ladies’ room was empty. Rachel led Ellie to one of the stalls, searching her purse for a dime, and then she saw—thank God—that the locks on three of them were broken. Over one broken lock, someone had written in grease pencil: SIR JOHN CRAPPER WAS A SEXIST PIG!

Rachel pulled the door open quickly; Ellie was now moaning and holding her stomach. She gagged twice, but nothing came up; it was just the dry heaves of total nervous exhaustion.

When Ellie said she felt a little better, Rachel took her over to the sinks and washed her daughter’s face. Ellie was terribly pale, and there were circles under her eyes.

“Ellie, what is wrong? Can’t you tell me?”

“I don’t know what’s wrong,” she said. “But I knew something was wrong ever since Daddy told me about the trip. Because something was wrong with him.”

Louis, what are you hiding? You were hiding something. I could see it; even Ellie could see it.

It struck her that she had also been nervous all day, as if waiting for something bad to happen. She felt the way she did in the two or three days before her period, tense and on edge, ready to laugh or cry or get a headache that would hit hard and then be gone three hours later.

“What?” she said now to Ellie’s reflection in the mirror. “Honey, what could be wrong with Daddy?”

“I don’t know,” Ellie said. “It was the dream. Something about Gage. Or maybe it was Church. I don’t remember. I don’t know.”

“Ellie, what was your dream?”

“I dreamed I was in the Pet Sematary,” Ellie said. “Paxcow took me to the Pet Sematary and said Daddy was going to go there and something terrible was going to happen.”

“Paxcow?” A jolt of terror, both sharp and unclear, struck her. What was that name, and why did it feel familiar? She felt she had heard that name—or one like it—but she couldn’t remember where. “You dreamed someone named Paxcow took you to the Pet Sematary?”

“Yes, that’s what he said his name was. And—” Her eyes suddenly widened.

“Do you remember something else?”

“He said that he was sent to warn but that he couldn’t interfere. He said that he was… I don’t know… that he was near Daddy because they were together when his soul was ta—ta—I can’t remember!” she cried.

“Honey,” Rachel said, “I think you dreamed about the Pet Sematary because you’re still thinking about Gage. And I’m sure Daddy is fine. Do you feel any better now?”

“No,” Ellie whispered. “Mommy, I’m scared. Aren’t you scared?”

“Huh-uh,” Rachel said, with a quick little shake of her head and a smile—but she was, she was scared; and that name, Paxcow, troubled her with its familiarity. She felt she had heard it in some awful situation months or even years ago, and that uneasy feeling would not leave her.

She felt something—something big, growing, and about to explode. Something awful that needed to be stopped. But what? What?

“I’m sure everything is okay,” she told Ellie. “Want to go back to Grandma and Grandda?”

“I guess so,” Ellie said without energy.

A Puerto Rican woman led her very young son into the ladies’ room, scolding him. A large wet stain had spread on the crotch of the little boy’s shorts, and Rachel was reminded of Gage with a kind of paralyzing sadness. This new sorrow was like novocaine, numbing her worries.

“Come on,” she said. “We’ll call Daddy from Grandda’s house.”

“He was wearing shorts,” Ellie said suddenly, looking back at the little boy.

“Who was, honey?”

“Paxcow,” Ellie said. “He was wearing red shorts in my dream.”

That brought the name into focus for a moment, and Rachel felt that knee-weakening fear again… then it disappeared.

They could not get close to the luggage carousel; Rachel could just see the top of her father’s hat, the one with the feather. Dory Goldman was holding two seats against the wall for them and waving. Rachel brought Ellie over.

“Are you feeling any better, dear?” Dory asked.

“A little bit,” Ellie said. “Mommy—”

She turned to Rachel and stopped. Rachel was sitting straight up, her hand covering her mouth, her face pale. She understood. It had suddenly hit her with a terrible thud. Of course, she should have known right away, but she had tried not to think about it. Of course.

“Mommy?”

Rachel turned slowly to her daughter, and Ellie could hear her neck creak. She took her hand away from her mouth.

“Did the man in your dream tell you his first name, Eileen?”

“Mommy, are you all—”

“Did the man in your dream tell you his first name?”

Dory was looking at her daughter and granddaughter as if they had both lost their minds.

“Yes, but I can’t remember… Mommy, you’re hurrrrting me—”

Rachel looked down and saw her free hand was gripping Ellie’s arm tightly.

“Was it Victor?”

Ellie gasped. “Yes, Victor! He said his name was Victor! Mommy, did you dream about him too?”

“Not Paxcow,” Rachel said. “Pascow.”

“That’s what I said. Paxcow.”

“Rachel, what’s wrong?” Dory said. She took Rachel’s free hand and felt how cold it was. “And what’s wrong with Eileen?”

“It’s not Eileen,” Rachel said. “It’s Louis, I think. Something is wrong with Louis. Or something will be wrong. Sit with Ellie, Mom. I want to call home.”

She got up and went to the telephones, searching her purse for a quarter. She called collect, but no one answered. The phone just kept ringing.

“Will you try your call later?” the operator asked her.

“Yes,” Rachel said and hung up.

She stood there, staring at the phone.

He said he was sent to warn, but he couldn’t step in. He said he was… he was near Daddy because they were together when his soul was dis—dis—I can’t remember!

“Discorporated,” Rachel whispered. Her fingers dug into her handbag. “Oh my God, was that the word?”

She tried to grab her thoughts, to sort them out. Was something happening, something more than just sadness over Gage’s death and this strange trip that felt like running away? How much did Ellie know about the young man who died on Louis’s first day at work?

Nothing, her mind replied firmly. You hid it from her, like you hid anything about death—even her cat possibly dying, remember that dumb argument in the pantry? You hid it. Because you were scared then, and you’re scared now. His name was Pascow, Victor Pascow, and how desperate is this now, Rachel? How bad is it? What in God’s name is going on?

Her hands shook so much she needed two tries to put the quarter in. She called the university infirmary and got Charlton, who seemed a bit confused but took the call. No, she hadn’t seen Louis and would be surprised if he came in today. She offered Rachel her sympathy again. Rachel accepted it and asked Charlton to have Louis call her at her parents’ house if he did come in. Yes, he had the number, she answered Charlton’s question, not wanting to tell the nurse (who probably knew anyway; Charlton didn’t miss much) that her parents’ house was across the country.

She hung up, feeling hot and shaky.

She heard Pascow’s name somewhere else, that’s all. You don’t raise a kid in a bubble, like a… hamster. She heard it on the radio. Or some kid told her at school, and she remembered it. Even that word she couldn’t say—if it was a mouthful like “discorporated” or “discorporeal,” so what? That proves nothing except that the subconscious is a sticky trap like they say.

She remembered a college psych teacher who said that if things were right, your memory could replay the names of everyone you’d met, every meal you ate, the weather every day of your life. He made a good case, saying the mind was a computer with tons of memory—not 16K, 32K, or 64K, but maybe a billion K: a thousand billion. How much could each of these “chips” hold? No one knew. But there were so many, he said, they didn’t need to be erased to be reused. The conscious mind had to dim some as protection against overload. “You might not remember where your socks are,” the teacher said, “if the whole Encyclopedia Britannica was in the next memory cells.”

The class laughed politely.

But this isn’t a psych class under good lights with all that easy jargon on the board and some know-it-all professor happily rambling through the last fifteen minutes. Something is terribly wrong here and you know it—you feel it. I don’t know what it has to do with Pascow, or Gage, or Church, but it has something to do with Louis. What? Is it—

Suddenly a thought as cold as ice struck her. She picked up the telephone again and felt in the coin slot for her dime. Was Louis thinking about suicide? Was that why he had gotten rid of them, almost pushed them out the door? Had Ellie somehow had a… a… oh, forget psychology! Had she had a psychic feeling of some sort?

This time she made the call collect to Jud Crandall. It rang five times… six… seven. She was about to hang up when his voice, breathless, answered. “H’lo?”

“Jud! Jud, this is—”

“Just a minute, ma’am,” the operator said. “Will you accept a collect call from Mrs. Louis Creed?”

“Ayuh,” Jud said.

“Pardon, sir, is that yes or no?”

“I guess I will,” Jud said.

There was a pause as the operator translated country talk into regular English. Then: “Thank you. Go ahead, ma’am.”

“Jud, have you seen Louis today?”

“Today? I don’t think so, Rachel. But I went to Brewer this morning for groceries. I was in the garden behind the house this afternoon. Why?”

“Oh, it’s probably nothing, but Ellie had a nightmare on the plane, and I thought I’d calm her down if I could.”

“Plane?” Jud’s voice seemed to get a bit sharper. “Where are you, Rachel?”

“Chicago,” she said. “Ellie and I came back to visit my parents.”

“Louis didn’t come with you?”

“He’ll be here by the end of the week,” Rachel said, trying to keep her voice steady. There was something in Jud’s voice she didn’t like.

“Did he want you to go there?”

“Well… yes. Jud, what’s wrong? Something is wrong, isn’t it? And you know what it is.”

“Maybe you should tell me the child’s dream,” Jud said after a long pause. “I’d like you to.”
