Pet Sematary

Chapter 30

Gage’s virus lasted a week, then disappeared. A week later, he got bronchitis. Ellie caught it too, and then Rachel; before Christmas, all three coughed like old, weak hunting dogs. Louis didn’t get sick, and Rachel seemed annoyed by this.

The last week of classes was busy for Louis, Steve, Surrendra, and Charlton. There was no flu—not yet—but lots of bronchitis and some cases of mononucleosis and mild pneumonia. Two days before Christmas break, their friends brought in six drunk, groaning fraternity boys. It was a confusing scene, like the Pascow incident. All six fools had piled onto one medium-sized toboggan (the sixth was sitting on the last guy’s shoulders, Louis figured out) and rode down the hill above the steam plant. Hilarious. But the toboggan went off course and hit a Civil War cannon. The result: two broken arms, a broken wrist, seven broken ribs, a concussion, and too many bruises to count. Only the boy on the shoulders escaped unharmed. He flew over the cannon and landed headfirst in the snow. Cleaning up the mess wasn’t fun, and Louis scolded the boys while stitching them up, but later, telling Rachel, he laughed until he cried. Rachel looked at him strangely, not understanding what was so funny. Louis couldn’t explain that it was a stupid accident, and people were hurt, but they’d be okay. His laughter was relief, but also a victory—I won one today, Louis.

The bronchitis in his family cleared up around December 16, when Ellie’s school closed for the holidays, and they settled in for a happy, old-fashioned Christmas. The house in North Ludlow, which had seemed strange when they arrived in August (even hostile, with Ellie cutting herself and Gage getting stung by a bee), now felt like home.

After the kids were asleep on Christmas Eve, Louis and Rachel snuck downstairs from the attic, carrying brightly colored boxes—Matchbox cars for Gage, who loved toy cars, Barbie and Ken dolls for Ellie, a Turn ‘n’ Go, a big trike, doll clothes, a toy oven with a light inside, and other things.

They sat together by the Christmas tree lights, arranging the gifts. Rachel wore silk pajamas, and Louis wore his robe. He couldn’t remember a nicer evening. A fire burned in the fireplace, and they added more wood now and then.

Winston Churchill brushed against Louis, who pushed the cat away, feeling a bit disgusted—that smell. Later, Church tried to lie next to Rachel, who also pushed it away, saying “Scat!” Louis then saw his wife rub her silk-covered thigh, like she’d touched something dirty. He didn’t think Rachel knew she was doing it.

Church walked to the brick hearth and collapsed in front of the fire. The cat had no grace now; it had lost it on that night Louis tried not to think about. And Church had lost something else. Louis knew it, but it took a month to figure out. The cat never purred anymore, even though it used to purr loudly, especially when sleeping. Louis had sometimes had to close Ellie’s door so he could sleep.

Now the cat slept like a stone. Like the dead.

No, he remembered, there was one exception. The night he woke up on the hide-a-bed with Church on his chest like a stinking blanket… Church had been purring that night. It had made some sound, anyway.

But as Jud Crandall had known—or guessed—it had not been all bad. Louis found a broken window in the basement behind the furnace, and when the glass guy fixed it, he had saved them some money in wasted heating oil. For pointing out the broken pane, which he might not have found for weeks—months, maybe—he figured he even owed Church a thank you.

Ellie no longer wanted Church to sleep with her, that was true, but sometimes when she was watching TV, she would let the cat jump onto her lap and fall asleep. But just as often, he thought, looking through the bag of plastic things that were supposed to hold Ellie’s Bat-Cycle together, she would push him off after a few minutes, saying, “Go away, Church, you stink.” She fed him regularly and with love, and even Gage would sometimes tug Church’s tail… more in a friendly way than a mean one, Louis was sure; he was like a tiny monk pulling a furry bell rope. When this happened, Church would slowly crawl under one of the radiators where Gage couldn’t get him.

We might have noticed more differences with a dog, Louis thought, but cats are such independent animals anyway. Independent and odd. Almost magical. It didn’t surprise him that the old Egyptian queens and pharaohs had wanted their cats mummified and put into their pyramid tombs with them to act as spirit guides in the next world. Cats were strange.

“How’s that Bat-Cycle coming, Chief?”

He held out the finished toy. “Here it is!”

Rachel pointed at the bag, which still had three or four plastic things in it. “What are those?”

“Extras,” Louis said, smiling like he was guilty.

“You better hope they’re extras. The kid will break her little neck.”

“That comes later,” Louis said, trying to sound mean. “When she’s twelve and showing off on her new skateboard.”

She groaned. “Come on, Doc, be nice!”

Louis stood up, put his hands on his lower back, and twisted. His spine popped. “That’s all the toys.”

“And they’re all together. Remember last year?” She giggled and Louis smiled. Last year it seemed like everything they'd gotten needed to be put together, and they'd been up until almost four o’clock Christmas morning, both of them ending up grumpy and annoyed. And by the afternoon of Christmas, Ellie had decided the boxes were more fun than the toys.

“Gross-OUT!” Louis said, copying Ellie.

“Well, come on to bed,” Rachel said, “and I’ll give you a present early.”

“Woman,” Louis said, standing as tall as he could, “that is mine by right.”

“Don’t you wish,” she said and laughed into her hands. In that moment she looked a lot like Ellie… and like Gage.

“Just a minute,” he said. “There’s one other thing I have to do.”

He hurried into the front hall closet and brought back one of his boots. He took the fire screen away from the fading fire.

“Louis, what are you—”

“You’ll see.”

On the left side of the fireplace, the fire was out, leaving a thick layer of soft, gray ashes. Louis pressed his boot into them, making a deep mark. Then he pressed the boot onto the bricks around the fireplace, using it like a big rubber stamp.

“There,” he said, after putting the boot back in the closet. “You like?”

Rachel giggled again. “Louis, Ellie’s going to lose it.”

In the last two weeks of school, Ellie had heard a worrying rumor in kindergarten: that Santa Claus was really parents. This idea was made stronger by a rather thin Santa at the Bangor Mall, whom Ellie had seen in the Deering Ice Cream Parlor a few days ago. Santa was sitting on a stool, his beard pulled aside so he could eat a cheeseburger. This bothered Ellie a lot (it seemed to be the cheeseburger, more than the fake beard), even though Rachel said that the department store and Salvation Army Santas were just “helpers.” The real Santa was too busy finishing his work and reading children’s last-minute letters up north to be traveling around the world on public relations trips.

Louis carefully put the fire screen back. Now there were two clear boot tracks in their fireplace, one in the ashes and one on the hearth. They both pointed toward the Christmas tree, as if Santa had landed on one foot and immediately stepped out to leave the presents for the Creed family. The trick was perfect unless you noticed they were both left feet… and Louis didn’t think Ellie would notice that.

“Louis Creed, I love you,” Rachel said and kissed him.

“You married a winner, baby,” Louis said, smiling honestly. “Stick with me, and I’ll make you a star.”

They headed for the stairs. He pointed to the card table Ellie had put in front of the TV. There were oatmeal cookies and two Ring-Dings on it, along with a can of Michelob. FOR YOU, SANTA, the note said in Ellie’s big, blocky handwriting. “You want a cookie or a Ring-Ding?”

“Ring-Ding,” she said and ate half of it. Louis opened the beer.

“A beer this late will give me heartburn,” he said.

“Crap,” she said cheerfully. “Come on, Doc.”

Louis put down his beer and grabbed his robe pocket like he’d forgotten something—even though he'd known that small weight was there all night.

“Here,” he said. “For you. Open it now. It’s after midnight. Merry Christmas, babe.”

She turned the small box over in her hands. It was wrapped in silver paper and tied with wide, satiny blue ribbon. “Louis, what is it?”

He shrugged. “Soap. Shampoo sample. I don’t remember exactly.”

She opened it on the stairs, saw the Tiffany box, and squealed. She took out the cotton and just stood there, her mouth slightly open.

“Well?” he asked, worried. He’d never bought her real jewelry before, and he was nervous. “Do you like it?”

She took it out, laid the delicate gold chain over her fingers, and held the small sapphire to the hall light. It spun gently, seeming to send out cool blue light.

“Oh Louis, it’s so beautiful—” He saw she was crying a little and felt both moved and worried.

“Hey, babe, don’t do that,” he said. “Put it on.”

“Louis, we can’t afford—you can’t afford—”

“Shhh,” he said. “I saved some money here and there since last Christmas… but it wasn’t as much as you might think.”

“How much was it?”

“I’ll never tell you that, Rachel,” he said seriously. “No one could get it out of me, not even if they tortured me. Two thousand dollars.”

“Two thousand—!” She hugged him so suddenly and tightly that he almost fell down the stairs. “Louis, you’re crazy!”

“Put it on,” he said again.

She did. He helped her with the clasp, and then she turned around to look at him. “I want to go upstairs and look at it,” she said. “I think I want to admire myself.”

“Admire away,” he said. “I’ll let the cat out and turn on the lights.”

“When we succeed,” she said, looking straight into his eyes, “I want to take everything off except this.”

“Hurry up and admire, then,” Louis said, and she laughed.

He grabbed Church and held him over his arm—he didn’t use the broom much anymore. He figured that, despite everything, he had almost gotten used to the cat again. He went to the front door, turning off lights as he went. When he opened the door between the kitchen and garage, a rush of cold air blew around his ankles.

“Merry Christmas, Ch—”

He stopped. On the welcome mat was a dead crow. Its head was crushed. One wing was torn off and lay behind the body like a burnt piece of paper. Church immediately wiggled out of Louis’s arms and began to eagerly sniff the frozen body. As Louis watched, the cat’s head shot forward, its ears flat, and before he could look away, Church had ripped out one of the crow’s cloudy, blank eyes.

Church strikes again, Louis thought, feeling a bit sick, and turned his head—but not before he had seen the bloody, empty hole where the crow’s eye had been. Shouldn’t bother me, shouldn’t, I’ve seen worse, oh yeah, Pascow, for instance, Pascow was worse, a lot worse—

But it did bother him. His stomach flipped. The warm feeling of excitement had suddenly vanished. Christ, that bird’s almost as big as he is. Must have caught it by surprise. Completely by surprise.

This would have to be cleaned up. Nobody needed this kind of present on Christmas morning. And it was his job, wasn’t it? Of course. His and nobody else’s. He had known that in a hidden way even on the evening his family came home, when he had purposely driven over the torn-up body of the mouse Church had killed.

The ground of a man’s heart is harder, Louis.

This thought was so clear, somehow so real and loud, that Louis jumped a little, as if Jud had appeared next to him and spoken aloud.

A man grows what he can… and takes care of it.

Church was still hunched over the dead bird, eating greedily. He was working on the other wing now. There was a dark rustling sound as Church pulled it back and forth, back and forth. Never get it off the ground, Orville. That’s right, Wilbur, fucking bird’s just as dead as dogshit, might as well feed it to the cat, might as well—

Louis suddenly kicked Church, kicked him hard. The cat's rear end rose and landed awkwardly. It walked away, giving him another of its ugly yellow-green looks. "Eat me," Louis hissed at it, acting like a cat himself.

"Louis?" Rachel's voice came softly from their bedroom. "Coming to bed?"

"Be right there," he called back. I've just got this little mess to clean up, Rachel, okay? Because it's my mess. He felt around for the switch for the garage light. He quickly went back to the cupboard under the kitchen sink and got a green Hefty Bag. He took the bag back into the garage and took the shovel from its nail on the wall. He scraped up the crow and dropped it into the bag. Then he shoveled up the severed wing and slipped that in. He tied a knot in the top of the bag and dropped it into the bin on the far side of the Civic. By the time he had finished, his ankles were growing numb.

Church was standing by the garage doorway. Louis made a threatening gesture at the cat with the shovel, and it disappeared like black water.

Upstairs, Rachel was lying on her bed, wearing only the sapphire on its chain… as promised. She smiled at him lazily. "What took you so long, Chief?"

"The light over the sink was out," Louis said. "I changed the bulb."

"Come here," she said and gently tugged him toward her. Not by the hand. "He knows if you've been sleeping," she sang softly; a little smile curved up the corners of her lips. "He knows if you're awake… oh my, Louis dear, what's this?"

"Something that just woke up, I think," Louis said, slipping off his robe. "Maybe we ought to see if we can get it to sleep before Santa comes, what do you think?"

She rose on one elbow; he felt her breath, warm and sweet.

"He knows if you've been bad or good… so be good… for goodness sake… Have you been a good boy, Louis?"

“I think so,” he said. His voice trembled a bit.

“Let’s see if you taste as good as you look,” she said.

The sex was good, but Louis didn’t just drift away afterward like he usually did when the sex was good—drifting away easily with himself, his wife, his life. He lay in the darkness of Christmas morning, listening to Rachel’s slow, deep breathing, and he thought about the dead bird on the doorstep—Church’s Christmas present to him.

Keep me in mind, Dr. Creed. I was alive, then I was dead, and now I’m alive again. I’ve been around the track, and I’m here to tell you that you come out the other side with your purr gone and a hunger for the hunt. I’m here to tell you that a man grows what he can and takes care of it. Don’t forget that, Dr. Creed. I’m part of what your heart will grow now. There’s your wife and your daughter and your son… and there’s me. Remember the secret and take care of your garden.

At some point, Louis slept.
