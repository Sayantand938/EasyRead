Pet Sematary

Chapter 41

Gage was buried at two o’clock that afternoon. By then the rain had stopped. Scattered clouds still moved overhead, and most of the people who came to mourn carried black umbrellas the funeral home provided.

At Rachel’s request, the funeral director, who led the short, simple service by the grave, read the part from Matthew that starts with “Let the little children come to me.” Louis, standing on one side of the grave, looked across at his father-in-law. For a moment Goldman looked back at him, and then he looked down. He wasn't going to argue today. The bags under his eyes looked huge, and around his black silk cap, fine, white hair blew in the breeze. With his gray and black beard on his cheeks, he looked more like a homeless man than ever. He seemed like a man who didn't really know where he was. Louis tried, but still couldn't feel sorry for him.

Gage’s small white coffin, its latch fixed, sat on shiny metal bars over the grave. The edges of the grave were covered with bright green fake grass that hurt Louis’s eyes. Several baskets of flowers sat on top of this fake and cheerful surface. Louis looked past the funeral director. There was a low hill, covered with graves, family plots, and one tall monument with the name PHIPPS on it. Just above the sloping top of PHIPPS, he could see a bit of yellow. Louis looked at this, thinking about it. He kept looking at it even after the funeral director said, “Let us bow our heads for a moment of quiet prayer.” It took Louis a few minutes, but he understood. It was a digger. A digger parked over the hill where the mourners wouldn’t have to see it. And, when the funeral was over, the worker would crush his cigarette with his workboot, put it in his pocket (workers at the cemetery were usually fired for leaving their cigarettes on the ground—it looked bad because many people died of lung cancer), jump in the digger, start it up, and cut his son off from the sun forever… or at least until the day of the Resurrection.

Resurrection… ah, that’s a word

(that you should forget about and you know it).

When the funeral director said “Amen,” Louis took Rachel’s arm and led her away. Rachel protested a little—she wanted to stay longer, please, Louis—but Louis insisted. They went to the cars. He saw the funeral director taking umbrellas with the home’s name on the handles from the mourners and giving them to an assistant. The assistant put them in an umbrella stand that looked strange on the wet grass. He held Rachel’s arm with his right hand and Ellie’s white-gloved hand with his left. Ellie was wearing the same dress she had worn to Norma Crandall’s funeral.

Jud came over as Louis helped his family into the car. Jud also looked like he’d had a hard night.

“You okay, Louis?”

Louis nodded.

Jud bent to look into the car. “How are you, Rachel?” he asked.

“I’m all right, Jud,” she whispered.

Jud touched her shoulder gently and then looked at Ellie. How about you, dear one?”

“I’m fine,” Ellie said and gave him a big, fake smile to show him how fine she was.

“What’s that picture you got there?”

For a moment Louis thought she would keep it to herself, but then shyly she passed it to Jud. He held it in his big fingers, fingers that looked clumsy and fit for working on big trucks or trains—but they were also the fingers that had pulled a bee stinger from Gage’s neck like a magician… or a surgeon.

“Why, that’s real nice,” Jud said. “You pulling him on a sled. Bet he liked that, didn’t he, Ellie?”

Ellie nodded, starting to cry.

Rachel started to say something, but Louis squeezed her arm—be still awhile.

“I used to pull him a lot,” Ellie said, crying, “and he’d laugh and laugh. Then we’d go in and Mommy would make us cocoa and say, ‘Put your boots away,’ and Gage would grab them all up and scream ‘Boots! Boots!’ so loud it hurt your ears. Remember that, Mom?”

Rachel nodded.

“Yeah, I bet that was fun,” Jud said, handing the picture back. “And he may be dead now, Ellie, but you can keep your memories of him.”

“I’m going to,” she said, wiping her face. “I loved Gage, Mr. Crandall.”

“I know you did, dear.” He leaned in and kissed her, and when he pulled back, his eyes looked at Louis and Rachel coldly. Rachel looked back, confused and a little hurt, not understanding. But Louis understood: What are you doing for her? Jud’s eyes asked. Your son is dead, but your daughter is not. What are you doing for her?

Louis looked away. There was nothing he could do for her, not yet. She would have to deal with her grief as best she could. He was thinking too much about his son.
