Pet Sematary

Chapter 28

“Daddy!” Ellie screamed.

She ran up the jetway toward him, dodging passengers like a quarterback running with the ball. Most people stepped aside, smiling. Louis felt a bit awkward at her enthusiasm, but he couldn’t help but grin widely himself.

Rachel held Gage, and he saw Louis when Ellie yelled. “Dayeee!” he shouted excitedly and started to squirm in Rachel’s arms. She smiled, a bit tiredly, Louis thought, and put him down. He ran after Ellie, legs moving fast. “Dayeee! Dayeee!”

Louis noticed Gage wore a new jumper—another of Grandda's creations, it seemed. Then Ellie crashed into him and climbed him like a tree.

“Hi, Daddy!” she shouted and kissed his cheek hard.

“Hi, hon,” he said, bending to pick up Gage. He held him close and hugged them both. “I’m glad you’re back.”

Rachel approached, carrying her travel bag and purse over one arm, and Gage’s diaper bag over the other. I’LL BE A BIG BOY SOON was printed on the bag, likely more to encourage the parents than the baby. She looked like a photographer after a long, hard job.

Louis leaned in and kissed her. “Hi.”

“Hi, Doc,” she said, and smiled.

“You look exhausted.”

“I am exhausted. We got as far as Boston without a problem. We changed planes without a problem. We took off without a problem. But as the plane is turning over the city, Gage looks down and says, ‘Pretty, pretty,’ and then throws up all over himself.”

“Oh, Jesus.”

“I got him changed in the bathroom,” she said. “I don’t think it’s a bug or anything. He was just airsick.”

“Come on home,” Louis said. “I’ve got chili on the stove.”

“Chili! Chili!” Ellie screamed in Louis’s ear, filled with delight and excitement.

“Chiwwi! Chiwwi!” Gage screamed in Louis’s other ear, which at least made the ringing even.

“Come on,” Louis said. “Let’s get your suitcases and get out of here.

“Daddy, how’s Church?” Ellie asked as he set her down. It was a question Louis had expected, but not Ellie’s worried face, and the deep worry line that showed up between her dark blue eyes. Louis frowned and then looked at Rachel.

“She woke up screaming over the weekend,” Rachel said quietly. “She had a nightmare.”

“I dreamed that Church got run over,” Ellie said.

“Too many turkey sandwiches after the big day, I bet,” Rachel said. “She also had diarrhea. Calm down, Louis, and let’s leave this airport. I’ve seen enough airports in the last week to last me five years.”

“Church is fine, honey,” Louis said slowly.

Yes, he’s fine. He lies around the house all day and looks at me with those strange, dull eyes—as if he’d seen something that had destroyed most of a cat's intelligence. He’s just great. I put him out with a broom at night because I don’t like to touch him. I just sweep at him and he goes. And the other day when I opened the door, Ellie, he had a mouse—or what was left of it. He’d scattered the guts everywhere. And speaking of breakfast, I skipped mine that morning. Otherwise—

“He’s just fine.”

“Oh,” Ellie said, and the worry line between her eyes disappeared. “Oh, that’s good. When I had that dream, I was sure he was dead.”

“Were you?” Louis asked, and smiled. “Dreams are funny, aren’t they?”

“Dweems!” Gage hollered—he was at the parrot stage that Louis remembered from Ellie’s childhood. “Dweeeeeems!” He tugged Louis’s hair hard.

“Come on, gang,” Louis said, and they started down to the baggage area.

They had reached the station wagon in the parking lot when Gage began saying “Pretty, pretty,” in a strange, hiccupping voice. This time he threw up all over Louis, who had put on new pants for meeting them at the airport. Apparently Gage thought pretty meant I’m going to throw up now, sorry.

It turned out it was just a virus.

By the time they drove the seventeen miles from the Bangor airport to their house in Ludlow, Gage was showing signs of fever and had drifted into an uneasy sleep. Louis backed into the garage and saw Church out of the corner of his eye, tail up, strange eyes fixed on the car. It disappeared into the fading daylight, and a moment later Louis saw a mouse lying beside a stack of four summer tires—he had had the snow tires put on while Rachel and the kids were gone. The mouse’s insides looked pink and raw in the dim garage.

Louis got out quickly and purposely bumped the tires, stacked like black checkers. The top two fell over and covered the mouse. “Ooops,” he said.

“You’re clumsy, Daddy,” Ellie said, not unkindly.

“That’s right,” Louis said with a kind of forced cheerfulness. He felt like saying Pretty, pretty and throwing up. “Daddy’s clumsy.” He remembered Church killing only one rat before his strange return; he sometimes cornered mice and played with them in a deadly way that ended in killing them, but he or Ellie or Rachel had always stopped it. And once cats were fixed, he knew, few would do more than stare at a mouse, especially if they were well-fed.

“Are you going to stand there dreaming or help me with this kid?” Rachel asked. “Come back to Earth, Dr. Creed. I need you.” She sounded tired and annoyed.

“I’m sorry, babe,” Louis said. He went to get Gage, who was as hot as the coals in a warm stove.

So only the three of them ate Louis’s famous South Side Chili that night; Gage lay on the living room sofa, feverish and listless, drinking lukewarm chicken broth and watching cartoons.

After dinner Ellie went to the garage door and called Church. Louis, doing the dishes while Rachel unpacked upstairs, hoped the cat wouldn’t come, but it did—it walked in its new slow way, almost at once, as if it—as if it—had been waiting out there. Waiting. The word came to him.

“Church!” Ellie cried. “Hi, Church!” She picked up the cat and hugged it. Louis watched, his hands still as he searched the bottom of the sink for silverware. He saw Ellie’s happy face slowly change to confusion. The cat lay still in her arms, its ears back, its eyes on hers.

After a long moment—it seemed very long to Louis—she put Church down. The cat walked away toward the dining room without looking back. Killer of small mice, Louis thought randomly. God, what did we do that night?

He tried to remember, but it already seemed far away, like the awful death of Victor Pascow on the floor of the infirmary’s waiting room. He could remember gusts of wind moving in the sky and the white shine of snow in the field behind the house that went up to the woods. That was all.

“Daddy?” Ellie said quietly.

‘What, Ellie?”

“Church smells weird.”

“Does he?” Louis asked, his voice calm.

“Yes!” Ellie said, upset. “Yes, he does! He never smelled weird before! He smells like… he smells like poo!”

“Well, maybe he rolled in something bad, honey,” Louis said. “Whatever that bad smell is, he’ll get rid of it.”

“I really hope so,” Ellie said in a funny old lady’s voice. She walked off.

Louis found the last fork, washed it, and pulled the plug. He stood at the sink, looking out into the night while the soapy water went down the drain with a loud gurgling sound.

When the drain stopped gurgling, he heard the wind outside, a high, wild sound from the north, bringing winter. He realized he was afraid, just simply, dumbly afraid, like when a cloud suddenly covers the sun and you hear a strange ticking sound you can't place.

“A hundred and three?” Rachel asked. “Jesus, Lou! Are you sure?”

“It’s a virus,” Louis said. He tried not to let Rachel’s voice, which sounded almost like blame, bother him. She was tired. It had been a long day for her; she had traveled across the country with her kids. It was eleven o’clock, and the day wasn’t over. Ellie was sound asleep in her room. Gage was on their bed, barely awake. Louis had given him Liquiprin an hour ago. “The aspirin will lower his fever by morning, hon.”

“Aren’t you going to give him ampicillin or something?”

Patiently, Louis said, “If he had the flu or strep throat, I would. He doesn’t. He’s got a virus, and that stuff doesn’t do anything for viruses. It would just give him diarrhea and make him more dehydrated.”

“Are you sure it’s a virus?”

“Well, if you want another doctor’s opinion,” Louis snapped, “go ahead.”

“You don’t have to yell at me!” Rachel shouted.

“I wasn’t shouting!” Louis shouted back.

“You were,” Rachel began, “you were sh-sh-shouting—” And then her mouth trembled, and she put a hand to her face. Louis saw the dark circles under her eyes and felt ashamed.

“I’m sorry,” he said, and sat down beside her. “God, I don’t know what’s wrong with me. I apologize, Rachel.”

“Never complain, never explain,” she said, smiling weakly. “Isn’t that what you told me once? The trip was awful. And I’ve been afraid you’d be furious when you looked in Gage’s dresser drawers. I guess maybe I should tell you now, while you’re feeling sorry for me.”

“What’s there to be furious about?”

She smiled weakly. “My mother and father bought him ten new outfits. He was wearing one of them today.”

“I noticed he had on something new,” he said curtly.

“I noticed you noticing,” she replied and made a funny face that made him laugh, although he didn’t feel much like laughing. “And six new dresses for Ellie.”

“Six dresses!” he said, fighting the urge to yell. He was suddenly furious—miserably furious and hurt in a way he couldn’t explain. “Rachel, why? Why did you let him do that? We don’t need… we can buy…”

He stopped. His rage had made him unable to speak clearly, and for a moment he saw himself carrying Ellie’s dead cat through the woods, shifting the plastic bag from one hand to the other… and all the while Irwin Goldman, that dirty old bastard from Lake Forest, had been busy trying to buy his daughter’s affection by using his famous checkbook and his famous fountain pen.

For one moment he felt like shouting He bought her six dresses and I brought her goddam cat back from the dead, so who loves her more?

He stopped himself. He would never say anything like that. Never.

She touched his neck gently. “Louis,” she said. “It was both of them together. Please try to understand. Please. They love the children, and they don’t see them often. And they’re getting old. Louis, you’d hardly know my father. Really.”

“I’d know him,” Louis muttered.

“Please, honey. Try to understand. Try to be nice. It doesn’t bother you.”

He looked at her for a long time. “It does though,” he said finally. “Maybe it shouldn’t, but it does.”

She opened her mouth to reply, and then Ellie called out from her room: “Daddy! Mommy! Somebody!”

Rachel started to get up, and Louis pulled her back down. “Stay with Gage. I’ll go.” He thought he knew what the trouble was. But he had put the cat out, damn it; after Ellie had gone to bed, he had caught it in the kitchen sniffing around its dish and had put it out. He didn’t want the cat sleeping with her. Not anymore. Strange thoughts of disease, mixed with memories of Uncle Carl’s funeral home, had come to him when he thought of Church sleeping on Ellie’s bed.

She’s going to know that something’s wrong and Church was better before.

He had put the cat out, but when he went in, Ellie was sitting up in bed, more asleep than awake, and Church was spread out on the bedspread, like a dark shadow. The cat’s eyes were open and gleaming stupidly in the light from the hall.

“Daddy, put him out,” Ellie almost groaned. “He smells so bad.”

“Shhh, Ellie, go to sleep,” Louis said, surprised by how calm he sounded. It made him think of the morning after he walked in his sleep, the day after Pascow had died. Going to the infirmary and ducking into the bathroom to look at himself in the mirror, sure that he must look terrible. But he had looked pretty much okay. It made you wonder how many people were walking around with awful secrets hidden inside.

It’s not a secret, goddammit! It’s just the cat!

But Ellie was right. It stank really bad.

He took the cat out of her room and carried it downstairs, trying to breathe through his mouth. There were worse smells; shit was worse, if you wanted to be perfectly honest. A month ago they’d had trouble with the septic tank, and as Jud had said when he came over to watch Puffer and Sons pump the tank, “That ain’t Chanel Number Five, is it, Louis?” The smell of a gangrenous wound—what old Doctor Bracermunn at med school had called “hot flesh”—was worse too. Even the smell which came from the Civic’s catalytic converter when it had been idling in the garage for a while was worse.

But this smell was pretty damn bad. And how had the cat gotten in, anyway? He had put it out earlier, sweeping it out with the broom while all three of them—his people—were upstairs. This was the first time he had actually held the cat since the day it had come back, almost a week ago. It lay hotly in his arms, like a sleeping sickness, and Louis wondered, What hiding place did you find, you bastard?

He thought suddenly of his dream that other night—Pascow simply walking through the door between the kitchen and the garage.

Maybe there was no hiding place. Maybe it had just walked through the door, like a ghost.

“Forget that,” he whispered aloud, and his voice was slightly hoarse.

Louis became suddenly sure that the cat would begin to struggle in his arms, that it would scratch him. But Church lay totally still, giving off that stupid heat and that dirty stink, looking at Louis’s face as if it could read the thoughts going on behind Louis’s eyes.

He opened the door and tossed the cat out into the garage, maybe a little too hard. “Go on,” he said. “Kill another mouse or something.”

Church landed awkwardly, its hindquarters bunching beneath it and briefly collapsing. It seemed to shoot Louis a look of green, ugly hate. Then it strolled drunkenly off and was gone.

Jesus, Jud, he thought, I wish you hadn’t said anything.

He went to the sink and washed his hands and arms hard, like he was scrubbing for surgery. You do it because it grabs you… you come up with reasons… they seem like good reasons… but mostly you do it because once you’ve been up there, it’s your place, and you belong there… and you think up the best reasons in the world…

No, he couldn’t blame Jud. He had gone on his own and he couldn’t blame Jud.

He turned off the water and started to dry his hands and arms. Suddenly he stopped and stared straight ahead, looking out at the little bit of night in the window over the sink.

Does that mean it’s my place now? That it’s mine too?

No. Not if I don’t want it to be.

He threw the towel over the rack and went upstairs.

Rachel was in bed, the covers pulled up to her chin, and Gage was tucked in neatly beside her. She looked at Louis like she was sorry. “Would you mind, hon? Just for tonight? I’d feel better having him with me. He’s so warm.”

“No,” Louis said. “That’s fine. I’ll get the pull-out bed downstairs ready.”

“You really don’t mind?”

“No. It won’t hurt Gage, and it’ll make you feel better.” He paused, then smiled. “You’re going to catch his bug, though. That’s almost certain. I guess that doesn’t change your mind, does it?”

She smiled back and shook her head. “What was Ellie complaining about?”

“Church. She wanted me to get rid of Church.”

“Ellie wanted Church gone? That’s different.”

“Yeah, it is,” Louis agreed and then added, “She said he smelled bad, and I thought he was a little stinky. Maybe he rolled in some dirt, or something.”

“That’s too bad,” Rachel said, turning onto her side. “I really think Ellie missed Church as much as she missed you.”

“Uh-huh,” Louis said. He bent and kissed her mouth softly. “Go to sleep, Rachel.”

“I love you, Lou. I’m glad to be home. And I’m sorry about the couch.”

“It’s okay,” Louis said, and turned out the light.

Downstairs, he stacked the couch cushions, pulled out the hide-a-bed, and tried to get ready in his mind for a night of feeling the bar under the thin mattress dig into his back. The bed had sheets on it, at least; he wouldn’t have to make it from the beginning. Louis got two blankets from the top shelf in the front hall closet and spread them on the bed. He started to undress, then stopped.

You think going to church is popular again? Fine. Take a look around. Like you told Rachel, it won’t hurt. It might even help. And checking the doors won’t give you a virus.

He walked around the whole downstairs on purpose, checking the locks on doors and windows. He had done everything right the first time, and Church was nowhere to be seen.

“There,” he said. “Let’s see you get in tonight, you dumb cat.” He then hoped Church would freeze his balls off. But Church didn’t have any anymore, of course.

He turned off the lights and got into bed. The bar started pressing into his back right away, and Louis thought he’d be awake half the night when he fell asleep. He fell asleep uncomfortably on his side in the hide-a-bed, but when he woke up he was...

… in the burial ground past the Pet Sematary again. This time he was alone. He had killed Church himself this time and then decided to bring him back to life a second time for some reason. God knew why; Louis didn’t. He had buried Church deeper this time, though, and Church couldn’t dig his way out. Louis could hear the cat crying under the earth, sounding like a weeping child. The sound came up through the ground, through its stony skin—the sound and the smell, that awful sickish-sweet smell of rot. Just breathing it made his chest feel heavy, like something was pressing on it.

The crying… the crying...

… the crying was still going on....

...and the weight was still on his chest.

“Louis!” It was Rachel, and she sounded worried. “Louis, can you come?”

She sounded more than worried; she sounded scared, and the crying was choked and desperate. It was Gage.

He opened his eyes and stared into Church’s greenish-yellow eyes. They were less than four inches from his own. The cat was on his chest, neatly curled up there like something from an old wives’ tale about stealing breath. The stink came off it in slow, sickening waves. It was purring.

Louis cried out in disgust and surprise. He shot both hands out as if to push something away. Church thumped off the bed, landed on its side, and walked away with that stumbling sway.

Jesus! Jesus! It was on me! Oh God, it was right on me!

He couldn't have been more disgusted if he had woken up with a spider in his mouth. For a moment he thought he would throw up.

“Louis!”

He pushed the blankets back and stumbled to the stairs. Faint light came from their bedroom. Rachel was standing at the top of the stairs in her nightgown.

“Louis, he’s vomiting again… choking on it… I’m scared.”

“I’m here,” he said and came up to her, thinking: It got in. Somehow it got in. From the cellar, probably. Maybe there’s a broken cellar window. In fact there must be a broken window down there. I’ll check it tomorrow when I get home. Hell, before I go to work. I’ll—

Gage stopped crying and began to make an ugly, gargling choking sound.

“Louis!” Rachel screamed.

Louis moved fast. Gage was on his side, and vomit was leaking from his mouth onto an old towel Rachel had put down. He was vomiting, but not enough. Most of it was inside, and Gage was turning red as he started to suffocate.

Louis grabbed the boy under the arms, noticing how hot his son’s armpits were under the Dr. Denton suit. He put him on his shoulder as if to burp him. Then Louis snapped himself backward, jerking Gage with him. Gage’s neck snapped. He made a loud bark that was almost a burp, and a huge stream of vomit flew from his mouth, splattering on the floor and the dresser. Gage began to cry again, a strong, wailing sound that was music to Louis’s ears. To cry like that, you had to be getting plenty of air.

Rachel’s knees gave way, and she collapsed onto the bed, holding her head in her hands. She was shaking hard. “He almost died, didn’t he, Louis? He almost d-d-d-—oh my God—”

Louis walked around the room with his son in his arms. Gage’s cries were fading to whimpers; he was almost asleep again.

“He probably would have cleared it himself, Rachel. I just helped him a little.”

“But he was close,” she said. She looked up at him, her eyes wide and shocked. “Louis, he was so close.”

He suddenly remembered her shouting at him in the sunny kitchen: He’s not going to die, no one is going to die around here.

“Honey,” Louis said, “we’re all close. All the time.”

It was probably the milk that had caused him to vomit again. Gage had woken up around midnight, she said, about an hour after Louis had gone to sleep, with his “hungry cry,” and Rachel had given him a bottle. She had dozed off again while he was still drinking it. About an hour later, he started choking.

No more milk, Louis said, and Rachel agreed, almost meekly. No more milk.

Louis got back downstairs around 1:15 and spent fifteen minutes looking for the cat. While searching, he found the door between the kitchen and basement slightly open, as he thought he would. He remembered his mother telling him about a cat that was good at opening old latches, like the one on their cellar door. She'd said the cat would climb the door's edge and tap the latch until the door opened. Louis thought it was a cute trick, but he didn't want Church doing it often. After all, the cellar door had a lock too. He found Church sleeping under the stove and tossed it out the front door quickly. On his way back to the hide-a-bed, he closed the cellar door again.

And this time locked it.
