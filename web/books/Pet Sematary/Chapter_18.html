Pet Sematary

Chapter 18

When the Upjohn salesman didn’t show up at ten, Louis gave in and called the registrar. He spoke to a Mrs. Stapleton, who said she’d send over Victor Pascow’s records right away. As soon as Louis hung up, the Upjohn guy arrived. He didn’t try to sell Louis anything, just asked if he wanted to buy discounted season tickets to the New England Patriots’ games.

“Nope,” Louis said.

“I didn’t think so,” the Upjohn guy said sadly, and left.

At noon, Louis went to the Bear’s Den and got a tuna sandwich and a Coke. He took them back to his office and ate lunch while looking over Pascow’s records. He was searching for a link to himself or to North Ludlow, where the Pet Sematary was… a feeling, he guessed, that there had to be a logical reason even for something as strange as this. Maybe the guy had grown up in Ludlow—had, maybe, even buried a dog or cat there.

He didn’t find the link he wanted. Pascow was from Bergenfield, New Jersey, and had come to UMO to study electrical engineering. In those few typed pages, Louis couldn’t see any connection between himself and the young man who had died in the reception room—other than the fact that they both could die.

He finished his Coke, listening to the straw rattle at the bottom, and then threw his trash in the wastebasket. Lunch had been small, but he’d enjoyed it. He felt pretty good, actually. Not now. The shaking hadn’t come back, and even the morning’s horror now felt like a bad, random surprise, like a dream, unimportant.

He tapped his fingers on his desk pad, shrugged, and picked up the phone again. He called the EMMC and asked for the morgue.

After he got the pathology clerk, he said who he was and said, “You have one of our students there, a Victor Pascow—”

“Not anymore,” the voice on the other end said. “He’s gone.”

Louis’s throat tightened. He finally managed, “What?”

“His body was flown back to his parents late last night. A guy from Brookings-Smith Mortuary came and took him. They put him on Delta, uh”—papers rustling—”Delta Flight 109. Where did you think he went? Out dancing at the Show Ring?”

“No,” Louis said. “No, of course not. It’s just…” It was just what? What was he doing pursuing this, anyway? There was no sensible way to handle it. It had to be let go, forgotten. Anything else was asking for pointless trouble. “It’s just that it seemed very fast,” he finished weakly.

“Well, he was autopsied yesterday afternoon”—that faint rustle of papers again—”at around three-twenty by Dr. Rynzwyck. By then his father had made all the arrangements. I imagine the body got to Newark by two in the morning.”

“Oh. Well, in that case—”

“Unless one of the airlines messed up and sent it somewhere else,” the pathology clerk said cheerfully. “We’ve had that happen, you know, although never with Delta. Delta’s actually pretty good. We had a guy who died on a fishing trip way up in Aroostook County, in one of those little towns that just have a couple of map coordinates for a name. Jerk choked on a pop-top while he was chugging a can of beer. Took his buddies two days to drag him out of the wilderness, and you know that by then it’s a gamble whether or not the embalming fluid will work. But they injected it and hoped for the best. Sent him home to Grand Falls, Minnesota, in the cargo hold of some airliner. But there was a mix-up. They shipped him first to Miami, then to Des Moines, then to Fargo, North Dakota. Finally somebody figured it out, but by then another three days had gone by. Nothing worked. They might as well have injected him with Kool-Aid instead of embalming fluid. The guy was totally black and smelled like spoiled pork. That’s what I heard, anyway. Six baggage handlers got sick.”

The voice on the other end of the line laughed loudly.

Louis closed his eyes and said, “Well, thank you—”

“I can give you Dr. Rynzwyck’s home phone if you want it, Doctor, but he usually plays golf up in Orono in the morning.”

“That’s okay,” Louis said.

He hung up the telephone. That settles it, he thought. While you were having that crazy dream, or whatever it was, Pascow’s body was almost certainly in a Bergenfield funeral home. That’s the end of it; let it be over.

Driving home that afternoon, a simple explanation for the dirt at the foot of the bed finally came to him, filling him with relief.

He had experienced a single episode of sleepwalking, triggered by the unexpected and extremely upsetting event of having a student seriously injured and then dying in his infirmary during his first real day on the job.

It explained everything. The dream had seemed extremely real because large parts of it were real—the feel of the carpet, the cold dew, and, of course, the dead branch that had scratched his arm. It explained why Pascow had been able to walk through the door and he had not.

A picture rose in his mind, a picture of Rachel coming downstairs last night and catching him bumping against the back door, trying in his sleep to walk through it. The thought made him grin. It would have really shocked her.

With the sleepwalking idea in mind, he was able to analyze the reasons for the dream—and he did so eagerly. He had walked to the Pet Sematary because it had become linked to another recent stressful moment. It had in fact caused a serious argument between him and his wife… and also, he thought with growing excitement, it was linked in his mind to his daughter’s first encounter with the idea of death—something his own subconscious must have been dealing with last night when he went to bed.

Damn lucky I got back to the house okay—I don’t even remember that part. Must have come back automatically.

It was a good thing he had. He couldn’t imagine what it would have been like to have woken up this morning by the grave of Smucky the Cat, confused, covered with dew, and probably scared shitless—as Rachel also would have been, undoubtedly.

But it was over now.

That’s the end of it, Louis thought with immense relief. Yes, but what about the things he said when he was dying?, his mind tried to ask, and Louis shut it up fast.

That evening, while Rachel ironed and Ellie and Gage sat in the same chair, both captivated by “The Muppet Show,” Louis mentioned casually to Rachel that he thought he'd go for a short walk—to get some fresh air.

“Will you be back in time to help me put Gage to bed?” she asked, not looking up from her ironing. “You know he goes down easier when you’re here.”

“Sure,” he said.

“Where are you going, Daddy?” Ellie asked, her eyes glued to the TV. Kermit was about to be punched in the eye by Miss Piggy.

“Just out back, hon.”

Louis went outside.

Fifteen minutes later, he stood in the Pet Sematary, looking around with curiosity and a strong sense of déjà vu. He knew he had been here before: the small grave marker for Smucky the Cat was knocked over. He had done that when he saw Pascow in what he could remember of the dream. Louis righted it without thinking and walked over to the pile of fallen trees.

He didn’t like it. The memory of the weather-beaten branches and dead trees turning into a pile of bones still made him uneasy. He made himself reach out and touch one. Balanced on the unstable pile, it rolled and fell, bouncing down the side. Louis jumped back to avoid it hitting his shoe.

He walked along the fallen trees, first to the left, then to the right. On both sides, the underbrush was so thick it was impossible to get through. And it wasn’t the kind of brush you’d want to try to push through—not if you were smart, Louis thought. There were large patches of poison ivy growing close to the ground (Louis had always heard people claim they were immune, but he knew almost no one truly was), and deeper in were some of the biggest, nastiest thorns he had ever seen.

Louis walked back to the approximate center of the fallen trees. He looked at it, his hands in the back pockets of his jeans.

You’re not going to try to climb that, are you?

Not me, boss. Why would I want to do something that stupid?

Great. You worried me there for a second, Lou. Looks like a good way to end up in the hospital with a broken ankle, doesn’t it?

Sure does! Also, it’s getting dark.

Sure that he knew exactly what he was doing, Louis started to climb the pile of fallen trees.

He was halfway up when he felt it move under his feet with a strange creaking noise.

Roll the dice, Doc.

When the pile moved again, Louis started to climb back down. His shirt had come untucked.

He made it back to the ground safely and brushed bits of bark off his hands. He walked back to the start of the path that led to his house—to his children who would want a bedtime story, to Church, who was enjoying his last day as a tomcat and lady-killer, to tea in the kitchen with his wife after the kids were asleep.

He looked at the clearing again before leaving, struck by its quiet greenness. Wisps of fog had appeared out of nowhere and were starting to wrap around the stones. Those circles... as if, without realizing it, the children of North Ludlow had built a small Stonehenge.

But, Louis, is this all there is?

Even though he had only briefly glanced over the top of the deadfall before the uneasy feeling made him anxious, he could have almost said there was a path beyond, going further into the woods.

None of your concern, Louis. You need to forget about this.

Okay, boss.

Louis turned and went home.

He stayed up an hour after Rachel went to bed that night, reading a pile of medical journals he had already read, not wanting to admit that the thought of going to bed—going to sleep—made him uneasy. He had never sleepwalked before, and there was no way to know if it was just a one-time thing… until it either happened again or didn't.

He heard Rachel get out of bed, and then she called down quietly, “Lou? Honey? Are you coming up?”

“Just about to,” he said, turning off the lamp over his desk and standing up.

It took much longer than seven minutes to turn off the machine that night. As he listened to Rachel breathing deeply and calmly in her sleep next to him, the image of Victor Pascow seemed less like a dream. He would close his eyes and see the door burst open and there he was, Our Special Guest Star, Victor Pascow, standing there in his jogging shorts, pale under his summer tan, his collarbone sticking out.

He would start to fall asleep, think about what it would be like to wake up completely, coldly, in the Pet Sematary, to see those uneven circles lit by moonlight, to have to walk back, awake, along the path through the woods. He would think these things and then suddenly wake up again.

It was after midnight when sleep finally caught him off guard. He had no dreams. He woke up right at seven-thirty, to the sound of cold autumn rain hitting the window. He pulled back the sheets with some worry. The ground sheet on his bed was perfect. No one would call his feet, with their circles of rough skin, perfect, but they were at least clean.

Louis started whistling in the shower.
