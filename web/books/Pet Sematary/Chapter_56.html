Pet Sematary

Chapter 56

Rachel slapped her face until it tingled, but she still kept nodding off. Once she snapped fully awake (she was in Pittsfield now and had the turnpike all to herself), it seemed for a second that many silvery, cruel eyes were looking at her, twinkling like cold, hungry fire.

Then they turned out to be the small reflectors on the guardrail posts. The Chevette had drifted far into the breakdown lane.

She jerked the wheel to the left, the tires screeching, and she thought she heard a faint tick! that might have been her right front bumper just barely touching a guardrail post. Her heart leaped and began to bang so hard that she saw spots before her eyes, growing and shrinking with its beat. And yet a moment later, despite her close call, her scare, and Robert Gordon shouting “Red Hot” on the radio, she was dozing off again.

A crazy, paranoid thought came to her. “Paranoid, sure,” she muttered under the rock and roll. She tried to laugh—but she couldn’t. Not really. Because the thought stayed, and in the dark, it seemed strangely believable. She felt like a cartoon character who has run into a giant slingshot's rubber band. The poor guy finds it harder and harder to move forward, until the rubber band's energy equals the runner's energy… inertia becomes what?… basic physics… something holding her back… stay out of this… and an object at rest stays at rest… Gage’s body, for instance… once moving…

This time the tires screamed louder, the shave was much closer; for a moment there was the high, grating sound of the Chevette running along the guardrail cables, scraping paint down to the shining metal, and for a moment the wheel didn’t respond, and then Rachel was standing on the brake, sobbing; she had been asleep this time, not just dozing but asleep and dreaming at sixty miles an hour, and if there had been no guardrail… or if there had been an overpass support…

She pulled over, put the car in park, and wept into her hands, confused and afraid.

Something is trying to keep me away from him.

When she felt in control, she began to drive again—the car's steering seemed fine, but she figured Avis would have some questions when she returned their car to BIA tomorrow.

Never mind. One thing at a time. I need coffee—that’s the first thing.

When the Pittsfield exit appeared, Rachel took it. About a mile down the road she saw bright streetlights and heard the steady low rumble of diesel engines. She pulled in, had the Chevette filled up (“Someone put a pretty good dent along the side of her,” the gas station attendant said, almost impressed), and then went into the diner, which smelled of fried grease, rubbery eggs… and, thankfully, of good strong coffee.

Rachel had three cups, one after another, like medicine—black, with lots of sugar. A few truckers sat at the counter or in the booths, teasing the waitresses, who all looked like tired nurses with bad news under the bright fluorescent lights in the early morning.

She paid and went back out to the Chevette. It wouldn’t start. When she turned the key, the solenoid made a dry clicking sound, but nothing else.

Rachel began to hit the steering wheel slowly and weakly with her fists. Something was trying to stop her. There was no reason for this car, brand-new and with less than five thousand miles on it, to have died like this, but it had. Somehow it had, and here she was, stuck in Pittsfield, still almost fifty miles from home.

She listened to the steady hum of the big trucks, and it suddenly struck her with cruel certainty that the truck that had killed her son was here among them… not humming but laughing.

Rachel lowered her head and began to cry.
