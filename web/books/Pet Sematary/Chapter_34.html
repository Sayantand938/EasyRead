Pet Sematary

Chapter 34

Ellie turned six. She came home from kindergarten on her birthday with a paper hat crooked on her head, some pictures her friends had drawn of her (in the best one, Ellie looked like a friendly scarecrow), and sad stories about getting in trouble on the playground during recess. The flu went away. They had to send two students to the EMMC in Bangor, and Surrendra Hardu probably saved the life of a very sick freshman boy with the awful name of Peter Humperton, who had seizures soon after arriving. Rachel got a small crush on the blond grocery bagger at the A & P in Brewer and told Louis at night how tight his jeans looked. “It’s probably just toilet paper,” she added. “Feel it sometime,” Louis suggested. “If he yells, it’s probably not.” Rachel laughed until she cried. The cold, quiet, very freezing time of February passed and brought the changing rains and freezes of March, potholes, and those orange roadside signs that warn about bumps. The sharpest, most personal, and most painful sadness of Jud Crandall passed, that sadness that psychologists say starts about three days after someone dies and lasts hard for four to six weeks in most cases—like that time New Englanders sometimes call “deep winter.” But time passes, and time changes one feeling into another until they become like a rainbow. Strong sadness becomes a softer sadness; soft sadness becomes mourning; mourning finally becomes remembering—a process that can take from six months to three years and still be normal. The day of Gage’s first haircut came and went, and when Louis saw his son’s hair growing in darker, he joked about it and mourned himself—but only inside.

Spring came, and it stayed for a while.
