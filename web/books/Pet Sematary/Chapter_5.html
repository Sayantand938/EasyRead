Pet Sematary

Chapter 5

By nine o’clock the movers were gone. Ellie and Gage, both exhausted, were sleeping in their new rooms, Gage in his crib, Ellie on a mattress on the floor surrounded by a small mountain of boxes—her billions of Crayolas, whole, broken, and dull; her Sesame Street posters; her picture books; her clothes; heaven knew what else. And of course Church was with her, also sleeping and growling rustily in the back of his throat. That rusty growl seemed the closest the big tom could come to purring.

Rachel had walked around the house restlessly with Gage in her arms earlier, questioning the places where Louis had told the movers to leave things, getting them to rearrange, change, or restack. Louis had not lost their check; it was still in his breast pocket, along with the five ten-dollar bills he had set aside for a tip. When the van was finally emptied, he handed both the check and the cash over, nodded at their thanks, signed the receipt, and stood on the porch, watching them head back to their big truck. He figured they would probably stop in Bangor and have a few beers to settle the dust. A couple of beers would be good right now. That made him think of Jud Crandall again.

He and Rachel sat at the kitchen table, and he saw the circles under her eyes. “You,” he said, “go to bed.”

“Doctor’s orders?” she asked, smiling a little.

“Yeah.”

“Okay,” she said, standing. “I’m beat. And Gage is likely to be up in the night. You coming?”

He hesitated. “I don’t think so, just yet. That old guy across the street—”

“Road. You call it a road, out in the country. Or if you’re Judson Crandall, I guess you call it a rud.”

“Okay, across the rud. He invited me over for a beer. I think I’m going to do that. I’m tired, but I’m too wired to sleep.”

Rachel smiled. “You’ll get Norma Crandall to tell you everything, even what kind of mattress she sleeps on.”

Louis laughed, thinking how funny—funny and scary—it was that wives could read their husbands’ minds after a while.

“He was here when we needed him,” he said. “I can do him a favor, I guess.”

“Barter system?”

He shrugged, not wanting to explain that he had quickly grown fond of Crandall. “How’s his wife?”

“Very sweet,” Rachel said. “Gage sat on her lap. I was surprised because he’s had a hard day, and you know he doesn’t like new people, especially when he's tired. And she had a dolly she let Eileen play with.”

“How bad would you say her arthritis is?”

“Quite bad.”

“In a wheelchair?”

“No… but she walks very slowly, and her fingers… ” Rachel held her own slim fingers up and hooked them into claws to show him. Louis nodded. “Anyway, don’t be late, Lou. Strange houses give me the creeps.”

"It won't feel strange for long," Louis said, and kissed her.
