Pet Sematary

Chapter 48

“You want to what?” Dory asked again. “Rachel… you’re upset… a night’s sleep…”

Rachel only shook her head. She couldn’t explain to her mother why she had to go back. The feeling had grown in her like a rising wind—a faint stirring in the grass, barely noticeable; then the air starts to move faster and harder, and there is no peace; then the gusts become strong enough to make eerie screaming sounds around the edges of the roof; then they are shaking the house, and you realize it’s like a hurricane, and if the wind gets much stronger, things will fall down.

It was six o’clock in Chicago. In Bangor, Louis was just sitting down to his big, bland meal. Rachel and Ellie had barely touched their dinners. Rachel kept looking up from her plate to see her daughter staring at her, asking what she was going to do about whatever trouble Daddy was in, asking her what she was going to do.

She waited for the phone to ring, for Jud to call and say Louis had come home, and once it did ring—she jumped, and Ellie almost spilled her milk—but it was just a lady from Dory’s bridge club, wanting to know if she had gotten home okay.

They were having their coffee when Rachel suddenly threw down her napkin and said, “Daddy… Mom… I’m sorry, but I have to go home. If I can get a flight, I’m going tonight.”

Her mother and father stared at her, but Ellie closed her eyes with a grown-up look of relief—it would have been funny if not for the pale, strained look of her skin.

They didn’t understand, and Rachel couldn’t explain any more than she could explain how those tiny puffs of wind, so weak they barely move the tips of grass, can slowly grow strong enough to knock down a steel building. She didn’t believe that Ellie had heard about Victor Pascow’s death on the news and stored it away in her mind.

“Rachel. Honey.” Her father spoke slowly, kindly, as if talking to someone in a temporary but dangerous fit. “This is just a reaction to your son’s death. You and Ellie are both reacting strongly to that, and who could blame you? But you’ll just collapse if you try to—”

Rachel didn’t answer him. She went to the phone in the hall, found AIRLINES in the Yellow Pages, and dialed Delta’s number while Dory stood nearby, telling her they should think about this, shouldn’t they, they should talk about it, maybe make a list… and behind her, Ellie stood, her face still dark—but now lit by enough hope to give Rachel some courage.

“Delta Airlines,” the voice on the other end said brightly. “This is Kim, may I help you?”

“I hope so,” Rachel said. “It’s really important that I get from Chicago to Bangor tonight. It’s… it’s kind of an emergency, I’m afraid. Can you check the flights for me?”

With doubt: “Yes, ma’am, but this is really last minute.”

“Well, please check,” Rachel said, her voice breaking a little. “I’ll take standby, anything.”

“All right, ma’am. Please hold.” The line went smoothly silent.

Rachel closed her eyes, and after a moment she felt a cool hand on her arm. She opened her eyes and saw that Ellie had moved next to her. Irwin and Dory stood together, talking quietly and looking at them. The way you look at people you think are crazy, Rachel thought tiredly. She forced a smile for Ellie.

“Don’t let them stop you, Mommy,” Ellie said in a low voice. “Please.”

“No way, big sister,” Rachel said and then winced—it was what they had called her ever since Gage was born. But she was no one’s big sister anymore, was she?

“Thank you,” Ellie said.

“It’s really important, isn’t it?”

Ellie nodded.

“Honey, I think so. But you could help me by telling me more. Is it just the dream?”

“No,” Ellie said. “It’s… it’s everything now. It’s all through me now. Can’t you feel it, Mommy? Something like a—”

“Something like a wind.”

Ellie sighed, a little shaky.

“But you don’t know what it is? You don’t remember anything else about your dream?”

Ellie thought hard and then shook her head, not wanting to. “Daddy. Church. And Gage. That’s all I remember. But I don’t remember how they fit together, Mommy!”

Rachel hugged her tightly. “It will be all right,” she said, but her heart still felt heavy.

“Hello, ma’am,” the clerk said.

“Hello?” Rachel held Ellie and the phone even tighter.

“I think I can get you to Bangor, ma’am—but you’ll be getting in very late.”

“That doesn’t matter,” Rachel said.

“Do you have a pen? It’s complicated.”

“Yes, right here,” Rachel said, taking a small pencil from the drawer. She found the back of an envelope to write on.

Rachel listened closely, writing everything down. When the airline clerk was done, Rachel smiled slightly and made an O with her thumb and forefinger to show Ellie it would work. Probably going to work, she thought. Some of the connections looked very, very close… especially in Boston.

“Please book it all,” Rachel said. “And thank you.”

Kim took Rachel’s name and credit card number. Rachel finally hung up, drained but relieved. She looked at her father. “Daddy, will you drive me to the airport?”

“Maybe I should say no,” Goldman said. “I think I might need to stop this craziness.”

“Don’t you dare!” Ellie cried loudly. “It’s not crazy! It’s not!”

Goldman blinked and stepped back at this small but fierce outburst.

“Take her, Irwin,” Dory said quietly into the silence that followed. “I’m starting to feel nervous too. I’ll feel better knowing Louis is okay.”

Goldman looked at his wife and finally turned to Rachel. “I’ll take you, if that’s what you want,” he said. “I… Rachel, I’ll come with you, if you want that.”

Rachel shook her head. “Thank you, Daddy, but I got all the last seats. It’s like God saved them for me.”

Irwin Goldman sighed. He looked very old at that moment, and Rachel suddenly realized that her father looked like Jud Crandall.

“You have time to pack a bag, if you want,” he said. “We can be at the airport in forty minutes, if I drive the way I used to when your mother and I were first married. Find her your tote bag, Dory.”

“Mommy,” Ellie said. Rachel turned to her. Ellie’s face was now shiny with light sweat.

“What, honey?”

“Be careful, Mommy,” Ellie said.
