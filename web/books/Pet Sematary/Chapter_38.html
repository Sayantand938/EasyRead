Pet Sematary

Chapter 38

They sat at the kitchen table with beers—the first time they'd had them in the kitchen, Louis realized, a little surprised. From across the living room, Ellie cried out in her sleep, and they both froze like statues in a game. The cry didn't happen again.

“Okay,” Louis said, “why are you here at twelve-fifteen the morning my son is buried? You’re a friend, Jud, but this is too much.”

Jud drank, wiped his mouth with his hand, and looked straight at Louis. There was something clear and sure in his eyes, and Louis finally looked away.

“You know why I’m here,” Jud said. “You’re thinking about things you shouldn't be thinking about, Louis. Worse, I think you’re planning them.”

“I was just thinking about going to bed,” Louis said. “I have a burial to go to tomorrow.”

“I've caused you more pain tonight than I should have,” Jud said softly. “I might even be responsible for your son's death.”

Louis looked up, surprised. “What—? Jud, don’t be crazy!”

“You’re thinking of trying to take him up there,” Jud said. “Don’t say you haven't thought about it, Louis.”

Louis didn't answer.

“How far does its influence go?” Jud said. “Can you tell me that? No. I can’t answer that myself, and I’ve lived my whole life in this part of the world. I know about the Micmacs, and they always thought of that place as holy… but not in a good way. Stanny B. told me that. My father told me too—later on. After Spot died the second time. Now the Micmacs, the state of Maine, and the U.S. government are fighting in court about who owns that land. Who does own it? No one really knows, Louis. Not anymore. Different people claimed it at one time or another, but no claim ever lasted. Anson Ludlow, the great-grandson of the town's founder, for one. His claim was maybe the best for a white man, since Joseph Ludlow the Elder got the whole thing as a gift from Good King Georgie back when Maine was just a big part of Massachusetts. But even then, he would have been in a tough court fight because other Ludlows also claimed the land, along with a guy named Peter Dimmart, who said he could prove he was a Ludlow from an affair. And Joseph Ludlow the Elder was poor in money but rich in land near the end of his life, and now and then he’d just give someone two or four hundred acres when he was drunk.”

“Were none of those papers recorded?” Louis asked, fascinated.

“Oh, our grandfathers were real sticklers for recording papers,” Jud said, lighting a new cigarette from the end of the old one. “The original paper for your land goes like this.” Jud closed his eyes and quoted, “ ‘From the great old maple on top of Quinceberry Ridge to the edge of Orrington Stream; that's how the land runs from north to south.’ ” Jud grinned, but not happily. “But the great old maple fell down around 1882 and was rotten to moss by 1900, and Orrington Stream filled with dirt and turned to marsh in the ten years between the end of World War I and the stock market crash. It made a real mess! It didn’t matter to old Anson, anyway. He was killed by lightning in 1921, right up around that cemetery.”

Louis stared at Jud. Jud sipped his beer.

“It doesn’t matter. There are lots of places where the history of who owns what is so mixed up it never gets straightened out; only the lawyers make money. Hell, Dickens knew that. I guess the Indians will get it back in the end, and I think that’s right. But that doesn’t really matter, Louis. I came over here tonight to tell you about Timmy Baterman and his dad.”

“Who’s Timmy Baterman?”

“Timmy Baterman was one of about twenty boys from Ludlow who went overseas to fight Hitler. He left in 1942. He came back in a coffin with a flag on it in 1943. He died in Italy. His daddy, Bill Baterman, lived his whole life in this town. He almost went crazy when he got the telegram… and then he got very quiet. He knew about the Micmac cemetery, you see. And he’d decided what he wanted to do.”

The chill was back. Louis stared at Jud for a long time, trying to see if the old man was lying. He wasn’t. But the fact that this story was coming up just now was too convenient.

“Why didn’t you tell me this the other night?” he said finally. “After we… after we buried the cat? When I asked you if anyone had ever buried a person up there, you said no one ever had.”

“Because you didn’t need to know,” Jud said. “Now you do.”

Louis was silent for a long time. “Was he the only one?”

“The only one I know myself,” Jud said seriously. “The only one to ever try it? I doubt that, Louis. I really doubt it. I’m like the preacher in Ecclesiastes—I don’t think there’s anything new in the world. Sometimes the glitter on top changes, but that’s all. What’s been tried once has been tried before… and before… and before.”

He looked down at his age-spotted hands. In the living room, the clock softly chimed twelve-thirty.

“I figured a man in your job is used to seeing the diseases behind the symptoms… and I decided I had to be honest with you when Mortonson at the funeral home told me you’d ordered a grave liner instead of a sealing vault.”

Louis looked at Jud for a long time, saying nothing. Jud blushed deeply but didn’t look away.

Finally Louis said: “Sounds like you did some investigating, Jud. I’m sorry that happened.”

“I didn’t ask him what you bought.”

“Not directly, maybe.”

But Jud didn’t reply, and even though his blush had gotten worse—his face was almost purple now—his eyes still didn’t move.

At last, Louis sighed. He felt incredibly tired. “Oh, forget it. I don’t care. Maybe you’re right. Maybe I was thinking about it. If I was, it wasn’t a serious thought. I didn’t think much about what I was ordering. I was thinking about Gage.”

“I know you were thinking about Gage. But you knew the difference. Your uncle was an undertaker.”

Yes, he had known the difference. A sealing vault was a construction project, something meant to last a very long time. Concrete was poured into a rectangle made of forms and reinforced with steel, and after the service at the grave, a crane lowered a slightly curved concrete top onto it. The lid was sealed with something like the stuff road crews used to patch potholes. Uncle Carl had told Louis that sealant—called Ever-Lock—really gripped tight after all that weight had been on it for a while.

Uncle Carl, who loved to tell stories (especially to his own kind, and Louis, who had worked with him during the summers, was like an apprentice undertaker), told his nephew about an order to dig up a body that he’d gotten from the Cook County D.A.’s office. Uncle Carl went to Groveland to watch the exhumation. They could be tricky, he said—people who only knew about digging up bodies from horror movies with Boris Karloff as Dr. Frankenstein’s monster and Dwight Frye as Igor were wrong about it. Opening a sealing vault wasn't a job for two guys with picks and shovels—unless they had about six weeks to spare. This one went okay… at first. The grave was opened, and the crane hooked onto the top of the vault. But the top didn’t just come off, like it should have. Instead, the whole vault, its concrete sides already a little wet and stained, started to rise out of the ground. Uncle Carl yelled for the crane operator to stop. Uncle Carl wanted to go back to the funeral home and get something to weaken the sealant's grip.

The crane operator either didn’t hear or wanted to lift the whole thing, like a kid playing with a toy crane at an arcade. Uncle Carl said that the fool almost did it, too. The vault was mostly out—Uncle Carl and his helper could hear water dripping from the bottom of the vault into the grave (it had been a wet week in Chicago) when the crane just tipped over and crashed into the grave. The crane operator hit the windshield and broke his nose. That day cost Cook County about $3,000—$2,100 more than usual. The real point of the story for Uncle Carl was that the crane operator was elected president of the Chicago Teamsters union six years later.

Grave liners were simpler. A liner was just a plain concrete box, open at the top. It was put in the grave on the morning of the funeral. After the service, the coffin was lowered into it. Then the sextons brought the top, which was usually in two pieces. These pieces were lowered into the ends of the grave, where they stood up like bookends. Iron rings were set into the concrete at the ends of each piece. The sextons would run chains through them and lower them gently onto the top of the grave liner. Each section weighed sixty, maybe seventy pounds—eighty at most. And no sealer was used.

It was easy for a man to open a grave liner; that’s what Jud was suggesting.

Easy for a man to dig up his son's body and bury it somewhere else.

Shhhhh… shhhh. We won't talk about such things. These are secrets.

“Yes, I guess I knew the difference between a sealing vault and a grave liner,” Louis said. “But I wasn’t thinking about… about what you think I was thinking about.”

“Louis—”

“It’s late,” Louis said. “It’s late, I’m drunk, and my heart hurts. If you feel like you have to tell me this story, then tell me and let’s finish it.” Maybe I should have started with martinis, he thought. Then I could have been safely passed out when he came knocking.

“All right, Louis. Thank you.”

“Just go on.”

Jud paused a moment, thinking, then began to speak.
