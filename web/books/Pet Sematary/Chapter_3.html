Pet Sematary

Chapter 3

He had watched them arrive from across the street and had come over to see if he could help when it seemed they were "in a bit of a jam," as he said.

While Louis held the baby on his shoulder, Crandall stepped close, looked at the swelling on Gage’s neck, and reached out with one blocky, twisted hand. Rachel started to protest—his hand looked terribly clumsy and almost as big as Gage’s head—but before she could say anything, the old man’s fingers had made a single sure movement, as skillful and quick as the fingers of a man shuffling cards or making coins disappear. And the stinger lay in his palm.

“Big one,” he said. “Not the biggest, but good enough to show off, I guess.” Louis burst out laughing.

Crandall looked at him with that crooked smile and said, “Yep, a good one, isn't it?”

“What did he say, Mommy?” Eileen asked, and then Rachel burst out laughing too. Of course it was terribly rude, but somehow it was okay. Crandall pulled out a pack of Chesterfield Kings, stuck one in the wrinkled corner of his mouth, nodded at them kindly as they laughed—even Gage was giggling now, despite the swelling from the bee sting—and lit a wooden match with his thumbnail. The old have their tricks, Louis thought. Small ones, but some of them are good.

He stopped laughing and held out the hand that wasn’t holding Gage’s bottom—Gage’s definitely wet bottom. “I’m happy to meet you, Mr.—”

“Jud Crandall,” he said and shook his hand. “You’re the doctor, I guess.”

“Yes. Louis Creed. This is my wife Rachel, my daughter Ellie, and the kid with the bee sting is Gage.”

“Nice to meet all of you.”

I didn’t mean to laugh… that is, we didn’t mean to laugh… it’s just that we’re… a little tired.

That—how little he said about it—made him giggle again. He felt completely worn out.

Crandall nodded. “Of course you are,” he said, which sounded like: Co’s you aaa. He looked at Rachel. “Why don’t you take your little boy and your daughter to the house for a bit, Missus Creed? We can put some baking soda on a cloth and cool it down a little. My wife would like to say hello too. She doesn’t get out much. Her arthritis has been bad the last two or three years.”

Rachel looked at Louis, who nodded.

“That would be very nice of you, Mr. Crandall.”

“Oh, just call me Jud,” he said.

There was a sudden loud honk, a motor slowing, and then the big blue moving van was turning—heavily—into the driveway.

“Oh Christ, and I don’t know where the keys are,” Louis said.

“That’s okay,” Crandall said. “I have a set. Mr. and Mrs. Cleveland… the ones who lived here before you—gave me a set, oh, it must have been fourteen, fifteen years ago. They lived here a long time. Joan Cleveland was my wife’s best friend. She died two years ago. Bill went to that senior apartment complex over in Orrington. I’ll bring them back over. They’re yours now, anyway.”

“You’re very kind, Mr. Crandall,” Rachel said.

“Not at all,” he said. “Looking forward to having kids around again.” Except that the sound of this, as strange to their Midwestern ears as a foreign language, was yowwuns “You just want to watch them around the road, Mrs. Creed. Lots of big trucks on that road.”

Now there was the sound of slamming doors as the moving men hopped out of the cab and came toward them.

Ellie had wandered away a little, and now she said, “Daddy what’s this?”

Louis, who had started to meet the moving men, glanced back. At the edge of the field, where the lawn stopped and high summer grass took over, a path about four feet wide had been cut, smooth and short. It wound up the hill, curved through a low stand of bushes and a small group of birches, and out of sight.

“Looks like a path of some kind,” Louis said.

“Oh, yes,” Crandall said, smiling. “Tell you about it sometime, missy. You want to come over and we’ll fix your baby brother up?”

“Sure,” Ellie said and then added with a certain hopefulness “Does baking soda sting?”
