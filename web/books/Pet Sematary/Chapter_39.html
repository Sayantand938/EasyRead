Pet Sematary

Chapter 39

“Back then—during the war, I mean—the train still stopped in Orrington, and Bill Baterman had a hearse at the depot to meet the freight train carrying his son Timmy’s body. Four railroad men unloaded the coffin. I was one of them. There was an army guy on board from Graves Registration—the army’s wartime undertakers, Louis—but he didn’t get off. He was drunk in a boxcar with twelve coffins.

“We put Timmy in the back of the hearse—back then, they were sometimes called ‘hurry-up wagons’ because the main thing was to bury them before they decayed. Bill Baterman stood by, his face hard and… dry, I guess. He didn’t cry. Huey Garber drove the train that day and said the army guy was having quite a trip. Huey said they’d flown a lot of coffins to Limestone in Presque Isle, then the coffins and their keeper took the train south.

“The army guy walks up to Huey, pulls a fifth of rye whiskey from his shirt, and says in a soft, southern voice, ‘Well, Mr. Engineer, you’re driving a mystery train today, did you know that?’

“Huey shakes his head.

“ ‘Well, you are. That’s what they call a funeral train in Alabama.’ Huey said the guy took out a list and squinted at it. ‘We’ll drop off two coffins in Houlton, then one for Passadumkeag, two for Bangor, one for Derry, one for Ludlow, and so on. I feel like a milkman. Want a drink?’

“Huey says no, because the Bangor and Aroostook doesn’t like train drivers drinking rye, and the Graves Registration guy doesn’t mind, and Huey doesn’t mind the guy’s drunkenness. They even shook hands on it, Huey said.

“So they left, dropping off coffins every other stop. Eighteen or twenty in all. Huey said it went on to Boston, and there were weeping relatives at every stop except Ludlow… where he saw Bill Baterman, who looked dead inside and waiting for his soul to rot. When he got off that train, he woke up the army guy, and they went to some bars—fifteen or twenty—and Huey got drunker than ever and went to a prostitute, which he’d never done, and woke up with crabs so bad they made him shiver, and he said if that was a mystery train, he never wanted to drive one again.

“Timmy’s body was taken to Greenspan Funeral Home on Fern Street—across from the New Franklin Laundry—and two days later he was buried in Pleasantview Cemetery with military honors.

“You know, Louis: Mrs. Baterman had been dead ten years, along with her second child, and that mattered. A second child might have eased the pain, don’t you think? Might have reminded Bill that others feel pain and need help. I guess you’re luckier—having another child, I mean. A child and a wife who are alive and well.”

According to the letter Bill got from the lieutenant in charge of his boy’s platoon, Timmy was shot down on the road to Rome on July 15, 1943. His body was shipped home two days later, and it got to Limestone on the nineteenth. It was put aboard Huey Garber’s mystery train the very next day. Most of the GIs who got killed in Europe were buried in Europe, but all of the boys who went home on that train were special—Timmy had died charging a machine-gun nest, and he had won the Silver Star. After he died.

Timmy was buried—I might be wrong about this, but I think it was on July 22. It was four or five days later that Marjorie Washburn, who was the mailwoman in those days, saw Timmy walking up the road toward York’s Livery Stable. Well, Margie almost drove right off the road, and you can understand why. She went back to the post office, tossed her leather bag with all her undelivered mail still in it on George Anderson’s desk, and told him she was going home and to bed right then.

“Margie, are you sick?” George asks. “You are just as white as a gull’s wing.”

“I’ve had the fright of my life, and I don’t want to talk to you about it,” Margie Washburn says. “I’m not going to talk to Brian about it, or my mom, or anybody. When I get up to heaven, if Jesus asks me to talk to Him about it, maybe I will. But I don’t believe it.” And out she goes.

Everybody knew Timmy was dead; there was his obituary in the Bangor Daily News and the Ellsworth American just the week before, picture and all, and half the town turned out for his funeral up to the city. And here Margie saw him, walking up the road—stumbling up the road, she finally told old George Anderson—only this was twenty years later, and she was dying, and George told me it seemed to him like she wanted to tell somebody what she’d seen. George said it seemed to him like it bothered her, you know.

Pale he was, she said, and dressed in an old pair of chino pants and a faded flannel hunting shirt, although it must have been ninety degrees in the shade that day. Margie said all his hair was sticking up in the back. “His eyes were like raisins stuck in bread dough. I saw a ghost that day, George. That’s what scared me so. I never thought I’d see such a thing, but there it was.”

Well, word got around. Pretty soon some other people saw Timmy, too. Missus Stratton—well, we called her “missus,” but so far as anyone knew she could have been single or divorced or a widow; she had a little two-room house down where the Pedersen Road joins the Hancock Road, and she had a lot of jazz records, and sometimes she’d be willing to throw you a little party if you had a ten-dollar bill that wasn’t being used. Well, she saw him from her porch, and she said he walked right up to the edge of the road and stopped there.

He just stood there, she said, his hands dangling at his sides and his head pushed forward, looking like a boxer who’s ready to get knocked out. She said she stood there on her porch, heart beating fast, too scared to move. Then she said he turned around, and it was like watching a drunk man try to turn around. One leg went way out and the other foot turned, and he almost fell over. She said he looked right at her and all the strength just left her hands and she dropped the basket of washing she had, and the clothes fell out and got dirty all over again.

She said his eyes… she said they looked as dead and dusty as marbles, Louis. But he saw her… and he grinned… and she said he talked to her. Asked her if she still had those records because he wouldn’t mind dancing with her. Maybe that very night. And Missus Stratton went back inside, and she wouldn’t come out for most of a week, and by then it was over anyway.

A lot of people saw Timmy Baterman. Many of them are dead now—Missus Stratton is, for one, and others have moved on, but there are a few old folks like me left around who’ll tell you if you ask them.

“We saw him, I tell you, walking back and forth along the Pedersen Road, a mile east of his daddy’s house and a mile west. Back and forth he went, back and forth all day, and for all anyone knew, all night. Shirt untucked, pale face, hair all stuck up in spikes, fly unzipped sometimes, and this look on his face… this look…”

Jud paused to light a cigarette, then shook the match out, and looked at Louis through the haze of drifting blue smoke. And although the story was, of course, completely insane, there was no lie in Jud’s eyes.

“You know, they have these stories and these movies—I don’t know if they’re true—about zombies down in Haiti. In the movies they just sort of shuffle along, with their dead eyes staring straight ahead, real slow and sort of clumsy. Timmy Baterman was like that, Louis, like a zombie in a movie, but he wasn’t. There was something more. There was something going on behind his eyes, and sometimes you could see it and sometimes you couldn’t see it. Something behind his eyes, Louis. I don’t think that thinking is what I want to call it. I don’t know what in the hell I want to call it.

“It was sly, that was one thing. Like him telling Missus Stratton he wanted to dance with her. There was something going on in there, Louis, but I don’t think it was thinking and I don’t think it had much—maybe nothing at all—to do with Timmy Baterman. It was more like a… a radio signal that was coming from somewhere else. You looked at him and you thought, ‘If he touches me, I’m gonna scream.’ Like that.

“Back and forth he went, up and down the road, and one day after I got home from work—this must have been, oh, I’m going to say it was July 30 or so—here is George Anderson, the postmaster, don’t you know, sitting on my back porch, drinking iced tea with Hannibal Benson, who was then our second selectman, and Alan Purinton, who was fire chief. Norma sat there too but never said a thing.

“George kept rubbing the stump at the top of his right leg. Lost most of that leg working on the railroad, he did, and the stump used to bother him something fierce on those hot and humid days. But here he was, misery or not.

“ ‘This has gone far enough,’ George says to me. ‘I got a mail-woman who won’t deliver out on the Pedersen Road—that’s one thing. It’s starting to cause trouble with the government, and that’s something else.’

“ ‘What do you mean, it’s causing trouble with the government?’ I asked.

“Hannibal said he’d had a call from the War Department. Some lieutenant named Kinsman whose job it was to sort out bad behavior from plain old foolishness. ‘Four or five people have written anonymous letters to the War Department,’ Hannibal says, ‘and this Lieutenant Kinsman is starting to get a little bit concerned. If it was just one fellow who had written one letter, they’d laugh it off. If it was just one fellow writing a whole bunch of letters, Kinsman says he’d call the state police up in Derry Barracks and tell them they might have a crazy person who hates the Baterman family in Ludlow. But these letters all came from different people. He said you could tell that by the handwriting, name or no name, and they all say the same crazy thing—that if Timothy Baterman is dead, he makes one hell of a lively corpse walking up and down Pedersen Road with his bare face hanging out.

“ ‘This Kinsman is going to send a fellow out or come himself if this doesn’t settle down,’ Hannibal finishes up. ‘They want to know if Timmy’s dead, or AWOL, or what because they don’t like to think their records are all messed up. Also they’re gonna want to know who was buried in Timmy Baterman’s box, if he wasn’t.’

“Well, you can see what a mess it was, Louis. We sat there almost an hour, drinking iced tea and talking it over. Norma asked us if we wanted sandwiches, but no one did.

“We talked it over and over, and finally decided we had to go out there to the Baterman place. I’ll never forget that night, not if I live to be twice as old as I am now. It was hot, hotter than hell, with the sun going down like a bucket of guts behind the clouds. None of us wanted to go, but we had to. Norma knew it before any of us. She got me inside on some excuse and said, ‘Don’t let them waste time and put this off, Judson. You've got to get this taken care of. It’s a terrible thing.”

Jud looked at Louis steadily.

“That’s what she called it, Louis. It was her word. Terrible thing. And she kind of whispered in my ear, ‘If anything happens, Jud, you just run. Never mind the others; they’ll have to look out for themselves. You remember me and get out of there as fast as you can if anything happens.’

“We drove over in Hannibal Benson’s car—that son of a bitch got all the gas he wanted, I don’t know how. Nobody said much, but all four of us were smoking like chimneys. We were scared, Louis, as scared as we could be. But the only one who really said anything was Alan Purinton. He says to George, ‘Bill Baterman has been up to no good in that woods north of Route 15, and I’ll swear to it.’ Nobody answered, but I remember George nodding his head.

“Well, we got there, and Alan knocked, but nobody answered, so we went around to the back, and there the two of them were. Bill Baterman was sitting there on his back steps with a pitcher of beer, and Timmy was at the back of the yard, just staring up at that red, bloody sun as it went down. His whole face was orange with it, like he’d been skinned alive. And Bill… he looked like the devil had gotten him after his time to relax. He was swimming in his clothes, and I figured he’d lost forty pounds. His eyes had gone back in their sockets until they were like little animals in a pair of caves… and his mouth kept twitching on the left side.”

Jud paused, seemed to think, and then nodded slightly. “Louis, he looked damned.

“Timmy looked around at us and grinned. Just seeing him grin made you want to scream. Then he turned and went back to looking at the sun go down. Bill says, ‘I didn’t hear you boys knock,’ which was a total lie, of course, since Alan banged on that door loud enough to wake… to wake up a deaf man.

“No one seemed like they were going to say anything, so I says, ‘Bill, I heard your boy was killed over in Italy.’

“ ‘That was a mistake,’ he says, looking right at me.

“ ‘Was it?’ I says.

“ ‘You see him standing right there, don’t you?’ he says.

“ ‘So who do you think was in that coffin you had buried out at Pleasantview?’ Alan Purinton asks him.

“ ‘I’ll be damned if I know,’ Bill says, ‘and I’ll be damned if I care.’ He goes to get a cigarette and spills them all over the back porch, then breaks two or three trying to pick them up.

“ ‘They'll probably have to dig it up,’ Hannibal says. ‘You know that, don’t you? I had a call from the goddam War Department, Bill. They are going to want to know if they buried some other mother’s son under Timmy’s name.’

“ ‘Well, what the hell?’ Bill says in a loud voice. ‘That’s nothing to me, is it? I got my boy. Timmy came home the other day. He’s been shell-shocked or something. He’s a little strange now, but he’ll come around.’

“ ‘Let’s stop this, Bill,’ I says, and all at once I was pretty mad at him. ‘If they dig up that army coffin, they’re gonna find it dead empty, unless you went to the trouble of filling it up with rocks after you took your boy out of it, and I don’t think you did. I know what happened, Hannibal and George and Alan here know what happened, and you know what happened too. You been messing around up in the woods, Bill, and you have caused yourself and this town a lot of trouble.’

“ ‘You fellas know your way out, I guess,’ he says. ‘I don’t have to explain myself to you, or answer to you, or nothing. When I got that telegram, the life ran right out of me. I felt her go, just like piss down the inside of my leg. Well, I got my boy back. They had no right to take my boy. He was only seventeen. He was all I had left of his dear mother, and it was illegal. So screw the army, and screw the War Department, and screw the United States of America, and screw you boys too. I got him back. He’ll come around. And that’s all I got to say. Now you all just march your boots back where you came from.’

“And his mouth is twitching, and there’s sweat all over his forehead in big drops, and that was when I saw he was crazy. It would have driven me crazy too. Living with that… that thing.”

Louis was feeling sick to his stomach. He had drunk too much beer too fast. Pretty soon it was all going to come up. The heavy feeling in his stomach told him it would be coming up soon.

Well, there wasn’t much else we could do. We got ready to go. Hannibal says, ‘Bill, God help you.’

“Bill says, ‘God never helped me. I helped myself.’

“That was when Timmy walked over to us. He even walked wrong, Louis. He walked like an old, old man. He’d put one foot high up and then bring it down and then kind of shuffle and then lift the other one. It was like watching a crab walk. His hands dangled down by his legs. And when he got close enough, you could see red marks across his face on the slant, like pimples or little burns. I figure that’s where the Kraut machine gun got him. Must have damn near blown his head off.

“And he stank of the grave. It was a black smell, like everything inside him was just lying there, spoiled. I saw Alan Purinton put a hand up to cover his nose and mouth. The stench was just awful. You almost expected to see grave maggots squirming around in his hair—”

“Stop,” Louis said hoarsely. “I’ve heard enough.”

“You ain’t,” Jud said. He spoke with worn-out seriousness. “That’s it, you ain’t. And I can’t even make it as bad as it was. Nobody could understand how bad it was unless they was there. He was dead, Louis. But he was alive too. And he… he… he knew things.”

“Knew things?” Louis sat forward.

“Yep. He looked at Alan for a long time, kind of grinning—you could see his teeth, anyway—and then he spoke in this low voice; you felt like you had to strain forward to hear it. It sounded like he had gravel down in his throat. ‘Your wife is sleeping with that man she works with down at the drugstore, Purinton. What do you think of that? She screams when she comes. What do you think of that?’

“Alan, he kind of gasped, and you could see it had hit him. Alan’s in a nursing home up in Gardener now, or was the last I heard—he must be pushing ninety. Back when all this happened, he was forty or so, and there had been some talk around about his second wife. She was his second cousin, and she had come to live with Alan and Alan’s first wife, Lucy, just before the war. Well, Lucy died, and a year and a half later Alan up and married this girl. Laurine, her name was. She was no more than twenty-four when they married. And there had been some talk about her, you know. If you were a man, you might have called her ways sort of free and easy and let it go at that. But the women thought she might be loose. And maybe Alan had had a few thoughts in that direction too because he says, ‘Shut up! Shut up or I’ll knock you down, whatever you are!’

“ ‘Shush now, Timmy,’ Bill says, and he looks worse than ever, you know, like maybe he’s going to puke or faint dead away, or do both. ‘You shush, Timmy.’

“But Timmy didn’t pay any attention. He looks around at George Anderson and he says, ‘That grandson you think so highly of is just waiting for you to die, old man. He only wants the money, the money he thinks you have locked away in your lockbox at the Bangor Eastern Bank. That’s why he sucks up to you, but behind your back he makes fun of you, him and his sister. Old wooden-leg, that’s what they call you,’ Timmy says, and Louis, his voice—it changed. It got mean. It sounded like the way that grandson of George’s would have sounded if… you know, if the things Timmy was saying were true.

“ ‘Old wooden-leg,’ Timmy says, ‘and won’t they be surprised when they find out you’re poor as a church mouse because you lost it all in 1938? Won’t they be surprised, George? Won’t they just be surprised?’

“George, he backed away then, and his wooden leg buckled under him, and he fell back on Bill’s porch and spilled his pitcher of beer, and he was as white as your undershirt, Louis.

“Bill, he gets him back on his feet somehow, and he’s yelling at his boy, ‘Timmy, you stop it! You stop it!’ But Timmy wouldn’t. He said something bad about Hannibal, and then he said something bad about me too, and by then he was… raving, I’d say. Yeah, he was raving, all right. Screaming. And we started to back away, and then we started to run, dragging George along the best we could by the arms because he’d gotten the straps and harnesses on that fake leg twisted somehow, and it was all off to one side with the shoe turned around backward and dragging on the grass.

“The last I saw of Timmy Baterman, he was on the back lawn by the clothesline, his face all red in the setting sun, those marks standing out on his face, his hair all crazy and dusty somehow… and he was laughing and screeching over and over again ‘Old wooden-leg! Old wooden-leg! And the cuckold! And the whoremaster! Goodbye, gentlemen! Goodbye! Goodbye!’ And then he laughed, but it was screaming, really… something inside him… screaming… and screaming… and screaming.”

Jud stopped. His chest moved up and down rapidly.

“Jud,” Louis said. “The thing this Timmy Baterman told you—was it true?”

“It was true,” Jud muttered. “Christ! It was true. I used to go to a whorehouse in Bangor sometimes. Nothing many a man hasn’t done, although I suppose there are plenty that walk the straight and narrow. I just would get the urge—the need, maybe—to sleep with a stranger now and then. Or pay some woman to do the things a man can’t bring himself to ask his wife to do. Men have secrets, Louis. It wasn’t a terrible thing, what I did, and all of that has been behind me for the last eight or nine years, and Norma would not have left me if she had known. But something in her would have died forever. Something dear and sweet.”

Jud’s eyes were red and swollen and bleary. The tears of the old are particularly unlovely, Louis thought. But when Jud reached across the table for Louis’s hand, Louis took it firmly.

“He told us only the bad,” he said after a moment. “Only the bad. God knows there is enough of that in any human being’s life, isn’t there? Two or three days later, Laurine Purinton left Ludlow for good, and folks in town who saw her before she got on the train said she had two black eyes and had cotton stuffed up both sides of her nose. Alan, he would never talk about it. George died in 1950, and if he left anything to that grandson and granddaughter of his, I never heard about it. Hannibal got kicked out of office because of something that was just like what Timmy Baterman accused him of. I won’t tell you exactly what it was—you don’t need to know—but misusing town funds for his own use comes close enough to cover it, I reckon. There was even talk of trying him on fraud charges, but it never amounted to much. Losing the position was enough punishment for him anyway; his whole life was playing the big shot.

But there was good in those men too. That’s what I mean; that’s what people always find it so hard to remember. Hannibal got the fund started for the Eastern General Hospital, right before the war. Alan Purinton was one of the most generous, giving men I ever knew. And old George Anderson only wanted to keep running the post office forever.

It was only the bad it wanted to talk about though. It was only the bad it wanted us to remember because it was bad… and because it knew we meant danger for it. The Timmy Baterman that went off to fight the war was a nice, regular kid, Louis, maybe a little slow but goodhearted. The thing we saw that night, looking up into that red sun… that was a monster. Maybe it was a zombie or a dybbuk or a demon. Maybe there’s no name for such a thing as that, but the Micmacs would have known what it was, name or no.

“What?” Louis said, feeling numb.

“Something that had been touched by the Wendigo,” Jud said calmly. He took a deep breath, held it for a moment, let it out, and looked at his watch.

“Welladay. It’s late, Louis. I’ve talked nine times as much as I meant to.”

“I doubt that,” Louis said. “You’ve been very expressive. Tell me what happened in the end.”

“There was a fire at the Baterman place two nights later,” Jud said. “The house burned to the ground. Alan Purinton said there was no doubt the fire was set on purpose. Heating oil had been splashed all over that little house. You could smell it strongly for three days after the fire was out.”

“So they both burned up.”

“Oh, yes, they burned. But they were dead before that. Timmy was shot twice in the chest with a pistol Bill Baterman kept nearby, an old Colt’s. They found it in Bill’s hand. What he’d done, or so it looked like, was kill his boy, lay him on the bed, and then spill out that heating oil. Then he sat down in his easy chair by the radio, flicked a match, and killed himself with that Colt .45.”

“Jesus,” Louis said.

“They were pretty burned, but the county doctor thought Timmy Baterman had been dead for two or three weeks.”

Silence, ticking out.

Jud got up. “I wasn’t kidding when I said I might have killed your boy, Louis, or helped it happen. The Micmacs knew that place, but that doesn’t mean they made it what it was. The Micmacs weren’t always here. They came maybe from Canada, maybe from Russia, maybe from Asia a long time ago. They stayed here in Maine for a thousand years, or maybe two—it’s hard to tell, because they didn’t change the land much. And now they’re gone again… like we’ll be gone, someday, though I guess we’ll change things more, for better or worse. But the place will stay no matter who’s here, Louis. It’s not like someone owned it and could take its secret when they left. It’s an evil, rotten place, and I shouldn’t have taken you up there to bury that cat. I know that now. It has a power you’ll avoid if you care about your family and yourself. I wasn’t strong enough to fight it. You saved Norma’s life, and I wanted to do something for you, and that place twisted my good wish into its own evil purpose. It has a power… and I think that power changes, like the moon. It’s been full of power before, and I’m scared it’s getting full again. I’m scared it used me to get to you through your son. Do you see what I mean, Louis?” His eyes begged Louis.

“You’re saying the place knew Gage was going to die, I think,” Louis said.

“No, I’m saying the place might have made Gage die because I showed you the power in the place. I’m saying I might have murdered your son with good intentions, Louis.”

“I don’t believe it,” Louis said at last, shakily. Didn’t; wouldn’t. Couldn’t.

He held Jud’s hand tightly. “We’re burying Gage tomorrow. In Bangor. And he will stay in Bangor. I don’t plan to go up there to the Pet Sematary or past it ever again.”

“Promise me!” Jud said sharply. “Promise.”

“I promise,” Louis said.

But in his mind, the thought stayed—a small, bright hope that wouldn’t go away.
