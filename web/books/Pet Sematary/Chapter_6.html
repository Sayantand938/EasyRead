Pet Sematary

Chapter 6

Louis came back later feeling small. No one asked him to examine Norma Crandall; when he crossed the street (remember, he told himself, smiling), the lady had already gone to bed. Jud was a dim shape behind the screens of the enclosed porch. There was the nice squeak of a rocker on old linoleum. Louis knocked on the screen door, which rattled softly against its frame. Crandall’s cigarette glowed like a big, friendly firefly in the summer darkness. From a radio, low, came the sound of a Red Sox game, and it all gave Louis Creed a strange feeling of coming home.

“Doc,” Crandall said. “I thought that was you.”

“Hope you meant what you said about the beer,” Louis said, coming in.

“Oh, I never lie about beer,” Crandall said. “A man who lies about beer makes enemies. Sit down, Doc. I put a couple extra on ice, just in case.”

The porch was long and narrow, with rattan chairs and sofas. Louis sank into one and was surprised at how comfortable it was. At his left was a tin pail filled with ice cubes and a few cans of Black Label. He took one.

“Thank you,” he said and opened it. The first two swallows felt like a blessing.

“You’re welcome,” Crandall said. “I hope you’ll be happy here, Doc.”

“Amen,” Louis said.

“Hey! If you want crackers or something, I could get some. I have some cheese that’s just about ready.”

“A piece of what?”

“Rat cheese.” Crandall sounded a little amused.

“Thanks, but just the beer for me.”

“Well then, we’ll just let it go.” Crandall belched, sounding happy.

“Your wife in bed?” Louis asked, wondering why he was talking like this.

“Yep. Sometimes she stays up. Sometimes she doesn’t.”

“Her arthritis is very painful, right?”

“You ever see a case that wasn’t?” Crandall asked.

Louis shook his head.

“I guess it’s okay,” Crandall said. “She doesn’t complain much. She’s a good old girl, my Norma.” His voice showed a deep and real love. Out on Route 15, a tanker truck rumbled by, so big and long that Louis couldn’t see his house across the road for a moment. The word ORINCO was written on the side, just visible in the fading light.

“One hell of a big truck,” Louis said.

“Orinco’s near Orrington,” Crandall said. “Chemical fertilizer plant. They come and go, all right. And the oil tankers, and the dump trucks, and the people who go to work in Bangor or Brewer and come home at night.” He shook his head. “That’s the one thing about Ludlow I don’t like anymore. That damn road. No peace from it. They go all day and all night. Wake Norma up sometimes. Hell, wake me up sometimes, and I sleep like a goddam log.”

Louis, who thought this strange Maine landscape almost eerily quiet after the constant roar of Chicago, just nodded.

“One day soon the Arabs will stop the oil, and they’ll be able to grow African violets right on the road line,” Crandall said.

“You might be right.” Louis tilted his can back and was surprised to find it empty.

Crandall laughed. “Just grab yourself another, Doc.”

Louis hesitated and then said, “All right, but just one more. I have to get back.”

“Sure you do. Not moving easily, huh?”

“It is,” Louis agreed, and then for a time they were silent. The silence was comfortable, as if they had known each other for a long time. This was a feeling Louis had read about in books, but never felt until now. He felt ashamed of his earlier thoughts about free medical advice.

On the road a semi roared by, its running lights twinkling like stars.

“That’s one mean road, all right,” Crandall repeated thoughtfully, almost vaguely, and then turned to Louis. There was a strange little smile on his wrinkled mouth. He stuck a Chesterfield cigarette into one corner of the smile and lit a match with his thumbnail. “You remember the path there that your little girl mentioned?”

For a moment Louis didn’t; Ellie had mentioned a lot of things before finally falling asleep for the night. Then he did remember. That wide, mowed path going up through the small group of trees and over the hill.

“Yes, I do. You promised to tell her about it sometime.”

“I did, and I will,” Crandall said. “That path goes up into the woods about a mile and a half. The local kids around Route 15 and Middle Drive keep it nice because they use it. Kids come and go… there’s a lot more moving around than there used to be when I was a boy; then you picked a place and stayed there. But they seem to tell each other, and every spring a bunch of them mows that path. They keep it nice all summer long. Not all of the adults in town know it’s there—a lot of them do, of course, but not all—but all of the kids do. I’d bet on it.”

“Know what’s there?”

“The pet cemetery,” Crandall said.

“Pet cemetery,” Louis repeated, puzzled.

“It’s not as strange as it probably sounds,” Crandall said, smoking and rocking. “It’s the road. It kills a lot of animals, that road does. Dogs and cats, mostly, but that’s not all. One of those big Orinco trucks ran over the pet raccoon the Ryder children used to have. That was back—Christ, must have been in ‘73, maybe earlier. Before the state made keeping a raccoon or even a skunk that couldn’t spray illegal, anyway.”

“Why did they do that?”

“Rabies,” Crandall said. “A lot of rabies in Maine now. There was a big old St. Bernard dog that got rabies down south a couple of years ago and killed four people. That was a terrible thing. The dog hadn’t had his shots. If those people had made sure that dog had its shots, it never would have happened. But a raccoon or a skunk, you can vaccinate it twice a year and it still doesn’t always work. But that raccoon the Ryder boys had, that was what the old-timers used to call a ‘sweet raccoon.’ It would waddle right up to you—gosh, he was fat!—and lick your face like a dog. Their dad even paid a vet to neuter him and take out his claws. That must have cost him a fortune!”

Ryder, he worked for IBM in Bangor. They moved to Colorado five years ago… or maybe six. Funny to think of those two almost old enough to drive. Were they still sad about that raccoon? I guess they were. Matty Ryder cried so much his mom got worried and wanted to take him to the doctor. I guess he’s over it now, but they never forget. When a good animal gets killed on the road, a kid never forgets.

Louis’s mind turned to Ellie as he had last seen her tonight, fast asleep with Church purring softly on the foot of the bed.

“My daughter’s got a cat,” he said. “Winston Churchill. We call him Church for short.”

“Do they climb when he walks?”

“I’m sorry?” Louis had no idea what he was talking about.

“Does he still have his balls or has he been fixed?”

“No,” Louis said. “No, he hasn’t been fixed.”

In fact there had been some trouble over that back in Chicago. Rachel had wanted to get Church spayed, had even made the appointment with the vet. Louis canceled it. Even now he wasn’t really sure why. It wasn’t as simple or as stupid as thinking his masculinity was linked to his daughter’s tomcat, or even his annoyance at the idea that Church would have to be neutered so the fat housewife next door wouldn’t have to bother securing her plastic garbage cans—those things were part of it, but mostly it was a vague but strong feeling that it would ruin something in Church that he himself valued—that it would take away the defiant look in the cat’s green eyes. Finally he had pointed out to Rachel that they were moving to the country, and it shouldn’t be a problem. Now here was Judson Crandall, pointing out that part of country living in Ludlow involved dealing with Route 15, and asked him if the cat was fixed. Try a little irony, Dr. Creed—it’s good for your blood.

“I’d get him fixed,” Crandall said, crushing his cigarette between his thumb and forefinger. “A fixed cat doesn’t tend to wander as much. But if he’s always crossing back and forth, his luck will run out, and he’ll end up there with the Ryder kids’ raccoon and little Timmy Dessler’s cocker spaniel and Mrs. Bradleigh’s parakeet. Not that the parakeet got run over in the road, you understand. It just died one day.”

“I’ll think about it,” Louis said.

“You do that,” Crandall said and stood up. “How about that beer? I think I’ll have a bit of that old Mr. Rat after all.”

“Beer’s gone,” Louis said, also standing, “and I should get going, too. Big day tomorrow.”

“Starting at the university?”

Louis nodded. “The kids don’t come back for two weeks, but I should know what I’m doing by then, don’t you think?”

“Yeah, if you don’t know where the pills are, I guess you’ll have trouble.” Crandall offered his hand and Louis shook it, remembering again that old bones hurt easily. “Come over any evening,” he said. “I want you to meet my Norma. I think she’d like you.”

“I will,” Louis said. “Nice to meet you, Jud.”

“Same here. You’ll settle in. You might even stay a while.”

“I hope we do.”

Louis walked down the uneven path to the side of the road and had to wait while another truck, followed by five cars headed toward Bucksport, went by. Then, raising his hand in a quick wave, he crossed the street (road, he reminded himself again) and went into his new house.

It was quiet, with the sounds of sleep. Ellie didn’t seem to have moved, and Gage was still in his crib, sleeping like Gage always did, spread out on his back, a bottle nearby. Louis stopped there, looking at his son, his heart suddenly filled with such strong love for the boy that it felt almost risky. He thought part of it was just missing all the familiar Chicago places and faces that were now gone, wiped away so well by the distance that they might never have existed. People move around a lot more than they used to… you used to pick a place and stay there. There was some truth to that.

He went to his son, and because no one was watching, not even Rachel, he kissed his fingers and then gently touched them to Gage’s cheek through the crib bars.

Gage made a soft noise and turned onto his side.

“Sleep well, baby,” Louis said.

He quietly undressed and got into his side of the bed, which was just two single mattresses together on the floor for now. He felt the day’s stress start to fade. Rachel didn’t move. Unpacked boxes loomed like ghosts in the room.

Just before falling asleep, Louis propped himself up on his elbow and looked out the window. Their room faced the front, and he could see the Crandall’s house across the road. It was too dark to make out shapes—he could have on a moonlit night—but he saw the glow of a cigarette over there. Still awake, he thought. He might be up for a while. Old people don’t sleep well. Maybe they’re standing guard.

Against what?

Louis was thinking about that as he fell asleep. He dreamed he was in Disney World, driving a bright white van with a red cross on the side. Gage was next to him, and in the dream Gage was at least ten years old. Church was on the dashboard of the white van, looking at Louis with his bright green eyes, and out on Main Street by the 1890s train station, Mickey Mouse was shaking hands with the children around him, his big white cartoon gloves swallowing their small, trusting hands.
