Pet Sematary

Chapter 12

The first thing he noticed turning into the university grounds was how suddenly and dramatically the traffic increased. There were cars, bikes, and lots of joggers. He had to stop suddenly to avoid two joggers coming from Dunn Hall. Louis braked hard, tightening his seatbelt, and honked. He was always annoyed at how joggers (and bicyclists) seemed to think they had no responsibilities once they started moving. They were, after all, exercising. One of them flipped Louis off without looking. Louis sighed and drove on.

The second thing was that the ambulance was missing from its spot in the small infirmary parking lot, which worried him. The infirmary could handle almost any short-term illness or accident. It had three well-equipped exam rooms off the main lobby and two wards with fifteen beds each. But there was no operating room. For serious accidents, the ambulance rushed people to Eastern Maine Medical Center. Steve Masterton, the physician’s assistant who had shown Louis around, had proudly shown Louis the log from the past two years. There had only been thirty-eight ambulance trips in that time, which was good considering the student population was over ten thousand and the total university population was almost seventeen thousand.

And here he was, on his first day, and the ambulance was gone.

He parked in the spot with the freshly painted sign RESERVED FOR DR. CREED and hurried inside.

He found Charlton, a fit, graying woman of about fifty, in the first exam room, taking the temperature of a girl in jeans and a halter top. The girl had a bad sunburn, Louis saw, with peeling skin.

“Good morning, Joan,” he said. “Where’s the ambulance?”

“Oh, we had a real tragedy,” Charlton said, taking the thermometer from the student’s mouth and reading it. “Steve Masterton came in this morning at seven and found a big puddle under the engine and front wheels. The radiator broke. They took it away.”

“Great,” Louis said, relieved. At least it wasn’t out on a call, which he had feared. “When do we get it back?”

Joan Charlton laughed. “Knowing the University Motor Pool,” she said, “it’ll be back around December fifteenth, wrapped in Christmas ribbon.” She glanced at the student. “You have a slight fever,” she said. “Take two aspirins and stay out of bars and dark alleys.”

The girl got down. She gave Louis a quick look and then went out.

“Our first customer of the new semester,” Charlton said unhappily. She began to shake the thermometer down with quick snaps.

“You don’t seem too happy about it.”

“I know the type,” she said. “Oh, we get the other type too—athletes who keep playing with bone chips and tendonitis and everything else because they don’t want to be benched, they have to be macho men, not let the team down, even if they’re risking pro careers later on. Then you’ve got little Miss Half-Degree of Fever—” She jerked her head toward the window, where Louis could see the girl with the peeling sunburn walking toward the Gannett-Cumberland-Androscoggin dorms. In the examining room the girl had seemed like someone who didn't feel well but was trying not to show it. Now she was walking quickly, her hips swinging prettily, noticing and being noticed.

“Your basic college hypochondriac.” Charlton dropped the thermometer into a sterilizer. “We’ll see her two dozen times this year. Her visits will be more frequent before each round of midterms. A week or so before finals, she’ll be sure she has either mono or pneumonia. Bronchitis is the backup. She’ll get out of four or five tests—the ones where the instructors are weak, to use their word—and get easier makeups. They always get sicker if they know the midterm or final is going to be a multiple-choice test rather than an essay exam.”

“My, aren’t we cynical this morning,” Louis said. He was, in fact, a little surprised.

She winked at him, which made him grin. “I don’t worry about it. Doctor. Neither should you.”

“Where’s Stephen now?”

“In your office, answering mail and trying to understand the latest load of bureaucratic nonsense from Blue Cross-Blue Shield,” she said.

Louis went in. Despite Charlton’s cynicism, he felt comfortably at work.

Looking back, Louis would think—when he could bear to think about it at all—that the nightmare really began when they brought the dying boy, Victor Pascow, into the infirmary around ten that morning.

Until then, things were quiet. At nine, half an hour after Louis arrived, the two student volunteers who would be working the nine-to-three shift came in. Louis gave them each a doughnut and a cup of coffee and talked to them for about fifteen minutes, explaining their jobs, and more importantly, what they shouldn't do. Then Charlton took over. As she led them out of Louis’s office, Louis heard her ask: “Either of you allergic to shit or puke? You’ll see a lot of both here.”

“Oh God,” Louis murmured and covered his eyes. But he was smiling. A tough old woman like Charlton was not always a bad thing.

Louis began filling out the long insurance forms, which were a complete list of drugs and medical equipment (“Every year,” Steve Masterton said, annoyed. “Every goddam year the same thing. Why don’t you write down Complete heart transplant facility, worth about eight million dollars, Louis? That’ll fool them!”), and he was really focused, barely thinking about how good a cup of coffee would be, when Masterton screamed from the waiting room: “Louis! Hey, Louis, get out here! We got a mess!”

The panic in Masterton’s voice made Louis move fast. He jumped out of his chair almost as if he had been expecting this. A scream, as sharp as broken glass, came from where Masterton was shouting. It was followed by a slap and Charlton saying, “Stop that or get the hell out of here! Stop it right now!”

Louis ran into the waiting room and first noticed the blood—there was a lot of blood. One of the student volunteers was sobbing. The other, very pale, had her hands covering her mouth, pulling her lips into a wide, disgusted grin. Masterton was kneeling, trying to hold the head of the boy lying on the floor.

Steve looked up at Louis, his eyes wide and scared. He tried to speak, but nothing came out.

People were gathering at the Student Medical Center’s big glass doors, looking in, their hands around their faces to block the light. Louis suddenly thought of something strange: sitting in the living room as a young kid with his mother in the morning before she went to work, watching TV. Watching the old “Today” show, with Dave Garroway. People were outside, staring at Dave and Frank Blair and J. Fred Muggs. He looked around and saw other people standing at the windows. He couldn’t do anything about the doors, but—

“Shut the drapes,” he snapped at the student volunteer who had screamed.

When she didn’t move right away, Charlton slapped her butt. “Do it, girl!”

The candy-striper sprang into action. Instantly, green drapes were pulled shut over the windows. Charlton and Steve Masterton moved quickly between the boy on the floor and the doors, blocking the view as well as they could.

“Hard stretcher, Doctor?” Charlton asked.

“Get it if we need it,” Louis said, kneeling beside Masterton. “I haven’t even looked at him yet.”

“Come on,” Charlton said to the girl who had closed the drapes. She was pulling the corners of her mouth with her fists again, making that humorless, screaming grin. She looked at Charlton and moaned, “Oh, ag,”

“Yeah, oh, ag is right. Come on.” She gave the girl a hard tug and got her moving, her red and white striped skirt swishing against her legs.

Louis bent over his first patient at the University of Maine at Orono.

He was a young man, about twenty years old, and it took Louis less than three seconds to make the only important diagnosis: The young man was going to die. Half of his head was crushed. His neck was broken. One collarbone stuck out from his swollen and twisted right shoulder. From his head, blood and a yellow, oozing fluid slowly seeped into the carpet. Louis could see the man’s brain, whitish-gray and pulsing through a shattered section of skull. It was like looking through a broken window. The hole was about five centimeters wide; he could almost have delivered a baby from his skull, like Zeus delivering from his forehead. It was unbelievable that he was still alive. Suddenly, he heard Jud Crandall saying sometimes you could feel it bite you. And his mother: dead is dead. He felt a crazy urge to laugh. Dead was dead, all right. That’s for sure.

“Call for the ambulance,” he snapped at Masterton. “We—”

“Louis, the ambulance is—”

“Oh Christ,” Louis said, slapping his own forehead. He turned to Charlton. “Joan, what do you do in a case like this? Call Campus Security or the EMMC?”

Joan looked flustered and upset—something Louis rarely saw. But her voice was calm as she replied, “Doctor, I don’t know. We’ve never had anything like this happen while I’ve been at the Medical Center.”

Louis thought quickly. “Call the campus police. We can’t wait for EMMC to send an ambulance. If needed, they can take him to Bangor in a fire engine. At least it has a siren and lights. Go do it, Joan.”

She left, but not before he saw her deeply sympathetic glance and understood it. This young man, deeply tanned and muscular—maybe from a summer working on a road crew, painting houses, or teaching tennis—and now wearing only red gym shorts with white trim, was going to die no matter what. He would be just as dead even if their ambulance had been parked outside, engine running, when he arrived.

Incredibly, the dying man was moving. His eyes fluttered and opened. Blue eyes, the whites streaked with blood. They stared blankly around, seeing nothing. He tried to move his head, and Louis pressed down to stop him, remembering the broken neck. The head injury didn’t mean he couldn’t feel pain.

The hole in his head, oh God, the hole in his head.

“What happened to him?” he asked Steve, knowing it was a stupid and pointless question. A bystander’s question. But the hole in the man’s head confirmed his role; he was just a bystander. “Did the police bring him?”

“Some students brought him in a blanket. I don’t know what happened.”

He had to think about what to do next. That was his responsibility too. “Go find them,” Louis said. “Take them around to the other door. I want them nearby, but I don’t want them to see any more of this than they already have.”

Masterton, looking relieved to leave the room, went to the door and opened it, letting in a mix of excited, curious, confused voices. Louis could also hear a police siren. Campus Security was here. Louis felt a miserable sense of relief.

The dying man was gurgling. He tried to speak. Louis heard sounds—at least, they sounded like sounds—but the words were slurred and unclear.

Louis leaned over him and said, “You’re going to be all right, fella.” He thought of Rachel and Ellie as he said it, and his stomach gave a great, unpleasant lurch. He put a hand over his mouth and held back a burp.

“Caaa,” the young man said. “Gaaaaaa—”

Louis looked around and saw that he was alone for a moment with the dying man. He could hear Joan Charlton yelling at the candy-stripers that the hard stretcher was in the supply closet off Room Two. Louis doubted if they knew Room Two from anything; it was, after all, their first day on the job. They had gotten a terrible introduction to the world of medicine. The green carpet was now soaked a muddy purple in a growing circle around the young man’s ruined head; the leaking of brain fluid had, thankfully, stopped.

“In the Pet Sematary,” the young man croaked… and he began to grin. This grin was remarkably like the joyless, wild grin of the candy-striper who had closed the drapes.

Louis stared down at him, at first not believing what he had heard. Then Louis thought he must be hearing things. He made some more of those sounds and my mind made them into something clear, matched the sounds to my own experience. But that was not what had happened, and a moment later he had to realize it. A dizzying, crazy terror hit him and his skin began to crawl, seeming to actually move up and down his arms and along his belly in waves… but even then he just didn’t want to believe it. Yes, the words had been on the bloody lips of the man on the carpet as well as in Louis’s ears, but that only meant he was seeing and hearing things.

“What did you say?” he whispered.

And this time, as clear as the words of a talking parrot or a crow whose tongue had been cut, the words were easy to understand: “It’s not the real cemetery.” The eyes were empty, not seeing, edged with blood: the mouth grinning the wide grin of a dead fish.

Horror washed through Louis, grabbing his warm heart in its cold hands, squeezing. It made him smaller and smaller, until he felt like running from this bloody, twisted, talking head on the floor of the infirmary waiting room. He was a man with no strong religious beliefs, not interested in the strange or supernatural. He was not ready for this… whatever it was.

Fighting the urge to run with everything in him, he made himself lean even closer. “What did you say?” he asked again.

The grin. That was bad.

“The soil of a man’s heart is harder, Louis,” the dying man whispered. “A man grows what he can… and takes care of it.”

Louis, he thought, hearing nothing with his conscious mind after his own name. Oh my God he called me by my name.

“Who are you?” Louis asked in a trembling, weak voice. “Who are you?”

“Indian bring my fish.”

“How did you know my—”

“Stay away, us. Know—”

“You—”

“Caa,” the young man said, and now Louis thought he could smell death on his breath, internal injuries, lost rhythm, failure, ruin.

“What?” A crazy urge came to shake him.

“Gaaaaaaaa—”

The young man in the red gym shorts began to tremble all over. Suddenly he seemed to freeze, every muscle tight. His eyes briefly lost their blank look and seemed to find Louis’s eyes. Then everything released at once. There was a terrible smell. Louis thought he had to speak again. Then the eyes went back to their blank expression… and began to cloud over. The man was dead.

Louis sat back, barely noticing that all his clothes were sticking to him; he was soaked with sweat. Darkness bloomed, gently covering his eyes, and the world began to spin unpleasantly. Realizing what was happening, he turned away from the dead man, put his head down between his knees, and pressed his left thumb and forefinger nails into his gums hard enough to draw blood.

After a moment the world began to clear again.
