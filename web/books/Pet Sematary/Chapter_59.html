Pet Sematary

Chapter 59

“Try it now, ma’am,” the truck driver said. He was looking into the engine area of Rachel’s rented car.

She turned the key. The Chevette’s engine roared to life. The truck driver slammed the hood down and came around to her window, wiping his hands on a big blue handkerchief. He had a pleasant, red face. A Dysart’s Truck-Stop cap was tilted back on his head.

“Thank you so much,” Rachel said, close to tears. “I just didn’t know what I was going to do.”

“Aw, a kid could have fixed that,” the trucker said. “But it was strange. Never seen something like that happen on such a new car, anyway.”

“Why? What was it?”

“One of your battery cables came right off. Nobody messed with it, did they?”

“No,” Rachel said, and she thought again of that feeling she’d had, that feeling of running into the rubber band of the world’s biggest slingshot.

“Must have shaken loose just from driving, I guess. But you won’t have any more trouble with your cables anyway. I tightened them up real good.”

“Could I give you some money?” Rachel asked shyly.

The trucker roared with laughter. “Not me, lady,” he said. “We’re the knights of the road, remember?”

She smiled. “Well… thank you.”

“You’re welcome.” He grinned, surprisingly cheerful this early.

Rachel smiled back and drove carefully across the parking lot to the access road. She checked for traffic and five minutes later was back on the turnpike, headed north. The coffee had helped more than she thought it would. She felt wide awake, not sleepy at all, her eyes wide open. That slight feeling of unease touched her again, that strange feeling that she was being controlled. The battery cable coming off like that…

So she could be delayed just long enough for…

She laughed nervously. Long enough for what?

For something that couldn’t be undone to happen.

That was dumb. Ridiculous. But Rachel started to drive faster anyway.

At five o’clock, as Jud was fighting off a scalpel stolen from his friend Dr. Louis Creed’s bag, and as her daughter woke up suddenly in bed, screaming from a nightmare she couldn't remember, Rachel left the turnpike, drove the Hammond Street Cutoff near the cemetery where only a shovel was now buried in her son’s coffin, and crossed the Bangor-Brewer Bridge. By 5:15, she was on Route 15 and headed for Ludlow.

She had decided to go straight to Jud’s; she would at least keep that promise. The Civic wasn’t in their driveway, and although it might be in the garage, their house looked empty and asleep. She didn't feel that Louis might be home.

Rachel parked behind Jud’s truck and got out of the Chevette, looking around carefully. The grass was wet with dew, shining in the clear, new light. A bird sang somewhere and then stopped. A few times since she was a child, she had been awake and alone at dawn without having to do something. Then, she felt lonely but also uplifted—a strange sense of newness and lasting connection. This morning, she felt nothing so clean and good. There was only a heavy feeling of unease that she couldn’t blame entirely on the terrible day before and her recent loss.

She walked up the porch steps and opened the screen door, planning to use the old-fashioned bell on the front door. She had loved that bell the first time she and Louis came over; you twisted it, and it made a loud but musical sound that was old-fashioned and delightful.

She reached for it now, then looked down at the porch floor and frowned. There were muddy footprints on the mat. Looking around, she saw they went from the screen door to the front door. Very small footprints. A child’s footprints, it seemed. But she had been driving all night, and it hadn't rained. Windy, but no rain.

She looked at the footprints for a long time—too long, really—and realized she had to force herself to touch the bell. She grabbed it… and then her hand dropped again.

I’m just nervous, that’s all. Nervous about the sound of that bell in this quiet. He’s probably asleep and it will startle him awake…

But that wasn’t what she was afraid of. She had been anxious, scared in some deep way ever since she had struggled to stay awake, but this sharp fear was new, something only about those small footprints. Footprints that were the size—

Her mind tried to stop this thought, but it was too tired, too slow.

—of Gage’s feet.

Oh, stop it, please stop it?

She reached out and twisted the bell.

The sound was even louder than she remembered, but less musical—a harsh, choked scream in the stillness. Rachel jumped back, letting out a nervous little laugh that wasn't funny at all. She waited for Jud’s footsteps, but they didn't come. There was silence, and more silence, and she was starting to wonder if she could bring herself to turn that iron-butterfly shape again, when a sound did come from behind the door, a sound she never would have imagined.

Waow!… Waow!… Waow!

“Church?” she asked, surprised and confused. She leaned forward, but of course couldn't see inside; the door's glass panel was covered with a neat white curtain. Norma’s work. “Church, is that you?”

Waow!

Rachel tried the door. It was unlocked. Church was there, sitting in the hallway with its tail coiled neatly around its feet. The cat’s fur was streaked with something dark. Mud, Rachel thought, and then saw that the drops of liquid caught in Church’s whiskers were red.

He raised one paw and began to lick it, his eyes never leaving her face.

“Jud?” she called out, really worried now. She stepped just inside the door.

The house gave back no answer; only silence.

Rachel tried to think, but suddenly images of her sister Zelda began to fill her mind, blurring her thoughts. How her hands had twisted. How she used to slam her head against the wall sometimes when she was angry—the paper was all torn there, the plaster beneath torn and broken. This was no time to think of Zelda, not when Jud might be hurt. What if he had fallen down? He was an old man.

Think about that, not about the dreams you had as a kid, dreams of opening the closet and having Zelda jump out at you with her blackened, grinning face, dreams of being in the bathtub and seeing Zelda’s eyes peering out of the drain, dreams of Zelda hiding in the basement behind the furnace, dreams—

Church opened his mouth, showing his sharp teeth, and cried Waow! again.

Louis was right, we should never have had him fixed. He's never seemed right since then. But Louis said it would take away all his aggressive instincts. He was wrong about that, anyway; Church still hunts. He—

Waow! Church cried again, then turned and ran up the stairs.

"Jud?" she called again. "Are you up there?"

Waow! Church cried from the top of the stairs, as if to say yes, and then he disappeared down the hall.

How did he get in, anyway? Did Jud let him in? Why?

Rachel shifted from one foot to the other, wondering what to do next. The worst part was that all of this seemed... seemed somehow planned, as if something wanted her to be here, and—

And then there was a groan from upstairs, low and full of pain—Jud's voice, definitely Jud's voice. He's fallen in the bathroom or maybe tripped, broken a leg, or sprained his hip, maybe. Old bones break easily, and what in the world are you thinking, girl, standing here shifting back and forth like you have to pee. That was blood on Church, blood. Jud's hurt and you're just standing here! What's wrong with you?

"Jud!" The groan came again, and she ran up the stairs.

She had never been up here before, and because the hall's only window faced west, toward the river, it was still very dark. The hallway ran straight and wide beside the stairwell and toward the back of the house, the cherrywood rail gleaming with soft elegance. There was a picture of the Acropolis on the wall and

(it’s Zelda all these years she’s been after you and now it’s her turn open the right door and she’ll be there with her hunched and twisted back smelling of pee and death it’s Zelda it’s her turn and finally she caught up with you)

The groan came again, low, from behind the second door on the right.

Rachel began to walk toward that door, her heels clicking on the floorboards. It seemed to her that she was going through some kind of warp—not a time warp or a space warp but a size warp. She was getting smaller. The picture of the Acropolis was floating higher and higher, and that cut-glass doorknob would soon be at eye level. Her hand stretched out for it… and before she could even touch it, the door was yanked open.

Zelda stood there.

She was hunched and twisted, her body so cruelly deformed that she had become a dwarf, little more than two feet high; and for some reason Zelda was wearing the suit they had buried Gage in. But it was Zelda, all right, her eyes alight with an insane glee, her face a wrinkled purple; it was Zelda screaming, “I finally came back for you, Rachel, I’m going to twist your back like mine and you’ll never get out of bed again never get out of bed again NEVER GET OUT OF BED AGAIN—”

Church was perched on one of her shoulders and Zelda’s face swam and changed, and Rachel saw with spiraling, sickening horror that it really wasn’t Zelda at all—how could she have made such a stupid mistake? It was Gage. His face was not black but dirty, smeared with blood. And it was swollen, as if he had been terribly hurt and then put back together again by rough, uncaring hands.

She cried his name and held her arms out. He ran to her and climbed into them, and all the time one hand remained behind his back, as if holding a bunch of flowers picked in someone’s back field.

“I brought you something, Mommy!” he screamed. “I brought you something, Mommy! I brought you something, I brought you something!”
