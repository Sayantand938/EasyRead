Pet Sematary

Chapter 53

Louis located a new roll of packing tape in a kitchen drawer, and a coil of rope sat in the garage corner near the snow tires from last winter. He used the tape to stick the pick and shovel together tightly and the rope to make a simple sling.

Tools in the sling. Gage in his arms.

He put the sling over his back, then opened the car door, taking the bundle out. Gage felt much heavier than Church had. He might be crawling by the time he carried his boy up to the Micmac burial ground—and he still had to dig the grave, struggling with the hard, rocky ground.

Well, he would handle it. Somehow.

Louis Creed walked out of his garage, pausing to turn off the light with his elbow, and stood for a moment where the driveway met the grass. He could see the path to the Pet Sematary ahead despite the darkness; the short grass of the path seemed to glow.

The wind moved through his hair, and for a moment the old fear of the dark came back, making him feel weak, small, and scared. Was he really going into the woods with this dead child, under the trees where the wind blew, from darkness into darkness? And all alone this time?

Don’t think. Just do.

Louis started walking.

When he reached the Pet Sematary twenty minutes later, his arms and legs were shaking with tiredness, and he collapsed with the tarp on his knees, breathing hard. He rested there for another twenty minutes, almost falling asleep, no longer scared—exhaustion had taken over, it seemed.

Finally he got to his feet again, not really believing he could climb the fallen trees, only knowing in some numb way that he must try. The bundle in his arms seemed to weigh two hundred pounds instead of forty.

But what had happened before happened again; it was like suddenly, vividly remembering a dream. No, not remembering; reliving. When he placed his foot on the first fallen treetrunk, that queer sensation rushed through him again, a feeling that was almost joy. The weariness did not leave him, but it became bearable—unimportant, really.

Just follow me. Follow me and don’t look down, Louis. Don’t hesitate and don’t look down. I know the way through, but it has to be done quick and sure.

Quick and sure, yes—the way Jud had removed the stinger.

I know the way through.

But there was only one way through, Louis thought. Either it let you through or it did not. Once before, he had tried to climb the fallen trees by himself and hadn’t been able to. This time he mounted it quickly and surely, as he had on the night Jud had shown him the way.

Up and up, not looking down, his son’s body in its canvas shroud cradled in his arms. Up until the wind funneled secret passages and chambers through his hair again, flipping it, parting it against the grain.

He stood on the top for a moment and then descended quickly, as if going down a set of stairs. The pick and shovel rattled and clinked dully against his back. In no more than a minute, he was standing on the springy, needle-covered ground of the path again, the fallen trees bulking behind him, higher than the graveyard fence had been.

He moved up the path with his son, listening to the wind moan in the trees. The sound held no terror for him now. The night’s work was almost done.
