Pet Sematary

Chapter 42

By evening, new clouds had rolled in and a strong west wind had started. Louis put on his light jacket, zipped it, and took the car keys from the hook on the wall.

“Where you going, Lou?” Rachel asked. She didn’t sound very interested. After supper she had started crying again, and even though she was weeping softly, she couldn’t seem to stop. Louis had made her take a Valium. Now she sat with the newspaper open to the barely started crossword puzzle. In the other room, Ellie sat quietly watching “Little House on the Prairie” with Gage’s picture on her lap.

“I thought I’d get a pizza.”

“Didn’t you eat enough earlier?”

“I just wasn’t hungry then,” he said, telling the truth, and then adding a lie: “I am now.”

That afternoon, between three and six, the final part of Gage’s funeral had happened at the Ludlow house. This was the part with food. Steve Masterton and his wife came with a hamburger and noodle casserole. Charlton showed up with a quiche. “It will keep until you want it, if you don’t eat it all,” she told Rachel. “Quiche is easy to warm up.” The Dannikers from up the road brought a baked ham. The Goldmans appeared—neither of them would speak to Louis or even get close to him, which he didn’t mind—with a variety of cold cuts and cheeses. Jud also brought cheese—a large wheel of his old favorite, Mr. Rat. Missy Dandridge brought a key lime pie. And Surrendra Hardu brought apples. The food seemed to go beyond religious differences.

This was the funeral party, and even though it was quiet, it wasn’t completely sad. There was less drinking than at a normal party, but there was some. After a few beers (just the night before he had promised he would never drink again, but in the cold afternoon light the previous evening seemed like a very long time ago) Louis thought about sharing some little funeral stories his Uncle Carl had told him—that at Sicilian funerals unmarried women sometimes cut off a piece of the dead person’s shroud and slept with it under their pillows, believing it would bring them luck in love; that at Irish funerals fake weddings were sometimes performed, and the toes of the dead were tied together because of an old Celtic belief that it kept the dead person’s ghost from walking. Uncle Carl said that the custom of tying D.O.A. tags to the big toes of dead bodies had started in New York, and since all of the early morgue workers had been Irish, he thought this was a leftover of that old superstition. Then, looking at their faces, he decided such stories would be taken the wrong way.

Rachel had broken down only once, and her mother was there to comfort her. Rachel held onto Dory Goldman and cried against her shoulder in a way that let everything out, which she hadn’t been able to do with Louis, maybe because she saw them both as responsible for Gage’s death, or maybe because Louis, lost in his own strange thoughts, hadn’t encouraged her to grieve. Either way, she had turned to her mother for comfort, and Dory was there to give it, her tears mixing with her daughter’s. Irwin Goldman stood behind them, his hand on Rachel’s shoulder, and looked at Louis with a bitter victory across the room.

Ellie walked around with a silver tray full of canapés, little rolls with a toothpick stuck through each one. She kept her picture of Gage tucked tightly under her arm.

Louis received condolences. He nodded and thanked those offering sympathy. And if his eyes seemed distant, his manner a little cold, people assumed he was thinking of the past, of the accident, of the future without Gage; none (perhaps not even Jud) would have guessed that Louis had started to consider the methods of grave robbing… only as a thought experiment, of course; he didn't plan to do anything. It was just a way to keep his mind busy.

It wasn't as if he planned to do anything.

Louis stopped at the Orrington Corner Store, bought two six-packs of cold beer, and called Napoli’s to order a pepperoni-and-mushroom pizza.

“Can I get a name for the order, sir?”

Oz the Great and Terrible, Louis thought.

“Lou Creed.”

“Okay, Lou, we’re really busy, so it’ll be about forty-five minutes—is that okay?”

“Sure,” Louis said and hung up. As he got back into the Civic and started the engine, he realized that even though there were probably twenty pizza places in the Bangor area, he had chosen the one closest to Pleasantview, where Gage was buried. Well, what the hell? he thought uncomfortably. They make good pizza. No frozen dough. They toss it in the air and catch it on their fists, right where you can watch, and Gage used to laugh—

He stopped that thought.

He drove past Napoli’s to Pleasantview. He figured he had known he would do that, but what was the harm? None.

He parked across the street and crossed the road to the wrought-iron gates, which shone in the last light of day. Above them, in a semicircle, were wrought-iron letters spelling PLEASANTVIEW. The view was, to Louis, neither pleasant nor unpleasant. The cemetery was nicely landscaped on rolling hills; there were long rows of trees (but in these last few minutes of fading light, the shadows those trees cast seemed deep and as darkly unpleasant as still water in a quarry) and a few lonely weeping willows. It wasn’t quiet. The turnpike was nearby—the drone of traffic came on the steady, cold wind—and the glow in the darkening sky was Bangor International Airport.

He reached for the gate, thinking, They’ll be locked, but they were not. Maybe it was too early to lock them, and if they locked them at all it would only be to protect the place from drunks, vandals, and teenage lovers. The days of the body snatchers

(there’s that word again)

were over. The right-hand gate swung open with a faint screech, and after a glance over his shoulder to make sure he was alone, Louis stepped through. He closed the gate behind him and heard the click of the latch.

He stood in this small suburb of the dead, looking around.

A fine and private place, he thought, but none, I think, do there embrace. Who? Andrew Marvel? And why did the human mind store up such amazing piles of useless junk, anyway?

Jud’s voice spoke in his mind then, worried and—scared? Yes. Scared.

Louis, what are you doing here? You’re looking down a road you don’t want to take.

He pushed the voice aside. If he was hurting anyone, it was only himself. No one needed to know he had been here as the daylight faded to dark.

He began to walk toward Gage’s grave, taking one of the winding paths. In a moment he was in a lane of trees; they rustled their new leaves mysteriously over his head. His heart was beating too loudly in his chest. The graves and monuments were in rough rows. Somewhere there would be a caretaker’s building, and in it would be a map of Pleasantview’s twenty or so acres, neatly and logically divided into sections, each section showing the occupied graves and the unsold plots. Real estate for sale. One-room apartments. Sleepers.

Not much like the Pet Sematary, he thought, and this made him stop and think for a moment, surprised. No, it wasn’t. The Pet Sematary had given him the feeling of order rising almost unnoticed from chaos. Those rough circles, one inside the other, going in toward the center, the crude markers, crosses made of boards. As if the children who buried their pets there had made the pattern without even realizing it, as if…

For a moment Louis saw the Pet Sematary as a kind of advertisement… a come-on, like they had on freak row at the carnival. They’d bring out the fire-eater and let you watch his show for free because the owners knew you wouldn’t buy the steak unless you saw it cooking, you wouldn’t pay the money if you didn’t see the flash—

Those graves, those graves in their almost Druid-like circles.

The graves in the Pet Sematary copied the oldest religious symbol of all: circles getting smaller showing a spiral going down, not to a point, but to forever; order from chaos or chaos from order, depending on how you thought. It was a symbol the Egyptians had carved on the tombs of the Pharaohs, a symbol the Phoenicians had drawn on the burial mounds of their dead kings; it was found on cave walls in ancient Mycenae; the leaders of Stonehenge had made it as a clock to measure the universe; it appeared in the Bible as the whirlwind from which God had spoken to Job.

The spiral was the oldest sign of power in the world, man’s oldest symbol of that twisty bridge that may exist between the world and the unknown.

Louis reached Gage’s grave at last. The earthmover was gone. The fake grass had been taken away, rolled up by some whistling worker thinking about a beer after work at the Fairmount Lounge, and stored in a shed somewhere. Where Gage lay there was a neat rectangle of bare, raked earth, maybe five feet by three feet. The headstone hadn’t been put up yet.

Louis kneeled. The wind blew through his hair, messing it up. The sky was almost completely dark now. Clouds were moving fast across it.

No one has shined a light in my face and asked me what I’m doing here. No dog has barked. The gate was open. The days of the body snatchers are over. If I came up here with a pick and a shovel—

He snapped back to reality. He was just playing a dangerous game with himself if he thought that Pleasantview was unguarded at night. What if he was found up to his waist in his son’s new grave by the caretaker or the watchman? It might not get in the papers, but then again it might. He could be charged with a crime. What crime? Grave robbing? Not likely. Damaging property would be more likely. And whether it was in the paper or not, people would find out. People would talk; it was too good a story not to tell: Local doctor is caught digging up his two-year-old son, who recently died in a car accident. He would lose his job. Even if he didn't, Rachel would be scared by the rumors, and Ellie might be teased at school until her life was miserable. He might have to prove he was sane to avoid charges.

But I could bring Gage back to life! Gage could live again!

Did he really believe it?

The truth was, he did. He had told himself over and over, both before Gage's death and after, that Church wasn't really dead, just stunned. That Church had dug his way out and come home. A children's story with scary parts—Winnie the Pooh. A master unknowingly piles stones over a living animal. The loyal animal digs out and comes home. Fine. Except it wasn't true. Church had been dead. The Micmac burial ground had brought him back.

He sat by Gage's grave, trying to put all the facts in order as logically as this dark magic allowed.

Timmy Baterman, now. First, did he believe the story? And second, did it matter?

Despite how easy it would be, he believed most of it. It was clear that if a place like the Micmac burial ground existed (and it did) and if people knew about it (and some of the older people in Ludlow did), then sooner or later someone would try it. Human nature, as Louis understood it, made it hard to believe it had stopped at a few pets and valuable animals.

All right, then—did he also believe that Timmy Baterman had turned into some kind of all-knowing demon?

That was a harder question, and he was careful because he didn't want to believe it, and he had seen what that kind of thinking could lead to before.

No, he didn't want to believe Timmy Baterman had been a demon, but he wouldn't—absolutely couldn't—let what he wanted affect his judgment.

Louis thought about Hanratty, the bull. Hanratty, Jud said, had turned mean. So, in his way, had Timmy Baterman. Hanratty had later been "put down" by the same man who had somehow dragged the bull's body up to the Micmac burial ground on a sled. Timmy Baterman had been "put down" by his father.

But because Hanratty had gone bad, did that mean all animals went bad? No. Hanratty the bull didn't prove that was always the case; Hanratty was actually an exception. Look back at the other animals—Jud's dog Spot, the old woman's parakeet, Church himself. They had all come back changed, and the change had been obvious in all cases, but in the case of Spot, at least, the change hadn't been so great that Jud had hesitated to recommend the process of… of…

(resurrection)

Yes, of resurrection to a friend years later. Of course, later he had tried to justify, and beat around the bush, and had said a lot of scary, confused nonsense that wasn't even real philosophy.

How could he refuse this chance—this unbelievable chance—because of the Timmy Baterman story? One example didn't prove anything.

You’re twisting the facts to fit what you want to believe, his mind argued. At least be honest about the change in Church. Even if you don't count the animals—the mice and the birds—what about how he is now? Confused… that's the best word, it says it all. The day we were out with the kite. Remember how Gage was that day? How full of life, reacting to everything? Wouldn't it be better to remember him like that? Do you want to bring back a zombie from a cheap horror movie? Or even just a mentally slow little boy? A boy who eats with his hands and stares blankly at the TV and will never learn to write his name? What did Jud say about his dog? “It was like washing a piece of meat.” Is that what you want? A piece of living meat? And even if you're okay with that, how do you explain your son's return to your wife? To your daughter? To Steve Masterton? To the world? What happens when Missy Dandridge sees Gage riding his tricycle in the yard? Can't you hear her screams, Louis? Can't you see her scratching her face? What do you tell the reporters? What do you say when a TV crew from “Real People” shows up, wanting to film your resurrected son?

Did any of this matter, or was it just fear talking? Did he think these things couldn't be handled? That Rachel wouldn't greet her dead son with joy?

Yes, he knew Gage might come back… changed. But would that change how much he loved him? Parents loved children who were born blind, Siamese twins, children born with messed-up insides. Parents begged for mercy for children who had grown up to rape, murder, and torture innocent people.

Did he think he couldn't love Gage if Gage wore diapers until he was eight? If he didn't learn to read until he was twelve? If he never learned at all? Could he just throw his son away like a… a mistake, when there was another option?

But, Louis, God, you don't live alone! People will say—

He cut that thought off sharply. Of all the things to worry about now, public attention was the least important.

Louis looked down at Gage’s grave and felt a wave of awe and horror. Without thinking, his fingers had made a pattern in the dirt—a spiral.

He swept his hands through the dirt, erasing the pattern. Then he left Pleasantview, hurrying and feeling like he was trespassing, convinced he would be seen, stopped, and questioned at every turn.

He was late picking up his pizza, and even though it had been left on top of one of the big ovens, it was partly cold and greasy, tasting like cooked clay. Louis ate one slice and then threw the rest out the window, box and all, as he drove back to Ludlow. He wasn't usually one to litter, but he didn't want Rachel to see a mostly uneaten pizza in the trash at home. It might make her wonder if pizza was really what he had gone to Bangor for.

Louis started thinking about the timing and circumstances.

Time. Time could be extremely, even critically, important. Timmy Baterman had been dead for a while before his father could take him to the Micmac burial ground. Timmy was shot on the nineteenth… Timmy was buried—I might be wrong, but I think it was July twenty-second. Four or five days later, Marjorie Washburn… saw Timmy walking up the road.

Okay, say Bill Baterman did it four days after his son's burial… no. If he was going to guess, he should guess conservatively. Say three days. For the sake of argument, assume Timmy Baterman came back to life on July twenty-fifth. That was six days between the boy's death and his return, a conservative estimate. It might have been as long as ten days. For Gage, it had been four days. Time was slipping away, but he could still beat Bill Baterman's time by a lot. If…

If he could create similar conditions to those that brought Church back to life. Church had died at the perfect time, right? His family was away when Church was hit and killed. No one knew, except for him and Jud.

His family had been in Chicago.

For Louis, the final piece clicked into place.

"You want us to what?" Rachel asked, staring at him in shock.

It was a quarter to ten. Ellie was in bed. Rachel had taken another Valium after cleaning up from the funeral party ("funeral party" was another horrible phrase full of hidden contradictions, like "visiting hours," but there seemed to be no other way to describe how they had spent their afternoon) and had seemed dazed and quiet ever since he got back from Bangor… but this had gotten through to her.

“To go back to Chicago with your mother and father,” Louis repeated patiently. “They’re leaving tomorrow. If you call them now and Delta right after, you might get on their plane.”

“Louis, are you crazy? After you and my father fought—”

Louis heard himself speaking quickly and smoothly, which wasn't like him. It gave him a strange, cheap thrill. He felt like a substitute football player who suddenly gets the ball and runs for a seventy-yard touchdown, dodging players and thinking fast with surprising ease. He wasn't usually a good liar, and he hadn't planned this conversation, but now a stream of believable lies, half-truths, and clever excuses flowed out of him.

“The fight is one reason I want you and Ellie to go back with them. We need to fix things, Rachel. I knew it… felt it… at the funeral home. When the fight started, I was trying to make peace.”

“But this trip… I don’t think it’s a good idea, Louis. We need you. And you need us.” She looked at him doubtfully. “At least, I hope you need us. And we’re not in a good place to—”

“—not in a good place to stay here,” Louis said firmly. He felt like he was getting a fever. “I’m glad you need me, and I need you and Ellie. But right now, this is the worst place for you, honey. Gage is everywhere in this house, in every corner. For you and me, sure. But it’s even worse for Ellie, I think.”

He saw pain in her eyes and knew he had affected her. Part of him felt guilty for this easy victory. All the books he’d read about death said that a grieving person's first strong desire is to leave the place where it happened… but giving in to that urge can be harmful because it lets them avoid facing the new reality. The books said it was best to stay, to fight grief on its own ground until it faded into memory. But Louis couldn't risk that with his family at home. He had to get them away, at least for a while.

“I know,” she said. “It just… hits you everywhere. I moved the couch while you were in Bangor… I thought vacuuming would distract me… from things, and I found four of his little Matchbox cars under there, like they were waiting for him to come back and… you know, play with them…” Her voice trembled, then broke. Tears ran down her face. “And that’s when I took the second Valium because I started crying again, like now… oh, this is such a mess… hold me, Lou, please hold me?”

He held her, and he did it well, but he felt fake. His mind raced with ways to use her tears to his advantage. Some nice guy, huh? Oh well, let's go.

“How long does it last?” she cried. “Does it ever stop? If we could just have him back, Louis, I swear I’d watch him better, it would never happen, and just because that driver was speeding doesn’t excuse me—us. I didn’t know pain like this was possible, really. It comes, again and again, and it hurts so much, Louis, I can’t rest, even when I sleep. I dream it, over and over, I see him running to the road… and I scream at him…”

“Shhh,” he said. “Rachel, shhhh.”

She lifted her puffy face to him. “It wasn’t even like he was doing something wrong, Louis. It was just a game to him… the truck came at the wrong time… and Missy Dandridge called while I was still crying… and said she read in the Ellsworth American that the driver tried to kill himself.”

“He tried to hang himself in his garage. He’s in shock and very depressed, the paper said…”

He wasn’t on drugs. He didn’t have any speeding tickets before. He said that when he got to Ludlow, he just felt like speeding up. He said he didn’t even know why. So around and around it goes.”

He just felt like speeding up.

The place has a power…

The Goldmans were thrilled with Rachel’s idea. They weren’t so crazy about Louis joining them in three or four days, but in the end they wouldn’t have to worry about it, of course. Louis had no intention of going to Chicago. He had suspected that if there was a problem, it would be getting plane tickets so late. But luck was on his side there too. There were still seats available on Delta’s Bangor to Cincinnati flight, and a quick check showed two cancellations on a Cincinnati to Chicago flight. It meant that Rachel and Ellie would only be able to travel with the Goldmans as far as Cincinnati, but they would get to Chicago less than an hour later.

It’s almost like magic, Louis thought, hanging up the telephone, and Jud’s voice responded quickly, It’s been full of power before, and I’m scared...

Oh, get fucked, he told Jud’s voice rudely. I’ve learned to accept a lot of strange things in the last ten months, my good old friend. But am I ready to believe that a haunted piece of land can affect airline tickets? I don’t think so.

But wouldn't that be like murdering his son? Killing him again?

A voice inside tried to argue that it wasn't, but he wouldn't listen. He quickly shut the voice up.

Rachel was surrounded by clothes when he came upstairs. Blouses on the beds, bras over the backs of chairs, slacks on hangers hung over the doorknob. When Gage was nine months old, Louis had become very worried about the size of his head. It was way off Louis’s chart, which showed the normal head sizes for babies each month. At four months, Gage’s head size had started to get bigger than normal, and then it kept growing. He could hold his head up fine—which was a good sign—but Louis had still taken him to George Tardiff, who was maybe the best nerve doctor in the Midwest. Rachel had wanted to know what was wrong, and Louis had told her the truth: he was worried that Gage might have water on the brain. Rachel’s face had turned very white, but she had stayed calm.

“He seems normal to me,” she said.

Louis nodded. “He does to me too. But I don’t want to ignore this, babe.”

“No, you mustn’t,” she said. “We mustn’t.”

Tardiff had measured Gage’s skull and frowned. Tardiff poked two fingers at Gage’s face, Three Stooges style. Gage flinched. Tardiff smiled. Louis’s heart warmed a little. Tardiff gave Gage a ball to hold. Gage held it for a while and then dropped it. Tardiff picked up the ball and bounced it, watching Gage’s eyes. Gage’s eyes followed the ball.

“I’d say there’s a fifty-fifty chance he’s hydrocephalic,” Tardiff said to Louis in his office later. “No—the odds may actually be a bit higher than that. If so, it’s mild. He seems very alert. The new shunt operation should take care of the problem easily… if there is a problem.”

“A shunt means brain surgery,” Louis said.

“Minor brain surgery.”

Louis had looked into the process soon after he began to worry about the size of Gage’s head, and the shunt operation, designed to drain excess fluid, had not looked very minor to him. But he kept his mouth shut, telling himself just to be grateful the operation existed at all.

“Of course,” Tardiff went on, “there’s still a good chance that your kid just has a real big head for a nine-month-old. I think a CAT-scan is the best place to start. Do you agree?”

Louis had agreed.

Gage spent a night in Our Sisters of Charity Hospital and was given general anesthesia. His sleeping head was put into a device that looked like a giant clothes dryer. Rachel and Louis waited downstairs while Ellie spent the day at Grandma and Grandda’s, watching “Sesame Street” nonstop on Grandda’s new video recorder. For Louis, those had been long, gray hours in which he found himself adding up possibilities of varying horribleness and comparing results. Death under general anesthesia, death during a shunt operation, mild slowness as a result of hydrocephalus, extreme slowness as a result of same, epilepsy, blindness… oh, there were all sorts of possibilities. For really complete disaster plans, Louis remembered thinking, see your local doctor.

Tardiff had come into the waiting room around five o’clock. He had three cigars. He stuck one in Louis’s mouth, one in Rachel’s (she was too stunned to argue), and one in his own.

“The kid is fine. No water on the brain.”

“Light this thing,” Rachel had said, crying and laughing at the same time. “I’m going to smoke it till I throw up.”

Smiling, Tardiff lit their cigars.

God was saving him for Route 15, Dr. Tardiff, Louis thought now.
