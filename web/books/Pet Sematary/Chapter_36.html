Pet Sematary

Chapter 36

It’s probably wrong to think there’s a limit to the horror a person can feel. It seems the worse things get, the more awful they become. It's hard to admit, but life often shows that when things are at their darkest, one horror leads to another, one bad thing leads to worse ones, until it feels like darkness covers everything. The scariest question is how much horror can someone handle and still stay sane. It almost goes without saying that these events can seem absurdly complicated. At some point, it starts to feel almost funny. Maybe that’s when sanity either saves itself or breaks down, when your sense of humor starts to come back.

Louis Creed might have thought about this if he could think clearly after his son Gage William Creed’s funeral on May 17th. But rational thought stopped at the funeral home, where a fight with his father-in-law led to something even worse—a final, shocking, dramatic event that destroyed what was left of Rachel’s control. That day's events ended when she was dragged, screaming, from the East Room of the Brookings-Smith Mortuary, where Gage lay in his closed coffin, and sedated in the foyer by Surrendra Hardu.

The irony was that she wouldn’t have had that final, horrible breakdown if the fight between Louis Creed and Mr. Irwin Goldman of Dearborn had happened during the morning visiting hours (10 to 11:30 A.M.) instead of the afternoon (2 to 3:30 P.M.). Rachel hadn’t been there in the morning; she couldn’t bring herself to come. She stayed home with Jud Crandall and Steve Masterton. Louis didn't know how he would have gotten through the past two days without them.

It was good for Louis—good for all three of them—that Steve had arrived when he did. Louis couldn't make any decisions, not even something as small as giving his wife a shot to calm her grief. Louis hadn’t even noticed that Rachel had almost gone to the morning viewing in her housecoat, buttoned wrong. Her hair was a mess, unwashed and tangled. Her eyes were empty, bulging from sockets so deep they looked like a skull. Her skin was pale and loose. She sat at the table, eating plain toast and talking in nonsense phrases. At one point she said, “About that Winnebago you want, Lou—” Louis had last mentioned buying a Winnebago in 1981.

Louis just nodded and ate his breakfast. He was eating Cocoa Bears. It had been one of Gage’s favorite cereals, and Louis wanted it this morning. It tasted awful, but he still wanted it. He was dressed in his best suit—not black, he didn’t have one, but it was dark gray. He had shaved, showered, and combed his hair. He looked fine, even though he was in shock.

Ellie wore jeans and a yellow shirt. She brought a picture to the table. It was a blown-up Polaroid that Rachel had taken with the camera Louis and the kids had given her for her birthday. It showed Gage, smiling in his ski parka, sitting on her sled as Ellie pulled him. Rachel had caught Ellie looking back and smiling at Gage. Gage was smiling back at her.

Ellie carried the picture, but she didn’t say much.

Louis couldn't see how his wife or daughter were doing. He ate breakfast, replaying the accident over and over, but in his mind, it ended differently. In his mind, he was faster, and Gage only got a spanking for not stopping when they called out.

Steve saw how Rachel and Ellie were doing. He told Rachel not to go to the morning viewing (though “viewing” wasn’t really the right word because the coffin was closed; if it was open, Louis thought, they’d all run screaming, including him) and told Ellie not to go at all. Rachel argued. Ellie just sat, quiet and serious, holding the picture of her and Gage.

Steve gave Rachel the shot she needed and Ellie a teaspoon of clear liquid. Ellie usually complained about taking medicine, but she drank this silently and without a face. By ten that morning, she was asleep in her bed, still holding the picture of her and Gage. Rachel sat in front of the TV, watching “Wheel of Fortune.” She answered Steve's questions slowly. She was drugged, but her face no longer had the crazy look that had worried and scared the P.A. that morning.

Jud had made all the arrangements with the same calm efficiency he had used for his wife three months before. But Steve Masterton took Louis aside just before Louis left for the funeral home.

“I’ll make sure she’s there this afternoon if she can handle it,” he told Louis.

“Okay.”

“The shot will have worn off by then. Your friend Mr. Crandall says he’ll stay with Ellie during the afternoon viewing hours—”

“Right.”

“—and play Monopoly or something with her—”

“Uh-huh.”

“But—”

“Right.”

Steve stopped. They were standing in the garage, Church’s territory, the place where he brought his dead birds and dead rats. The ones that Louis owned. Outside was May sunshine, and a robin hopped across the top of the driveway, as if it had important business somewhere. Maybe it did.

“Louis,” Steve said, “you’ve got to pull yourself together.”

Louis looked at Steve, politely questioning. Not much of what Steve had said had registered—he had been thinking that if he had been a little faster he could have saved his son’s life—but a little of this last got through.

“I don’t think you’ve noticed,” Steve said, “but Ellie isn’t talking. And Rachel has had such a bad shock that her sense of time seems to be messed up.”

“Right!” Louis said. More force in reply seemed needed here. He wasn’t sure why.

Steve put a hand on Louis’s shoulder. “Lou,” he said, “they need you more now than they ever have in their life. More than they ever will again, maybe. Please, man… I can give your wife a shot, but… you… see, Louis, you gotta… oh, Christ, Louis, what a fucking mess this is!”

Louis saw with something like alarm that Steve was starting to cry. “Sure,” he said, and in his mind he saw Gage running across the lawn toward the road. They were yelling at Gage to come back, but he wouldn’t—lately the game had been to run away from Mommy-Daddy—and then they were chasing him, Louis quickly outpacing Rachel, but Gage had a big head start, Gage was laughing, Gage was running away from Daddy—that was the game—and Louis was closing the distance but too slowly, Gage was running down the gentle slope of the lawn now to the edge of Route 15, and Louis prayed to God that Gage would fall down—when little kids ran fast, they almost always fell down because a person’s control over his legs didn’t get really good until he was maybe seven or eight. Louis prayed to God that Gage would fall down, fall down, yes, fall down bloody his nose crack his skull need stitches whatever, because now he could hear the drone of a truck coming toward them, one of those big ten-wheelers that went back and forth endlessly between Bangor and the Orinco plant in Bucksport, and he had screamed Gage’s name then, and he believed that Gage had heard him and tried to stop. Gage seemed to realize that the game was over, that your parents didn’t scream at you when it was just a game, and he had tried to put on the brakes, and by then the sound of the truck was very loud, the sound of it filled the world. It was thundering. Louis had thrown himself forward in a long flying tackle, his shadow tracking the ground beneath him as the shadow of the Vulture had tracked the white late-winter grass of Mrs. Vinton’s field that day in March, and he believed that the tips of his fingers had actually brushed the back of the light jacket Gage had been wearing, and then Gage’s forward motion had carried him out into the road, and the truck had been thunder, the truck had been sunlight on high chrome, the truck had been the deep-throated, shrieking bellow of an air horn, and that had been Saturday, that had been three days ago.

“I’m okay,” he said to Steve. “I ought to go now.”

“If you can get yourself together and help them,” Steve said, wiping at his eyes with the arm of his jacket, “you’ll be helping yourself too. The three of you have got to get through it together, Louis. That’s the only way. That’s all anybody knows.”

“That’s right,” Louis agreed, and in his mind it all started to happen again, only this time he leaped two feet farther right at the end, and grabbed the back of Gage’s jacket, and none of this was happening.

While this was happening in the East Room, Ellie was idly—and quietly—moving her Monopoly piece around the board with Jud Crandall. She shook the dice with one hand and held the Polaroid of her pulling Gage on her sled with the other.

Steve Masterton had decided it was okay for Rachel to go to the viewing that afternoon—a decision he later deeply regretted.

The Goldmans had flown into Bangor that morning and were staying at the Holiday Inn. Her father had called four times by noon, and Steve had to be increasingly firm—almost threatening—with the old man by the fourth call. Irwin Goldman wanted to come out, saying that nothing could keep him from his daughter in her time of need. Steve replied that Rachel needed this time before going to the funeral home to get over some of her initial shock. He didn’t know about all that, he said, but he knew one physician’s assistant who had no intention of letting anyone into the Creed home until Rachel had appeared in public on her own. After the viewing in the afternoon, Steve said, he would gladly let the relatives take over. Until then, he wanted her left alone.

The old man swore at him and hung up the phone, breaking the connection. Steve waited to see if Goldman would show up, but Goldman had apparently decided to wait. By noon, Rachel seemed a little better. She was at least aware of the time, and she had gone out to the kitchen to see if there was anything for sandwiches. People would probably want to come back to the house after, wouldn’t they? she asked Steve.

Steve nodded.

There was no bologna or cold roast beef, but there was a Butterball turkey in the freezer, and she put it on the counter to thaw. Steve looked into the kitchen a few minutes later and saw her standing by the sink, staring at the turkey on the counter and crying.

“Rachel?”

She looked at Steve. “Gage really liked these. He especially liked the white meat. It just occurred to me that he was never going to eat another Butterball turkey.”

Steve sent her upstairs to get dressed—the final test of her ability to cope—and when she came down wearing a simple black dress belted at the waist and carrying a small black purse, Steve decided she was all right, and Jud agreed.

Steve drove her into town. He stood with Surrendra Hardu in the lobby of the East Room and watched Rachel walk down the aisle toward the flower-covered coffin like a ghost.

“How’s it going, Steve?” Surrendra asked quietly.

“Going fucking terrible,” Steve said in a low, harsh voice. “How do you think it’s going?”

“I thought it was probably going fucking terrible,” Surrendra said and sighed.

The trouble really started at the morning viewing, when Irwin Goldman refused to shake hands with his son-in-law.

The sight of so many friends and relatives had actually pulled Louis out of his shock a little, had made him notice what was going on and be present. He had reached that stage of flexible grief that funeral directors are so used to managing and using to their advantage. Louis was moved around like a piece in a board game.

Outside the East Room was a small foyer where people could smoke and sit in comfortable, large chairs. The chairs looked like they might have come from a bargain sale at some old, failing English men’s club. Beside the door to the viewing room was a small easel, black metal with gold designs, and on this easel was a small sign that said simply GAGE WILLIAM CREED. Across this large white building, which looked deceptively like a comfortable old house, was an identical foyer, this one outside the West Room, where the sign on the easel read ALBERTA BURNHAM NEDEAU. At the back of the house was the Riverfront Room. The easel to the left of the door between the foyer and this room was blank; it was not in use on this Tuesday morning. Downstairs was the coffin showroom, each model lit by a small spotlight on the ceiling. If you looked up—Louis had, and the funeral director had frowned at him—it looked like there were many strange animals perched up there.

Jud had come with him on Sunday, the day after Gage had died, to choose a coffin. They had gone downstairs, and instead of turning right into the coffin showroom, Louis, confused, had continued straight down the hallway toward a plain white swinging door, the kind you see between restaurant dining rooms and the kitchen. Both Jud and the funeral director had said quickly and at the same time, “Not that way,” and Louis had followed them away from that swinging door obediently. He knew what was behind that door though. His uncle had been an undertaker.

The East Room was furnished with neat rows of folding chairs—the expensive ones with soft seats and backs. At the front, in an area that seemed like a combination of church and garden, was Gage’s coffin. Louis had picked the American Casket Company’s rosewood model—Eternal Rest, it was called. It was lined with soft pink silk. The mortician agreed that it was a beautiful coffin and apologized that he did not have one with a blue lining. Louis responded that he and Rachel had never cared about such things. The mortician nodded. The mortician asked Louis if he had thought about how he would pay for Gage’s funeral. If not, he said, he could take Louis into his office and quickly go over three of their more popular plans—

In Louis’s mind, an announcer suddenly spoke up cheerfully: I got my kid’s coffin free, for Raleigh coupons!

Feeling like he was in a dream, he said, “I’m going to pay for everything with my MasterCard.”

“Fine,” the mortician said.

The coffin was only four feet long—a tiny coffin. Yet it cost over six hundred dollars. Louis guessed it was on supports, but the flowers hid them, and he didn’t want to get too close. The flower smell made him want to throw up.

At the front of the aisle, by the door to the lobby, was a book on a stand. A pen was chained to the stand. The funeral director put Louis here to "greet his friends and family."

People were supposed to write their names and addresses in the book. Louis had no idea why this was done and didn't ask now. He figured he and Rachel would keep the book after the funeral. That seemed the strangest thing. He had a high school yearbook, a college yearbook, and a med school yearbook. There was also a wedding book, with MY WEDDING DAY stamped on the cover, starting with Rachel trying on her veil with her mother and ending with a photo of two pairs of shoes outside a hotel room. There was also a baby book for Ellie, but they stopped filling it out quickly. It was too cute, with spaces for MY FIRST HAIRCUT (add a lock of hair) and WHOOPS! (add a picture of baby falling).

Now, this book would be added to the others. What should they call it?

Louis wondered as he stood beside the stand, waiting for people to arrive. MY DEATHBOOK? FUNERAL AUTOGRAPHS? THE DAY WE BURIED GAGE? Or something more serious, like A DEATH IN THE FAMILY?

He turned the book to its cover, which was like the wedding book's cover.

The cover was blank.

As expected, Missy Dandridge arrived first that morning. Good-hearted Missy had watched Ellie and Gage many times. Louis remembered that Missy had taken the kids the night Victor Pascow died. She had taken the kids, and Rachel had made love to him, in the tub and then in bed.

Missy was crying hard. When she saw Louis's calm face, she cried more and reached for him. Louis hugged her, realizing that this was how it worked—some kind of human connection that eased the pain of loss, releasing it, breaking up the shock with sorrow.

"I'm so sorry," Missy was saying, brushing her dark blond hair back from her pale face. "Such a dear sweet little boy. I loved him so much, Louis, I’m so sorry, it's a terrible road, I hope they put that truck driver in jail forever, he was going way too fast, he was so sweet, so dear, so bright, why would God take Gage, I don't know, we can't understand, can we, but I'm sorry, sorry, so sorry."

Louis comforted her, held her and comforted her. He felt her tears on his collar, the press of her breasts against him. She wanted to know where Rachel was, and Louis told her that Rachel was resting. Missy promised to go see her and that she would sit with Ellie anytime, for as long as they needed her. Louis thanked her.

She had started away, still sniffing, her eyes redder than ever above her black handkerchief. She was moving toward the coffin when Louis called her back. The funeral director, whose name Louis could not even remember, had told him to have them sign the book, and he was determined to have them do it.

"Mystery guest, sign in please," he thought and almost burst into loud, hysterical laughter.

It was Missy's sad, heartbroken eyes that drove the laughter away.

"Missy, would you sign the book?" he asked her, and because something else seemed to be needed, he added, "For Rachel."

"Of course," she said. "Poor Louis and poor Rachel." And suddenly Louis knew what she was going to say next, and for some reason he dreaded it; yet it was coming, unavoidable, like a black bullet from a killer's gun, and he knew that he would be struck over and over by this bullet in the next endless ninety minutes, and then again in the afternoon, while the wounds of the morning were still bleeding:

"Thank God he didn't suffer, Louis. At least it was quick."

"Yes, it was quick, all right," he thought about saying to her—ah, how that would shatter her face all over again, and he felt a strong urge to do it, to simply throw the words into her face. "It was quick, no doubt about that, that's why the coffin's closed, nothing could have been done about Gage even if Rachel and I liked dressing up dead relatives in their best like store mannequins and putting makeup on their faces. It was quick, Missy-my-dear, one minute he was there on the road and the next minute he was lying in it, but way down by the Ringers' house. It hit him and killed him and then it dragged him and you better believe it was quick. A hundred yards or more all told, the length of a football field. I ran after him, Missy, I was screaming his name over and over again, almost as if I expected he would still be alive, me, a doctor. I ran ten yards and there was his baseball cap and I ran twenty yards and there was one of his Star Wars sneakers, I ran forty yards and by then the truck had run off the road and the trailer had jackknifed in that field beyond the Ringers’ barn. People were coming out of their houses and I went on screaming his name, Missy, and at the fifty-yard line there was his jumper, it was turned inside-out, and on the seventy-yard line there was the other sneaker, and then there was Gage."

Suddenly the world went gray. Everything disappeared from his view. Dimly he could feel the corner of the stand which held the book digging into his palm, but that was all.

“Louis?” Missy’s voice. Far away. The faint sound of pigeons in his ears.

“Louis?” Closer now. Worried.

The world came back into focus.

“Are you okay?”

He smiled. “Fine,” he said. “I’m okay, Missy.”

She signed for herself and her husband—Mr. and Mrs. David Dandridge—in neat, round handwriting; she added their address—Rural Box 67, Old Bucksport Road—and then looked quickly at Louis before looking away, as if just writing her address on the road where Gage had died was a crime.

“Take care, Louis,” she whispered.

David Dandridge shook his hand and mumbled something unclear, his large adam’s apple moving up and down. Then he hurried after his wife down the aisle for the usual look at a coffin made in Storyville, Ohio, a place Gage had never been and no one knew him.

After the Dandridges, they all came, moving slowly in a line, and Louis greeted them, their handshakes, their hugs, their tears. His collar and the upper sleeve of his dark gray suit coat quickly became damp. The smell of the flowers began to reach even the back of the room, filling the place with the smell of a funeral. It was a smell he remembered from his childhood—that sweet, heavy, mortuary smell of flowers. Louis was told thirty-two times how lucky it was that Gage hadn’t suffered. He was told twenty-five times that God works in mysterious ways. Ending the line was he’s with the angels now, a total of twelve times.

It began to bother him. Instead of these little sayings losing what little meaning they had (like your own name loses its meaning if you say it over and over), they seemed to hit harder each time, digging deeper. By the time his mother-in-law and father-in-law showed up, he felt like a beaten-up boxer.

His first thought was that Rachel had been right—and how right. Irwin Goldman had indeed aged. He was—what? Fifty-eight, fifty-nine? Today he looked like a carved and serious seventy. He looked almost ridiculously like Israel’s Prime Minister Menachem Begin with his bald head and Coke-bottle glasses. Rachel had told Louis that Goldman had aged when she came back from her Thanksgiving trip, but Louis had not expected this. Of course, he thought, maybe it hadn’t been this bad at Thanksgiving. The old man hadn’t lost one of his two grandchildren at Thanksgiving.

Dory walked beside him, her face almost hidden under two—maybe three—layers of heavy black netting. Her hair was fashionably blue, the color that older, wealthy American women liked. She held her husband’s arm. All Louis could really see behind the veil was the shine of her tears.

Suddenly he decided it was time to forgive and forget. He could not hold the old grudge any longer. Suddenly it was too heavy. Maybe it was the combined weight of all those clichés.

“Irwin. Dory,” he murmured. “Thank you for coming.”

He gestured with his arms, as if to shake hands with Rachel’s father and hug her mother at the same time, or maybe even to hug them both. Either way he felt his own tears start for the first time, and for a moment he had the crazy idea that they could fix everything, that Gage would do that much for them in his dying, as if this were some romantic novel where death led to reconciliation, where it could cause something better than this endless, stupid, grinding pain which just went on and on and on.

Dory started toward him, gesturing, maybe starting to hold out her own arms. She said something—”Oh, Louis…” and something else that was mumbled—and then Goldman pulled his wife back. For a moment the three of them stood in a scene that no one noticed except themselves (unless maybe the funeral director, standing quietly in the far corner of the East Room, saw—Louis figured that Uncle Carl would have seen), Louis with his arms partly outstretched, Irwin and Dory Goldman standing as stiff and straight as a couple on a wedding cake.

Louis saw that there were no tears in his father-in-law’s eyes; they were bright and clear with hate (does he think I killed Gage to hurt him? Louis wondered). Those eyes seemed to size Louis up, to find him the same small and useless man who had taken his daughter away and brought her to this sorrow… and then to dismiss him. His eyes shifted to Louis’s left—to Gage’s coffin, in fact—and only then did they soften.

Still Louis made one last try. “Irwin,” he said. “Dory. Please. We have to work together on this.”

“Louis,” Dory said again—kindly, Louis thought—and then they were past him, Irwin Goldman maybe pulling his wife along, not looking to the left or the right, certainly not looking at Louis Creed. They approached the coffin, and Goldman fumbled a small black skullcap out of his suit coat pocket.

You didn’t sign the book, Louis thought, and then a silent surge of such bitter acid rose through his stomach that his face twisted in pain.

The morning viewing ended at last. Louis called home. Jud answered and asked him how it had gone. All right, Louis said. He asked Jud if he could talk to Steve.

“If she can dress herself, I’m going to let her come this afternoon,” Steve said. “Okay by you?”

“Yes,” Louis said.

“How are you, Lou? No bullshit and straight on—how are you?”

“All right,” Louis said briefly. “Coping.” I had all of them sign the book. All of them except Dory and Irwin, and they wouldn’t.

“All right,” Steve said. “Look, shall we meet you for lunch?”

Lunch. Meeting for lunch. This seemed like such a strange idea that Louis thought of the science fiction novels he had read as a teenager—novels by Robert A. Heinlein, Murray Leinster, Gordon R. Dickson. The natives here on Planet Quark have an odd custom when one of their children dies, Lieutenant Abelson: they “meet for lunch.” I know how grotesque and barbaric that sounds, but remember, this planet has not been changed to be like Earth yet.

“Sure,” Louis said. “What’s a good restaurant for halftime between funeral viewings, Steve?”

“Take it easy, Lou,” Steve said, but he didn’t seem entirely displeased. In this state of crazy calm, Louis felt better able to see into people than ever before in his life. Perhaps it was an illusion, but right now he suspected Steve was thinking that even a sudden burst of sarcasm, squirted out like an abrupt mouthful of bile, was preferable to his earlier state of disconnection.

“Don’t worry,” he said to Steve now. “What about Benjamin’s?”

“Sure,” Steve said. “Benjamin’s would be fine.”

He had made the call from the funeral director's office. Now, as Louis walked past the East Room on his way out, he saw that it was almost empty. Irwin and Dory Goldman sat in the front row, heads bowed. They looked to Louis as if they might sit there forever.

Benjamin’s was the right choice. Bangor was a town where people ate lunch early, and around one o’clock it was almost deserted. Jud had come with Steve and Rachel, and the four of them ate fried chicken. At one point, Rachel went to the ladies’ room and stayed there so long that Steve became worried. He was about to ask a waitress to check on her when she came back to the table, her eyes red.

Louis picked at his chicken and drank a lot of Schlitz beer. Jud drank the same amount, not talking much.

Their four meals were mostly uneaten. Louis, with his unusually clear understanding, saw the waitress, a heavy girl with a pretty face, wondering whether to ask if their meals were all right. She looked again at Rachel’s red eyes and decided it would be the wrong question. Over coffee, Rachel said something so suddenly and bluntly that it shocked them all—especially Louis, who was finally getting sleepy from the beer. “I’m going to give his clothes to the Salvation Army.”

“Are you?” Steve said after a moment.

“Yes,” Rachel said. “They can still be used. All his sweaters… his corduroy pants… his shirts. Someone will be glad to have them. They’re all in good condition. Except for the ones he was wearing, of course. They’re… ruined.”

The last word was a choked sob. She tried to drink coffee, but couldn’t. A moment later, she was crying into her hands.

There was a strange moment then. There were crossing lines of tension then. They all seemed to focus on Louis. He felt this with the same clear understanding he’d had all day, and this was the clearest and surest of all. Even the waitress felt those lines of awareness coming together. He saw her stop at a table near the back where she was putting down placemats and silverware. For a moment, Louis was puzzled, and then he understood: they were waiting for him to comfort his wife.

He couldn’t do it. He wanted to do it. He knew it was his job to do it. But he couldn’t. It was the cat that stopped him. Suddenly and for no good reason. The cat. The damn cat. Church with his ripped mice and the birds he had killed. When he found them, Louis had cleaned up the messes right away, without complaining or saying anything, certainly without arguing. He had, after all, bought them. But had he bought this?

He saw his fingers. Louis saw his fingers. He saw his fingers lightly brushing Gage’s jacket. Then Gage’s jacket was gone. Then Gage was gone.

He looked into his coffee cup and let his wife cry beside him, without comfort.

After a moment—it was probably a short time, but it felt long then and later—Steve put an arm around her and hugged her gently. He looked at Louis with angry eyes. Louis turned away toward Jud, but Jud was looking down, as if ashamed. There was no help there.
