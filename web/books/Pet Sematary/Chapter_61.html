Pet Sematary

Chapter 61

Louis stopped on the side of the road to let a big truck carrying fertilizer speed past, then crossed to Jud’s house, his shadow stretching behind him. He held a can of cat food.

Church saw him coming and sat up, watching.

“Hi, Church,” Louis said, looking at the quiet house. “Want some food?”

He put the cat food on the trunk of the car and watched Church jump down and start eating. Louis put his hand in his pocket. Church looked at him, stiffening, as if he knew what was coming. Louis smiled and moved away from the car. Church ate again, and Louis took out a syringe. He unwrapped it and filled it with morphine. He put the bottle back in his jacket and walked to Church, who looked wary. Louis smiled at the cat and said, “Eat up, Church. Let’s go, okay?” He petted the cat, felt its back arch, and when Church ate again, Louis grabbed it and stuck the needle into its leg.

Church went wild, struggling, spitting, and clawing, but Louis held on and pushed the plunger all the way. Then he let go. The cat jumped off the car, hissing, eyes wild. The needle fell out and broke. Louis didn’t care. He had more.

The cat started for the road, then turned toward the house, as if remembering something. It got halfway and then began to wobble. It made it to the steps, jumped to the first one, then fell. It lay on its side at the bottom of the steps, breathing weakly.

Louis glanced into the car. If he needed more proof, he had it: Rachel’s purse, her scarf, and plane tickets spilling out of a folder.

When he turned to walk to the porch, Church’s side had stopped moving. Church was dead. Again.

Louis stepped over it and went up the porch steps.

“Gage?”

It was cool in the front hall. Cool and dark. The single word dropped into the silence like a stone down a deep well. Louis threw another.

“Gage?”

Nothing. Even the ticking of the clock in the parlor had stopped. This morning there had been no one to wind it.

But there were tracks on the floor.

Louis went into the living room. There was the smell of cigarettes, old and long gone. He saw Jud’s chair by the window. It was pushed to the side, as if he had gotten up quickly. There was an ashtray on the windowsill, and in it a neat roll of cigarette ash.

Jud sat here watching. Watching for what? For me of course, watching for me to come home. Only he missed me. Somehow he missed me.

Louis glanced at the four beer cans lined up in a neat row. Not enough to make him fall asleep, but maybe he had gotten up to go to the bathroom. Whatever it was, it was just a little too perfect to be completely accidental, wasn’t it?

The muddy tracks went toward the chair by the window. Mixed with the human tracks were a few faint, ghostly cat prints. As if Church had walked in and out of the grave dirt left by Gage’s small shoes. Then the tracks headed for the swinging door leading into the kitchen.

Heart pounding, Louis followed the tracks.

He pushed the door open and saw Jud’s feet sticking out, his old green work pants, his checked flannel shirt. The old man was lying flat in a large puddle of drying blood.

Louis put his hands over his face, as if to block what he was seeing. But he couldn't; he saw eyes, Jud’s eyes, open, seeming to blame him, maybe even blaming himself for starting this.

But did he? Louis wondered. Did he really do that?

Jud had been told by Stanny B., and Stanny B. had been told by his father, and Stanny B.’s father had been told by his father, the last trader to the Indians, a Frenchman from the north when Franklin Pierce was President.

“Oh Jud, I’m so sorry,” he whispered.

Jud’s empty eyes stared at him.

“So sorry,” Louis repeated.

His feet seemed to move on their own, and he was suddenly thinking about last Thanksgiving, not the night he and Jud had taken the cat up to the Pet Sematary and beyond, but the turkey dinner Norma had made, all of them laughing and talking, the two men drinking beer and Norma with a glass of white wine, and she had taken the white lawn tablecloth from the lower drawer as he was now, but she had put it on the table and held it down with lovely pewter candlesticks, while he—

Louis watched it fall over Jud’s body like a parachute, hiding that dead face. Almost at once, tiny rose-shaped spots of deep, dark red began to stain the white lawn.

“I’m sorry,” he said for a third time. “So sor—”

Then something moved above, something scraped, and he stopped speaking. It was soft, it was quiet, but it was done on purpose. Yes, he was sure of it. A sound he was meant to hear.

His hands wanted to shake, but he wouldn't let them. He walked to the kitchen table with its checkered oilcloth and reached into his pocket. He took out three more Becton-Dickson syringes, removed their paper covers, and lined them up. He took out three more vials and filled each syringe with enough morphine to kill a horse—or Hanratty the bull, if needed. He put them back in his pocket.

He left the kitchen, crossed the living room, and stood at the bottom of the stairs.

"Gage?"

From somewhere in the shadows above came a giggle—a cold, dark laugh that made Louis's skin crawl.

He started up.

It was a long walk to the top of those stairs. He could picture a man about to be executed taking a walk almost as long (and as horribly short) to the gallows with his hands tied, knowing he would lose control when he couldn't even whistle.

He reached the top at last, one hand in his pocket, staring at the wall. How long did he stand there? He didn't know. He could feel his sanity starting to break down. It was a real feeling, a true thing. It was interesting. He imagined a tree weighed down with ice in a bad storm would feel this way—if trees could feel anything—right before falling over. It was interesting… and kind of funny.

"Gage, want to go to Florida with me?"

That giggle again.

Louis turned and saw his wife, to whom he had once brought a rose in his teeth, lying halfway down the hall, dead. Her legs were spread out like Jud’s had been. Her back and head were bent at an angle against the wall. She looked like a woman who had fallen asleep while reading in bed.

He walked toward her.

Hello, darling, he thought, you came home.

Blood had splashed the wallpaper in strange, silly shapes. She had been stabbed a dozen times, two dozen, who knew? His scalpel had done this.

Suddenly he saw her, really saw her, and Louis Creed began to scream.

His screams echoed loudly through the house where only death now lived and walked. Eyes wide, face pale, hair standing up, he screamed; the sounds came from his sore throat like the bells of hell, terrible cries that meant the end not of love but of his mind; all the awful images rushed into his head at once. Victor Pascow dying on the infirmary floor, Church coming back with bits of green plastic in his whiskers, Gage’s baseball cap lying in the road, full of blood, but most of all that thing he had seen near Little God Swamp, the thing that had pushed the tree over, the thing with the yellow eyes, the Wendigo, creature of the north, the dead thing whose touch brings awful desires.

Rachel had not just been killed.

Something had been… something had been at her.

(!CLICK!)

That click was in his head. It was the sound of something breaking and burning out for good, the sound of lightning striking right on target, the sound of a door opening.

He looked up, dazed, the scream still stuck in his throat, and there was Gage at last, his mouth bloody, his chin dripping, his lips pulled back in a horrible grin. In one hand he held Louis’s scalpel.

As he brought it down, Louis pulled back without thinking. The scalpel zipped past his face, and Gage lost his balance. He is as clumsy as Church, Louis thought. Louis kicked his feet out from under him. Gage fell awkwardly, and Louis was on him before he could get up, straddling him, one knee holding down the hand with the scalpel.

“No,” the thing under him gasped. Its face twisted. Its eyes were evil, like an insect's in their dumb hate. “No, no, no—”

Louis grabbed for one of the syringes, got it out. He had to be quick. The thing under him was like a slippery fish and it wouldn’t let go of the scalpel no matter how hard he pressed on its wrist. And its face seemed to change as he looked at it. It was Jud’s face, dead and staring; it was the damaged face of Victor Pascow, eyes rolling blankly; it was, like a mirror, Louis’s own, pale and crazy. Then it changed again and became the face of that thing in the woods—the low forehead, the dead yellow eyes, the tongue long and split, grinning and hissing.

“No, no, no-no-no—”

It bucked under him. The syringe flew out of Louis’s hand and rolled a short way down the hall. He reached for another, got it out, and jammed it straight into Gage’s lower back.

It screamed under him, body arching, nearly throwing him off. Grunting, Louis got the third syringe and jammed this one into Gage’s arm, pushing the plunger all the way down. He got off then and began to back slowly down the hallway. Gage slowly got to his feet and began to stumble toward him. After five steps, the scalpel fell from its hand. It hit the floor blade first and stuck in the wood, shaking. After ten steps, that strange yellow light in its eyes began to fade. After a dozen, it fell to its knees.

Now Gage looked up at him and for a moment Louis saw his son—his real son—his face sad and filled with pain.

“Daddy!” he cried, and then fell forward on his face.

Louis stood there for a moment, then went to Gage, moving carefully, expecting a trick. But there was no trick, no sudden grab with clawed hands. He slid his fingers down Gage’s throat, found the pulse, and held it. He was a doctor for the last time in his life, checking the pulse, checking until there was nothing, nothing inside, nothing outside.

When it was finally over, Louis got up and walked slowly down the hall to a distant corner. He crouched there, curling into a ball, squeezing himself into the corner, tighter and tighter. He realized he could make himself smaller by putting a thumb in his mouth, so he did.

He stayed like that for over two hours… and then, slowly, a dark and believable idea came to him. He took his thumb out of his mouth. It made a small popping sound. Louis got himself

(hey-ho let’s go)

moving again.

In the room where Gage had hidden, he pulled the sheet off the bed and carried it into the hall. He wrapped his wife’s body in it, gently, with love. He was humming without realizing it.

He found gasoline in Jud’s garage. Five gallons of it in a red can next to the Lawnboy. More than enough. He started in the kitchen where Jud still lay under the Thanksgiving tablecloth. He soaked it, then went into the living room with the can still turned over, spraying yellow gas over the rug, the sofa, the magazine rack, the chairs, and then out into the downstairs hall and toward the back bedroom. The smell of gas was strong and heavy.

Jud’s matches were by the chair where he had kept his pointless watch, on top of his cigarettes. Louis took them. At the front door, he tossed a lit match back over his shoulder and stepped out. The heat was instant and fierce, making the skin on his neck feel too tight. He shut the door carefully and only stood on the porch for a moment, watching the orange glow behind Norma’s curtains. Then he crossed the porch, pausing for a moment, remembering the beers he and Jud had drunk here long ago, listening to the soft, growing roar of fire inside the house.

Then he stepped out.
