Pet Sematary

Chapter 10

“Hope Ellie didn’t take it too hard,” Jud Crandall said. Louis thought, not for the first time, that the man had an odd—and uncomfortable—way of knowing exactly what was bothering him.

He, Jud, and Norma Crandall sat on the Crandalls’ porch in the cool evening, drinking iced tea instead of beer. On Route 15, traffic was heavy with people going home after the weekend. Louis figured they knew this might be the last good weekend of the summer. Tomorrow, he'd be back to work at the University of Maine infirmary. Students had been arriving all day yesterday and today, filling apartments in Orono and dorms on campus, making their beds, seeing old friends, and probably complaining about early classes and cafeteria food. Rachel had been cold to him all day—no, freezing was more like it—and he knew that when he went home tonight, she'd already be in bed, Gage sleeping with her, the two of them so far over on her side that the baby might fall off. His side of the bed would have grown to three quarters of the bed, all of it looking like a big, empty desert.

“I said I hoped—”

“Sorry,” Louis said. “Just thinking. She was a little upset, yeah. How did you know?”

“Seen it happen plenty of times,” Jud said. Jud gently took his wife’s hand and grinned at her. “Haven’t we, dear?”

“Lots and lots,” Norma Crandall said. “We like the children.”

“Sometimes that pet cemetery is the first time they really face death,” Jud said. “They see people die on TV, but they know it’s not real, like those old Westerns they used to show at the movies on Saturday afternoons. On TV and in those movies, they just hold their stomachs or chests and fall over. That place up on the hill seems a lot more real to most of them than all those movies and TV shows, you know.”

Louis nodded, thinking: Tell my wife that, why don’t you?

“Some kids aren’t affected at all, or at least it doesn’t show, but I’d guess most of them kind of… kind of take it home with them to think about later, like all the other stuff they collect. Most of them are fine. But some… you remember the little Holloway boy, Norma?”

She nodded. Ice chattered softly in the glass she held. Her glasses hung on her chest, and the headlights of a passing car lit up the chain for a moment. “He had such nightmares,” she said. “Dreams about dead bodies coming out of the ground and all sorts of things. Then his dog died—ate some poisoned bait, that’s all anyone in town could figure, wasn’t it, Jud?”

“Poisoned bait,” Jud said, nodding. “That’s what most people thought, yes. That was 1925. Billy Holloway was maybe ten then. Went on to become a state senator. Ran for the U.S. House of Representatives later, but he lost. That was just before Korea.”

“He and some of his friends had a funeral for the dog,” Norma remembered. “It was just a mutt, but he loved it a lot. I remember his parents were a little against the burying, because of the bad dreams and all, but it went off fine. Two of the bigger boys made a coffin, didn’t they, Jud?”

Jud nodded and finished his iced tea. “Dean and Dana Hall,” he said. “Them and that other kid Billy hung out with—I can’t remember his first name, but I’m sure he was one of the Bowie kids. You remember the Bowies that used to live up on Middle Drive in the old Brochette house, Norma?”

“Yes!” Norma said, as excited as if it had happened yesterday… and perhaps in her mind, it seemed that way. “It was a Bowie! Alan or Burt—”

“Or maybe it was Kendall,” Jud agreed. “Anyways, I remember they had a pretty good argument about who was going to be pallbearers. The dog wasn’t very big, so there was only room for two. The Hall boys said they should be the ones since they made the coffin, and also because they were twins—sort of a matching pair, you see. Billy said they didn’t know Bowser—that was the dog—well enough to be the pallbearers. ‘My dad says only close friends get to be pallbearers,’ was his argument, ‘not just any carpenter.’ Jud and Norma both laughed at this, and Louis grinned.

“They were just about ready to fight over it when Mandy Holloway, Billy’s sister, brought out the fourth volume of the Encyclopedia Britannica,” Jud said. “Her dad, Stephen Holloway, was the only doctor around here in those days, Louis, and they were the only family in Ludlow that could afford a set of encyclopedias.”

“They were also the first to have electric lights,” Norma broke in.

“Anyway,” Jud resumed, “Mandy came out all flustered, head up and acting important, all of eight years old, petticoats flying, that big book in her arms. Billy and the Bowie kid—I think it must have been Kendall, the one who crashed and burned up in Pensacola where they were training fighter pilots in early 1942—they were getting ready to take on the Hall twins over the privilege of carrying that poor old poisoned mutt up to the graveyard.”

Louis started giggling. Soon he was laughing out loud. He could feel the day's tension—old feelings left from the bitter argument with Rachel—beginning to loosen.

“So she says, ‘Wait! Wait! Look at this!’ And they all stop and look. And damn if she isn’t—”

“Jud,” Norma said, warningly.

“Sorry, dear; I get carried away telling stories, you know that.”

“I guess you do,” she said.

“And darned if she doesn’t have that book open to FUNERALS, and there’s a picture of Queen Victoria getting her last goodbye, and there are about forty—eleven people on each side of her coffin, some sweating and struggling to lift the thing, some just standing around in their funeral coats and fancy collars like they were waiting for the race to start. And Mandy says, ‘When it’s a ceremonial funeral of state, you can have as many as you want! The book says so!’ ”

“That solved it?” Louis asked.

“That did the trick. They ended up with about twenty kids, and damn if they didn’t look just like the picture Mandy had found, except maybe for the ruffles and tall hats. Mandy took charge, she did. Got them lined up and gave each of them a wildflower—a dandelion or a lady’s slipper or a daisy—and off they went. By golly, I always thought the country missed out when Mandy Holloway never got voted to the U.S. Congress.” He laughed and shook his head. “Anyway, that was the end of Billy’s bad dreams about the Pet Sematary. He mourned his dog and finished mourning and moved on. Which is what we all do, I guess.”

Louis thought again of Rachel’s near-hysteria.

“Your Ellie will get over it,” Norma said and shifted position. “You must be thinking that death is all we talk about around here, Louis. Jud and I are getting older, but I hope neither of us has gotten too morbid yet—”

“No, of course not, don’t be silly,” Louis said.

“—But it’s not a bad idea to know something about it. These days… I don’t know… no one wants to talk about it or think about it, it seems. They took it off TV because they thought it might hurt the children somehow… hurt their minds… and people want closed coffins so they don’t have to look at the body or say goodbye… it just seems like people want to forget it.”

“And at the same time they brought in cable TV with all those movies showing people”—Jud looked at Norma and cleared his throat—”showing people doing what people usually do in private,” he finished. “Funny how things change from one generation to the next, isn’t it?”

“Yes,” Louis said. “I suppose it is.”

“Well, we come from a different time,” Jud said, sounding almost apologetic “We knew death better. We saw the flu after the Great War, and mothers dying in childbirth, and children dying from infections and fevers that doctors seem to fix with ease these days. When me and Norma were young, if you got cancer, that was a death sentence. No radiation treatments back in the 1920s! Two wars, murders, suicides…”

He fell silent for a moment.

“We knew it as a friend and as an enemy,” he said finally. “My brother Pete died from a burst appendix in 1912, back when Taft was President. Pete was just fourteen, and he could hit a baseball farther than any kid in town. Back then, you didn’t need a college course to study death, or whatever they call it. Back then it came into the house and said hello, sometimes it ate with you, and sometimes you felt it bite you.”

This time Norma didn’t correct him; instead she nodded silently.

Louis stood up, stretched. “I have to go,” he said. “Big day tomorrow.”

“Yes, the fun begins for you tomorrow, doesn’t it?” Jud said, also standing. Jud saw Norma was also trying to get up and gave her a hand. She rose with a grimace.

“Bad tonight, is it?” Louis asked.

“Not so bad,” she said.

“Put some heat on it when you go to bed.”

“I will,” Norma said. “I always do. And Louis… don’t worry about Ellie. She’ll be too busy making new friends this fall to worry much about that old place. Maybe someday they’ll all go up and repaint some of the signs, or pull weeds, or plant flowers. Sometimes they do, when they feel like it. And she’ll feel better about it. She’ll start to get used to it.”

Not if my wife has anything to say about it.

“Come over tomorrow night and tell me how it went at the college, if you get the chance,” Jud said. “I’ll beat you at cribbage.”

“Well, maybe I’ll get you drunk first,” Louis said. “Shut you out completely.”

“Doc,” Jud said with great sincerity, “the day I get shut out at cribbage is the day I’d let a quack like you treat me.”

He left on their laughter and crossed the road to his own house in the late-summer dark.

Rachel was sleeping with the baby, curled up on her side of the bed in a fetal, protective position. He supposed she would get over it—there had been other arguments and times of coldness in their marriage, but this one was surely the worst of them all. He felt sad and angry and unhappy all at the same time, wanting to make things right but not sure how, not even sure that he should be the first to try. It was all so pointless—just a puff of wind somehow blown up to hurricane size by a trick of the mind. Other fights and arguments, yes, sure, but only a few as bitter as the one over Ellie’s tears and questions. He supposed it didn’t take many blows like that before the marriage was damaged… and then one day, instead of reading about it in a note from a friend (“Well, I suppose I should tell you before you hear it from someone else, Lou; Maggie and I are splitting…”) or in the newspaper, it was you.

He undressed to his shorts quietly and set the alarm for 6 A.M. Then he showered, washed his hair, shaved, and crunched up a Rolaid before brushing his teeth—Norma’s iced tea had given him acid indigestion. Or maybe it was coming home and seeing Rachel way over on her side of the bed. Territory is what defines everything else, hadn’t he read that in some college history class?

Everything done, the evening neatly finished, he went to bed… but couldn’t sleep. Something else bothered him. The last two days replayed in his mind as he listened to Rachel and Gage breathing almost together. GEN. PATTON… HANNAH THE BEST DOG THAT EVER LIVED… MARIA OUR PET RABIT… Ellie, angry. I don’t want Church to ever be dead!… He’s not God’s cat! Let God have His own cat! Rachel, just as angry. You as a doctor should know… Norma Crandall saying It just seems like people want to forget it… And Jud, his voice very sure, very certain, a voice from another time: Sometimes it ate with you and sometimes you felt it bite you.

And that voice mixed with his mother’s, who had lied to Louis Creed about sex at four but told him the truth about death at twelve, when his cousin Ruthie died in a stupid car accident. She was crushed in her father’s car by a kid who found the keys in a Public Works Department payloader and decided to take it for a ride and then couldn’t stop it. The kid had only minor cuts; his Uncle Carl’s Fairlane was destroyed. She can’t be dead, he said to his mother’s plain statement. He heard the words, but he didn’t understand them. What do you mean, she’s dead? What are you talking about? And then: Who’s going to bury her? Because although Ruthie’s father, Louis’s uncle, was an undertaker, he couldn’t imagine that Uncle Carl would do it. Confused and afraid, he focused on this question. It was a puzzle, like who cuts the town barber’s hair.

I guess Donny Donahue will do it, his mother said. Her eyes were red; she looked tired. His mother looked worn out. He’s your uncle’s best friend in the business. Oh, but Louis… Sweet little Ruthie… I can’t stand to think she suffered… pray with me, will you, Louis? Pray with me for Ruthie. I need you to help me.

So they knelt in the kitchen, he and his mother, and they prayed, and the praying made it real to him; if his mother was praying for Ruthie Creed’s soul, then her body was gone. He imagined Ruthie coming to his thirteenth birthday party with her decaying eyeballs hanging on her cheeks and blue mold growing in her red hair, and this image caused not just horror but a terrible love.

He cried out in the worst mental pain of his life, “She can’t be dead! MOMMA, SHE CAN’T BE DEAD—I LOVE HER!”

And his mother’s reply, her voice flat but full of images: dead fields under a November sky, scattered rose petals brown and turning up at the edges, empty pools covered with algae, rot, decay, dust:

She is, my darling. I’m sorry, but she is. Ruthie is gone.

Louis shuddered, thinking, Dead is dead—what else do you need?

Suddenly Louis knew what he had forgotten to do, why he was still awake on this night before his new job, thinking about old griefs.

He got up, headed for the stairs, and then went to Ellie’s room. She was sleeping peacefully, mouth open, wearing her blue baby-doll pajamas that she had outgrown. My God, Ellie, he thought, you’re growing fast. Church lay between her legs, also asleep. Sorry for the joke.

Downstairs, a bulletin board hung by the phone, covered with messages, notes, and bills. At the top, Rachel had written in neat caps: THINGS TO PUT OFF AS LONG AS POSSIBLE. Louis found the phone book, looked up a number, and wrote it on a blank note. Below it, he wrote: Quentin L. Jolander, D.V.M.—call about Church—if Jolander doesn’t neuter animals, he will recommend someone.

He looked at the note, wondering if it was time, but knowing it was. Something real had to come from all this bad feeling, and he had decided sometime between this morning and tonight—without even knowing it—that he didn’t want Church crossing the road anymore, if he could prevent it.

His old feelings about it returned, the idea that neutering would make the cat less, turn him into a fat old tomcat too soon, happy just to sleep on the radiator until fed. He didn’t want Church like that. He liked Church as he was, lean and tough.

Outside in the dark, a big truck rumbled by on Route 15, and that settled it. He pinned up the note and went to bed.
