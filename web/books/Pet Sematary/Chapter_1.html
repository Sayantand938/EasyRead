Pet Sematary

Chapter 1

Louis Creed, who had lost his father at three and never knew a grandfather, didn't expect to find a father figure in his middle age, but that's what happened… though he called this man a friend, as a grown man must when he finds the man who should have been his father later in life. He met this man the evening he, his wife, and their two children moved into the big white house in Ludlow. Winston Churchill moved in with them. Church was his daughter Eileen’s cat.

The university search committee had been slow, the hunt for a house near the university had been stressful, and by the time they neared the house—all the landmarks were right… like bad omens before a disaster, Louis thought grimly—they were all tired, tense, and on edge. Gage was teething and fussy. He wouldn't sleep, no matter how much Rachel sang. She offered him her breast even though it wasn't his usual time. Gage knew his feeding schedule as well as she did—maybe better—and bit her with his new teeth. Rachel, still unsure about moving to Maine from Chicago, where she'd lived her whole life, burst into tears. Eileen joined her. In the back of the station wagon, Church paced restlessly, as he had for the three days it took to drive from Chicago. His yowling from the carrier had been bad, but his pacing after they let him loose in the car had been almost as upsetting.

Louis felt like crying. A wild idea came to him: he'd suggest they go back to Bangor for food while waiting for the moving van, and when his three loved ones got out, he'd speed away without looking back, the car burning gas. He'd drive south to Orlando, Florida, get a job at Disney World as a medic under a new name. Before hitting the highway, he'd dump the damn cat, too.

Then they rounded the last curve, and there was the house he'd only seen before. He'd flown out to see each of the seven they'd picked from photos after he got the job at the University of Maine, and this was the one he'd chosen: a big old New England house (newly sided and insulated; heating costs were high but not unreasonable), three big rooms downstairs, four up, a long shed for more rooms later—all surrounded by a big lawn, green even in the August heat.

Beyond the house was a large field for the children to play in, and beyond the field were woods that seemed endless. The property bordered state land, the realtor had said, and there would be no development soon. The local Native American tribe had claimed nearly eight thousand acres and the legal battle might last for years.

Rachel stopped crying. She sat up. “Is that—”

“That’s it,” Louis said. He felt uneasy—no, scared. He felt terrified. He had committed a big part of their lives for this; it wouldn’t be paid off until Eileen was seventeen.

He swallowed.

“What do you think?”

“I think it’s beautiful,” Rachel said, and that was a huge weight off his chest—and his mind felt clearer. He saw she wasn't joking; it was in how she looked at it as they turned into the paved driveway that curved around to the shed in back, her eyes taking in the empty windows, her mind already thinking about things like curtains and oilcloth for the cupboards, and who knew what else.

“Daddy?” Ellie said from the back seat. She had stopped crying, too. Even Gage had stopped fussing. Louis enjoyed the quiet.

“What is it, love?”

Her eyes, brown under dark blond hair in the rearview mirror, also looked over the house, the lawn, the roof of another house in the distance to the left, and the big field stretching up to the woods.

“Is this home?”

“It’s going to be, honey,” he said.

“Hooray!” she shouted, almost making his ear ring. And Louis, who could sometimes get very annoyed with Ellie, decided he didn’t care if he ever saw Disney World in Orlando.

He parked in front of the shed and turned off the car’s engine.

The engine ticked. In the silence, which felt very big after Chicago and the busy State Street and the Loop, a bird sang sweetly in the late afternoon.

“Home,” Rachel said softly, still looking at the house.

“Home,” Gage said, happy on her lap.

Louis and Rachel looked at each other. In the rearview mirror, Eileen’s eyes got wider.

“Did you—”

“Did he—”

“Was that—”

They all spoke at once, then all laughed together. Gage didn’t care; he just kept sucking his thumb. He had been saying “Ma” for almost a month and had tried to say something that might have been “Daaa” or just what Louis wanted to hear.

But this, maybe by chance, was a real word. Home.

Louis took Gage from his wife’s lap and hugged him.

That was how they came to Ludlow.
