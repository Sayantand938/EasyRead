Pet Sematary

Chapter 44

As Louis left the BIA terminal, a coldness settled over him. He realized he was really going to do this. His mind, which had been sharp enough to get him through med school mostly on a scholarship and what his wife made selling coffee and pastries from 5 to 11 A.M. six days a week, had taken over the problem and broken it down into pieces, as if it were just another exam—the biggest he had ever faced. And he planned to ace it, one hundred percent.

He drove to Brewer, the small city across the Penobscot River from Bangor. He found a parking spot across the street from Watson’s Hardware.

“Can I help you?” the clerk asked.

“Yes,” Louis said. “I’d like a heavy flashlight—one of the square ones—and something to cover the light.”

The clerk was a small, thin man with a high forehead and sharp eyes. He smiled now, but it wasn't a friendly smile. “Going hunting at night, are you?”

“I’m sorry?”

“Gonna use a light to hunt deer tonight?”

“Not at all,” Louis said, not smiling. “I don’t have a license for that.”

The clerk blinked and then decided to laugh. “In other words, mind your own business, huh? Well, look—you can’t cover one of those big lights completely, but you can get a piece of felt and poke a hole in the middle. It'll make the beam very narrow.”

“That sounds fine,” Louis said. “Thanks.”

“Sure. Anything else for you today?”

“Yes,” Louis said. “I need a pick, a shovel, and a spade. Short-handled shovel, long-handled spade. A strong piece of rope, eight feet long. A pair of work gloves. A canvas tarp, maybe eight by eight.”

“I can get all that,” the clerk said.

“I’ve got a septic tank to dig up,” Louis said. “It seems I’m breaking the zoning rules, and I’ve got some very nosy neighbors. I don’t know if covering my light will help, but I thought I’d try it. I could get a big fine.”

“Uh-oh,” the clerk said, “better get a clothespin for your nose while you’re at it.”

Louis laughed politely. His purchases cost $58.60. He paid cash.

As gas prices rose, they used the big station wagon less and less. It had a bad wheel-bearing for a while, but Louis kept putting off the repair. This was partly because he didn’t want to spend the two hundred it would probably cost, but mostly because it was a pain. Now, when he really needed the big old car, he didn’t dare risk it. The Civic was a hatchback, and Louis was worried about going back to Ludlow with the pick, shovel, and spade in there. Jud Crandall’s eyes were sharp, and he was smart too. He would know what was going on.

Then he realized there was no real reason to go back to Ludlow anyway. Louis crossed the Chamberlain Bridge back into Bangor and checked into the Howard Johnson’s Motor Lodge on the Odlin Road—again near the airport, again near Pleasantview Cemetery where his son was buried. He checked in under the name Dee Dee Ramone and paid cash for his room.

He tried to nap, thinking he would be grateful for the rest before tomorrow morning. Like in some old novel, there was hard work ahead of him tonight—enough to last a lifetime.

But his brain just wouldn't stop.

He lay on the generic motel bed under a plain motel print of pretty boats docked by a quaint old wharf in a pretty New England harbor, fully dressed except for his shoes, his wallet, coins, and keys on the night table beside him, his hands behind his head. He still felt cold and totally disconnected from his family, the places he knew so well, and even his work. This could have been any Howard Johnson's in the world—in San Diego, Duluth, Bangkok, or Charlotte Amalie. He was nowhere, and now and then a strange thought struck him: before he saw those familiar places and faces again, he would see his son.

His plan kept playing in his mind. He looked at it from every angle, examining it for weaknesses. He felt like he was walking on a narrow beam over a pit of insanity. Madness was all around him, softly fluttering like the wings of owls with big golden eyes: he was heading into madness.

The voice of Tom Rush echoed softly in his head: O death your hands are clammy… I feel them on my knees… you came and took my mother… won’t you come back after me?

Madness. Madness all around, close, hunting him.

He walked the balance beam of reason; he studied his plan.

Tonight, around eleven o'clock, he would dig up his son's grave, take the body from the coffin, wrap Gage in a piece of the tarp, and put it in the trunk of the Civic. He would put the coffin back and fill in the grave. He would drive to Ludlow, take Gage's body from the trunk… and take a walk. Yes, he would take a walk.

If Gage returned, the single path split into two possibilities. In one, he saw Gage returning as Gage, maybe stunned or slow or even mentally impaired (only deep down did Louis let himself hope that Gage would return whole, and just as he had been—but surely even that was possible, wasn’t it?), but still his son, Rachel’s son, Ellie’s brother.

In the other, he saw some kind of monster coming out of the woods behind the house. He had accepted so much that he didn't question the idea of monsters, or even demons, bodiless beings of evil from another world that might take over a reanimated body that had lost its original soul.

Either way, he and his son would be alone. And he would…

I will figure it out.

Yes. That's what he'd do.

I will figure it out, not just his body but his spirit too. I'll think about the trauma of the accident, which he might not even remember. Remembering what happened with Church, I'll expect some mental slowness, maybe a little, maybe a lot. I'll decide if we can bring Gage back into our family based on what I see in a day or two. And if he's too far gone—or if he comes back like Timmy Baterman did, like something evil—I'll kill him.

As a doctor, he felt he could easily kill Gage, if Gage was just a body holding something else, some other being. He wouldn't let himself be fooled by its cries or tricks. He'd kill it like he'd kill a rat with the plague. No need to make a big deal about it. A pill in water, maybe two or three. If he had to, a shot. He had morphine in his bag. The next night, he'd put the body back in Pleasantview and bury it again, just hoping he'd get lucky a second time (you don't even know if you'll get lucky once, he reminded himself). He thought about the easier and safer option of the Pet Sematary, but he didn't want his son up there. There were many reasons. A kid burying their pet years later might find the body—that was one reason. But the biggest reason was simpler. The Pet Sematary might be… too close.

After the reburial, he'd fly to Chicago and be with his family. Rachel and Ellie would never need to know about his failed experiment.

Then, thinking about the other way things could go—the way he desperately hoped for with all his love for his son: he and Gage would leave the house when he was done examining him, they'd leave at night. He'd take some papers with him and never come back to Ludlow. He and Gage would stay at a motel—maybe even this one he was lying in now.

The next morning, he'd cash out all their accounts, turning everything into American Express traveler's checks (don't leave home with your resurrected son without them, he thought) and cash. He and Gage would fly somewhere—Florida, most likely. From there, he'd call Rachel, tell her where he was, tell her to bring Ellie and get on a plane without telling her parents where she was going. Louis thought he could convince her. Don't ask questions, Rachel. Just come. Come now. Right away.

He'd tell her which motel they were at. She and Ellie would arrive in a rental car. He'd bring Gage to the door when they knocked. Maybe Gage would be wearing a bathing suit.

And then—

But he didn't dare think further than that; instead, he went back to the beginning of the plan and started going over it again. He figured that if things worked out, it would mean getting all the little details needed for new identities so Irwin Goldman couldn't use his money to find them. It could be done.

He dimly remembered arriving at the Ludlow house, tense, tired, and more than a little scared, and having some daydream about just driving down to Orlando and getting a job as a medic at Disney World. Maybe that wasn’t so crazy after all.

He pictured himself, dressed in white, reviving a pregnant woman who had foolishly gone on the Magic Mountain ride and had fainted. Stand back, stand back, give her some air, he heard himself saying, and the woman opened her eyes and smiled gratefully at him.

As his mind played out this pleasant daydream, Louis fell asleep. He slept as his daughter awoke in an airplane somewhere above Niagara Falls, screaming from a nightmare of clutching hands and stupid yet merciless eyes; he slept as the stewardess hurried down the aisle to see what was wrong; he slept as Rachel, totally upset, tried to calm her; he slept as Ellie cried over and over again: It’s Gage! Mommy! It’s Gage! it’s Gage! Gage is alive! Gage has got the knife from Daddy’s bag! Don’t let him get me! Don’t let him get Daddy!

He slept as Ellie finally quieted and lay shaking against her mother’s breast, her eyes wide and tearless, and as Dory Goldman thought what an awful thing all of this had been for Eileen, and how much she reminded Dory of Rachel after Zelda had died.

He slept and woke up at a quarter past five, with the afternoon light beginning to slant down toward the coming night.

Strange stuff, he thought dully and got up.
