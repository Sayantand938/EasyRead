Pet Sematary

Chapter 13

Then the room filled with people, like actors waiting for their lines. This made Louis feel even more unreal and lost. He had studied these feelings in psychology but never felt them himself, and their strength scared him. He guessed it was how someone would feel if someone slipped LSD into their drink.

Like a play just for me, he thought. The room is cleared so the dying Sibyl can give me a cryptic prophecy, and then everyone comes back as soon as he’s dead.

The candy-stripers clumsily brought in the hard stretcher used for spinal injuries. Joan Charlton followed, saying the campus police were coming. A young man had been hit by a car while jogging. Louis thought of the joggers he'd seen that morning and his stomach churned.

Behind Charlton came Steve Masterton with two Campus Security cops. “Louis, the people who brought Pascow in are…” He stopped and said sharply, “Louis, are you all right?”

“I’m okay,” he said and got up. He felt faint again, then it passed. He reached out. “Pascow is his name?”

One of the campus cops said, “Victor Pascow, according to the girl he was jogging with.”

Louis glanced at his watch and subtracted two minutes. From the room where Masterton had put the people who brought Pascow in, he heard a girl sobbing loudly. Welcome back to school, he thought. Have a nice semester. “Mr. Pascow died at 10:09 A.M.,” he said.

One of the cops wiped his mouth with the back of his hand.

Masterton said again, “Louis, are you really okay? You look terrible.”

Louis opened his mouth to answer, and one of the candy-stripers suddenly dropped her end of the hard stretcher and ran out, vomiting down the front of her pinafore. A phone began to ring. The girl who had been sobbing now began to scream the dead man’s name—”Vic! Vic! Vic!”—over and over. Chaos. Confusion. One of the cops was asking Charlton if they could have a blanket to cover him up, and Charlton was saying she didn’t know if she had the right to get one, and Louis found himself thinking of a line from Maurice Sendak: “Let the wild rumpus start!”

Those awful giggles rose in his throat again, and somehow he managed to hold them back. Had this Pascow really said the words Pet Sematary? Had this Pascow really spoken his name? Those were the things that were throwing him off balance, the things that had sent him spinning out of control. But already his mind seemed to be wrapping those few moments in a protective film—shaping, changing, disconnecting. Surely he had said something else (if he had indeed spoken at all), and in the shock and sad emotion of the moment, Louis had misunderstood it. More likely, Pascow had only moved his lips, as he had at first thought.

Louis looked for himself, for that part of himself that had caused the administration to give him this job over the other fifty-three applicants for the position. There was no one in charge here, no progress; the room was full of people milling around.

“Steve, go give that girl a sedative,” he said, and just saying the words made him feel better. It was as if he were in a rocketship taking off now, moving away from a tiny moon. Said moon being, of course, that irrational moment when Pascow had spoken. Louis had been hired to take charge; he was going to do it.

“Joan. Give the cop a blanket.”

“Doctor, we haven’t taken inventory—”

“Give it to him anyway. Then check on that candy-striper.” He looked at the other girl, who still held her end of the hard stretcher. She was staring at Pascow’s remains with a kind of hypnotized fascination. “Candy-striper!” Louis said sharply, and her eyes snapped away from the body.

“W-W-Wh—”

“What’s the other girl’s name?”

“W-Who?”

“The one who threw up,” he said, his voice hard.

“Juh-Juh-Judy. Judy DeLessio.”

“Your name?”

“Carla.” The girl sounded a bit calmer now.

“Carla, go check on Judy. And get that blanket. There’s a stack of them in the small supply closet near Examining Room One. Go on, all of you. Let’s try to look like we know what we’re doing.”

They started moving. Soon, the screaming in the other room died down. The phone, which had stopped, started ringing again. Louis pressed the hold button without answering it.

The older campus cop looked more composed, and Louis spoke to him. “Who do we need to tell? Can you give me a list?”

The cop nodded and said, “We haven’t had one of these in six years. This is a terrible way to start the semester.”

“It is,” Louis said. He picked up the phone and pressed the hold button again.

“Hello? Who is—” an excited voice started, but Louis interrupted. He began to make his calls.
