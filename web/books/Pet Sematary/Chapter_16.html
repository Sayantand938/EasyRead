Pet Sematary

Chapter 16

Something woke him much later, a crash loud enough that he sat up in bed, wondering if Ellie had fallen or Gage's crib had broken. Then the moon came out from behind a cloud, filling the room with cold light, and he saw Victor Pascow in the doorway. The crash had been Pascow throwing the door open.

He stood there with his head smashed in behind his left temple. The dried blood on his face made maroon stripes like warpaint. His collarbone stuck out, white. He was grinning.

"Come on, Doctor," Pascow said. "We have places to go."

Louis looked around. His wife was a shape under her yellow blanket, sound asleep. He looked back at Pascow, who was dead but somehow not. Yet Louis felt no fear. He quickly knew why.

It’s a dream, he thought, and only in his relief did he realize he had been afraid. The dead don’t come back; it’s impossible. This young man is in a drawer in Bangor with the pathologist’s mark—a Y-cut sewn back up—on him. The pathologist probably threw his brain back into his chest after taking a sample and filled the skull with paper to stop leaks—easier than fitting the brain back in like a puzzle piece. Uncle Carl, father of poor Ruthie, had told him pathologists did that, and other things that would terrify Rachel with her fear of death. But Pascow wasn’t here—no way. Pascow was in a cold locker with a tag on his toe. And he’s certainly not wearing those red shorts in there.

Yet the urge to get up was strong. Pascow was watching him.

He threw back the covers and put his feet on the floor. The rug—a gift from Rachel’s grandmother years ago—felt cold under his feet. The dream felt very real. It was so real that he wouldn’t follow Pascow until Pascow turned and walked back down the stairs. The urge to follow was strong, but he didn’t want to be touched by a walking corpse, even in a dream.

But he did follow. Pascow’s shorts shone.

They crossed the living room, dining room, kitchen. Louis thought Pascow would unlock and unlatch the door between the kitchen and the shed where he parked the station wagon and Civic, but Pascow didn’t. Instead, he simply walked through the door. And Louis, watching, thought with mild surprise: Is that how it’s done? Amazing! Anyone could do that!

He tried it himself—and was a little amused to find the wood unyielding. He was a realist, even in his dreams. Louis twisted the knob, lifted the latch, and entered the shed-garage. Pascow was not there. Louis wondered if Pascow had simply vanished. Dream figures often did. So did places—one moment you were naked by a pool, discussing wife swapping with friends; then you blinked and were climbing a volcano. Maybe he had lost Pascow because this was the start of something new.

But as Louis left the garage, he saw him again, standing in the dim moonlight at the back of the lawn—at the path's entrance.

Now fear arrived, softly seeping into his body and filling the empty spaces with dirty smoke. He didn’t want to go there. He stopped.

Pascow glanced back, and in the moonlight, his eyes were silver. Louis felt a wave of horror in his stomach. That jutting bone, those dried blood clots. But he couldn't resist those eyes. This was a dream of being hypnotized, controlled... unable to change things, like Pascow's death. Twenty years of school couldn't help when a man was brought in, his skull crushed by a tree. They might as well have called a plumber.

Even as these thoughts raced through his mind, he was drawn onto the path. He followed the jogging shorts, as maroon as the dried blood on Pascow’s face in this light.

He didn’t like this dream. Not at all. It was too real. The cold rug, the inability to pass through the shed door when one should be able to in a dream... and now the cool dew on his bare feet, and the light night wind on his naked body, save for his shorts. Under the trees, pine needles stuck to his soles… another detail that was too real.

Never mind. Never mind. I am home in bed. It’s just a dream, no matter how vivid, and it will seem ridiculous in the morning. My mind will find its flaws.

A small dead branch poked his arm rudely, and he winced. Up ahead, Pascow was just a moving shadow, and Louis’s terror had become clear: I am following a dead man into the woods, to the Pet Sematary, and this is no dream. God help me, this is real.

They walked down the wooded hill. The path curved between the trees and plunged into the underbrush. No boots now. The ground became cold jelly under his feet, grabbing and holding, reluctantly letting go. There were sucking noises. He felt the mud between his toes, trying to separate them.

He desperately tried to cling to the idea it was a dream.

It wouldn’t work.

They reached the clearing, and the moon escaped its cloud cover again, filling the graveyard with a ghostly light. The leaning markers—bits of wood and tin cans cut with a father’s tools and hammered into rough squares, broken pieces of shale and slate—stood out clearly, casting sharp, black shadows.

Pascow stopped near SMUCKY THE CAT, HE WAS OBEDIANT and turned to Louis. The horror, the terror—he felt these things would grow in him until his body exploded under their soft but unstoppable pressure. Pascow was grinning. His bloody lips were pulled back from his teeth, and his healthy tan from working on the road, in the moon’s harsh light, looked like the white of a corpse ready to be wrapped.

He lifted an arm and pointed. Louis looked and moaned. His eyes widened, and he pressed his knuckles against his mouth. His cheeks felt cool, and he knew that he had started to cry from the extreme terror.

The fallen trees from which Jud Crandall had called Ellie in alarm had become a pile of bones. The bones were moving. They twisted and clicked, jaws and femurs and ulnas and molars and incisors; he saw the grinning skulls of humans and animals. Finger bones rattled. Here, the remains of a foot bent its pale joints.

Ah, it was moving; it was creeping—

Pascow was walking toward him, his bloody face grim in the moonlight, and the last of Louis’s clear mind began to slip away in a repeating thought: You have to scream yourself awake it doesn’t matter if you scare Rachel Ellie Gage wake the whole house the whole neighborhood have to scream yourself awake screamscreamscreamyourselfawakeawakeawake—

But only a soft breath of air came out. It was the sound of a little kid sitting and trying to learn how to whistle.

Pascow came closer and spoke.

“The door must not be opened,” Pascow said. He was looking down at Louis because Louis had fallen to his knees. Louis thought he saw compassion in his face. It wasn’t really compassion, just a terrible kind of patience. He pointed at the moving pile of bones. “Don’t go beyond, no matter how much you feel you need to, Doctor. The barrier was not meant to be broken. Remember this: there is more power here than you know. It is old and always restless. Remember.”

Louis tried again to scream. He could not.

“I come as a friend,” Pascow said—but was friend really the word Pascow had used? Louis thought not. It was like Pascow had spoken in another language that Louis could understand through dream magic… and “friend” was the closest word his mind could find for what Pascow had said. “Your destruction and the destruction of all you love is very near, Doctor.” He was close enough for Louis to smell death on him.

Pascow, reaching for him.

The soft, maddening click of the bones.

Louis started to fall as he tried to get away from that hand. His own hand hit a gravestone and knocked it over. Pascow’s face, leaning down, filled the sky.

“Doctor—remember.”

Louis tried to scream, and the world spun away—but he still heard the click of moving bones in the moonlit crypt of the night.
