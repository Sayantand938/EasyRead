Pet Sematary

Chapter 43

The next day was cloudy but very warm, and Louis was sweating a lot by the time he had checked Rachel’s and Ellie’s luggage and gotten their tickets from the computer. He thought that being able to keep busy was a good thing, and he felt only a small, painful reminder of the last time he had put his family on a plane to Chicago, at Thanksgiving.

Ellie seemed far away and a little strange. Several times that morning Louis had looked up and seen a look of strange thought on her face.

"She's up to something," he told himself.

She didn’t say anything when told they were all going to Chicago, she and Mommy first, maybe for the whole summer, and just kept eating her breakfast (Cocoa Bears). After breakfast she went quietly upstairs and put on the dress and shoes Rachel had set out for her. She had brought the picture of her pulling Gage on her sled to the airport with her, and she sat quietly in one of the plastic chairs in the lower lobby while Louis waited in line for their tickets and the loudspeaker announced arriving and departing flights.

Mr. and Mrs. Goldman arrived forty minutes before the flight. Irwin Goldman looked smart (and didn’t seem to be sweating) in a cashmere coat even though it was sixty degrees; he went to the Avis desk to return his car while Dory Goldman sat with Rachel and Ellie.

Louis and Goldman joined the others at the same time. Louis was a little afraid that they might repeat the "my son, my son" scene, but he was spared. Goldman just gave him a weak handshake and mumbled hello. The quick, embarrassed look he gave his son-in-law confirmed what Louis had woken up thinking that morning: the man must have been drunk.

They went upstairs on the escalator and sat in the boarding lounge, not talking much. Dory Goldman nervously touched her copy of an Erica Jong novel but didn’t open it. She kept looking, a little nervously, at the picture Ellie was holding.

Louis asked his daughter if she wanted to walk over to the bookstore with him and pick out something to read on the plane.

Ellie had been looking at him in that thoughtful way again. Louis didn’t like it. It made him nervous.

“Will you behave at Grandma and Grandda’s?” he asked her as they walked over.

“Yes,” she said. “Daddy, will I get in trouble for missing school? Andy Pasioca says there’s someone who catches kids who skip school.”

“Don’t you worry about that,” he said. “I’ll sort things out with the school, and you can start again in the fall without any problems.”

“I hope I’ll be okay in the fall,” Ellie said. “I’ve never been in a grade before. Only kindergarten. I don’t know what kids do in grades. Homework, maybe.”

“You’ll be fine.”

“Daddy, are you still angry at Grandda?”

He stared at her. “What makes you think I don’t like your grandda, Ellie?”

She shrugged as if she didn't care much about it. “When you talk about him, you always look angry.”

“Ellie, that’s rude.”

“Sorry.”

She gave him that strange, dreamy look and then wandered off to look at the shelves of children’s books—Mercer Meyer and Maurice Sendak and Richard Scarry and Beatrix Potter and that famous old favorite, Dr. Seuss. How do they figure this stuff out? Or do they just know? How much does Ellie know? How’s it affecting her? Ellie, what’s going on behind that pale little face? Mad at him—Jesus!

“Can I have these, Daddy?” She was holding out a Dr. Seuss and a book Louis hadn’t seen since he was a kid—the story of Little Black Sambo and how the tigers had gotten his clothes one day.

I thought they’d taken that one out of print, Louis thought, surprised.

“Yes,” he said, and they stood in a short line at the cash register. “Your grandpa and I get along fine,” he said and thought again of his mother’s story of how when a woman really wanted a baby, she “found” one. He remembered his own dumb promises to himself that he would never lie to his own children. Over the last few days he had become quite a good liar, he felt, but he would not let himself think about it now.

“Oh,” she said and went quiet.

The silence made him nervous. To break it he said, “So do you think you’ll have a good time in Chicago?”

“No.”

“No? Why not?”

She looked up at him with that dreamy expression. “I’m scared.”

He put his hand on her head. “Scared? Honey, what of? You’re not scared of the plane, are you?”

“No,” she said. “I don’t know what I’m scared of. Daddy, I dreamed we were at Gage’s funeral, and the funeral man opened his coffin, and it was empty. Then I dreamed I was home, and I looked in Gage’s crib, and that was empty too. But there was dirt in it.”

Lazarus, come forth.

For the first time in months, he remembered the dream he had after Pascow’s death—the dream, and then waking up to find his feet dirty and the foot of the bed covered in pine needles and mud.

The hairs on the back of his neck prickled.

“Just dreams,” he said to Ellie, and his voice sounded, at least to him, perfectly normal. “They’ll go away.”

“I wish you were coming with us,” she said, “or that we were staying here. Can we stay, Daddy? Please? I don’t want to go to Grandma and Grandpa’s… I just want to go back to school. Okay?”

“Just for a little while, Ellie,” he said. “I’ve got”—he swallowed—”a few things to do here, and then I’ll be with you. We can decide what to do next.”

He expected an argument, maybe even an Ellie-style tantrum. He might even have welcomed it—something he knew, because that look was not. But there was only that pale, disturbing silence that seemed so deep. He could have asked her more but found he didn’t dare; she had already told him more than he maybe wanted to hear.

Soon after he and Ellie returned to the waiting area, the flight was called. Boarding passes were shown, and the four of them got in line. Louis hugged his wife and kissed her hard. She held onto him for a moment and then let him go so he could pick Ellie up and kiss her cheek.

Ellie looked at him seriously with her wise eyes. “I don’t want to go,” she said again but so quietly that only Louis could really hear over the shuffling and talking of the boarding passengers. “I don’t want Mommy to go either.”

“Ellie, come on,” Louis said. “You’ll be fine.”

“I’ll be fine,” she said, “but what about you? Daddy, what about you?”

The line had started to move. People were walking down the jetway to the 727. Rachel pulled Ellie’s hand, and for a moment she resisted, holding up the line, her eyes fixed on her father—and Louis remembered her impatience last time, her cries of come on—come on—come on.

“Daddy?”

“Go now, Ellie. Please.”

Rachel looked at Ellie and saw that dark, dreamy look for the first time. “Ellie?” she said, surprised and, Louis thought, a little afraid. “You’re holding up the line, baby.”

Ellie’s lips trembled and turned white. Then she let herself be led into the jetway. She looked back at him, and he saw pure terror in her face. He raised his hand to her, pretending to be cheerful.

Ellie did not wave back.
