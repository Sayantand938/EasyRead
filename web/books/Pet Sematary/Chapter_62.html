Pet Sematary

Chapter 62

Steve Masterton rounded the curve just before Louis’s house and immediately saw the smoke—not from Louis’s, but from the old woman’s house across the street.

He had come out this morning because he was worried about Louis—very worried. Charlton had told him about Rachel’s call the day before, and that had made him wonder where Louis was… and what he was doing.

His worry was vague, but it bothered him—he wouldn’t feel right until he had gone out there and checked to see if things were okay… or as okay as they could be under the circumstances.

The spring weather had emptied the infirmary quickly, and Surrendra had told him to go ahead; he could handle anything that came up. So Steve had jumped on his Honda, which he had taken out of the garage just last weekend, and headed for Ludlow. Maybe he rode a little faster than he needed to, but the worry was there; it bothered him. And with it came the strange feeling that he was already too late. Stupid, of course, but in his stomach he felt like he had last fall when that Pascow thing happened—a feeling of miserable surprise and disappointment. He wasn’t a religious man (in college Steve had been in the Atheist’s Society for two semesters and had quit only when his advisor told him—privately and unofficially—that it might hurt his chances of getting a medical school scholarship later), but he guessed he was as likely as anyone else to have whatever biological or rhythmic conditions caused premonitions, and Pascow’s death seemed to set a tone for the year that followed. It wasn’t a good year. Two of Surrendra’s relatives had been jailed back home for some political thing, and Surrendra had told him that he thought one of them—an uncle he loved very much—might be dead. Surrendra had cried, and the tears from the usually calm Indian had scared Steve. And Charlton’s mother had had a radical mastectomy. The strong nurse wasn’t optimistic about her mother living another five years. Steve himself had been to four funerals since Victor Pascow’s death—his wife’s sister, killed in a car crash; a cousin, killed in a weird accident because of a bar bet (he had been electrocuted while trying to climb to the top of a power pole); a grandparent; and Louis’s little boy.

He liked Louis a lot, and he wanted to make sure Louis was okay. Louis had been through hell lately.

When he saw the smoke, his first thought was that this was another thing caused by Victor Pascow, who seemed, in dying, to have removed some kind of protection between these ordinary people and a string of bad luck. But that was stupid, and Louis’s house proved it. It stood calm and white, a nice piece of New England architecture in the morning sun.

People were running toward the old woman’s house, and as Steve turned his bike across the road and into Louis’s driveway, he saw a man run up onto the old woman’s porch, go to the front door, and then back away. It was lucky he did; a moment later the glass in the center of the door blew out, and flames poured through the opening. If the fool had opened the door, the blast would have burned him badly.

Steve got off his bike and put it on its stand, Louis forgotten for a moment. He was drawn by the mystery of fire. Maybe half a dozen people had gathered; except for the would-be hero, who stayed on the Crandalls’ lawn, they kept a safe distance. Now the windows between the porch and the house blew out. Glass flew in the air. The would-be hero ducked and ran. Flames ran up the inner wall of the porch like reaching hands, blistering the white paint. As Steve watched, one of the rattan chairs smoldered and then burst into flame.

Over the crackling sounds, he heard the would-be hero shout with a strange and silly optimism: “She’s gonna burn! Gonna burn for sure! If Jud’s in there, he’s done for! I told him about the creosote in that chimney a hundred times!”

Steve started to shout across the street to ask if anyone had called the fire department, but then he heard sirens in the distance, getting closer. Lots of them. Someone had called, but the man was right: the house was lost. Flames reached through several broken windows, and the front of the roof now had a see-through layer of fire over the bright green shingles.

He turned back, remembering Louis—but if Louis was here, wouldn’t he be with the others across the street?

Then Steve noticed something, just barely, out of the corner of his eye.

Beyond the top of Louis’s driveway was a field that went up a long, gentle hill. The tall grass, still green, had grown high this May. Steve saw a path, cut as neatly as a golf course. It twisted up the hill, toward the woods that started, thick and green, just below the horizon. There, where the light green grass met the darker green woods, Steve saw movement—a quick flash of bright white. It disappeared almost as soon as he saw it, but it seemed like he had seen a man carrying a white bundle.

That was Louis, his mind told him with sudden certainty. That was Louis, and you need to get to him fast because something bad has happened, and something even worse will happen if you don’t stop him.

He stood at the top of the driveway, unsure, shifting his weight from one foot to the other.

Steve, you’re scared to death right now, aren’t you?

Yes. He was. He was scared to death for no reason at all. But there was also something… something

(drawing him in)

yes, something drawing him in, something about that path, that path leading up the hill and into the woods—surely that path had to go somewhere, didn’t it? Yes, of course it did. All paths eventually went somewhere.

Louis. Don’t forget Louis, you idiot! Louis was the man you came to see, remember? You didn’t come to Ludlow to explore the damn woods.

“What do you have there, Randy?” the wannabe hero shouted. His voice, still high and somehow hopeful, carried easily.

Randy’s reply was almost lost in the growing sound of the fire sirens. “Dead cat.”

“Burnt?”

“Doesn’t look burnt,” Randy said. “Just looks dead.”

And Steve's mind returned stubbornly, as if the conversation across the street had something to do with what he had seen—or what he thought he had seen: That was Louis.

He started to move then, jogging up the path toward the woods, leaving the fire behind him. He was sweating by the time he reached the edge of the woods, and the shade felt cool and good. There was the sweet smell of pine and spruce, bark and sap.

Once in the woods, he broke into a run, not sure why he was running, not sure why his heart was beating so fast. His breath came in and out quickly. He was able to run faster going downhill—the path was surprisingly clear—but he reached the arch that marked the entrance to the Pet Sematary at just a fast walk. There was a sharp pain high in his right side, just under his armpit.

His eyes barely noticed the circles of graves—the bent tin squares, the bits of board and slate. His gaze was fixed on the strange sight at the far side of the circular clearing. It was fixed on Louis, who was climbing a fallen tree, seemingly defying gravity. He climbed the steep tree step by step, his eyes straight ahead, like a man who was hypnotized or sleepwalking. In his arms was the white thing that Steve had glimpsed. Up close, it was clear—it was a body. One foot, in a black shoe with a low heel, stuck out. And Steve knew with a sudden, sickening feeling that Louis was carrying Rachel’s body.

Louis’s hair had turned white.

“Louis!” Steve screamed.

Louis didn’t hesitate, didn’t pause. He reached the top of the fallen trees and started down the other side.

He’ll fall, Steve thought, his thoughts jumbled. He’s been so lucky, unbelievably lucky, but soon he’s going to fall, and if his leg is the only thing he breaks—

But Louis did not fall. He reached the other side of the fallen trees, disappeared for a moment, and then came back into view as he walked toward the woods again.

“Louis!” Steve yelled again.

This time Louis stopped and turned around.

Steve was stunned by what he saw. Besides the white hair, Louis’s face looked ancient.

At first, Louis didn't seem to recognize him at all. Then, little by little, recognition grew, as if someone was turning up a light in his mind. Louis’s mouth was twitching. After a moment, Steve realized Louis was trying to smile.

“Steve,” he said, his voice raspy and unsure. “Hello, Steve. I’m going to bury her. I guess I have to do it with my bare hands. It might take until dark. The ground up there is very rocky. I don’t suppose you’d want to help me?”

Steve opened his mouth, but couldn’t speak. Despite his shock, despite his horror, he did want to help Louis. Somehow, up here in the woods, it felt very right, very… very natural.

“Louis,” he finally choked out, “what happened? God, what happened? Was she… was she in the fire?”

“I waited too long with Gage,” Louis said. “Something got to him because I waited too long. But it will be different with Rachel, Steve. I know it will.”

He swayed a bit, and Steve saw that Louis was insane—he saw it plainly. Louis was insane and terribly tired. But somehow only the tiredness mattered in his confused mind.

“I could use some help,” Louis said.

“Louis, even if I wanted to help you, I couldn’t climb over that pile of wood.”

“Oh yes,” Louis said. “You could. If you just move slowly and don’t look down. That’s the trick, Steve.”

He turned then, and though Steve called his name, Louis walked into the woods. For a few moments Steve could see the white sheet flash between the trees. Then it was gone.

He ran to the pile of wood and started to climb it without thinking, first feeling for good places to grab, trying to crawl up it, and then standing up. As he did, a wild, bold excitement rushed through him—like breathing pure oxygen. He believed he could do it—and he did. Moving quickly and steadily, he reached the top. He stood there for a moment, unsteady, watching Louis move along the path—the path that went on past the pile of wood.

Louis turned and looked back at Steve. He held his wife, wrapped in a bloody sheet, in his arms.

“You may hear sounds,” Louis said. “Sounds like voices. But they are just the loons, down south toward Prospect. The sound travels. It’s strange.”

“Louis—”

But Louis had turned away.

For a moment Steve almost followed him—it was a very close thing.

I could help him, if that’s what he wants… and I want to help him, yes. That’s the truth because there’s more to this than I know, and I want to find out what it is. It seems very… well… very important. It seems like a secret. Like a mystery.

Then a branch snapped under his tilted foot. It made a dry, dusty sound like a starting gun. It brought him back to exactly where he was and what he was doing. Terror jumped into him, and he turned around awkwardly, arms out for balance, his tongue and throat thick with fear, his face showing the shocked look of a man who wakes up to find he has sleepwalked onto a high ledge.

She’s dead and I think that maybe Louis killed her, Louis has gone mad, completely mad, but—

But there was something worse than madness here—something much, much worse. It was as if there was a magnet somewhere in those woods, and he could feel it pulling at something in his brain. Pulling him toward that place where Louis was taking Rachel.

Come on, walk the path… walk the path and see where it goes. We got stuff to show you out here, Steverino, stuff they never told you about in the Atheists’ Society back in Lake Forest.

And then, perhaps simply because it had enough to feed on for one day and lost interest in him, the call of the place in his mind simply stopped. Steve took two stumbling, unsteady steps back down the side of the deadfall. Then more branches broke with a grinding sound, and his left foot plunged into the tangled deadwood; rough sharp splinters tore off his sneaker and then cut into his flesh as he yanked free. He fell forward into the Pet Sematary, barely missing a piece of orange crate that could have easily stabbed into his stomach.

He got to his feet, staring around, confused, wondering what had happened to him… or if anything had happened to him. Already it had begun to seem like a dream.

Then, from the deep woods behind the deadfall, woods so deep the light looked green and dull even on the brightest days, a low, chuckling laugh arose. The sound was huge. Steve couldn’t imagine what creature could make such a sound.

He ran, one shoe off and one shoe on, trying to scream but unable to. He was still running when he reached Louis’s house, and still trying to scream when he finally got his bike started and swerved onto Route 15. He almost hit a fire engine from Brewer. Inside his helmet, his hair stood on end.

By the time he got back to his apartment in Orono, he couldn't quite remember going to Ludlow at all. He called in sick at the infirmary, took a pill, and went to bed.

Steve Masterton never really remembered that day… except in deep dreams, those that come in the early morning. And in these dreams he would feel that something huge had moved past him—something which had reached out to touch him… and had then pulled back its inhuman hand at the last second.

Something with great yellow eyes which gleamed like foglamps.

Steve sometimes awoke screaming from these dreams, his eyes wide, and he would think: You think you are screaming, but it’s only the sound of the loons, down south, in Prospect. The sound carries. It’s funny.

But he did not know, could not remember, what such a thought might mean. The next year he took a job far away, in St. Louis.

Between his last sight of Louis Creed and his move to the Midwest, Steve never went into Ludlow again.
