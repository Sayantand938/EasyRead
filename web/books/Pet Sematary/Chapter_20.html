Pet Sematary

Chapter 20

Indian summer didn't last long. The trees turned bright colors, blazed for a short time, and then faded. After a cold, heavy rain in mid-October, the leaves began to fall. Ellie started bringing home Halloween decorations she had made at school and told Gage the story of the Headless Horseman. Gage spent that evening happily talking about someone named Itchybod Brain. Rachel started giggling and couldn’t stop. It was a happy time for them, that early autumn.

Louis’s job at the university had become a busy but enjoyable routine. He saw patients, attended meetings, and wrote the required letters to the student newspaper. He advised the students about the confidentiality of the infirmary's treatment for STDs and urged them to get flu shots, as the A-type was likely to be common again that winter. He participated in discussions. He led discussions. During the second week in October, he went to the New England Conference on College and University Medicine in Providence and presented a paper on the legal issues of treating students. Victor Pascow was mentioned in his paper under the fake name of “Henry Montez.” The paper was well received. He started working on the infirmary budget for the next school year.

His evenings fell into a pattern: time with the kids after dinner, a beer or two with Jud Crandall later. Sometimes Rachel came with him if Missy was free to babysit, and sometimes Norma joined them, but usually it was just Louis and Jud. Louis found the old man as comfortable as an old shoe, and he would talk about Ludlow history going back three hundred years as if he had lived through it all. He talked but never went on and on. He never bored Louis, although he had seen Rachel yawn while trying to hide it more than once.

He would cross the road back to his house before ten most evenings, and, more often than not, he and Rachel would make love. They hadn't made love so often since the first year of their marriage, and never so well or enjoyably. Rachel said she thought it was something in the well water; Louis thought it was the Maine air.

The awful death of Victor Pascow on the first day of the fall semester began to fade from the students' minds and from Louis’s own; Pascow’s family was no doubt still grieving. Louis had spoken to Pascow’s father on the phone, his voice full of tears but thankfully not visible; the father had only wanted to be sure that Louis had done everything he could, and Louis had assured him that everyone had. He didn’t tell him about the confusion, the spreading stain on the carpet, and how his son had been dead almost as soon as he was brought in, although Louis thought he would never forget those things. But for those who saw Pascow as just another death, he was already fading from memory.

Louis still remembered the dream and the sleepwalking that went with it, but it now seemed almost as if it had happened to someone else, or on a TV show he had once watched. His one visit to a prostitute in Chicago six years ago seemed like that now; they were equally unimportant, side trips that seemed more important than they were, like sounds in an echo chamber.

He didn’t think at all about what the dying Pascow had or had not said.

There was a hard frost on Halloween night. Louis and Ellie started at the Crandalls’. Ellie laughed with pleasure, pretended to ride her broom around Norma’s kitchen, and was told she was “Just the cutest thing I ever saw… isn’t she, Jud?”

Jud agreed and lit a cigarette. “Where’s Gage, Louis? I thought you’d have him dressed up too.”

They had planned to take Gage around—Rachel especially had been looking forward to it because she and Missy Dandridge had made a bug costume with twisted coat hangers wrapped in crepe paper for feelers—but Gage had gotten a bad, bronchial cold. After listening to his lungs, which sounded a bit wheezy, and seeing the thermometer outside read only forty degrees at six o’clock, Louis had said no. Rachel, though sad, had agreed.

Ellie had promised to give Gage some of her candy, but her overdone sadness made Louis wonder if she wasn’t a little happy Gage wouldn’t slow her down… or steal some attention.

“Poor Gage,” she had said in a voice usually used for people who were dying. Gage, not knowing what he was missing, sat on the sofa watching “Zoom” with Church sleeping next to him.

“Ellie-witch,” Gage replied without much interest and went back to the TV.

“Poor Gage,” Ellie said again, sighing. Louis thought of fake tears and grinned. Ellie grabbed his hand and started pulling him. “Let’s go, Daddy. Let’s go—let’s go—let’s go."

“Gage has a bit of the croup,” Louis said to Jud now.

“Well, that’s too bad,” Norma said, “but it will be better for him next year. Hold out your bag, Ellie… whoops!”

She had taken an apple and a small Snickers bar from the treat bowl on the table, but they had both fallen out of her hand. Louis was a little shocked at how bony that hand looked. He bent over and picked up the apple as it rolled across the floor. Jud got the Snickers and dropped it into Ellie’s bag.

“Oh, let me get you another apple, honey,” Norma said. “That one will get bruised.”

“It’s fine,” Louis said, trying to put it into Ellie’s bag, but Ellie stepped away, holding her bag closed.

“I don’t want a bruised apple, Daddy,” she said, looking at her father like he was crazy. “Brown spots… yuck!”

“Ellie, that’s very rude!”

“Don’t scold her for telling the truth, Louis,” Norma said. “Only children tell the whole truth, you know. That’s what makes them children. The brown spots are yucky.”

“Thank you, Mrs. Crandall,” Ellie said, giving her father a satisfied look.

“You’re very welcome, honey,” Norma said.

Jud walked them out to the porch. Two little ghosts were coming up the walk, and Ellie knew they were both friends from school. She took them back to the kitchen, and for a moment Jud and Louis were alone on the porch.

“Her arthritis has gotten worse,” Louis said.

Jud nodded and put out his cigarette in an ashtray. “Yeah. It’s been harder on her every fall and winter, but this is the worst it’s ever been.”

“What does her doctor say?”

“Nothing. He can’t say nothing because Norma hasn’t gone back to see him.”

“What? Why not?”

Jud looked at Louis, and in the light from the station wagon's headlights, waiting for the ghosts, he looked strangely vulnerable. “I wanted to ask you this when things were better, Louis, but I guess there's no good time to ask a friend for a favor. Would you take a look at her?”

From the kitchen, Louis could hear the two ghosts booing and Ellie doing her cackling—which she had been practicing all week—again. It all sounded very nice and Halloweenish.

“What else is wrong with Norma?” he asked. “Is she afraid of something else, Jud?”

“She’s been having chest pains,” Jud said quietly. “She won’t go to Dr. Weybridge anymore. I’m a little worried.”

“Is Norma worried?”

Jud hesitated and then said, “I think she’s scared. I think that’s why she doesn’t want to go to the doctor. One of her oldest friends, Betty Coslaw, died in the EMMC just last month. Cancer. She and Norma were the same age. She’s scared.”

“I’d be glad to examine her,” Louis said. “No problem at all.”

“Thanks, Louis,” Jud said, relieved. “If we catch her one night, all work together, I think—”

Jud stopped, tilting his head to the side. He looked at Louis.

Later, Louis couldn't quite recall how his feelings changed. Trying to understand it just made him confused. All he knew was that curiosity quickly turned into a feeling that something was terribly wrong. He and Jud looked at each other, both caught off guard. It took him a moment to figure out how to react.

“Hoooo-hoooo,” the Halloween ghosts in the kitchen chanted. “Hooo-hooo.” Then, the h-sound disappeared, and the cry grew louder, truly scary: “oooo-OOOOOO—”

And then one of the ghosts began to scream.

“Daddy!” Ellie’s voice was frantic and scared. “Daddy! Missus Crandall fell down!”

“Ah, Jesus,” Jud almost groaned.

Ellie ran onto the porch, her black dress flapping. She held her broom in one hand. Her green face, now long with worry, looked like a tiny drunkard in the final stages of sickness. The two little ghosts followed her, crying.

Jud rushed through the door, surprisingly quick for a man over eighty. No, more than quick. Almost agile. He was calling his wife’s name.

Louis bent down and put his hands on Ellie’s shoulders. “Stay right here on the porch, Ellie. Understand?”

“Daddy, I’m scared,” she whispered.

The two ghosts ran past them and down the walk, candy bags rattling, screaming their mother’s name.

Louis ran down the front hall and into the kitchen, ignoring Ellie, who was calling for him to come back.

Norma lay on the lumpy linoleum by the table in a mess of apples and small Snickers bars. It seemed she had knocked over the bowl as she reached for something. It lay nearby like a small Pyrex flying saucer. Jud was rubbing one of her wrists, and he looked up at Louis with a worried face.

“Help me, Louis,” he said. “Help Norma. She’s dying, I think.”

“Move aside,” Louis said. He kneeled and landed on an apple, crushing it. He felt juice soak through the knee of his old pants, and the sweet smell of apple filled the kitchen.

Here it is again, like Pascow, Louis thought and then pushed the thought away so quickly it was like it was on wheels.

He felt for her pulse and found it weak, faint, and fast—not really a beat but just small twitches. A very irregular heartbeat, close to cardiac arrest. You and Elvis Presley, Norma, he thought.

He opened her dress, showing a creamy yellow silk slip. Moving smoothly now, he turned her head to the side and started CPR.

“Jud, listen to me,” he said. “Put the heel of your left hand a third of the way up her breastbone—four centimeters above that little notch. Grip your left wrist with your right hand, to add pressure. Keep it firm, but easy on her ribs—no need to panic yet. And don’t crush her lungs.”

“I’m here,” Jud said.

“Take Ellie,” he said. “Go across the street. Be careful—watch for cars. Tell Rachel what’s happened. Tell her I want my bag. Not the one in the study, but the one on the high shelf in the upstairs bathroom. She’ll know which one. Tell her to call Bangor MedCu and send an ambulance.”

“Bucksport is closer,” Jud said.

“Bangor is faster. Go. Don’t call; let Rachel do that. I need that bag.” And once she knows what’s happening, Louis thought, I don’t think she’ll bring it over.

Jud left. Louis heard the screen door slam. He was alone with Norma Crandall and the smell of apples. From the living room came the steady tick of the clock.

Norma suddenly made a long, snoring sound. Her eyelids fluttered. And Louis was suddenly filled with a cold, awful certainty.

She’s going to open her eyes… oh God, she’s going to open her eyes and start talking about the Pet Sematary.

But she only looked at Louis with a confused kind of recognition, and then her eyes closed again. Louis was ashamed of himself and this silly fear that was so unlike him. At the same time, he felt hope and relief. There had been some pain in her eyes but not terrible pain. His first thought was that this had not been a serious seizure.

Louis was breathing hard now and sweating. Only TV paramedics could make CPR look easy. A good, steady chest massage used a lot of energy, and the muscles between his arms and shoulders would ache tomorrow.

“Can I do anything?”

He looked around. A woman in pants and a brown sweater stood uncertainly in the doorway, one hand in a fist between her breasts. The mother of the ghosts, Louis thought.

“No,” he said, and then: “Yes. Wet a cloth, please. Squeeze it out. Put it on her forehead.”

She moved to do it. Louis looked down, Norma’s eyes were open again.

“Louis, I fell down,” she whispered. “Think I fainted.”

“You’ve had some kind of heart problem,” Louis said. “Doesn’t seem too bad. Just relax and don’t talk, Norma.”

He paused for a moment and then checked her pulse again. It was too fast. Her heart was sending a signal: it would beat normally, then speed up briefly in a way that almost but didn't become a dangerous rhythm, and then go back to beating normally. Beat-beat-beat, WHACK-WHACK-WHACK, beat-beat-beat-beat-beat. It wasn’t good, but it was a little better than a totally irregular heartbeat.

The woman came over with the cloth and put it on Norma’s forehead. She stepped away nervously. Jud came back in with Louis’s bag.

“Louis?”

“She’s going to be fine,” Louis said, looking at Jud but really talking to Norma. “Medics on their way?”

“Your wife is calling them,” Jud said. “I didn’t stay.”

“No… hospital,” Norma whispered.

“Yes, hospital,” Louis said. “Five days to watch you, some medicine, then home to rest, Norma. And if you say another word, I’ll make you eat all these apples. Cores and everything.”

She smiled weakly, then closed her eyes again.

Louis opened his bag, searched around, found the Isodil, and shook one of the pills, so tiny it easily could have fit on the moon of one fingernail, into his palm. He recapped the bottle and pinched the pill between his fingers.

“Norma, can you hear me?”

“Yes.”

“Want you to open your mouth. You did your trick, now you get your treat. I’m going to put a pill under your tongue. Just a small one. I want you to hold it there until it dissolves. It’s going to taste a little bitter but never mind that. All right?”

She opened her mouth. Stale breath from her dentures came out, and Louis felt a moment of aching sorrow for her, lying here on her kitchen floor in a mess of apples and Halloween candy. It occurred to him that once she had been seventeen, her breasts admired by the young men of the neighborhood, all her teeth her own, and the heart under her blouse a tough little engine.

She settled her tongue over the pill and grimaced a little. The pill tasted a little bitter, all right. It always did. But she was no Victor Pascow, beyond help and beyond reach. He thought Norma was going to live to fight another day. Her hand reached in the air, and Jud took it gently.

Louis got up then, found the overturned bowl, and began to pick up the treats. The woman, who introduced herself as Mrs. Buddinger from down the road, helped him and then said she thought she had better go back to the car. Her two boys were frightened.

“Thank you for your help, Mrs. Buddinger,” Louis said.

“I didn’t do anything,” she said simply. “But I’ll get on my knees tonight and thank God you were here, Dr. Creed.”

Louis waved a hand, embarrassed.

“That goes for me, too,” Jud said. His eyes met Louis’s and held them. They were steady. He was in control again. His brief moment of confusion and fear was gone. “I owe you one, Louis.”

“Get off it,” Louis said and nodded toward Mrs. Buddinger as she left. She smiled and waved back. Louis found an apple and began to eat it. The Spy was so sweet that Louis’s taste buds cramped for a moment… but it wasn't totally unpleasant. Won one tonight, Lou, he thought and ate the apple with pleasure. He was very hungry.

“I do though,” Jud said. “When you need a favor, Louis, come to me first.”

“All right,” Louis said, “I’ll do that.”

The ambulance from Bangor MedCu arrived twenty minutes later. As Louis stood outside watching the orderlies load Norma into the back, he saw Rachel looking out the living room window. He waved to her. She lifted a hand back.

He and Jud stood together and watched the ambulance pull away, lights flashing, siren silent.

“Guess I’ll go up to the hospital now,” Jud said.

“They won’t let you see her tonight, Jud. They’ll want to do an EKG and then put her in intensive care. No visitors for the first twelve hours.”

“Is she going to be okay, Louis? Really okay?”

Louis shrugged. “No one can promise that. It was a heart attack. If it means anything, I think she’ll be fine. Maybe better than ever, once she starts taking some medicine.”

“Ayuh,” Jud said, lighting a Chesterfield.

Louis smiled and looked at his watch. He was surprised to see it was only ten minutes to eight. It felt like much more time had passed.

“Jud, I want to get Ellie so she can finish trick-or-treating.”

“Yeah, of course you do.” This came out as Coss y’do. “Tell her to get all the treats she can, Louis.”

“I will,” Louis promised.

Ellie was still in her witch costume when Louis got home. Rachel had tried to get her into her nightgown, but Ellie had refused, hoping that the game, stopped because of the heart attack, might still happen. When Louis told her to put her coat back on, Ellie whooped and clapped.

“It’s going to be very late for her, Louis.”

“We’ll take the car,” he said. “Come on, Rachel. She’s been looking forward to this for a month.”

“Well…” She smiled. Ellie saw it and shouted again. She ran for the coat closet. “Is Norma all right?”

“I think so.” He felt good. Tired but good. “It was a small one. She’s going to have to be careful, but when you’re seventy-five you have to realize you can't do everything you used to anyway.”

“It’s lucky you were there. Almost like divine intervention.”

“I’ll settle for luck.” He grinned as Ellie came back. “You ready, Witch Hazel?”

“I’m ready,” she said. “Come on—come on—come on!”

On the way home with half a bag of candy an hour later (Ellie protested when Louis finally stopped, but not too much; she was tired), his daughter surprised him by saying: “Did I make Mrs. Crandall have the heart attack, Daddy? When I wouldn’t take the apple with the bruise on it?”

Louis looked at her, surprised, wondering where children got such strange, partly superstitious ideas. Step on a crack, break your mother’s back. Loves me, loves me not. Daddy’s stomach, Daddy’s head, smile at midnight, Daddy’s dead. That made him think of the Pet Sematary again and those simple circles. He wanted to smile at himself but couldn't quite.

“No, honey,” he said. “When you were with those two ghosts—”

“Those weren’t ghosts, just the Buddinger twins.”

“Well, when you were with them, Mr. Crandall was telling me that his wife had been having little chest pains. In fact, you might have been responsible for saving her life or at least for keeping it from being much worse.”

Now it was Ellie’s turn to look surprised.

Louis nodded. “She needed a doctor, honey. I’m a doctor. But I was only there because it was your night to go trick-or-treating.”

Ellie thought about this for a long time and then nodded. “But she’ll probably die anyway,” she said plainly. “People who have heart attacks usually die. Even if they live, pretty soon they have another one and another one and another one until… boom!”

“And where did you learn these wise words, may I ask?”

Ellie only shrugged—a shrug much like Louis's, he was amused to see.

She let him carry in her bag of candy—a huge sign of trust—and Louis thought about her attitude. The thought of Church’s death had made her almost hysterical. But the thought of grandmotherly Norma Crandall dying… Ellie seemed to take that calmly, as if it were expected, a given. What had she said? Another one and another one, until… boom!

The kitchen was empty, but Louis could hear Rachel moving around upstairs. He set Ellie’s candy down on the counter and said, “It doesn’t always happen that way, Ellie. Norma’s heart attack was very small, and I was able to treat her right away. I don’t think her heart was damaged much at all. She—”

“Oh, I know,” Ellie agreed, almost happily. “But she’s old, and she’ll die pretty soon anyway. Mr. Crandall too. Can I have an apple before I go to bed, Daddy?”

“No,” he said, looking at her thoughtfully. “Go up and brush your teeth, babe.”

Does anyone really think they understand kids? he wondered.

When the house was quiet and they were in their side-by-side twin beds, Rachel asked softly, “Was it very bad for Ellie, Lou? Was she upset?”

No, he thought. She knows old people die regularly, just like she knows to let the grasshopper go when it spits… like she knows that if you stumble on the number thirteen when you’re skipping rope, your best friend will die… like she knows that you put the graves in shrinking circles up in the Pet Sematary…

“Nope,” he said. “She handled herself very well. Let’s go to sleep, Rachel, okay?”

That night, as they slept in their house and as Jud lay awake in his, there was another hard frost. The wind rose in the early morning, ripping most of the remaining leaves, which were now a dull brown, from the trees.

The wind woke Louis, and he sat up on his elbows, mostly asleep and confused. There were steps on the stairs… slow, dragging steps. Pascow had come back. Only now, he thought, two months had passed. When the door opened he would see a rotting horror, the jogging shorts covered with mould, the flesh fallen away in big holes, the brain decayed to mush. Only the eyes would be alive… horribly bright and alive. Pascow would not speak this time; his vocal cords would be too decayed to make sounds. But his eyes… they would beckon him to come.

“No,” he breathed, and the steps died out.

He got up, went to the door, and pulled it open, his lips pulled back in a grimace of fear and resolve, his flesh shrinking. Pascow would be there, and with his raised arms he would look like a long-dead conductor about to call for the first thundering phrase of Walpurgisnacht.

Nothing like that, as Jud might have said. The landing was empty… silent. There was no sound but the wind. Louis went back to bed and slept.
