Pet Sematary

Chapter 35

Louis Creed came to believe that the last truly happy day of his life was March 24, 1984. The bad things that were coming, hanging over them like a heavy weight, were still more than seven weeks away, but looking at those weeks he didn't see anything as bright. He guessed that even if those terrible things hadn't happened, he would have remembered the day anyway. Days that feel truly good—good all the way through—are rare enough, he thought. There might only be a few really good ones in a normal person’s life, even in the best situations. It seemed to Louis that God was much more generous with pain than with happiness.

That day was a Saturday, and he was home watching Gage in the afternoon while Rachel and Ellie went to get groceries. They went with Jud in his old, noisy truck, not because their car wasn’t working, but because the old man really liked their company. Rachel asked Louis if he would be okay with Gage, and he said he would be. He was happy to see her leave; after a winter in Maine, mostly in Ludlow, he thought she needed to get out as much as possible. She had been a good sport about it, but she seemed to be getting a little restless.

Gage woke up from his nap around two o’clock, grumpy and fussy. He had discovered the terrible twos and was fully embracing them. Louis tried several ways to entertain him, but Gage didn't like any of them. To make things worse, the kid had a huge bowel movement, and Louis wasn't happy to see a blue marble in the middle of it. It was one of Ellie’s marbles. The kid could have choked. He decided the marbles had to go—Gage put everything in his mouth—but that decision didn't help keep the kid entertained until his mother got back.

Louis listened to the early spring wind blowing around the house, sending flashes of light and shadow across Mrs. Vinton’s field next door. He suddenly thought of the kite he had bought without thinking a few weeks before, on his way home from the university. Had he also bought string? He had, by God!

“Gage!” he said. Gage had found a green crayon under the couch and was scribbling in one of Ellie’s favorite books—something that would cause sibling rivalry, Louis thought and grinned. If Ellie got really mad about the scribbles Gage had made in Where the Wild Things Are before Louis could take it away, Louis would just mention the treasure he had found in Gage’s diaper.

“What!” Gage replied smartly. He was talking pretty well now; Louis thought the kid might actually be smart.

“You wanna go out?”

“Wanna go out!” Gage agreed excitedly. “Wanna go out. Where my neeks, Daddy?”

This sentence, written as it sounded, would be: Weh ma neeks, Dah-dee? That meant Where are my sneakers, Father? Louis was often struck by Gage’s speech, not because it was cute, but because he thought that small children sounded like immigrants learning a foreign language in a disorganized but friendly way. He knew that babies make all the sounds a human can… the rolling sound that’s hard for French students, the throat sounds of Australian natives, the hard sounds of German. They lose that ability as they learn English, and Louis wondered if childhood was more about forgetting than learning.

Gage’s toys were finally found… they were also under the couch. One of Louis’s other ideas was that in families with small kids, the space under living room couches starts to build up a strong and strange pull that eventually sucks in all sorts of junk—everything from bottles and diaper pins to green Crayolas and old Sesame Street magazines with food rotting between the pages.

Gage’s jacket, though, wasn’t under the couch—it was halfway down the stairs. His Red Sox cap, which Gage always wore, was the hardest to find because it was where it should be—in the closet. That was, of course, the last place they looked.

“Where goin, Daddy?” Gage asked happily, giving his father his hand.

“Going over to Mrs. Vinton’s field,” he said. “We’re going to fly a kite, buddy.”

“Kiiiyte?” Gage asked, unsure.

“You’ll like it,” Louis said. “Wait a minute, kiddo.”

They were in the garage now. Louis found his keys, unlocked the small storage closet, and turned on the light. He searched through the closet and found the Vulture, still in its bag from the store with the receipt attached. He had bought it in the middle of February, when he felt like he needed some hope.

“Dat?” Gage asked. This was Gage-speak for “What in the world do you have there, Father?”

“It’s the kite,” Louis said and pulled it out of the bag. Gage watched, interested, as Louis unfolded the Vulture, which spread its wings over about five feet of tough plastic. Its bulging, bloodshot eyes stared out at them from its small head on top of its thin, pink neck.

“Birt!” Gage yelled. “Birt, Daddy! Got a birt!”

“Yeah, it’s a bird,” Louis agreed, slipping the sticks into the pockets at the back of the kite and rummaging again for the five hundred feet of kite string that he had bought that day. He looked back over his shoulder and repeated to Gage: “You’re gonna like it, big guy.”

Gage liked it.

They took the kite over to Mrs. Vinton’s field, and Louis got it up into the breezy late-March sky right away, although he had not flown a kite since he was… what? Twelve? Nineteen years ago? God, that was awful.

Mrs. Vinton was a woman almost Jud’s age but much more frail. She lived in a brick house at the edge of her field, but now she came out only rarely. Behind the house, the field ended and the woods began—the woods that led first to the Pet Sematary and then to the Micmac burial ground beyond it.

“Kite’s flying, Daddy!” Gage screamed.

“Yeah, look at it go!” Louis shouted back, laughing and excited. He let out kite string so fast that the string grew hot and made a thin burning line across his palm. “Look at that Vulture, Gage! She’s going to beat ass!”

“Beat-ass!” Gage cried and laughed, high and joyously. The sun came out from behind a big gray spring cloud, and the temperature seemed to go up a lot almost at once. They stood in the bright, unreliable warmth of March trying to be April in the high dead grass of Mrs. Vinton’s field; above them, the Vulture soared up toward the blue, higher, its plastic wings stretched tight against that steady flow of air, still higher, and as he had done as a child, Louis felt himself going up to it, going into it, looking down as the world took on its real shape, the one mapmakers must see in their dreams; Mrs. Vinton’s field, as white and still as cobwebs after the snow melted, not just a field now but a large four-sided shape marked by rock walls on two sides, and then the road at the bottom, a straight black line, and the river valley—the Vulture saw it all with its soaring, bloodshot eyes. It saw the river like a cool gray band of steel, chunks of ice still floating in it; on the other side, it saw Hampden, Newburgh, Winterport, with a ship at dock; maybe it saw the St. Regis Mill at Bucksport below its steaming cloud of smoke, or even the end of the land itself, where the Atlantic crashed against the bare rock.

“Look at her go, Gage!” Louis yelled, laughing.

Gage was leaning so far back he might fall over. A huge grin covered his face. He was waving to the kite.

Louis let out some string and told Gage to hold out one of his hands. Gage did, not even looking around. He couldn’t take his eyes from the kite, which swung and danced in the wind and raced its shadow back and forth across the field.

Louis wrapped the kite string around Gage’s hand twice, and Gage looked down, comically surprised by the strong tug and pull.

“What!” he said.

“You’re flying it,” Louis said. “You’ve got it, my man. It’s your kite.”

“Gage flyne it?” Gage said, as if asking himself, not his father, for confirmation. He pulled the string a little; the kite nodded in the windy sky. Gage pulled the string harder; the kite swooped. Louis and his son laughed together. Gage reached out his free hand, feeling around, and Louis took it. They stood together like that in the middle of Mrs. Vinton’s field, looking up at the Vulture.

It was a moment with his son that Louis never forgot. As he had gone up into the kite as a child, he now found himself going into Gage, his son. He felt himself shrink until he was inside Gage’s small house, looking out of his eyes—looking out at a world that was so big and bright, a world where Mrs. Vinton’s field was almost as big as the Bonneville Salt Flats, where the kite soared miles above him, the string buzzing in his fist like a live thing as the wind blew around him, messing up his hair.

“Kite flyne!” Gage cried out to his father, and Louis put his arm around Gage’s shoulders and kissed the boy’s cheek, which the wind had made as red as a wild rose.

“I love you, Gage,” he said—it was just between them, and that was fine.

And Gage, who now had less than two months to live, laughed loudly and happily. “Kite flyne! Kite flyne, Daddy!”

They were still flying the kite when Rachel and Ellie came home. He and Gage had gotten it so high that they had almost run out of string, and the face of the Vulture had disappeared; it was just a small black shape in the sky.

Louis was glad to see them, and he laughed loudly when Ellie dropped the string for a moment and chased it through the grass, catching it just before the tumbling, unraveling tube ran out of string. But having them around also changed things a little, and he was not too sad to go in when, twenty minutes later, Rachel said she thought Gage had been in the wind long enough. She was afraid he would get a chill.

So the kite was pulled back in, struggling against the sky with every twist of the string, until it finally gave up. Louis tucked it, black wings, buggy bloodshot eyes, and all, under his arm and locked it in the storage closet again. That night Gage ate a huge dinner of hot dogs and beans, and while Rachel was dressing him in his Dr. Dentons pajamas, Louis took Ellie aside and had a serious talk with her about leaving her marbles around. Normally, he might have ended up yelling at her because Ellie could get quite snobby—even rude—when accused of doing something wrong. It was just her way of handling criticism, but that didn't stop it from making Louis angry when she went too far or when he was especially tired. But tonight the kite flying had put him in a good mood, and Ellie was willing to be reasonable. She agreed to be more careful and then went downstairs to watch TV until 8:30, a Saturday treat she loved. Okay, that’s settled, and it might even help, Louis thought, not knowing that marbles weren't really the problem, and chills weren't really the problem, that a large Orinco truck was going to be the problem, that the road was going to be the problem… as Jud Crandall had warned them it might be on that first day of August.

He went upstairs that night about fifteen minutes after Gage had been put to bed. He found his son quiet but still awake, finishing a bottle of milk and looking thoughtfully up at the ceiling.

Louis took one of Gage’s feet in one hand and raised it up. He kissed it, lowered it. “Goodnight, Gage,” he said.

“Kite flyne, Daddy,” Gage said.

“It really did fly, didn’t it?” Louis said, and for no reason at all he felt tears behind his eyes. “Right up to the sky, my man.”

“Kite flyne,” Gage said. “Up to the kye.”

He rolled over on his side, closed his eyes, and slept. Just like that.

Louis was stepping into the hall when he glanced back and saw yellowy-green, floating eyes staring out at him from Gage’s closet. The closet door was open… just a crack. His heart jumped into his throat, and his mouth pulled back and down in a grimace.

He opened the closet door, thinking

(Zelda it’s Zelda in the closet her black tongue puffing out between her lips)

He wasn’t sure what, but it was just Church, the cat was in the closet, and when it saw Louis it arched its back like a Halloween cat. It hissed at him, its mouth open a little, showing its sharp teeth.

“Get out of there,” Louis whispered.

Church hissed again. It didn’t move.

“Get out, I said.” He grabbed the first thing he saw among Gage’s toys, a bright plastic Chuggy-Chuggy-Choo-Choo which looked like dried blood in the dim light. He waved it at Church; the cat didn’t budge and hissed again.

And then, without thinking, Louis threw the toy at the cat, not playing around; he threw it as hard as he could, angry and scared that it was hiding in the dark closet of his son’s room and wouldn’t leave, as if it belonged there.

The toy train hit the cat right in the middle. Church made a noise and ran, bumping into the door and almost falling as it left.

Gage moved, mumbled something, shifted, and was still again. Louis felt a little sick. He was sweating on his forehead.

“Louis?” Rachel called from downstairs, sounding worried. “Did Gage fall out of his crib?”

“He’s fine, honey. Church knocked over some of his toys.”

“Oh, all right.”

He felt—whether it made sense or not—the way he might have felt if he'd seen a snake crawling on his son or a big rat sitting on the bookshelf above Gage’s crib. It was, of course, irrational. But when it had hissed at him from the closet like that.

(Zelda did you think Zelda did you think Oz the Gweat and Tewwible?)

He closed Gage’s closet door, pushing some toys back in with it. He heard the small click of the latch. After a moment's pause, he turned the closet's lock.

He went back to Gage’s crib. The kid had kicked his two blankets down to his knees. Louis untangled him, pulled the blankets up, and then just stood there, watching his son, for a long time.
