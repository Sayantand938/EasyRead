Pet Sematary

Chapter 4

Crandall brought back the keys, but Louis had already found his. There was a space at the top of the glove compartment, and the small envelope had fallen down into the wiring. He pulled it out and let the movers in. Crandall gave him the extra set. They were on an old, dull keychain. Louis thanked him and put them in his pocket without thinking, watching the movers carry in boxes and dressers and all the things they had gathered over the ten years of their marriage. Seeing them like this, out of their usual places, made them seem smaller. Just a bunch of stuff in boxes, he thought, and suddenly he felt sad and down—he figured he was feeling what people called homesickness.

“Uprooted and transplanted,” Crandall said, suddenly beside him, and Louis jumped a little.

“You sound like you know the feeling,” he said.

“No, not really.” Crandall lit a cigarette—pop! went the match, flaring brightly in the early evening shadows. “My dad built that house across the street. Brought his wife there, and she got pregnant there, and that child was me, born in 1900.”

“That makes you—”

“Eighty-three,” Crandall said, and Louis was a little relieved that he didn’t add young at heart, a phrase he really hated.

“You look a lot younger than that.”

Crandall shrugged. “Anyway, I’ve always lived there. I joined up when we fought in World War I, but the closest I got to Europe was Bayonne, New Jersey. Nasty place. Even in 1917 it was a nasty place. I was happy to come back here. Got married to my Norma, worked on the railroad, and here we still are. But I’ve seen a lot of life right here in Ludlow. I really have.”

The moving men stopped by the shed entrance, holding the box spring that went under the big double bed he and Rachel shared. “Where do you want this, Mr. Creed?”

“Upstairs… just a minute, I’ll show you.” He started toward them, then paused for a moment and glanced back at Crandall.

“You go on,” Crandall said, smiling. “I’ll see how things are going with everyone. Send them back over and get out of your way. But moving in makes you real thirsty. I usually sit on my porch around nine and have a couple of beers. When it’s warm, I like to watch the night come. Sometimes Norma joins me. Come over, if you want.”

“Well, maybe I will,” Louis said, not planning to at all. Next thing would be an informal (and free) checkup of Norma’s arthritis on the porch. He liked Crandall, liked his crooked smile, his casual way of talking, his Yankee accent, which wasn’t harsh but so soft it was almost a drawl. A good man, Louis thought, but doctors quickly became wary of people. It was too bad, but sooner or later even your best friends wanted medical advice. And with old people, it never ended. “But don’t wait for me, or stay up—we’ve had a terrible day.”

“Just so long as you know you don’t need an invitation,” Crandall said—and there was something in his crooked smile that made Louis feel that Crandall knew exactly what Louis was thinking.

He watched the old guy for a moment before joining the movers. Crandall walked straight and easily, like a man of sixty instead of over eighty. Louis felt that first small pull of affection.
