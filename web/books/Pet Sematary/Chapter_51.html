Pet Sematary

Chapter 51

The smell hit him first, and Louis flinched back, gagging. He held onto the edge of the grave, breathing hard, and just when he thought he could control his stomach, his entire big, tasteless meal came up quickly. He threw up on the far side of the grave and then put his head on the ground, panting. Finally, the nausea passed. Teeth clenched, he took the flashlight out of his armpit and shone it down into the open coffin.

A deep horror that felt almost like awe came over him—the kind of feeling usually saved for the worst nightmares, the ones you can barely recall when you wake up.

Gage’s head was gone.

Louis’s hands were shaking so badly he had to hold the flashlight with both hands, gripping it like a policeman learns to grip his gun at the shooting range. Still, the beam jumped back and forth, and it took a moment before he could aim the narrow beam back into the grave.

It’s impossible, he told himself, just remember that what you thought you saw is impossible.

He slowly moved the narrow beam up Gage’s three-foot length, from the new shoes to the suit pants, the little coat (ah, Christ, no two-year-old should ever wear a suit), to the open collar, to—

His breath caught in a harsh sound that was too angry to be a gasp, and all his fury at Gage’s death came back in a rush, drowning fears of the supernatural, the unnatural, his growing certainty that he had gone into the land of the insane.

Louis fumbled in his back pocket for his handkerchief and pulled it out. Holding the light in one hand, he leaned into the grave again, almost losing his balance. If one of the pieces of grave liner had fallen now, it would have surely broken his neck. Gently he used his handkerchief to wipe away the damp moss that was growing on Gage’s skin—moss so dark that he had briefly thought Gage’s whole head was gone.

The moss was damp but just a thin layer. He should have expected it; there had been rain, and a grave liner was not waterproof. Shining his light to either side, Louis saw that the coffin was lying in a thin puddle. Beneath the light slime of growth, he saw his son. The mortician, knowing that the coffin could not be opened after such a terrible accident, had still done the best he could—morticians almost always did. Looking at his son was like looking at a badly made doll. Gage’s head bulged oddly. His eyes had sunken deep behind closed lids. Something white stuck out from his mouth like an albino tongue, and Louis thought at first that they had perhaps used too much embalming fluid. It was tricky to use, and with a child it was almost impossible to tell how much was enough… or too much.

Then he realized it was only cotton. He reached in and plucked it out of the boy’s mouth. Gage’s lips, oddly relaxed and seeming somehow too dark and too wide, closed with a faint but audible plip! He threw the cotton into the grave where it floated in the shallow puddle and gleamed a disgusting white. Now one of Gage’s cheeks had a hollow, old-man’s look.

“Gage,” he whispered, “going to take you out now, okay?”

He prayed no one would come along now, a caretaker making a 12:30 walk through the cemetery, something like that. But it was no longer a matter of not being caught; if someone else’s flashlight beam caught him as he stood here in the grave doing his awful work, he would grab the bent, scarred spade and smash it into the intruder’s head.

He worked his arms under Gage. The body flopped around loosely, and a sudden, awful certainty came over him: when he lifted Gage, Gage’s body would break apart and he would be left with the pieces. He would be left standing with his feet on the sides of the grave liner with the pieces, screaming. And that was how they would find him.

Go on, you chicken, go on and do it!

He got Gage under the arms, aware of the foul dampness, and lifted him that way, as he had lifted him so often from his evening bath. Gage’s head lolled all the way to the middle of his back. Louis saw the grinning circle of stitches which held Gage’s head onto his shoulders.

Somehow, panting, his stomach cramping from the smell and from the boneless, loose feel of his son’s horribly smashed body, Louis wrestled the body out of the coffin. At last he sat on the edge of the grave with the body in his lap, his feet dangling in the hole, his face a horrible pale color, his eyes black holes, his mouth drawn down in a trembling bow of horror and pity and sorrow.

“Gage,” he said and began to rock the boy in his arms. Gage’s hair lay against Louis’s wrist, as lifeless as wire. “Gage, It will be all right, I swear, Gage, it will be all right, this will end, this is just the night, please, Gage, I love you, Daddy loves you.”

Louis rocked his son.

By quarter of two, Louis was ready to leave the cemetery. Actually handling the body had been the worst of it—that was the point at which that inner traveler, his mind, seemed to float the farthest into the emptiness. And yet now, resting, his back a throbbing hurt in which exhausted muscles jumped and twitched, he felt it might be possible to get back. All the way back.

He put Gage’s body on the tarp and wrapped it up. He secured it with long strips of tape, then cut the rope in two and tied the ends neatly. It could have been a rolled-up rug, nothing more. He closed the coffin, then after a moment, reopened it and put the bent spade in. Let Pleasantview keep that old thing; it wouldn’t have his son. He closed the coffin and lowered half of the cement grave cover. He thought about just dropping the other half but worried it would break. After a moment, he threaded his belt through the iron rings and used it to gently lower the cement square into place. Then he used the shovel to fill the hole. There wasn’t enough dirt to fill it completely. The grave might look sunken. Maybe not. Maybe someone would notice and not care. He wouldn’t think about it or worry tonight—he still had too much to do. More crazy work. And he was very tired.

Hey-ho, let’s go.

“Indeed,” Louis muttered.

The wind picked up, howling through the trees and making him look around. He put the shovel, the unused pick, the gloves, and the flashlight beside the bundle. He wanted to use the light but didn’t. Leaving the body and tools, Louis walked back to the high iron fence, arriving about five minutes later. Across the street was his Civic, parked neatly. So close, yet so far.

Louis looked at it for a moment, then went in a different direction.

He moved away from the gate, walking along the fence until it turned away from Mason Street at a right angle. There was a drainage ditch, and Louis looked in. What he saw made him shudder. There were piles of rotting flowers, layer upon layer, washed down by rain and snow.

Christ.

No, not Christ. These offerings were for a much older God. People have called Him different things, but Rachel’s sister named Him well: Oz the Great and Terrible, God of dead things in the ground, God of rotting flowers in ditches, God of the Mystery.

Louis stared into the ditch as if hypnotized. Finally, he looked away with a gasp—like someone waking up or being called from a trance at the count of ten.

He went on. He hadn’t walked far before finding what he sought, a piece of information his mind had stored on the day of Gage’s burial.

Here, in the windy dark, was the cemetery's crypt.

Coffins were kept there in the winter when it was too cold to dig in the frozen ground. It was also used when there were many deaths at once.

Louis knew that these times of many deaths happened now and then. In any group of people, there were times when many died for no clear reason.

"It all evens out," Uncle Carl told him. "If nobody dies for two weeks in May, Lou, I can count on ten funerals for two weeks in November. But it's rarely November, and never around Christmas, even though people think that's when many die. That stuff about Christmas depression is nonsense. Just ask any funeral director. Most people are happy around Christmas and want to live. So they do live. We usually get a big rush in February. The flu gets the old people, and there's pneumonia, of course—but that's not all. There are people who fight cancer hard for a year or more. Then February comes, and they seem to get tired, and the cancer just finishes them off. On January 31, they're getting better and feel great. By February 24, they're buried. People have heart attacks, strokes, and kidney failure in February. It's a bad month. People get tired in February. We're used to it in this business. But then, for no reason, the same thing will happen in June or October. Never in August. August is slow. Unless a gas line explodes or a bus goes off a bridge, you never fill up the crypt in August. But there have been Februarys when we've had coffins stacked three high, hoping for a thaw so we can bury some before we have to rent an apartment."

Uncle Carl had laughed. And Louis, feeling like he knew something even doctors didn't, had laughed too.

The crypt's double doors were set into a grassy hill, a shape as natural and pleasing as a woman's breast. This hill (which Louis thought was man-made) reached just below the decorative arrow tips of the iron fence, which stayed level instead of following the hill's curve.

Louis looked around, then climbed up the hill. On the other side was an empty square of ground, maybe two acres. No… not quite empty. There was a single shed, separate from everything else. Probably belongs to the cemetery, Louis thought. That's where they keep their equipment.

The streetlights shone through the leaves of trees—old elms and maples—that hid this area from Mason Street. Louis saw no other movement.

He slid back down, afraid of falling and hurting his knee again, and went back to his son's grave. He almost tripped over the tarp. He saw he would have to make two trips, one with the body and another for the tools. He bent over, wincing at the pain in his back, and picked up the stiff tarp. He could feel Gage's body inside and tried to ignore the thought that he had gone crazy.

He carried the body to the hill with the crypt, which had two steel sliding doors (making it look like a two-car garage). He saw what he had to do to get the forty-pound bundle up the steep hill now that his rope was gone and got ready. He backed up and ran at the hill, leaning forward, letting his momentum carry him. He got almost to the top before his feet slipped on the short, slick grass, and he threw the tarp as far as he could as he fell. It landed almost at the top of the hill. He climbed the rest of the way up, looked around again, saw no one, and laid the rolled-up tarp against the fence. Then he went back for the rest of his things.

He gained the top of the hill again, put the gloves on, and piled the flashlight, pick, and shovel next to the tarp. Then he rested, back against the fence, hands on his knees. His new digital watch from Rachel told him it was 2:01.

He gave himself five minutes to rest and then tossed the shovel over the fence. He heard it hit the grass. He tried to stuff the flashlight into his pants, but it wouldn’t fit. He slipped it through the fence and listened to it roll down the hill, hoping it wouldn’t break. He wished he had a backpack.

He took his roll of tape from his jacket pocket and taped the pick to the canvas, going around and around, pulling the tape tight. He did this until the tape was gone and then put the empty roll back in his pocket. He lifted the bundle and threw it over the fence (his back hurt; he’d pay for it all week, he thought) and then let it drop, wincing at the soft thud.

Now he swung one leg over the fence, grabbed two of the points, and swung his other leg over. He skidded down, digging his shoes into the earth between the fence, and dropped to the ground.

He walked down the hill and felt through the grass. He found the shovel right away—the streetlights made the blade gleam. He couldn’t find the flashlight for a moment—how far could it have rolled? He got on his hands and knees and felt through the thick grass, his breath and heartbeat loud.

At last he spotted it, a black shadow a few feet away—like the hill hiding the cemetery, its shape gave it away. He grabbed it, covered the lens, and pushed the switch. His palm lit up briefly, and he switched the flashlight off. It was okay.

He used his pocketknife to cut the pick free and took the tools to the trees. He stood behind the biggest, looking both ways along Mason Street. It was deserted. He saw only one light on—a yellow square—in an upstairs room. Someone awake, perhaps.

Moving quickly, Louis stepped onto the sidewalk. After the darkness of the cemetery, he felt exposed under the streetlights; here he stood, near the cemetery, with a pick, shovel, and flashlight. If someone saw him, it would be obvious.

He crossed the street rapidly, heels clicking. There was his Civic, a short distance away. To Louis, it looked like miles. Sweating, he walked toward it, listening for a car, footsteps, or a window opening.

He got to his Honda, leaned the pick and shovel against it, and looked for his keys. They weren’t there, not in either pocket. He began to sweat. His heart raced, and he clenched his teeth against the panic.

He probably lost them when he fell from the tree, hit the grave marker with his knee, and rolled over. His keys were somewhere in the grass, and if he struggled to find his flashlight, how could he find his keys? It was over. One bit of bad luck and it was over.

Now wait, hold on. Check your pockets again. Your change is there—and if your change didn’t fall out, your keys didn’t either.

He checked his pockets again, slowly, taking out the change, even turning his pockets inside out.

No keys.

Louis leaned against the car, wondering what to do. He would have to climb back in, he guessed. Leave his son where he was, take the flashlight, climb back in, and spend the night searching for—

Suddenly, an idea struck him.

He bent down and looked into the Civic. His keys were dangling from the ignition.

He grunted softly, then ran to the driver’s side, yanked the door open, and grabbed the keys. He suddenly heard the firm voice of Karl Malden, the stern father figure with the potato nose and old-fashioned hat: Lock your car. Take your keys. Don’t let a good boy go bad.

He went to the back of the Civic and opened the hatchback. He put in the pick, shovel, and flashlight, then slammed it shut. He had walked twenty or thirty feet when he remembered his keys. This time, he had left them dangling from the hatchback lock.

Stupid! he cursed himself. If you’re going to be this stupid, just forget the whole thing!

He went back and got his keys.

He had Gage in his arms and was almost back to Mason Street when a dog started barking somewhere. No—it didn’t just start barking. It started to howl, its rough voice filling the street. Auggggh-ROOOO! Auggggh-ROOOOOO!

He stood behind one of the trees, wondering what could happen next, wondering what to do. He stood there expecting lights to start turning on all along the street.

But only one light did turn on, at the side of a house just across from where Louis stood in the shadows. A moment later a rough voice yelled, “Shut up, Fred!”

Auggggh-ROOOOOO! Fred responded.

“Shut him up, Scanlon, or I’m calling the police!” someone yelled from the side of the street Louis was on, making him jump, making him realize how wrong he was to think the street was empty. There were people all around him, hundreds of eyes, and that dog was ruining sleep, his only friend. Goddamn you, Fred, he thought. Oh, goddamn you.

Fred began another howl; he got well into the Auggggh, but before he could get past the start of a good ROOOOOO, there was a hard hit followed by soft whimpers and yips.

Silence followed by the faint slam of a door. The light at the side of Fred’s house stayed on for a moment, then clicked off.

Louis felt like he should stay in the shadows, to wait; it would be better to wait until the noise had died down. But time was passing.

He crossed the street with his bundle and walked back to the Civic, seeing no one. Fred was quiet. He held his bundle in one hand, got his keys, opened the hatchback.

Gage would not fit.

Louis tried the bundle up and down, side to side, then at an angle. The Civic’s back was too small. He could have bent and crammed the bundle in—Gage wouldn’t have minded—but Louis just couldn’t do it.

Come on, come on, come on, let’s get out of here, let’s not go too far.

But he stood, confused, out of ideas, holding the bundle with his son’s body. Then he heard a car coming, and without thinking, he took the bundle to the passenger side, opened the door, and put the bundle on the seat.

He shut the door, ran to the back of the Civic, and slammed the hatchback. A car sped through the intersection, and Louis heard loud, drunk voices. He got in the car, started it, and was about to turn on the headlights when a terrible thought hit him. What if Gage was facing the wrong way, sitting there with his knees and hips bent backward, his empty eyes looking out the back window instead of forward?

It doesn’t matter, his mind screamed, with a fury caused by exhaustion. Can you understand that? It just doesn’t matter!

But it does. It does matter. It’s Gage in there, not just a pile of rags!

He reached over and gently pressed his hands against the tarp, feeling for the shape underneath. He looked like a blind man trying to figure out what something was. Finally, he felt a bump that had to be Gage’s nose—facing the right way.

Only then could he start the car and drive the twenty-five minutes back to Ludlow.
