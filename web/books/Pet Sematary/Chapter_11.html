Pet Sematary

Chapter 11

The next morning at breakfast, Ellie saw the new memo on the bulletin board and asked him what it meant.

“It means he’s going to have a very small operation,” Louis said. “He’ll probably have to stay at the vet’s for one night afterward. And when he comes home, he’ll stay in our yard and not want to wander around so much.”

“Or cross the road?” Ellie asked.

She may be only five, Louis thought, but she’s certainly sharp. “Or cross the road,” he agreed.

“Yay!” Ellie said, and that was the end of the subject.

Louis, who had been ready for a difficult and maybe even frantic argument about Church being out of the house for even one night, was surprised by how easily she had agreed. And he realized how worried she must have been. Maybe Rachel wasn't completely wrong about how the Pet Sematary had affected her.

Rachel herself, who was feeding Gage his breakfast egg, gave him a grateful approving look, and Louis felt something loosen in his chest. The look told him that the tension was over; this particular conflict had ended. Forever, he hoped.

Later, after the big yellow school bus had taken Ellie away for the morning, Rachel came to him, put her arms around his neck, and kissed his mouth gently. “You were very sweet to do that,” she said, “and I’m sorry I was so awful.”

Louis returned her kiss, feeling a little uncomfortable nonetheless. It occurred to him that the I’m sorry I was so awful statement, while not exactly common, wasn't exactly something he’d never heard before either. It usually came after Rachel had gotten her way.

Gage, meanwhile, had waddled over to the front door and was looking out the bottom window at the empty road. “Bus,” he said, tugging casually at his droopy diaper. “Ellie-bus.”

“He’s growing up fast,” Louis said.

Rachel nodded. “Too fast for me, I think.”

“Wait until he’s out of diapers,” Louis said. “Then he can stop.”

She laughed, and everything was fine between them again—completely fine. She stepped back, adjusted his tie a little, and looked him up and down carefully.

“Do I look okay, Sarge?” he asked.

“You look very nice.”

“Yeah, I know. But do I look like a heart surgeon? A two-hundred-thousand-dollar-a-year man?”

“No, just old Lou Creed,” she said and giggled. “The rock-and-roll animal.”

Louis glanced at his watch. “The rock-and-roll animal has got to get going,” he said.

“Are you nervous?”

“Yeah, a little.”

“Don’t be,” she said. “It’s sixty-seven thousand dollars a year for putting on Ace bandages, prescribing for the flu and for hangovers, giving girls the pill—”

“Don’t forget the crab-and-louse lotion,” Louis said, smiling again. One of the things that had surprised him on his first tour of the infirmary had been the supplies of Quell, which seemed huge to him—more suited to an army base infirmary than one on a middle-sized university campus.

Miss Charlton, the head nurse, smiled cynically. “Off-campus apartments around here are pretty bad. You’ll see.”

He guessed he would.

“Have a good day,” she said and kissed him again, lingeringly. But when she pulled away, she was playfully stern. “And for Christ’s sake remember that you’re an administrator, not an intern or a second-year resident!”

“Yes, Doctor,” Louis said humbly, and they both laughed again. For a moment he thought of asking: Was it Zelda, babe? Is that what’s bothering you? Is that the problem? Zelda and how she died? But he wasn’t going to ask her that, not now. As a doctor he knew a lot of things, and while the fact that death was just as natural as childbirth might be the greatest, the fact that you don’t mess with a wound that has finally started to heal was also important.

So instead of asking, he only kissed her again and went out.

It was a good start, a good day. Maine was putting on a late-summer show, the sky was blue and cloudless, the temperature at a perfect seventy-two degrees. Driving to the end of the driveway and checking for traffic, Louis thought that so far he hadn’t seen any of the fall colors that were supposed to be so amazing. But he could wait.

He steered the Honda Civic they had bought as a second car toward the university and let it roll. Rachel would call the vet this morning, they would get Church neutered, and that would end this whole Pet Sematary nonsense (it was funny how that misspelling got into your head and began to seem right) and death fears. There was no need to be thinking about death on a beautiful September morning like this one.

Louis turned on the radio and dialed until he found the Ramones singing “Rockaway Beach.” He turned it up and sang along—not well but with hearty enjoyment.
