Pet Sematary

Chapter 9

Ellie came to him the next day, looking worried. Louis was working on a model in his study. This one was a 1917 Rolls-Royce Silver Ghost—680 pieces, over 50 moving parts. It was almost done, and he could almost see the chauffeur in uniform, like the coachmen of the past, sitting proudly behind the wheel.

He had been obsessed with models since he was ten. He started with a World War I Spad that his Uncle Carl had bought him, worked through most of the Revell airplanes, and moved on to bigger and better things as a teen and in his twenties. There was a time when he made boats in bottles, and another when he made war machines, and even one when he built guns so real it was hard to believe they wouldn’t fire—Colts and Winchesters and Lugers, even a Buntline Special. For the last five years or so, he had been making big cruise ships. A model of the Lusitania and one of the Titanic were on his shelves at his university office, and the Andrea Doria, finished just before they left Chicago, was currently displayed on the mantel in their living room. Now he had moved on to classic cars, and if his past was any indication, he figured he would want to do something new in four or five years. Rachel saw this, his only real hobby, with a wife's patience that held, he thought, some contempt; even after ten years of marriage she probably thought he would outgrow it. Maybe some of this came from her father, who still thought, just as he had when Louis and Rachel got married, that he had gotten a jerk for a son-in-law.

Maybe, he thought, Rachel is right. Maybe I’ll just wake up one morning at thirty-seven, put all these models in the attic, and start hang gliding.

Meanwhile Ellie looked serious.

Far away, in the clear air, he could hear the perfect sound of church bells calling people to worship on a Sunday morning.

“Hi, Dad,” she said.

“Hello, pumpkin. What’s up?”

“Oh, nothing,” she said, but her face said otherwise; her face said that plenty was up, and—none of it was good, thank you very much. Her hair was freshly washed and hung loose to her shoulders. In this light it was still more blond than the brown it was becoming. She was wearing a dress, and Louis realized that his daughter almost always wore a dress on Sundays, even though they didn’t go to church. “What are you building?”

Carefully gluing on a mudguard, he told her. “Look at this,” he said, carefully handing her a hubcap. “See those linked R’s? That’s a nice detail, huh? If we fly back to Chicago for Thanksgiving and we get on an L-1011, look out at the jet engines and you’ll see those same R’s.”

“Hubcap, so what?” She handed it back.

“Please,” he said. “If you have a Rolls-Royce, you call it a wheel covering. If you’re rich enough to own a Rolls, you can show off a little. When I make my second million, I’m going to buy one. Rolls-Royce Corniche. Then when Gage gets carsick, he can throw up into real leather.” And by the way, Ellie, what’s wrong? But it didn’t work like that with Ellie. You didn’t ask things directly. She was careful about sharing too much. It was something Louis admired.

“Are we rich, Daddy?”

“No,” he said, “but we won’t starve.”

“Michael Burns at school says all doctors are rich.”

“Well, you tell Michael Burns at school that many doctors get rich, but it takes twenty years… and you don’t get rich running a university clinic. You get rich as a specialist. A gynecologist or an orthopedist or a neurologist. They get rich faster. For general doctors like me, it takes longer.”

“Then why don’t you be a specialist, Daddy?”

Louis thought of his models again and how he had suddenly not wanted to build warplanes anymore, how he had also gotten tired of Tiger tanks and gun emplacements, how he had come to believe (almost suddenly, it seemed looking back) that building boats in bottles was pretty silly; and then he thought of what it would be like to spend your whole life checking children’s feet for hammertoe or putting on those thin Latex gloves so you could feel along a woman’s vaginal canal with one trained finger, feeling for bumps or sores.

“I just wouldn’t like it,” he said.

Church came into the office, stopped, looked at the situation with his bright green eyes. He jumped silently onto the windowsill and seemed to fall asleep.

Ellie glanced at him and frowned, which Louis thought was very strange. Usually, Ellie looked at Church with so much love it was almost sickening. She began to walk around the office, looking at the models, and said in a voice that was almost casual, “Wow, there were a lot of graves in the Pet Sematary, weren’t there?”

This is the heart of the matter, Louis thought, but he didn't look up. After checking his instructions, he started putting the carriage lamps on the Rolls.

“There were,” he said. “More than a hundred, I’d say.”

“Daddy, why don’t pets live as long as people?”

“Well, some animals do live about as long,” he said, “and some live much longer. Elephants live a very long time, and some sea turtles are so old that people don’t really know how old they are… or maybe they do, and they just can’t believe it.”

Ellie quickly dismissed that. “Elephants and sea turtles aren’t pets. Pets don’t live very long at all. Michael Burns says that every year a dog lives, it’s like nine of our years.”

“Seven,” Louis corrected without thinking. “I know what you mean, honey, and there’s some truth to it. A dog who lives to be twelve is an old dog. It’s because of something called metabolism, which seems to tell time. It does other things too—some people can eat a lot and stay thin because of their metabolism, like your mother. Other people—like me—can’t eat as much without getting fat. Our metabolisms are different, that’s all. But mostly, metabolism seems to act like a body clock for living things. Dogs have a fast metabolism. Human beings have a much slower metabolism. Most of us live to be about seventy-two. And believe me, seventy-two years is a very long time.”

Because Ellie looked really worried, he hoped he sounded more sincere than he felt. He was thirty-five, and it seemed like those years had passed as quickly as a draft under a door. “Sea turtles, on the other hand, have an even slower metabo—”

“What about cats?” Ellie asked, looking at Church again.

“Well, cats live about as long as dogs,” he said, “mostly.” This was a lie, and he knew it. Cats lived dangerous lives and often died violent deaths, usually just out of sight. Here was Church, dozing in the sun (or pretending to), Church who slept peacefully on his daughter’s bed every night, Church who had been so cute as a kitten, all tangled up in a ball of string. And yet Louis had seen him stalk a bird with a broken wing, his green eyes sparkling with curiosity and—yes, Louis would have sworn it—cold delight. He rarely killed what he stalked, but there had been one exception—a large rat, probably caught in the alley between their apartment building and the next. Church had really finished that rat off. It had been so bloody that Rachel, who was six months pregnant with Gage, had had to run to the bathroom and throw up. Dangerous lives, violent deaths. A dog would catch them and rip them open instead of just chasing them like the silly, easily fooled dogs in cartoons, or another tomcat would get them, or poisoned bait, or a car. Cats were like gangsters in the animal world, living outside the law and often dying there. Many of them never grew old by the fire.

But those were maybe not things to tell your five-year-old daughter, who was just beginning to understand what death meant.

“I mean,” he said, “Church is only three now, and you’re five. He could still be alive when you’re fifteen, in high school. And that’s a long time from now.”

“It doesn’t seem long to me,” Ellie said, her voice now shaking. “Not long at all.”

Louis stopped pretending to work on his model and asked her to come over. She sat on his lap, and he was again struck by how beautiful she was, made even more so by her being upset. She had dark skin, almost Middle Eastern looking. Tony Benton, a doctor he had worked with in Chicago, used to call her the Indian Princess.

“Honey,” he said, “if I could, I’d let Church live to be a hundred. But I don’t make the rules.”

“Who does?” she asked, and then, with great disdain: “God, I guess.”

Louis stopped himself from laughing. It was too serious.

“God or Someone,” he said. “Things just stop working—that’s all I know. Nothing is certain, babe.”

“I don’t want Church to be like all those dead pets!” she cried, suddenly tearful and angry. “I don’t want Church to ever die! He’s my cat! He’s not God’s cat! Let God have His own cat! Let God have all the old cats He wants, and kill them all! Church is mine!”

There were footsteps in the kitchen, and Rachel looked in, surprised. Ellie was now crying against Louis’s chest. The horror had been spoken; it was out in the open; it had been described and could be faced. Now, even if it couldn’t be changed, it could at least be cried about.

“Ellie,” he said, rocking her, “Ellie, Ellie, Church isn’t dead; he’s right over there, sleeping.”

“But he could be,” she wept. “He could be, any time.”

He held her and rocked her, believing, rightly or wrongly, that Ellie cried because death couldn't be stopped, that it didn't care about arguments or a little girl’s tears; that she cried because it was so cruel and unpredictable; and that she cried because people could turn symbols into ideas that were either good and noble or horribly scary. If all those animals had died and been buried, then Church could die

(any time!)

and be buried; and if that could happen to Church, it could happen to her mother, her father, her baby brother. To herself. Death was just an idea; the Pet Sematary was real. The rough markers held truths that even a child’s hands could feel.

It would be easy to lie now, like he had about how long cats live. But she would remember a lie later and maybe add it to the list children make about their parents. His own mother had told him a lie, a small one about women finding babies in the grass when they wanted them, and even though it wasn't a big deal, Louis had never forgiven his mother for telling it—or himself for believing it.

“Honey,” he said, “it happens. It’s a part of life.”

“It’s a bad part!” she cried. “It’s a really bad part!”

There was no answer for this. She wept. Eventually she would stop crying. It was the first thing she had to do to accept a truth that wouldn't go away.

He held his daughter and listened to church bells on Sunday morning, floating across the September fields; and it was some time after she had stopped crying that he realized she had fallen asleep, like Church.

He put her up in her bed and then came downstairs to the kitchen, where Rachel was beating cake batter too hard. He mentioned his surprise that Ellie should just conk out like that in the middle of the morning; it wasn’t like her.

“No,” Rachel said, setting the bowl down on the counter with a decisive thump. “It isn’t, but I think she was awake most of last night. I heard her tossing around, and Church cried to go out around three. He only does that when she’s restless.”

“Why would she…?”

“Oh, you know why!” Rachel said, angrily. “That damned pet cemetery is why! It really upset her, Lou. It was the first cemetery of any kind for her, and it just… upset her. I don’t think I’ll write your friend Jud Crandall any thank-you notes for that little hike.”

All at once he’s my friend, Louis thought, amused and troubled at the same time.

“Rachel—”

“And I don’t want her going up there again.”

“Rachel, what Jud said about the path is true.”

“It’s not the path and you know it,” Rachel said. She picked up the bowl again and began beating the cake batter even faster. “it’s that damned place. It’s unhealthy. Kids going up there and tending the graves, keeping the path… really sick is what it is. Whatever problems the kids in this town have got, I don’t want Ellie to catch it.”

Louis stared at her, puzzled. He strongly suspected that one of the things which had kept their marriage together when it seemed as if each year brought the news that two or three of their friends’ marriages had fallen apart was their respect for the unknown—the half-understood but never spoken idea that maybe, when you got right down to the heart of things, there was no such thing as marriage, no such thing as union, that each person stood alone and ultimately defied reason. That was the mystery. And no matter how well you thought you knew your partner, you occasionally ran into blank walls or fell into pits. And sometimes (rarely, thank God) you ran into a full-fledged area of alien strangeness, something like the clear-air turbulence that can buffet an airliner for no reason at all. An attitude or belief which you had never suspected, one so peculiar (at least to you) that it seemed nearly insane. And then you trod lightly, if you valued your marriage and your peace of mind; you tried to remember that anger at such a discovery was for fools who really believed it was possible for one mind to know another.

“Honey, it’s just a pet cemetery,” he said.

“The way she was crying in there,” Rachel said, pointing toward his office with a batter-covered spoon, “do you think it’s just a pet cemetery to her? It’s going to hurt her, Lou. No. She’s not going up there anymore. It’s not the path, it’s the place. She already thinks Church is going to die.”

For a moment, Louis felt like he was talking to Ellie. As if she were wearing stilts, one of her mother’s dresses, and a very good Rachel mask. Even her face was the same—serious and a bit sad on the surface, but hurt underneath.

He struggled, because suddenly the problem felt big to him, not something to ignore because of that mystery… or that loneliness. He struggled because it seemed like she was missing something huge, and you couldn’t miss it unless you were closing your eyes on purpose.

“Rachel,” he said, “Church is going to die.”

She stared at him, angry. “That’s not the point,” she said, speaking slowly and clearly, like she was talking to a child. “Church isn’t going to die today, or tomorrow—”

“I tried to tell her that—”

“Or the day after that, or probably for years—”

“Honey, we can’t know for sure—”

“Of course, we can!” she shouted. “We take good care of him, he’s not going to die, no one is going to die around here, so why do you want to upset a little girl about something she won’t understand until she’s older?”

“Rachel, listen.”

But Rachel wasn’t going to listen. She was furious. “It’s hard enough to deal with a death—a pet or a friend or family—when it happens, without making it into a… a goddam tourist spot… a F-F-Forest Lawn for a-animals…” Tears ran down her face.

“Rachel,” he said and tried to touch her shoulders. She quickly and sharply shook him off.

“Forget it,” she said. “You don’t understand at all.”

He sighed. “I feel like I fell into a secret hole and into a huge blender,” he said, hoping she’d smile. She didn’t; her eyes were fixed on his, dark and blazing. She was enraged, he realized; not just mad, but totally enraged. “Rachel,” he said suddenly, not sure what he’d say until it came out, “how did you sleep last night?”

“Oh please,” she said dismissively, turning away—but he saw a flash of pain in her eyes. “That’s really smart. Really smart. You never change, Louis. When things go wrong, blame Rachel, right? Rachel’s just being overly emotional.”

“That’s not right.”

“No?” She took the cake batter to the counter by the stove and set it down with a bang. She started greasing a cake pan, her lips tightly pressed together.

He said calmly, “It’s okay for a child to learn about death, Rachel. Actually, it’s important. Ellie’s reaction—her crying—seemed normal to me. It—”

“Oh, it sounded normal,” Rachel said, turning to face him again. “It sounded very normal to hear her crying her heart out over her perfectly healthy cat—”

“Stop it,” he said. “You’re not making any sense.”

“I don’t want to talk about it anymore.”

“Yes, but we’re going to,” he said, now angry too. “You had your say—now let me have mine?”

“She’s not going up there anymore. And as far as I’m concerned, that’s the end of it.”

“Ellie has known where babies come from since last year,” Louis said carefully. “We got her the Myers book and talked to her about it, remember? We both agreed that children should know where they come from.”

“That has nothing to do with—”

“It does, though!” he said harshly. “When I was talking to her in my office, about Church, I started thinking about my mother and how she told me that old story about babies coming from cabbage leaves when I asked her where women got babies. I’ve never forgotten that lie. I don’t think children ever forget the lies their parents tell them.”

“Where babies come from has nothing to do with a goddamn pet cemetery!” Rachel shouted at him, and her eyes told him Talk about the similarities all night and all day, if you want to, Louis; talk until you’re blue in the face, but I won’t accept it.

Still, he tried.

“She knows about babies; that place up in the woods just made her want to know something about what happens at the other end of life. It’s perfectly natural. In fact, I think it’s the most natural thing i—”

“Will you stop saying that!” she screamed suddenly—really screamed and Louis recoiled, startled. His elbow hit the open bag of flour on the counter. It fell off the edge and hit the floor, splitting open. Flour puffed up in a dry white cloud.

“Oh fuck,” he said sadly.

In an upstairs room, Gage began to cry.

“That’s nice,” she said, also crying now. “You woke the baby up too. Thanks for a nice, quiet, stress-free Sunday morning.”

She started past him and Louis put a hand on her arm. “Let me ask you something,” he said, “because I know that anything—literally anything—can happen to people. As a doctor I know that. Do you want to be the one to explain to her what happened if her cat gets sick or cancer—cats get cancer a lot, you know—or if he gets run over in that road? Do you want to be the one, Rachel?”

“Let me go,” she almost hissed. The anger in her voice was weaker than the hurt and confused fear in her eyes—I don’t want to talk about this, Louis, and you can’t make me, that look said. “Let me go, I want to get Gage before he falls out of his cr—”

“Because maybe you should be the one,” he said. “You can tell her we don’t talk about it, nice people don’t talk about it, they just bury it—oops! but don’t say ‘buried,’ you’ll upset her.”

“I hate you!” Rachel sobbed and pulled away from him.

Then he was of course sorry, and it was of course too late.

“Rachel—”

She pushed by him roughly, crying harder. “Leave me alone. You’ve done enough.” She paused in the kitchen doorway, turning toward him, the tears streaming down her cheeks. “I don’t want this discussed in front of Ellie anymore, Lou. I mean it. There’s nothing natural about death. Nothing. You, as a doctor, should know that.”

She whirled and was gone, leaving Louis in the empty kitchen, which still echoed with their voices. At last he went to the pantry to get the broom. As he swept, he thought about the last thing she had said and how huge this disagreement was, one they hadn't seen for so long. Because, as a doctor, he knew that death was, except maybe for childbirth, the most natural thing in the world. Taxes weren't a sure thing; human fights weren't; society's problems weren't; good and bad times weren't. In the end, there was only time passing, and the gravestones, which became worn and unreadable over time. Even sea turtles and the giant sequoias had to give out someday.

“Zelda,” he said aloud. “Christ, that must have been bad for her.”

The question was, should he just ignore it or should he try to fix it?

He tipped the dustpan over the trash can, and flour slid out with a soft puff, covering the thrown-out cartons and empty cans.
