Pet Sematary

Chapter 22

The Thanksgiving dinner Jud and Norma made was excellent. When it was over, Louis went home feeling full and sleepy. He went upstairs to the bedroom, enjoying the quiet a little, kicked off his shoes, and lay down. It was just after three o’clock; the day outside was bright with weak, winter sunshine.

I’ll just doze a little, he thought and fell fast asleep.

The phone ringing in the bedroom woke him up. He reached for it, trying to wake up fully, confused by the fact that it was almost dark outside. He could hear the wind blowing hard around the corners of the house and the faint, rough sound of the furnace.

“Hello,” he said. It would be Rachel, calling from Chicago again to wish him a happy Thanksgiving. She would put Ellie on and Ellie would talk and then Gage would get on and Gage would babble—and how the hell had he managed to sleep all afternoon when he had meant to watch the football game… ?

But it wasn’t Rachel. It was Jud.

“Louis? I’m afraid you might have a little problem.”

He swung out of bed, still trying to clear the sleep from his head. “Jud? What problem?”

“Well, there’s a dead cat over here on our lawn,” Jud said. “I think it might be your daughter’s.”

“Church?” Louis asked. There was a sudden sinking in his stomach. “Are you sure, Jud?”

“No, I’m not one hundred percent sure,” Jud said, “but it sure looks like him.”

“Oh. Oh shit. I’ll be right over, Jud.”

“All right, Louis.”

He hung up and just sat there for another minute. Then he went in and used the bathroom, put on his shoes, and went downstairs.

Well, maybe it isn’t Church. Jud himself said he wasn’t one hundred percent sure. Christ, the cat doesn’t even want to go upstairs anymore unless someone carries him… why would he cross the road?

But deep down he felt sure that it was Church… and if Rachel called this evening, as she almost certainly would, what was he going to say to Ellie?

Strangely, he imagined himself saying to Rachel: I know that anything, literally anything, can happen to living things. As a doctor I know that… do you want to be the one to explain to her what happened if he gets run over in the road? But he hadn’t really believed anything was going to happen to Church, had he?

He remembered one of the guys he played poker with, Wickes Sullivan, asking him once how he could be attracted to his wife but not to the naked women he saw every day. Louis had tried to explain that it wasn’t like in people’s fantasies—a woman coming in for a Pap smear or to learn how to do a breast self-exam didn’t suddenly drop a sheet and stand there like a goddess. You saw a breast, a vulva, a thigh. The rest was covered, and there was a nurse there, mostly to protect the doctor’s reputation. Wicky didn’t believe it. A tit is a tit, was Wicky’s idea, and a twat is a twat. You should either be horny all the time or never. All Louis could say was that your wife’s tit was different.

Just like your family’s supposed to be different, he thought now. Church wasn’t supposed to get killed because he was part of the family. What he hadn’t been able to make Wicky understand was that doctors separated things in their minds as easily and blindly as anyone else. A tit wasn’t a tit unless it was your wife’s tit. At the office, a tit was a case. You could talk about leukemia statistics in children until you were blue in the face and still not believe it if one of your own kids got sick. My kid? My kid’s cat, even? Doctor, you have to be kidding.

Never mind. Take this one step at a time.

But that was hard when he remembered how upset Ellie had become at the thought of Church dying someday.

Stupid cat, why did we ever have to get a cat, anyway?

But he wasn’t doing that anymore. That was supposed to keep him alive.

“Church?” he called, but there was only the furnace, humming and humming, wasting money. The couch in the living room, where Church had been spending most of his time lately, was empty. He wasn’t lying on any of the radiators. Louis rattled the cat’s dish, the one thing that always made Church come running if he could hear it, but no cat came running this time… and never would again, he was afraid.

He put on his coat and hat and headed for the door. Then he came back. Giving in to what his heart told him, he opened the cupboard under the sink and crouched down. There were two kinds of plastic bags in there—small white ones for the trash cans and big green garbage bags. Louis took one of the latter. Church had gained weight since he had been neutered.

He pushed the bag into one of the side pockets of his jacket, not liking the smooth, cold feel of the plastic under his fingers. Then he went out the front door and crossed the street to Jud’s house.

It was about five-thirty. Twilight was ending. The landscape looked lifeless. The last bit of sunset was a strange orange line on the horizon across the river. The wind blew straight down Route 15, numbing Louis’s cheeks and whipping away his white breath. He shuddered, but not from the cold. It was a feeling of loneliness that made him shudder. It was strong and convincing. There seemed no way to describe it simply. It was unseen. He just felt alone, untouched and unable to touch.

He saw Jud across the road, wrapped up in his big green coat, his face hidden by the shadow of the fur-trimmed hood. Standing on his frozen lawn, he looked like a statue, just another dead thing in this twilight scene where no bird sang.

Louis started across, and then Jud moved—waved him back. Shouted something Louis couldn’t hear over the loud whine of the wind. Louis stepped back, realizing the wind’s whine had gotten louder and sharper. A moment later an air-horn blasted and an Orinco truck roared past close enough to make his pants and jacket flap. He almost walked right in front of it.

This time he looked both ways before crossing. There was only the tanker’s taillights, disappearing into the twilight.

“Thought that ‘Rinco truck was gonna get you,” Jud said. “Be careful, Louis.” Even this close, Louis couldn’t see Jud’s face, and the uncomfortable feeling persisted that this could have been anyone… anyone at all.

“Where’s Norma?” he asked, still not looking down at the sprawled bundle of fur by Jud’s foot.

“Went to the Thanksgiving church service,” he said. “She’ll stay for the supper, I guess, although I don’t think she’ll eat anything. She’s lost her appetite.” The wind gusted, shifting the hood back momentarily, and Louis saw that it was indeed Jud—who else would it have been? “It’s mostly an excuse for a hen party,” Jud said. “They don’t eat much but sandwiches after the big meal at noon. She’ll be back around eight.”

Louis knelt down to look at the cat. Don’t let it be Church, he wished hard, as he turned its head gently on its neck with gloved fingers. Let it be someone else’s cat, let Jud be wrong.

But of course it was Church. He was not mangled or disfigured in any way; he had not been run over by one of the big tankers or semis that cruised Route 15 (just what was that Orinco truck doing out on Thanksgiving? he wondered randomly). Church’s eyes were half-open, as glazed as green marbles. A small flow of blood had come from his mouth, which was also open. Not a lot of blood; just enough to stain the white bib on his chest.

“Yours, Louis?”

“Mine,” he agreed and sighed.

He was aware for the first time that he had loved Church—maybe not as much as Ellie but in his own quiet way. In the weeks after he was neutered, Church had changed, had gotten fat and slow, had established a routine that took him between Ellie’s bed, the couch, and his dish but rarely out of the house. Now, in death, he looked to Louis like the old Church. The mouth so small and bloody, filled with needle-sharp cat’s teeth, was frozen in a fierce snarl. The dead eyes seemed furious. It was as if after the short and uneventful life as a neutered cat, Church had rediscovered his real nature in dying.

“Yeah, it’s Church,” he said. “I’ll be damned if I know how I’m going to tell Ellie about it.”

Suddenly he had an idea. He would bury Church up in the Pet Sematary with no marker or any of that foolishness. He would say nothing to Ellie on the phone tonight about Church; tomorrow he would mention casually that he hadn’t seen Church around; the day after he would suggest that perhaps Church had wandered off. Cats did that sometimes. Ellie would be upset, sure, but there would be none of the finality… no repeat of Rachel’s upsetting refusal to deal with death… just a fading away…

Coward, part of his mind said.

Yes… no argument there. But who wants this trouble?

“She really loves that cat, doesn’t she?” Jud asked.

“Yes,” Louis said, distracted. He moved Church’s head again. The cat had started to get stiff, but the head still moved much more easily than it should have. Broken neck. Right. Knowing that, he thought he could figure out what happened. Church had been crossing the road—God knows why—and a car or truck had hit him, breaking his neck and throwing him onto Jud Crandall’s lawn. Or maybe the cat’s neck broke when he hit the frozen ground. It didn’t matter. Either way, the result was the same. Church was dead.

He looked up at Jud, ready to tell him what he thought, but Jud was looking away at the fading orange light on the horizon. His hood had fallen back a bit, and his face looked thoughtful and serious… even hard.

Louis pulled the green garbage bag out of his pocket and opened it up, holding it tight so the wind wouldn’t blow it away. The sharp crackling sound of the bag seemed to bring Jud back to the present.

“Yes, I guess she really loves it,” Jud said. His use of the present tense felt a little strange… the whole scene, with the fading light, the cold, and the wind, felt strange and gothic to him.

Here’s Heathcliff out on the empty moors, Louis thought, wincing against the cold. Getting ready to put the family cat into a Hefty Bag. Great.

He grabbed Church’s tail, opened the bag, and lifted the cat. He made a disgusted, unhappy face at the sound the cat’s body made as he lifted it—rrrriiippp as he pulled it out of the frost it had settled into. The cat felt unbelievably heavy, as if death had become a real weight inside it. Christ, he feels like a bucket of sand.

Jud held the other side of the bag, and Louis dropped Church in, happy to be rid of that strange, unpleasant weight.

“What are you going to do with it now?” Jud asked.

“Put him in the garage, I guess,” Louis said. “Bury him in the morning.”

“In the Pet Sematary?”

Louis shrugged. “I guess so.”

“Going to tell Ellie?”

“I… I’ll have to think about that for a while.”

Jud was quiet for a moment, and then he seemed to make up his mind. “Wait here a minute, Louis.”

Jud moved away, not even thinking that Louis might not want to wait on this cold night. He moved with confidence and that easy grace that was so strange in an old man. And Louis found he had nothing to say anyway. He didn’t feel like himself. He watched Jud go, happy to stand there.

He lifted his face into the wind after the door clicked shut, the garbage bag with Church’s body in it rustling between his feet.

Content.

Yes, he was. For the first time since they had moved to Maine, he felt he belonged, that he was home. Standing alone in the fading light, on the edge of winter, he felt sad but also strangely excited and complete—more complete than he had been, or could recall being, since he was a child.

Something's going to happen here, Bubba. Something pretty weird, I think.

He looked up and saw cold winter stars in a darkening sky.

He didn't know how long he stood like that, but it couldn't have been long. Then a light came on Jud's porch, moved, went to the door, and down the steps. It was Jud with a big flashlight. In his other hand he held something Louis thought was a large X… then he saw it was a pick and shovel.

He gave the shovel to Louis, who took it.

"Jud, what are you doing? We can't bury him tonight."

"Yes, we can. And we will." Jud's face was hidden by the bright light.

"Jud, it's dark. It's late. And cold—"

"Come on," Jud said. "Let's get it done."

Louis shook his head and tried to start again, but the words were hard to find—the words of explanation and sense. They seemed so pointless against the wind's low cry, the many stars in the black sky.

“It can wait till tomorrow when we can see—”

“Does she love the cat?”

“Yes, but—”

Jud’s voice, soft and somehow reasonable: “And do you love her?”

“Of course I love her, she’s my dau—”

“Then come on.”

Louis went.

Twice—maybe three times—on the walk up to the Pet Sematary that night Louis tried to talk to Jud, but Jud didn’t answer. Louis gave up. That feeling of peace, strange considering the situation but completely real, stayed with him. It seemed to come from everywhere. The steady ache in his muscles from carrying Church in one hand and the shovel in the other was a part of it. The wind, deadly cold, numbing exposed skin, was a part of it; it twisted steadily in the trees. Once they got into the woods, there was hardly any snow. The bobbing light of Jud’s flash was a part of it. He felt the strong, undeniable, pull of some secret. Some dark secret.

The shadows disappeared and there was a feeling of space. Snow shone palely.

“Rest here,” Jud said, and Louis set the bag down. He wiped sweat off his forehead with his arm. Rest here? But they were here. He could see the markers in the moving, random sweep of Jud’s light as Jud sat down in the thin snow and put his face between his arms.

“Jud? Are you all right?”

“Fine. Just need to catch my breath.”

Louis sat down next to him and took several deep breaths.

“You know,” he said, “I feel better than I have in maybe six years. I know that sounds crazy when you’re burying your daughter’s cat, but it’s the truth, Jud. I feel good.”

Jud breathed deeply a couple of times. “Yeah, I know,” he said. “It happens sometimes. You don’t choose when you feel good, any more than you choose when you feel bad. And the place has something to do with it, but don’t trust that. Heroin makes addicts feel good when they shoot up, but it’s poisoning them the whole time. Poisoning their bodies and their minds. This place can be like that, Louis, and don’t forget it. I hope I’m doing the right thing. I think I am, but I can’t be sure. Sometimes my head gets mixed up. I think it's just old age.”

“I don’t know what you’re talking about.”

“This place has power, Louis. Not so much here, but… the place we’re going.”

“Jud—”

“Come on,” Jud said, standing up again. The flashlight shone on the pile of fallen trees. Jud was walking toward it. Louis suddenly remembered when he had sleepwalked. What did Pascow say in the dream he had then?

Don’t go beyond, no matter how much you want to, Doctor. The barrier should not be broken.

But now, tonight, that dream or warning or whatever it was felt like years ago, not just months. Louis felt good, almost magically alive, ready for anything, yet full of wonder. He thought this felt a lot like a dream.

Then Jud turned toward him, the hood making his face seem empty, and for a moment Louis thought it was Pascow himself standing there. He imagined the light turning around, shining on a grinning skull in fur, and his fear came back like a splash of cold water.

“Jud,” he said, “we can’t climb over that. We’ll both break a leg and probably freeze trying to get back.”

“Just follow me,” Jud said. “Follow me and don’t look down. Don’t stop and don’t look down. I know the way through, but we have to do it fast and sure.”

Louis started to think it might be a dream, that he had never woken up from his nap. If I was awake, he thought, I wouldn’t go up that mess of fallen trees any more than I’d get drunk and go skydiving. But I’m going to do it. I really think I am. So… I must be dreaming. Right?

He didn't struggle; he didn't climb hunched over like someone climbing a hill of rocks or sand. He just stepped up, as if climbing stairs. He walked like a man who knows exactly where to step next.

Louis followed the same way.

He didn't look down or look for places to put his feet. He felt strangely certain that the fallen trees couldn't hurt him unless he let them. It was completely stupid, of course, like a drunk driver who thinks he's safe as long as he's wearing his lucky charm.

But it worked.

There was no sharp snap of an old branch breaking, no sickening fall into a hole filled with sharp, weathered splinters, each ready to cut and tear. His shoes (Hush Puppy loafers—not ideal for climbing dead-falls) didn’t slip on the old dry moss covering the fallen trees. He didn’t fall forward or backward. The wind howled through the fir trees around them.

For a moment he saw Jud standing on top of the deadfall, and then he started down the other side, calves disappearing, then thighs, then hips and waist. The light flashed off the swaying branches of the trees on the other side of the… the barrier. Yes, that’s what it was—why pretend otherwise? The barrier.

Louis reached the top himself and paused, right foot on an old fallen tree angled upward, left foot on something springier—a tangle of old fir branches? He didn’t look down, but switched the heavy trashbag with Church’s body from his right hand to his left, exchanging it for the lighter shovel. He turned his face into the wind and felt it rush past him, lifting his hair. It was so cold, so clean… so constant.

Moving casually, almost strolling, he started down again. Once a branch about as thick as a strong man’s wrist snapped loudly under his foot, but he felt no worry—and his falling foot was stopped by a heavier branch a few inches down. Louis barely stumbled. He guessed he now understood how company commanders in World War I could stroll along the top of the trenches with bullets flying around them, whistling “Tipperary.” It was crazy, but the craziness made it incredibly exciting.

He walked down, looking straight ahead at Jud’s bright light. Jud was standing there, waiting. Then he reached the bottom, and the excitement burst within him like a shot of fuel on embers.

“We made it!” he shouted. He put the shovel down and clapped Jud on the shoulder. He remembered climbing an apple tree to the top where it swayed in the wind like a ship’s mast. He had not felt so young or so alive in twenty years or more. “Jud, we made it!”

“Did you think we wouldn’t?” Jud asked.

Louis started to say something—Think we wouldn’t? We’re lucky we didn’t kill ourselves!—and then stopped. He had never really doubted it, not from the moment Jud approached the deadfall. And he wasn’t worried about going back.

“I guess not,” he said.

“Come on. We still have a ways to walk. Three miles or more.”

They walked. The path did keep going. In some spots it seemed very wide, but the moving light didn't show much clearly. Mostly, it was a feeling of space, like the trees had moved back. Once or twice Louis looked up and saw stars turning between the thick dark edge of trees. Once, something ran across the path in front of them, and the light caught the reflection of greenish eyes—there and gone.

At other times, the path narrowed until bushes scratched stiff fingers across Louis’s coat. He switched the bag and the shovel more often, but his shoulders now ached constantly. He fell into a walking rhythm and became almost hypnotized by it. There was power here, he felt it. He remembered being a senior in high school. He, his girlfriend, and another couple had gone way out in the backwoods and ended up making out at the end of a dead-end dirt road near a power station. They hadn’t been there long before Louis’s girlfriend said she wanted to go home, or at least somewhere else, because all her teeth (especially the ones with fillings, which was most of them) were aching. Louis had been glad to leave too. The air around the power station had made him feel nervous and too alert. This was like that, but stronger. Stronger, but not unpleasant. It was—

Jud had stopped at the bottom of a long slope. Louis bumped into him.

Jud turned toward him. “We’re almost there now,” he said calmly. “This next part is like the deadfall—you have to walk steady and easy. Just follow me and don’t look down. Did you feel us going downhill?”

“Yes.”

“This is the edge of what the Micmacs called Little God Swamp. The fur traders called it Dead Man’s Bog, and most who came once and got out never came back.”

“Is there quicksand?”

“Oh, yes, plenty of quicksand! Streams that bubble up through a big pile of quartz sand left by the glacier. We always called it silica sand, but there's probably a real name for it.”

Jud looked at him, and for a moment Louis thought he saw something bright and not very nice in the old man’s eyes.

Then Jud moved the flashlight and that look disappeared.

“There are a lot of strange things down this way, Louis. The air feels heavier… more electric… or something.”

Louis jumped.

“What’s wrong?”

“Nothing,” Louis said, thinking about that night on the dead-end road.

“You might see St. Elmo’s fire—what sailors call ghost-lights. It makes strange shapes, but it’s nothing. If you see some of those shapes and they bother you, just look away. You may hear sounds like voices, but they are the loons down south toward Prospect. The sound travels. It's strange.”

“Loons?” Louis said, sounding unsure. “This time of year?”

“Oh, yes,” Jud said again, and his voice was terribly flat and impossible to read. For a moment Louis really wished he could see the old man’s face again. That look—

“Jud, where are we going? What are we doing out here in the middle of nowhere?”

“I’ll tell you when we get there.” Jud turned away. “Watch out for the clumps of grass.”

They began to walk again, stepping from one large mound to the next. Louis didn’t think about where to step. His feet seemed to find the mounds on their own, without him trying. He slipped only once, his left shoe breaking through a thin layer of ice and sinking into cold, slimy water. He pulled it out quickly and kept going, following Jud’s swaying light. That light, moving through the woods, reminded him of the pirate stories he had enjoyed reading as a kid. Bad guys off to bury gold coins in the dark of night… and of course, one of them would fall into the hole on top of the chest, with a bullet in his heart, because the pirates believed—or so the writers of these exciting stories claimed—that the dead friend’s ghost would stay there to protect the loot.

Except we're not burying treasure. Just my daughter’s neutered cat.

He felt like laughing out loud but held it back.

He didn’t hear any “sounds like voices,” or see any strange lights, but after stepping over several clumps of grass, he looked down and saw that his feet, calves, knees, and lower thighs had disappeared into a ground fog that was perfectly smooth, perfectly white, and completely blocked his view. It was like walking through the world’s lightest snow.

The air seemed to have a kind of light in it now, and it was warmer, he was sure of it. He could see Jud ahead of him, moving steadily, with the dull end of the pickaxe over his shoulder. The pickaxe added to the image of a man determined to bury treasure.

That strange feeling of excitement stayed with him, and he suddenly wondered if maybe Rachel was trying to call him; if, back at the house, the phone was ringing and ringing, making its normal, everyday sound. If—

He almost bumped into Jud’s back again. The old man had stopped in the middle of the path. His head was tilted to one side. His mouth was tight and strained.

Louis quieted down, looking around nervously. Here the ground fog was thinner, but he still couldn’t see his own shoes. Then he heard rustling bushes and snapping branches. Something was moving out there—something big.

He opened his mouth to ask Jud if it was a moose (he really thought it was a bear), then closed it. The sound carries, Jud had said.

He tilted his head, copying Jud without realizing it, and listened. The sound seemed far away, then very near, moving away and then coming toward them in a scary way. Louis felt sweat trickle down his face. He shifted the bag with Church in it from one hand to the other. His palm was damp, and the plastic felt slippery, like it would slide out of his hand. Now he didn’t know what he was thinking.

Then it moved away and vanished.

Louis felt like every part of him had turned to ice, and that he had gained weight, so much weight that if he tried to run, he would sink into the swampy ground.

The laughter rose, broke into dry cracks like a crumbling rock, reached a scream, then fell into a low chuckle that might have turned into sobs before it disappeared.

Somewhere, water dripped, and above them, like a river in the sky, the wind made a steady noise. Otherwise, Little God Swamp was quiet.

Louis started to shake all over. His skin, especially on his stomach, began to crawl. Yes, crawl was the right word; his skin really seemed to be moving. His mouth was totally dry. He felt like he had no spit left. Yet he still felt excited, a crazy feeling that wouldn't go away.

For a few moments Louis felt like he was in an open space, but the dim glow in the air was now gone, and he could barely see Jud’s back three feet in front of him. But up here none of that seemed important. The wind was more important, blowing freely around him in that steady flow, lifting his hair from his forehead and ears.

Jud sat down with his back against a tree, cupped his hands around a match, and lit a Chesterfield. “Want to rest before you start?”

“No, I’m okay,” Louis said. He could have asked more, but he didn't really want to. This felt wrong but also right, and he decided that was enough… for now. He really only needed to know one thing. “Will I really be able to dig him a grave? The soil looks thin.” Louis nodded toward the rock sticking out of the ground at the edge of the steps.

Jud nodded slowly. “Yeah,” he said. “Soil’s thin, alright. But soil that grows grass is usually deep enough to bury in, Louis. And people have been burying here a long, long time. It won’t be easy, though.”

And it wasn't. The ground was stony and hard, and he quickly saw he needed the pick to dig deep enough for Church. He started to switch back and forth, using the pick to loosen the hard earth and stones, then the shovel to dig out what he'd loosened. His hands started to hurt. His body warmed up. He felt a strong need to do a good job. He started to hum, something he did when stitching a wound. Sometimes the pick hit a rock hard enough to make sparks, and the shiver went up the handle and vibrated in his hands. He felt blisters forming on his palms and didn’t care, even though he was usually careful with his hands, like most doctors. Above and around him, the wind sang and sang, a tree-note melody.

Against this, he heard the soft drop and chunk of rock. He looked over his shoulder and saw Jud, crouched down and pulling out the bigger rocks he had dug up, making a pile.

“For your cairn,” he said when he saw Louis looking.

“Oh,” Louis said and went back to work.

He made the grave about two feet wide and three feet long—a Cadillac of a grave for a cat, he thought—and when it was about thirty inches deep and the pick was making sparks almost every time, he tossed it and the shovel aside and asked Jud if it was okay.

Jud got up and took a quick look. “Seems fine to me,” he said. “Anyway, what you think is what matters.”

“Will you tell me now what this is about?”

Jud smiled a little. “The Micmacs thought this hill was a magic place,” he said. “Thought this whole forest, from the swamp on north and east, was magic. They made this place special, and they buried their dead here, away from everything else. Other tribes stayed away from it—the Penobscots said these woods were full of ghosts. Later on, the fur trappers started saying much the same thing. I guess some of them saw the strange lights in Little God Swamp and thought they were seeing ghosts.”

Jud smiled, and Louis thought: That isn’t what you think at all.

“Later on, not even the Micmacs would come here. One of them said he saw a Wendigo here and that the ground had gone bad. They had a big meeting about it… or so I heard the story when I was young, Louis, but I heard it from that old drunk Stanny B.—that’s what we all called Stanley Bouchard—and Stanny B. would make things up if he didn’t know them.”

Louis, who only knew that the Wendigo was supposed to be a spirit of the north, said, “Do you think the ground’s gone bad?”

Jud smiled—or at least his lips moved. “I think it’s a dangerous place,” he said softly, “but not for cats or dogs or pet hamsters. Go on and bury your animal, Louis.”

Louis lowered the Hefty Bag into the hole and slowly shoveled the dirt back in. He was cold now and tired. The sound of the earth on the plastic was depressing, and while he didn’t regret coming up here, that excitement was fading, and he had begun to wish it was over. It was a long walk back home.

The sound softened, then stopped—there was only the thud of dirt on more dirt. He scraped the last bit into the hole with the shovel (there’s never enough, he thought, remembering something his uncle, who was a funeral director, had said to him a long time ago, never enough to fill the hole up again) and then turned to Jud.

“Your cairn,” Jud said.

“Look, Jud, I’m pretty tired and—”

“It’s Ellie’s cat,” Jud said, and his voice, although soft, was firm. “She’d want you to do it right.”

Louis sighed. “I suppose she would,” he said.

It took another ten minutes to pile up the rocks Jud handed him, one by one. When it was done, there was a low, cone-shaped pile of stones on Church’s grave, and Louis did feel a small, tired pleasure. It looked right, somehow, standing with the others in the starlight. He supposed Ellie would never see it—the thought of taking her through that swamp with quicksand would make Rachel’s hair turn white—but he had seen it, and it was good.

“Most of these have fallen over,” he said to Jud, standing and brushing at the knees of his pants. He was seeing more clearly now, and he could see scattered loose stones in several places. But Jud had made sure he built his cairn only from stones taken from the grave he himself had dug.

“Ayuh,” Jud said. “Told you: the place is old.”

“Are we done now?”

“Ayuh.” He clapped Louis on the shoulder. “You did good, Louis. I knew you would. Let’s go home.”

“Jud—” he began again, but Jud only grabbed the pick and walked off toward the steps. Louis got the shovel, had to trot to catch up, and then saved his breath for walking. He looked back once, but the cairn marking the grave of his daughter’s cat Winston Churchill had disappeared into the shadows, and he could not see it.

We just ran the film backward, Louis thought tiredly as they emerged from the woods and into the field overlooking his own house some time later. He did not know how much later; he had taken off his watch when he had lain down to doze that afternoon, and it would still be there on the windowsill by his bed. He only knew that he was beat, used up, done in. He could not remember feeling so worn out since his first day on Chicago’s rubbish-disposal crew one high-school summer sixteen or seventeen years ago.

They came back the same way they had gone, but he could remember very little about the trip. He stumbled on the deadfall, he remembered that—lurching forward and thinking absurdly of Peter Pan—oh Jesus, I lost my happy thoughts and down I come—and then Jud’s hand had been there, firm and hard, and a few moments later they had been trudging past the final resting places of Smucky the Cat and Trixie and Marta Our Pet Rabit and onto the path he had once walked not only with Jud but with his whole family.

It seemed that in some tired way he had thought about the dream of Victor Pascow, the one which had resulted in his sleepwalking, but he couldn't connect that night walk to this one. It had also occurred to him that the whole adventure had been dangerous—not in any dramatic way but in a very real one. That he had badly blistered his hands while almost sleepwalking was the least of it. He could have killed himself on the deadfall. Both of them could have. It was hard to see how that fit with being sober. In his current exhaustion, he was willing to blame it on confusion and emotional upset over the death of a pet the whole family had loved.

And after a while, there they were, home again.

They walked toward it together, not speaking, and stopped again in Louis’s driveway. The wind moaned and whined. Silently, Louis handed Jud his pick.

“I should get going,” Jud said at last. “Louella Bisson or Ruthie Parks will be bringing Norma home and she’ll wonder where I am.”

“Do you have the time?” Louis asked. He was surprised that Norma wasn’t home yet; he felt like it must be midnight.

“Oh, yes,” Jud said. “I keep track of the time as long as I’m dressed and then I don’t worry about it.”

He pulled a watch out of his pants pocket and flipped open the decorated cover.

“It’s eight-thirty,” he said and snapped the cover shut again.

“Eight-thirty?” Louis repeated dumbly. “That’s it?”

“How late did you think it was?” Jud asked.

“Later than that,” Louis said.

“I’ll see you tomorrow, Louis,” Jud said and started to walk away.

He turned to Louis, looking a bit puzzled.

“Jud, what did we do tonight?”

“Why, we buried your daughter’s cat.”

“Is that all we did?”

“Just that,” Jud said. “You’re a good man, Louis, but you ask too many questions. Sometimes people have to do things that just feel right. That feel right in their hearts, I mean. And if they do those things and then don’t feel right afterward, full of questions and kind of like they have a bad feeling, but inside their heads instead of their stomachs, they think they messed up. Do you know what I mean?”

“Yes,” Louis said, thinking that Jud must have been reading his mind as they walked downhill through the field toward the house lights.

“What they don’t realize is that maybe they should be questioning those feelings of doubt before they question their own hearts,” Jud said, watching him closely. “What do you think, Louis?”

“I think,” Louis said slowly, “that you might be right.”

“And the things that are in a man’s heart—it doesn’t help him much to talk about those things, does it?”

“Well—”

“No,” Jud said, as if Louis had already agreed. “It doesn’t.” And in his calm voice that was so sure and firm, in that voice which somehow chilled Louis, he said: “They are secret things. Women are supposed to be good at keeping secrets, and I guess they do keep some, but any woman who knows anything would tell you she’s never really seen into any man’s heart. A man’s heart is harder, Louis—like the ground up there in the old Micmac graveyard. Solid rock is close. A man grows what he can… and he takes care of it.”

“Jud—”

“Don’t ask questions, Louis. Accept what’s done and follow your heart.”

“But—”

“But nothing. Accept what’s done, Louis, and follow your heart. We did what was right this time… at least, I hope to God it was right. Another time it could be wrong—wrong as hell.”

“Will you at least answer one question?”

“Well, let’s hear it, and then we’ll see.”

“How did you know about that place?” This question had also come to Louis on the way back, along with the suspicion that Jud himself might be part Micmac—although he didn’t look like it; he looked like all his ancestors had been completely Anglo.

“Why, from Stanny B.,” he said, looking surprised.

“He just told you?”

“No,” Jud said. “It’s not the kind of place you just tell someone about. I buried my dog Spot up there when I was ten. He was chasing a rabbit and ran into some rusty barbed wire. The wounds got infected, and it killed him.”

Something about that was off, something that didn’t match something Louis had heard before, but he was too tired to figure out the difference. Jud said nothing more, just looked at him with his unreadable old man’s eyes.

“Goodnight, Jud,” Louis said.

“Goodnight.”

The old man crossed the road, carrying his pick and shovel.

“Thanks!” Louis called out without thinking.

Jud didn’t turn; he just raised one hand to show he heard.

And in the house, suddenly, the telephone began to ring.

Louis ran, wincing at the aches in his upper thighs and lower back, but by the time he got into the warm kitchen, the phone had already rung six or seven times. It stopped ringing right as he touched it. He picked it up anyway and said hello, but there was only the empty hum.

That was Rachel, he thought. I’ll call her back.

But suddenly it seemed like too much work to dial the number, to awkwardly chat with her mother—or worse, her father flashing his money—to be passed on to Rachel… and then to Ellie. Ellie would still be awake of course; it was an hour earlier in Chicago. Ellie would ask him how Church was doing.

Great, he’s fine. Got hit by an Orinco truck. Somehow I’m absolutely positive it was an Orinco truck. Anything else would lack a sense of drama, if you know what I mean. You don’t? Well, never mind. The truck killed him but didn’t hurt him much. Jud and I buried him up in the old Micmac burying ground—sort of an extra part of the Pet Sematary, if you know what I mean. Amazing walk, kiddo. I’ll take you up there sometime and we’ll put flowers by his marker—excuse me, his pile of stones. After the quicksand’s frozen over, that is, and the bears go to sleep for the winter.

He hung up the telephone, walked to the sink, and filled it with hot water. He removed his shirt and washed. He had been sweating a lot in spite of the cold, and he smelled bad.

There was some leftover meatloaf in the refrigerator. Louis cut it into slices, put them on a slice of bread, and added two thick slices of onion. He looked at this for a moment before covering it with ketchup and slamming down another slice of bread. If Rachel and Ellie had been around, they would have wrinkled their noses in the same way—yuck, gross.

Well, you missed it, ladies, Louis thought with real satisfaction and gobbled his sandwich. It tasted great. Confucius say he who smell like pig eat like wolf, he thought and smiled. He drank a lot of milk straight from the carton—another habit Rachel strongly disliked—and then he went upstairs, undressed, and got into bed without even brushing his teeth. His aches and pains had faded to one low throb that was almost comforting.

His watch was there where he had left it, and he looked at it. Ten minutes of nine. It really was amazing.

Louis turned off the light, turned over on his side, and slept.

He woke up sometime after three the next morning and shuffled to the bathroom, He was standing there urinating, blinking in the bright white bathroom light, when the problem suddenly appeared in his mind, and his eyes widened—it was as if two pieces of something which should have fitted together perfectly had instead bumped against one another and bounced back.

Tonight Jud had told him that his dog had died when he was ten—had died of infection after being cut up in rusty barbed wire. But on the late-summer day when all of them had walked up to the Pet Sematary together, Jud said that his dog had died of old age and was buried there—he had even pointed out the marker, although the years had worn away the writing.

Louis flushed the toilet, turned out the light, and went back to bed. Something else was wrong, too—and he quickly realized what it was. Jud had been born at the start of the century, and that day at the Pet Sematary he had told Louis his dog had died during the first year of World War I. That would have been when Jud was fourteen, if he meant when the war started in Europe. When he was seventeen, if he meant when America joined the war.

But tonight he had said that Spot died when he, Jud, was ten.

Well, he’s an old man, and old men get their memories mixed up, he thought uncomfortably. He’s said himself that he’s noticed he's becoming more forgetful—struggling to remember names and addresses that used to come to him easily, sometimes waking up and not remembering the chores he planned the night before. For a man his age, he’s doing pretty well… senility is probably too strong a word for Jud; forgetfulness is more accurate. It’s not surprising that a man would forget when a dog died seventy years ago. Or how it died, for that matter. Forget it, Louis.

But he couldn’t fall asleep again right away; for a long time he lay awake, too aware of the empty house and the wind that howled around the roof outside.

At some point he slept without realizing it; it must have been so, because as he drifted off, he thought he heard bare feet slowly climbing the stairs and he thought, Leave me alone, Pascow, leave me alone, what’s done is done and what’s dead is dead—and the steps faded away.

And although many other strange things happened as that year went on, Louis was never bothered by the ghost of Victor Pascow again, awake or asleep.
