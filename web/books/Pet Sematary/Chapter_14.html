Pet Sematary

Chapter 14

Things didn't slow down until almost four that afternoon, after Louis and Richard Irving, the head of Campus Security, spoke to the press. The young man, Victor Pascow, had been jogging with two friends, one of them his fiancée. A car driven by Tremont Withers, twenty-three, of Haven, Maine, had come up the road from the Lengyll Women’s Gymnasium toward the center of campus very fast. Withers’s car had hit Pascow and driven him head-first into a tree. Pascow had been brought to the infirmary in a blanket by his friends and two people who were passing by. He had died minutes later. Withers was being held, facing charges of reckless driving, driving under the influence, and vehicular manslaughter.

The editor of the campus newspaper asked if he could say that Pascow had died of head injuries. Louis, thinking of that broken window through which the brain itself could be seen, said he would rather let the Penobscot County coroner announce the cause of death. The editor then asked if the four young people who had brought Pascow to the infirmary in the blanket might have accidentally caused his death.

“No,” Louis replied. “Not at all. Sadly, Mr. Pascow was, in my opinion, fatally wounded when he was hit.”

There were other questions—a few—but that answer really ended the press conference. Now Louis sat in his office (Steve Masterton had gone home an hour before, right after the press conference—to see himself on the evening news, Louis suspected) trying to pick up the pieces of the day—or maybe he was just trying to hide what had happened, to put a thin layer of normalcy over it. He and Charlton were going over the cards in the “front file”—those students who were struggling through their college years despite some disability. There were twenty-three diabetics in the front file, fifteen epileptics, fourteen paraplegics, and others: students with leukemia, students with cerebral palsy and muscular dystrophy, blind students, two mute students, and one case of sickle-cell anemia, which Louis had never even seen.

Perhaps the worst moment of the afternoon had come just after Steve left. Charlton came in and put a pink memo slip on Louis’s desk. Bangor Carpet will be here at 9:00 tomorrow, it read.

“Carpet?” he had asked.

“It will have to be replaced,” she said apologetically. “There’s no way the stain’s going to come out, Doctor.”

Of course not. At that point Louis had gone into the dispensary and taken a Tuinal—what his first med school roommate had called Tooners. “Hop on the Toonerville Trolley, Louis,” he’d say, “and I’ll put on some Credence.” More often than not, Louis had declined the ride on the fabled Toonerville, and that was maybe just as well; his roomie had flunked out halfway through his third semester and had ridden the Toonerville Trolley all the way to Vietnam as a medical corpsman. Louis sometimes pictured him over there, stoned, listening to Credence do “Run Through the Jungle.”

But he needed something. If he was going to have to see that pink slip about the carpet on his note-minder board every time he glanced up from the front file spread out in front of them, he needed something.

He was doing okay when Mrs. Baillings, the night nurse, stuck her head in and said, “Your wife, Dr. Creed. Line one.”

Louis looked at his watch and saw it was almost five-thirty; he had planned to leave an hour and a half ago.

“Okay, Nancy. Thanks.”

He picked up the phone and hit line one. “Hi, honey. Just leaving—”

“Louis, are you okay?”

“Yeah, fine”

“I heard about it on the news. Lou, I’m so sorry.” She stopped for a moment. “It was on the radio news. They had you on, answering a question. You sounded fine.”

“Did I? Good.”

“Are you sure you’re okay?”

“Yes Rachel. I’m fine.”

“Come home,” she said.

“Yes,” he said. The idea of home appealed to him.
