Pet Sematary

Chapter 24

Steve Masterton called around nine-thirty and asked if Louis wanted to come to the university to play racket ball. He said happily that the place was empty and they could play all day if they wanted.

Louis understood the happiness. When the university was open, the waiting list for a racket ball court could be two days long. But he still said no, telling Steve he wanted to work on an article he was writing for The Magazine of College Medicine.

“You sure?” Steve asked. “Too much work makes you boring, you know.”

“Call me later,” Louis said. “Maybe I’ll want to then.”

Steve said he would and hung up. Louis had only told a half-lie this time. He did plan to work on his article, which was about treating contagious diseases like chicken-pox and mononucleosis in the infirmary, but the main reason he had said no to Steve was that he was in a lot of pain. He had noticed this as soon as he finished talking to Rachel and went to brush his teeth. His back muscles ached, his shoulders were sore from carrying the cat in that awful garbage bag, and the hamstrings behind his knees felt like guitar strings stretched too tight. Christ, he thought, and you thought you were in good shape. He would have looked silly trying to play racket ball with Steve, moving slowly like an old man with arthritis.

And speaking of old men, he hadn’t walked into the woods the night before alone; he had gone with a guy who was almost eighty-five. He wondered if Jud was as sore as he was this morning.

He spent an hour and a half working on his article, but it didn't go well. The emptiness and the silence began to bother him, and finally he stacked his yellow legal pads and the papers he had ordered from Johns Hopkins on the shelf above his typewriter, put on his parka, and crossed the road.

Jud and Norma weren’t there, but there was an envelope attached to the porch door with his name on it. He took it down and opened it with his thumb.

Louis,

The good wife and I are off to Bucksport to do some shopping and to look at a Welsh dresser at the Emporium Galorium that Norma’s had her eye on for what seems like a hundred years. We'll probably have lunch at McLeod’s while we’re there and come back in the late afternoon. Come over for a beer or two tonight, if you'd like.

Your family is your family. I don’t want to meddle, but if Ellie were my daughter, I wouldn’t rush to tell her that her cat got killed on the highway—why not let her enjoy her holiday?

By the way, Louis, I wouldn’t talk about what we did last night either, not around North Ludlow. There are other people who know about that old Micmac burial ground, and there are other people in town who have buried their animals there… you might say it’s another part of the “Pet Sematary.” Believe it or not, there is even a bull buried up there! Old Zack McGovern, who used to live out on Stackpole Road, buried his prize bull Hanratty in the Micmac burial ground back in 1967 or ’68. Ha, ha! He told me that he and his two boys had taken that bull out there and I laughed until I thought I would burst! But people around here don’t like to talk about it, and they don’t like people they consider to be “outsiders” to know about it, not because some of these old superstitions go back three hundred years or more (although they do), but because they sort of believe in those superstitions, and they think any “outsider” who knows that they do must be laughing at them. Does that make any sense? I suspect it doesn’t, but nevertheless that’s how it is. So just do me a favor and keep quiet about it, will you?

We will talk more about this, probably tonight, and by then you will understand more, but in the meantime I want to tell you that you did yourself proud. I knew you would.

Jud

PS—Norma doesn’t know what this note says—I told her something different—and I would just as soon keep it that way if it’s all the same to you. I’ve told Norma more than one lie in the fifty-eight years we’ve been married, and I'd guess that most men tell their wives some lies, but you know, most of them could stand before God and confess them without looking away from Him.

Well, come over tonight and we’ll have a few drinks.

J.

Louis stood on the top step leading to Jud and Norma’s porch—now bare, its comfortable rattan furniture stored to wait for another spring—frowning over this note. Don’t tell Ellie the cat had been killed—he hadn’t. Other animals buried there? Superstitions going back three hundred years?

… and by then you will understand more.

He touched this line lightly with his finger, and for the first time let his mind go back to what they had done the night before. It was blurred in his memory, like dreams or actions done under a light haze of drugs. He could recall climbing the deadfall and the strange, brighter light in the bog—that and how it had felt warmer there—but it was all like talking to the doctor before going under for surgery.

… and I’d guess most men tell their wives a lot of lies...

Wives and daughters too, Louis thought—but it was eerie, how Jud seemed to know what had happened this morning, both on the phone and in his own head.

Slowly he folded the note, written on lined paper like a schoolboy’s, and put it back in the envelope. He put the envelope in his hip pocket and crossed the road again.
