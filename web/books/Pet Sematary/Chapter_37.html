Pet Sematary

Chapter 37

“I knew something like this would happen,” Irwin Goldman said. That was how the trouble started. “I knew it when she married you. ‘You’ll have all the grief you can handle and more,’ I said. And look at this. Look at this… this mess.”

Louis slowly looked at his father-in-law, who had popped up like a nasty surprise in a skullcap; and then, without thinking, he looked around for Rachel, who should have been by the book on the stand—it was her afternoon shift—but Rachel was gone.

The afternoon viewing had fewer people, and after about half an hour, Louis had gone to the front row and sat on the aisle, barely noticing anything (just the sickening smell of the flowers) except that he was very tired and sleepy. It was partly the beer, he guessed. His mind was finally ready to shut down. Probably a good thing. Maybe after twelve or sixteen hours of sleep, he could comfort Rachel a little.

After a while, his head had dropped until he was looking at his hands, loosely clasped between his knees. The soft voices in the back were calming. He had been glad that Irwin and Dory weren’t there when the four of them got back from lunch, but he should have known it was too good to last.

“Where’s Rachel?” Louis asked now.

“With her mother. Where she should be.” Goldman spoke with the obvious victory of someone who has made a great deal. He smelled of Scotch. A lot of it. He stood in front of Louis like a small, aggressive lawyer facing a clearly guilty man in court. He was unsteady.

“What did you say to her?” Louis said, starting to feel alarmed. He knew Goldman had said something. It was obvious in the man’s face.

“Nothing but the truth. I told her this is what happens when you marry against your parents’ wishes. I told her—”

“Did you say that?” Louis asked in disbelief. “You didn’t really say that, did you?”

“That and more,” Irwin Goldman said, “I always knew it would come to this—this or something like it. I knew what kind of a man you were the first time I saw you.” He leaned forward, breathing out Scotch fumes. “I saw through you, you prancing little fake of a doctor. You tricked my daughter into a stupid, pointless marriage and then you turned her into a kitchen maid and then you let her son be run down in the highway like a… a chipmunk.”

Most of this confused Louis. He was still trying to understand how this stupid little man could have—

“You said that to her?” he repeated. “You said it?”

“I hope you rot in hell!” Goldman said, and heads turned quickly toward the sound of his voice. Tears started to come out of Irwin Goldman’s bloodshot brown eyes. His bald head shone under the dim fluorescent lights. “You made my wonderful daughter into a kitchen maid… ruined her future… took her away… and let my grandson die a dirty death on a country road.”

His voice rose to a loud, bossy scream.

“Where were you? Sitting around while he was playing in the road? Thinking about your stupid medical articles? What were you doing, you shit? You stinking shit! Killer of children! Ki—”

There they were. There they were at the front of the East Room. There they were, and Louis saw his arm go out. He saw the sleeve of his suit coat pull back from the cuff of his white shirt. He saw the soft shine of one cufflink. Rachel had given him the set for their third wedding anniversary, never knowing that her husband would someday wear these cufflinks to the funeral of their then-unborn son. His fist was just something tied to the end of his arm. It hit Goldman’s mouth. He felt the old man’s lips squash and spread back. It was a gross feeling, really—squashing a slug with your fist might feel something like that. There was no satisfaction in it. Beneath the flesh of his father-in-law’s lips he could feel the hard, firm shape of his dentures.

Goldman stumbled backward. His arm hit Gage’s coffin, knocking it sideways. One of the vases, heavy with flowers, fell over with a crash. Someone screamed.

It was Rachel, struggling with her mother, who was trying to hold her back. The people who were there—ten or fifteen in all—seemed stuck between fear and embarrassment. Steve had taken Jud back to Ludlow, and Louis was somewhat grateful for that. This was not a scene he would have wanted Jud to see. It was inappropriate.

“Don’t hurt him!” Rachel screamed. “Louis, don’t hurt my father!”

“You like to hit old men, do you?” Irwin Goldman, of the overflowing checkbook, yelled in a high voice. He was grinning, his mouth full of blood. “You like to hit old men? I’m not surprised, you stinking bastard. That doesn't surprise me at all.”

Louis turned toward him, and Goldman hit him in the neck. It was an awkward, sideways, chopping blow, but Louis wasn’t ready for it. A shocking pain that would make it hard to swallow for the next two hours exploded in his throat. His head snapped back, and he fell to one knee in the aisle.

First the flowers, now me, he thought. What do the Ramones say? Hey-ho, let’s go! He thought he wanted to laugh, but he couldn't. What came out of his sore throat was a small groan.

Rachel screamed again.

Irwin Goldman, his mouth dripping blood, marched over to where his son-in-law was kneeling and kicked Louis hard in the kidneys. The pain was a bright burst of agony. He put his hands down on the rug to keep from falling flat on his face.

“You don’t do so good even against old men, sonny!” Goldman yelled, his voice cracking with excitement. He kicked at Louis again, missing the kidney this time, hitting Louis on the upper part of his left buttock with his old, black shoe. Louis grunted in pain, and this time he fell onto the carpet. His chin hit with a loud crack. He bit his tongue.

“There!” Goldman yelled. “There’s the kick in the ass I should have given you the first time you came sniffing around, you bastard. There!” He kicked Louis in the butt again, this time hitting the other buttock. He was weeping and grinning. Louis saw for the first time that Goldman hadn’t shaved—a sign of mourning. The funeral director rushed toward them. Rachel had broken free from Mrs. Goldman and was also running toward them, screaming.

Louis rolled clumsily onto his side and sat up. His father-in-law kicked at him again, and Louis caught his shoe in both hands—it hit solidly into his palms like a well-caught football—and pushed backward as hard as he could.

Yelling, Goldman flew backward at an angle, waving his arms to keep his balance. He fell on Gage’s Eternal Rest casket, which had been made in the town of Storyville, Ohio, and which had been expensive.

Oz the Great and Terrible has just fallen on top of my son’s coffin, Louis thought, dazed. The casket fell from the stand with a huge crash. The left end fell first, then the right. The latch broke. Even over the screams and the crying, even over Goldman’s yells, who was just playing a children’s game of Pin the Tail on the Donkey, Louis heard the lock snap.

The coffin didn’t actually burst open, spilling Gage’s sad, hurt body onto the floor for everyone to stare at, but Louis felt sick knowing they were only spared that because the coffin had landed on its bottom instead of its side. It easily could have fallen the other way. Still, in that brief moment before the lid slammed shut on its broken latch, he saw a flash of gray—the suit they had bought to bury Gage in. And a bit of pink. Gage’s hand, maybe.

Sitting there on the floor, Louis covered his face and began to cry. He didn’t care about his father-in-law, or missiles, or stitches that last versus ones that dissolve, or the universe ending. Right then, Louis Creed wished he were dead. And suddenly, strangely, a picture popped into his head: Gage in Mickey Mouse ears, Gage laughing and shaking hands with a big Goofy on Main Street in Disney World. He saw it clearly.

One of the wooden supports had fallen over; the other leaned casually against the low platform where a minister might stand to give a speech. Goldman was lying in the flowers, also crying. Water dripped from the knocked-over vases. The flowers, some crushed, smelled even stronger.

Rachel was screaming and screaming.

Louis couldn’t react to her screams. The picture of Gage in Mickey Mouse ears was fading, but not before he heard a voice saying there would be fireworks later that night. He sat with his face in his hands, not wanting them to see him anymore, his tear-stained face, his loss, his guilt, his pain, his shame, most of all his cowardly wish to be dead and out of this darkness.

The funeral director and Dory Goldman led Rachel out. She was still screaming. Later, in another room (one Louis guessed was just for people who were very upset—the Crying Room, maybe) she went silent. Louis, still numb but calm and in control, gave her a sedative this time, after making sure they were alone.

At home, he led her to bed and gave her another shot. Then he pulled the covers up and looked at her pale face.

“Rachel, I’m sorry,” he said. “I’d give anything to take that back.”

“It’s all right,” she said in a strange, flat voice and then turned over on her side, away from him.

He felt the tired old question Are you all right? come to his lips and stopped it. It wasn’t a real question; it wasn’t what he really wanted to know.

“How bad are you?” he asked finally.

“Pretty bad, Louis,” she said, then made a sound that might have been a laugh. “I’m terrible, actually.”

He felt he should say more, but he couldn't. He suddenly resented her, Steve Masterton, Missy Dandridge and her husband with his pointy Adam's apple, the whole damn group. Why did he always have to be the one giving? What kind of crap was that?

He turned off the light and left. He realized he couldn't give much more to his daughter.

For a crazy moment, looking at her in the dim room, he thought she was Gage. He thought the whole thing had been a horrible nightmare, like his dream of Pascow leading him into the woods, and for a moment his tired mind clung to the idea. The shadows helped—there was only the moving light of the small TV Jud had brought up for her to fill the time. The long, long hours.

But it wasn't Gage, of course; it was Ellie, who was now not only holding the picture of her pulling Gage on the sled, but sitting in Gage's chair. She had taken it from his room and brought it into hers. It was a small director's chair with a canvas seat and back. The name GAGE was painted on the back. Rachel had ordered four of these chairs by mail. Each family member had one with their name on it.

Ellie was too big for Gage's chair. She was squeezed into it, and the canvas bottom sagged. She held the Polaroid to her chest and stared at the TV, where a movie was playing.

“Ellie,” he said, turning off the TV, “bedtime.”

She got out of the chair and folded it up. She seemed to want to take the chair to bed with her.

Louis hesitated, wanting to say something about the chair, and finally said, “Do you want me to tuck you in?”

“Yes, please,” she said.

“Do you… would you want to sleep with Mommy tonight?”

“No, thanks.”

“You sure?”

She smiled a little. “Yes. She steals the covers.”

Louis smiled back. “Come on then.”

Instead of trying to put the chair in bed with her, Ellie unfolded it by the head of the bed, and a funny image came to Louis—it looked like the world’s smallest psychiatrist's office.

She undressed, putting the picture of her and Gage on her pillow while she did. She put on her baby doll pajamas, picked up the picture, went into the bathroom, put it down to wash, brush, floss, and take her fluoride tablet. Then she picked it up again and got into bed with it.

Louis sat down beside her and said, “I want you to know, Ellie, that if we keep loving each other, we can get through this.”

Each word was like moving a handcar full of wet bales, and the effort made Louis feel exhausted.

“I’m going to wish really hard,” Ellie said calmly, “and pray to God for Gage to come back.”

“Ellie—”

“God can give him back if He wants to,” Ellie said. “He can do anything He wants to.”

“Ellie, God doesn’t do things like that,” Louis said uncomfortably, and he pictured Church sitting on the closed toilet seat, staring at him with those dirty eyes as Louis lay in the tub.

“He does so,” she said. “In Sunday School, the teacher told us about this man Lazarus. He was dead, and Jesus brought him back to life. He said ‘Lazarus, come forth,’ and the teacher said if he’d just said ‘Come forth,’ everyone in that graveyard would have come out, and Jesus only wanted Lazarus.”

Something ridiculous came out of his mouth (but the day had been full of ridiculous things): “That was a long time ago, Ellie.”

“I’m going to keep things ready for him,” she said. “I’ve got his picture, and I’m going to sit in his chair—”

“Ellie, you’re too big for Gage’s chair,” Louis said, taking her hot, sick hand. “You’ll break it.”

“God will help it not to break,” Ellie said. Her voice was calm, but Louis saw the dark circles under her eyes. Looking at her made his heart hurt so much that he turned away. Maybe when Gage’s chair broke, she would start to understand what had happened a little better.

“I’m going to carry the picture and sit in his chair,” she said. “I’m going to eat his breakfast too.” Gage and Ellie each had their own breakfast cereals; Ellie had once said Gage’s tasted like dead bugs. If Cocoa Bears was the only cereal in the house, Ellie would sometimes eat a boiled egg… or nothing at all. “I’ll eat lima beans even though I hate them, and I’ll read all of Gage’s picture books and I’ll… I’ll… you know… get things ready… in case…”

She was crying now. Louis didn't try to comfort her but just brushed her hair back from her forehead. What she said had a strange kind of logic. Keeping the lines open. Keeping things fresh. Keeping Gage alive in their minds, refusing to let him fade away; remembering when Gage did this… or that… yeah, that was great… good old Gage, what a kid. When it stopped hurting, it stopped mattering. She understood, Louis thought, how easy it would be to let Gage be dead.

"Ellie, don't cry anymore," he said. "This isn't forever."

She cried forever… for fifteen minutes. She actually fell asleep before her tears stopped. But eventually she slept, and downstairs the clock struck ten in the quiet house.

Keep him alive, Ellie, if that's what you want, he thought and kissed her. The therapists would probably say it's really unhealthy, but I'm for it. Because I know the day will come—maybe as soon as this Friday—when you forget to bring the picture and I'll see it lying on your bed in this empty room while you ride your bike around the driveway or walk in the field behind the house or go over to Kathy McGown's house to make clothes with her Sew Perfect. Gage won't be with you, and that's when Gage disappears from whatever place he holds in little girls' hearts and starts to become Something That Happened in 1984. A memory from the past.

Louis left the room and stood for a moment at the head of the stairs, thinking—not seriously—about going to bed.

He knew what he needed and went downstairs to get it.

Louis Albert Creed started carefully to get drunk. Downstairs in the cellar were five cases of Schlitz Light beer. Louis drank beer, Jud drank it, Steve Masterton drank it, Missy Dandridge would sometimes have a beer or two while watching the kids (kid, Louis reminded himself, going down the cellar stairs). Even Charlton, on the few times she had come over to the house, preferred a beer—as long as it was a light beer—to a glass of wine. So one day last winter Rachel had gone out and bought a huge ten cases when Schlitz Light was on sale at the Brewer A & P. Stop you running down to Julio's in Orrington every time somebody drops in, she had said. And you're always quoting Robert Parker to me, love—any beer that's in the refrigerator after the stores close is good beer, right? So drink this and think about the money you're saving. Last winter. When things had been okay. When things had been okay. It was funny how quickly and easily your mind made that important division.

Louis brought up a case of beer and shoved the cans into the fridge. Then he took one can, closed the fridge door, and opened the beer. Church came moving slowly and stiffly out of the pantry at the sound of the refrigerator door and stared questioningly up at Louis. The cat didn't come too close; Louis had maybe kicked it too many times.

"Nothing for you," he told the cat. "You had your can of Calo today. If you want something else, go kill a bird."

Church stood there, looking up at him. Louis drank half the can of beer and felt it go to his head almost at once.

“You don’t even eat them, do you?” he asked. “Just killing them is enough for you.”

Church walked into the living room, seeming to decide there would be no food, and Louis followed after a moment.

He thought again randomly, Hey-ho, let’s go.

Louis sat in his chair and looked at Church again. The cat was lying on the rug by the TV, watching Louis closely, probably ready to run if Louis got angry and decided to kick it.

Instead, Louis raised his beer. “To Gage,” he said. “To my son, who might have been an artist or an Olympic swimmer or the motherfucking President of the United States. What do you say, asshole?”

Church looked at him with those dull, strange eyes.

Louis drank the rest of his beer in big gulps that hurt his sore throat, got up, went to the fridge, and got another one.

After three beers, Louis felt somewhat balanced for the first time that day. After the first six-pack, he felt he might be able to sleep in an hour or so. He came back from the fridge with his eighth or ninth (he had lost count and was unsteady), and saw Church; the cat was dozing—or pretending to—on the rug. The thought came so easily it must have been there all along, waiting to come forward:

When are you going to do it? When are you going to bury Gage in the extra part of the Pet Sematary?

And right after that:

Lazarus, come forth.

Ellie’s sleepy, dazed voice:

The teacher said if he’d just said “Come forth,” probably everybody in that graveyard would have come out.

A chill struck him with such force that Louis grabbed himself as the shudder went through him. He suddenly remembered Ellie’s first day of school, how Gage had fallen asleep on his lap while he and Rachel listened to Ellie chatter about “Old MacDonald” and Mrs. Berryman; he had said Just let me put the baby to bed, and when he took Gage upstairs a horrible feeling had struck him, and now he understood: Back in September part of him had known Gage was going to die soon. Part of him had known that Oz the Great and Terrible was coming. It was nonsense, it was garbage, it was pure superstition… and it was true. He had known. Louis spilled some beer on his shirt, and Church looked up wearily to see if the evening’s cat-kicking was about to start.

Louis suddenly remembered the question he had asked Jud; he remembered how Jud’s arm had jerked, knocking two empty beer bottles off the table. One of them had shattered. You don’t even want to talk about such things, Louis!

But he did want to talk about them—or at least think about them. The Pet Sematary. What was beyond the Pet Sematary. The idea was deadly attractive. It made a kind of sense he couldn't deny. Church had been killed in the road; Gage had been killed in the road. Here was Church—changed of course, unpleasant in some ways—but here. Ellie, Gage, and Rachel all dealt with him. He killed birds, true, and had turned a few mice inside out, but killing small animals was a cat thing to do. Church had not turned into Frankencat. He was, in many ways, as good as ever.

You’re making excuses, a voice whispered. He’s not as good as ever. He’s spooky. The crow, Louis… remember the crow?

“Good God,” Louis said aloud in a shaky, distracted voice he barely recognized as his own.

God, oh yes, fine, sure. If there had ever been a time to call on God outside of a ghost or vampire novel, this was it. So just what—what in the name of God—was he thinking about? He was thinking about a dark, evil idea he still couldn’t believe. Worse, he was lying to himself. Not just making excuses, but outright lying.

So what’s the truth? You want the truth so bad, what’s the truth?

That Church wasn’t really a cat anymore—that’s the first thing. He looked and acted like a cat, but he was just a poor copy. People couldn’t see through it, but they could feel it. He remembered when Charlton was at the house for a small pre-Christmas dinner party. They were sitting here, talking after eating, and Church jumped onto her lap. Charlton pushed him off right away, her mouth twisting with quick distaste.

It wasn’t a big deal. No one even said anything. But… it happened. Charlton felt what the cat wasn’t. Louis finished his beer and got another. If Gage came back changed like that, it would be horrible.

He opened the can and drank a lot. He was drunk now, really drunk, and he’d have a bad headache tomorrow. How I Went to My Son’s Funeral with a Hangover by Louis Creed, author of How I Just Missed Him at the Crucial Moment and many other works.

Drunk. Of course. And he thought he’d gotten drunk so he could think about this crazy idea clearly.

Even with everything, the idea was tempting, with a sick glow and appeal. Yes, mostly it had appeal.

Jud was back, speaking in his mind:

You do it because it grabs you. You do it because that burial place is a secret, and you want to share it… you make up reasons… they seem good… but you mostly do it because you want to. Or because you have to.

Jud’s voice, low and slow with his Yankee accent, Jud’s voice making him cold, giving him goosebumps, making the hair on his neck stand up.

These are secret things, Louis… a man’s heart is like stony ground… like the soil in the old Micmac burying ground. A man grows what he can… and takes care of it.

Louis started thinking about the other things Jud had told him about the Micmac burying ground. He started gathering the information, sorting through it, and putting it together—just like he used to get ready for big tests.

The dog. Spot.

Louis dumped the rest of the beer down the sink, feeling suddenly that he was going to throw up. The room was moving around in great swinging motions.

Suddenly, Louis remembered the story of the monkey’s paw, and a cold fear filled him. He felt it strongly—like a dead hand from a fridge, a dead hand that had come alive and slipped under his shirt to grab his heart. It was a dumb image, excessive and dumb, but it didn’t feel dumb. No.
