Pet Sematary

Chapter 40

But none of that happened.

All of it—the loud Orinco truck, the fingers that barely touched Gage’s jacket and then slipped off, Rachel getting ready for the viewing in her housecoat, Ellie holding Gage’s picture and putting his chair next to her bed, Steve Masterton’s tears, the fight with Irwin Goldman, Jud Crandall’s awful story of Timmy Baterman—all of it existed only in Louis Creed’s mind during the few seconds he chased his laughing son toward the road. Behind him, Rachel screamed again—Gage, come back, don’t RUN!—but Louis didn’t waste his breath. It was going to be close, very close, and yes, one of those things did happen: from up the road he heard the truck’s engine and a memory sparked, and he heard Jud Crandall talking to Rachel on their first day in Ludlow: You want to watch them around the road, Missus Greed. It’s a bad road for kids and pets.

Now Gage was running down the gentle slope of lawn that met the edge of Route 15, his short, strong legs pumping, and he should have fallen, but he kept going, and now the truck was very loud, that low, rumbling sound Louis sometimes heard from his bed as he drifted to sleep. Then it seemed comforting, but now it terrified him.

Oh my dear God oh my dear Jesus let me catch him don’t let him get into the road!

Louis sped up and leaped, throwing himself forward like a football player making a tackle; he saw his shadow moving along the grass below him, and he thought of the kite, the Vulture, its shadow stretching across Mrs. Vinton’s field, and as Gage ran into the road, Louis’s fingers brushed the back of his jacket… and then caught it.

He yanked Gage backward and landed on the ground, his face hitting the rough gravel, giving himself a bloody nose. A sharp pain shot through his groin—Ohhh, if I’d known I was playing football, I would have worn my cup—but the pain in his nose and groin faded as he heard Gage’s cry of pain and anger when his bottom landed on the shoulder, and he fell backward onto the lawn, hitting his head. A moment later, his cries were drowned out by the truck and its loud air horn.

Louis managed to get up, despite the heavy feeling in his stomach, and held his son. Rachel ran to them, also crying, shouting to Gage, “Never run in the road, Gage! Never, never, never! The road is bad! Bad!” Gage was so surprised by this tearful lecture that he stopped crying and stared at his mother.

“Louis, your nose is bleeding,” she said, then hugged him so suddenly and tightly that he could barely breathe.

“That isn’t the worst of it,” he said. “I think I’m sterile, Rachel. Oh boy, the pain.”

And she laughed so hard that for a few moments he worried about her, and he thought: If Gage really had died, I think it would have broken her.

But Gage didn’t die; all of that was just a horribly detailed moment of imagination as Louis ran faster than his son’s death across a green lawn on a sunny May afternoon.

Gage went to elementary school, and at seven he started going to camp, where he showed a great and surprising talent for swimming. He also surprised his parents by handling a month apart with no obvious emotional issues. By the time he was ten, he was spending the whole summer at Camp Agawam in Raymond, and at eleven he won two blue ribbons and a red one at the Four Camps Swimathon that ended the summer. He grew tall, but he was still the same Gage, sweet and a little surprised by the things the world offered… and for Gage, things were never bitter or bad.

He was an honors student in high school and on the swimming team at John Bapst, the private school he wanted to go to because of its swimming pool. Rachel was upset, Louis not surprised when, at seventeen, Gage said he wanted to become Catholic. Rachel thought it was all because of the girl Gage was dating; she imagined them getting married soon (“if that little tramp with the St. Christopher’s medal isn’t sleeping with him, I’ll eat my hat, Louis,” she said), his college plans ruined, his Olympic dreams gone, and lots of little Catholics running around by the time Gage was forty. By then he would be (according to Rachel) a cigar-smoking truck driver with a beer belly, praying his way to an early death.

Louis thought his son’s reasons were better, and even though Gage converted (and on the day he did, Louis sent a mean postcard to Irwin Goldman; it said, Maybe you’ll have a Jesuit grandson yet. Your non-Jewish son-in-law, Louis), he didn’t marry the nice (and definitely not a tramp) girl he had dated through most of his senior year.

He went to Johns Hopkins, made the Olympic swimming team, and on one long, amazing, and incredibly proud afternoon sixteen years after Louis had raced a truck to save his son’s life, he and Rachel—who was now mostly gray, but colored it—watched their son win a gold medal for the U.S.A. When the NBC cameras zoomed in on him, standing with his wet head back, his eyes open and calm and looking at the flag as the national anthem played, the ribbon around his neck, and the gold against his skin, Louis cried. He and Rachel both cried.

“I guess this is the best it gets,” he said softly and turned to hug his wife. But she was looking at him with growing fear, her face seeming to age as if hurt by time; the national anthem faded and when Louis looked back at the TV he saw a different boy there, a black boy with curly hair that still had drops of water in it.

This is the best it gets.

His cap.

His cap is…

… oh dear God, his cap is full of blood.

Louis woke up in the cold, bleak light of a rainy seven o’clock, clutching his pillow. His head pounded fiercely with his heartbeat; the ache grew and faded, grew and faded. He burped acid that tasted like old beer, and his stomach churned miserably. He had been crying; the pillow was wet with his tears, as if he had wandered in and out of some corny country song in his sleep. Even in the dream, he thought, some part of him had known the truth and had cried for it.

He got up and staggered to the bathroom, heart racing weakly, his mind broken up by the strength of his hangover. He reached the toilet just in time and threw up a lot of last night’s beer.

He kneeled on the floor, eyes closed, until he felt able to stand up. He reached for the handle and flushed the toilet. He went to the mirror to see how bloodshot his eyes were, but the glass had been covered with a square of cloth. Then he remembered. Drawing on a past she claimed to hardly remember, Rachel had covered all the mirrors in the house, and she took off her shoes before coming inside.

No Olympic swimming team, Louis thought blankly as he walked back to his bed and sat down. The sour taste of beer coated his mouth and throat, and he swore to himself (not for the first time or the last) that he would never touch that poison again. No Olympic swimming team, no 3.0 GPA in college, no Catholic girlfriend or conversion, no summer camp, no nothing. His sneakers had been ripped off; his sweater turned inside out; his sweet little boy’s body, so strong and sturdy, nearly torn apart. His cap had been full of blood.

Now, sitting on his bed in the grip of this numbing hangover, rainwater trickling down the window beside him, his grief came for him completely, like some sad woman from a hospital ward. It came and broke him down, weakened him, took away whatever defenses were left, and he put his face in his hands and cried, rocking back and forth on his bed, thinking he would do anything to have a second chance, anything at all.
