Pet Sematary

Chapter 50

Rachel Creed almost caught her flight from Boston to Portland. Almost. Her Chicago plane left on time (a miracle), was cleared quickly into LaGuardia (another), and left New York only five minutes late. It arrived at the gate in Boston fifteen minutes late—at 11:12 P.M. That left her with thirteen minutes.

She still might have caught her connecting flight, but the shuttle bus that goes around the Logan terminals was late. Rachel waited, now in a constant state of worry, shifting from foot to foot as if she needed to use the restroom, switching the travel bag her mother had loaned her from one shoulder to the other.

When the shuttle still hadn’t come at 11:25, she began to run. Her heels were low but still high enough to cause her trouble. One of her ankles twisted painfully, and she stopped long enough to take off the shoes. Then she ran on in her pantyhose, past Allegheny and Eastern Airlines, breathing hard, starting to get a pain in her side.

Her breath was hot in her throat, that pain in her side sharper. Now she was running past the international terminal, and there, up ahead, was Delta’s triangular sign. She rushed in through the doors, almost dropped one shoe, caught it. It was 11:37.

One of the two clerks on duty looked up at her.

“Flight 104,” she gasped. “The Portland flight. Has it left?”

The clerk glanced behind him at the monitor. “Still at the gate, it says here,” he said, “but they announced final boarding five minutes ago. I’ll call ahead. Any bags to check?”

“No,” Rachel gasped, and brushed her sweaty hair out of her eyes. Her heart was racing in her chest.

“Then don’t wait for me to call. I will—but I suggest you run very fast.”

Rachel didn’t run very fast—she couldn’t anymore. But she tried her best. The escalator was off for the night, and she hurried up the stairs, tasting a metallic tang in her mouth. She reached security and almost threw the tote bag at the surprised guard, then waited for it to come through, her hands clenching and unclenching. She grabbed it as soon as it came out of the X-ray machine and ran again, the bag flying behind her and hitting her hip.

She looked up at one of the monitors as she ran.

FLIGHT 104 PORTLAND SCHED 11:25P GATE 31 BOARDING.

Gate 31 was at the far end of the concourse—and as she glanced at the monitor, BOARDING changed to DEPARTING, blinking fast.

A frustrated cry escaped her. She ran into the gate area just as the gate attendant removed the sign that read: FLIGHT 104 BOSTON—PORTLAND 11:25.

“It’s gone?” she asked, shocked. “It’s really gone?”

The attendant looked at her kindly. “It left the gate at 11:40. I’m sorry, ma’am. You tried hard, if that helps.” He pointed to the large windows. Rachel saw a big 727 with Delta markings, its lights shining brightly, beginning to take off.

“Christ, didn’t anyone tell you I was coming?” Rachel cried.

“When they called from downstairs, 104 was already moving. If I’d called it back, it would have been stuck with all the other planes going to Runway 30, and that pilot would have been furious. And there are a hundred or so passengers on board. I’m very sorry. If you’d been just four minutes sooner—”

She walked away, not hearing the rest. She was halfway back to security when she felt dizzy. She stumbled into another gate area and sat down until the feeling passed. Then she put her shoes back on, picking a flattened cigarette butt off her stocking first. My feet are dirty and I don’t care, she thought sadly.

She walked back toward the terminal.

The security guard looked at her kindly. “Missed it?”

“I missed it, all right,” Rachel said.

“Where were you going?”

“Portland. Then Bangor.”

“Well, why don’t you rent a car? If you really need to be there, that is? Usually, I’d say stay at a hotel near the airport, but if I ever saw someone who looked like they really needed to be there, it’s you.”

“I’m that lady, all right,” Rachel said. She thought about it. “Yes, I guess I could do that, couldn’t I? If any of the rental places has a car.”

The security guard laughed. “Oh, they’ll have cars. The only time they don’t have cars at Logan is when the airport’s fogged in. Which is often.”

Rachel barely heard her. She was already trying to figure it out in her head.

She couldn’t get to Portland in time to catch her Bangor flight even if she drove up the highway as fast as she could. So, think about driving straight through. How long? That depended on the distance. Two hundred and fifty miles, that's what she remembered. Something Jud had said maybe. It would be at least 12:15 before she left, probably closer to 12:30 A.M. It was all highway. She thought she had a good chance of driving the whole way at sixty-five without getting pulled over for speeding. She quickly did the math in her head, dividing sixty-five into two hundred and fifty. Almost four hours. Well… call it four even. She would have to stop once to use the bathroom. And even though sleep seemed impossible right now, she knew herself well enough to know she would also have to stop for a large black coffee. Still, she could be back in Ludlow before dawn.

Thinking about all this, she headed for the stairs—the car rental places were one floor below the main areas.

“Good luck, honey,” the security guard said. “Be safe.”

“Thanks,” Rachel said. She felt like she needed some good luck.
