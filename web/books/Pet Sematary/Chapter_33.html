Pet Sematary

Chapter 33

“For man—and woman—is like the flowers in the valley, blooming today and gone tomorrow: our time is short; it comes, and then it passes. Let us pray.”

Ellie, beautiful in a navy blue dress bought just for this, dropped her head so quickly that Louis, next to her in the pew, heard her neck pop. Ellie had been to few churches, and never a funeral; the combination had made her unusually quiet.

For Louis, it was a rare moment with his daughter. Usually blinded by his love for her, as he was for Gage, he rarely looked at her without bias; but today he felt like he was watching a perfect example of a child nearing the end of life’s first big stage; a being of pure curiosity, soaking up information like crazy. Ellie had been quiet even when Jud, looking odd but classy in his black suit and dress shoes (Louis thought it was the first time he'd seen him in anything but loafers or green boots), had leaned over, kissed her, and said: “Glad you could come, honey. I bet Norma is too.”

Ellie had stared at him, eyes wide.

Now the minister, Reverend Laughlin, was saying the benediction, asking God to watch over them and give them peace.

“Will the pallbearers come forward?” he asked.

Louis started to get up, and Ellie stopped him, pulling his arm hard. She looked scared. “Daddy!” she whispered loudly. “Where are you going?”

“I’m one of the pallbearers, honey,” Louis said, sitting back down for a moment and putting his arm around her. “That means I’m going to help carry Norma out. There are four of us—me, two of Jud’s nephews, and Norma’s brother.”

“Where will I find you?”

Louis glanced down. The other three pallbearers were there, along with Jud. The rest of the people were leaving, some of them crying.

“If you just go out on the steps, I’ll meet you there,” he said. “Okay, Ellie?”

“Yes,” she said. “Just don’t forget me.”

“No, I won’t.”

He got up again, and she tugged his hand again.

“Daddy?”

“What, honey?”

“Don’t drop her,” Ellie whispered.

Louis joined the others, and Jud told him who the nephews were. They were really second or third cousins… related to Jud's father's brother. They were big guys in their twenties who looked a lot alike. Norma’s brother was probably in his late fifties, Louis guessed. He looked sad because someone in his family had died, but he seemed to be doing okay.

“Nice to meet you all,” Louis said. He felt a little awkward—like he didn't belong.

They nodded at him.

“Ellie okay?” Jud asked and nodded toward her. She was hanging back in the entryway, watching.

Sure—she just wants to make sure I don’t disappear, Louis thought and almost smiled. But then that thought brought up another one: Oz the Great and Terrible. And the smile vanished.

“Yes, I think so,” Louis said and lifted a hand to her. She raised hers back and then went outside in a swirl of navy blue dress. For a moment Louis was uncomfortably struck by how grown-up she looked. It was the kind of illusion, no matter how brief, that could make a man stop and think.

“You guys ready?” one of the nephews asked.

Louis nodded; so did Norma’s younger brother.

“Take it easy with her,” Jud said. His voice had grown rough. Then he turned away and walked slowly up the aisle with his head down.

Louis moved to the back left corner of the steel-gray American Eternal coffin Jud had picked for his wife. He grabbed his handle, and the four of them slowly carried Norma’s coffin out into the bright, still cold of February first. Someone—the church caretaker, he guessed—had spread a good layer of cinders over the slick path of packed snow. At the curb, a Cadillac hearse sat idling, sending white exhaust into the winter air. The funeral director and his strong son stood beside it, watching them, ready to help if anyone (her brother, maybe) should slip or falter.

Jud stood beside him and watched as they slid the coffin inside.

“Goodbye, Norma,” he said and lit a cigarette. “I’ll see you in a while, old girl.”

Louis put his arm around Jud's shoulders, and Norma's brother stood close on his other side, pushing the mortician and his son back. The big nephews (or second cousins, or whatever they were) had already left, their simple job of lifting and carrying done. They had grown apart from this part of the family; they knew the woman's face from pictures and maybe a few visits—long afternoons in the living room eating Norma's cookies and drinking Jud's beer, maybe not really minding the old stories of times they hadn't lived through and people they hadn't known, but aware of things they could be doing (washing and waxing a car, bowling practice, maybe just watching a boxing match on TV with friends), and glad to leave when the duty was done.

Jud's part of the family was in the past now, as far as they were concerned; it was like a small planet drifting away from the main group, shrinking, little more than a speck. The past. Pictures in an album. Old stories told in rooms that perhaps seemed too hot to them—they were not old; their joints didn't hurt; their blood wasn't thin. The past was bodies to be lifted and carried and then let go. After all, if the human body was an envelope for the human soul—God's letters to the universe—as most churches taught, then the American Eternal coffin was an envelope for the human body, and to these strong young cousins or nephews or whatever they were, the past was just something dead to be filed away.

God save the past, Louis thought and shivered for no reason other than that the day would come when he would be a stranger to his own family—his own grandchildren if Ellie or Gage had kids and he lived to see them. Things changed. Family lines faded. Young faces looking out of old photographs.

God save the past, he thought again and squeezed the old man's shoulders.

The ushers put the flowers in the back of the hearse. The electric back window closed. Louis went back to his daughter, and they walked to the station wagon together, Louis holding Ellie's arm so she wouldn't slip in her good shoes with the leather soles. Car engines were starting.

"Why are they turning on their lights, Daddy?" Ellie asked, a little surprised. "Why are they turning on their lights in the middle of the day?"

"They do it," Louis began, his voice thick, "to honor the dead, Ellie." He turned on the wagon's headlights. "Come on."

They were going home at last, the ceremony over—actually it was held at the small Mount Hope Chapel; no grave would be dug for Norma until spring—when Ellie suddenly burst into tears.

Louis looked at her, surprised but not worried. "Ellie, what is it?"

"No more cookies," Ellie cried. "She made the best oatmeal cookies I ever ate. But she won't make them anymore because she's dead. Daddy, why do people have to be dead?"

“I don’t really know,” Louis said. “I guess it’s to make space for all the new people. Little people like you and your brother Gage.”

“I’m never going to get married or have sex and babies!” Ellie cried, even harder now. “Then maybe it’ll never happen to me! It’s awful! It’s m-m-mean!”

“But it stops the pain,” Louis said quietly. “And as a doctor, I see a lot of pain. One reason I wanted the job at the university was because I was tired of seeing it every day. Young people often have pain… even bad pain… but that’s not exactly the same as suffering.”

He paused.

“Believe it or not, honey, when people get very old, death doesn’t always seem as bad or scary as it does to you. And you have many, many years ahead of you.”

Ellie cried, then sniffed, and then stopped. Before they got home, she asked if she could play the radio. Louis said yes, and she found Shakin’ Stevens singing “This Ole House” on WACZ. Soon she was singing along. When they got home, she went to her mother and chattered about the funeral. Rachel, to her credit, listened quietly, kindly, and helpfully, though Louis thought she looked pale and thoughtful.

Then Ellie asked her if she knew how to make oatmeal cookies, and Rachel put away her knitting and stood up right away, as if she had been waiting for this or something like it. “Yes,” she said. “Want to make some?”

“Yay!” Ellie shouted. “Can we really, Mom?”

“We can if your father will watch Gage for an hour.”

“I’ll watch him,” Louis said. “I’d like that.”

Louis spent the evening reading and taking notes on a long article in The Duquesne Medical Digest; the old debate about dissolving stitches had started up again. In the small world of the few people on earth who cared about stitching small wounds, it seemed as endless as the old argument about nature versus nurture.

He planned to write a letter disagreeing tonight, proving that the writer’s main ideas were wrong, his examples biased, his research almost criminally careless. In short, Louis was looking forward—with great amusement—to completely destroying the idiot's argument. He was searching in the study bookcase for his copy of Troutman’s Treatment of Wounds when Rachel came halfway down the stairs.

“Coming up, Lou?”

“I’ll be a while.” He looked up at her. “Everything okay?”

“They’re sound asleep, both of them.”

Louis looked at her closely. “Them, yeah. You’re not.”

“I’m fine. Been reading.”

“You’re okay? Really?”

“Yes,” she said and smiled. “I love you, Louis.”

“Love you too, babe.” He looked at the bookcase, and there was Troutman, right where it always was. Louis put his hand on the textbook.

“Church brought a rat into the house while you and Ellie were gone,” she said and tried to smile. “Yuck, what a mess.”

“Jeez, Rachel, I’m sorry.” He hoped he didn't sound as guilty as he felt at that moment. “Was it really bad?”

Rachel sat down on the stairs. In her pink flannel nightgown, her face clean of makeup and her forehead shining, her hair tied back into a short ponytail with a rubber band, she looked like a child. “I took care of it,” she said, “but you know, I had to chase that dumb cat out the door with the vacuum cleaner thing before he would stop guarding the… the dead thing? He growled at me. Church never growled at me before. He seems different lately. Do you think he could be sick or something, Louis?”

“No,” Louis said slowly, “but I’ll take him to the vet, if you want.”

“I guess it’s okay,” she said and then looked at him openly. “But would you come up? I just… I know you’re working, but…”

“Of course,” he said, getting up as if it were nothing important at all. And, really, it wasn’t—except he knew that now the letter would never be written because things move on, and tomorrow would bring something new. But he had caused that rat, hadn’t he? The rat that Church had brought in, probably scratched to pieces, its insides hanging out, its head maybe gone. Yes. He had caused it. It was his rat.

“Let’s go to bed,” he said, turning off the lights. He and Rachel went up the stairs together. Louis put his arm around her waist and loved her the best he could… but even as he was inside her, hard and ready, he was listening to the winter wind outside the frosty windows, wondering about Church, the cat that used to belong to his daughter and now belonged to him, wondering where it was and what it was hunting or killing. A man’s heart is hard, he thought, and the wind sang its sad, dark song, and not so far away, Norma Crandall, who had once knitted his daughter and son matching hats, lay in her gray metal coffin on a stone slab in a Mount Hope tomb; by now the white cotton the funeral home would have used to fill out her cheeks would be turning black.
