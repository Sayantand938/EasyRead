Pet Sematary

Chapter 58

Jud Crandall woke with a start, nearly tumbling from his chair. He didn't know how long he'd slept—maybe fifteen minutes, maybe three hours. His watch showed five minutes to five. He felt like everything in the room had been slightly moved, and his back hurt from sleeping upright.

Oh you stupid old man, look what you gone and done!

But he knew better; deep down, he knew. It wasn't just him. He hadn't just dozed off; he'd been put to sleep.

That scared him, but something else scared him more: what had woken him? He thought he'd heard a sound, some—

He held his breath, listening over the soft rustle of his heart.

There it was—a sound, but not the one that woke him. The faint creak of hinges.

Jud knew every sound in this house—which boards creaked, which steps squeaked, where the wind liked to howl and sing when it was wild, like last night. He knew this sound too. The heavy front door, the one between his porch and the hall, had just opened. And now he remembered the sound that had awakened him: the slow stretching of the spring on the screen door between the porch and the walk.

“Louis?” he called, without much hope. It wasn't Louis out there. Whatever it was, it had come to punish an old man for his pride.

Footsteps moved slowly up the hall toward the living room.

“Louis?” he tried to call again, but only a faint croak came out because now he could smell the thing that had entered his house at the end of the night. It was a dirty, low smell—like poisoned mud flats.

Jud could see big shapes in the dim light—Norma’s wardrobe, the Welsh dresser, the tall chest of drawers—but no details. He tried to stand on legs that felt like water, his mind screaming that he needed more time, that he was too old to face this again without more time; Timmy Baterman had been bad enough, and Jud had been young then.

The swing door opened and let in shadows. One of the shadows was bigger than the others.

Dear God, that stink.

Shuffling steps in the darkness.

“Gage?” Jud finally stood up. He saw the neat roll of cigarette ash in the Jim Beam ashtray out of the corner of his eye. “Gage, is that y—”

A horrible crying sound came, and for a moment Jud’s bones turned to ice. It was not Louis’s son returned from the grave but some horrible monster.

No. It was neither.

It was Church, crouched in the hall doorway, making that sound. The cat’s eyes glowed like dirty lamps. Then his eyes moved and stared at the thing that had come in with the cat.

Jud began to back away, trying to grab his thoughts, trying to stay sane in the face of that smell. Oh, it was cold in here—the thing had brought its coldness with it.

Jud wobbled on his feet—the cat was winding around his legs, making him unsteady. It was purring. Jud kicked at it, chasing it away. It showed its teeth and hissed.

Think! Oh, think, you stupid old man, it might not be too late, even now it might not be too late… it’s back but it can be killed again… if you can just do it… if you can just think…

He backed away toward the kitchen, and he suddenly remembered the drawer beside the sink. There was a meat cleaver in that drawer.

His thin legs bumped the swinging door to the kitchen and he pushed it open. The thing that had come into his house was still unclear, but Jud could hear it breathing. He could see one white hand swinging back and forth—there was something in that hand, but he couldn’t make it out. The door swung back as he entered the kitchen, and Jud finally turned and ran to the drawer. He yanked it open and found the cleaver’s worn wooden handle. He grabbed it and turned toward the door again; he even took a step or two toward it. Some of his courage had returned.

Remember, it isn’t a kid. It might scream or something when it sees you’ve figured it out; it might cry. But you won’t be fooled. You’ve been fooled too many times already, old man. This is your last chance.

The swing door opened again, but at first only the cat came through. Jud’s eye followed it for a moment and then he looked up again.

The kitchen faced east, and dawn’s first light came in through the windows, faint and milky white. Not much light but enough. Too much.

Gage Creed came in, wearing his burial suit. Moss was growing on the suit’s shoulders and lapels. Moss had dirtied his white shirt. His fine blond hair was covered in dirt. One eye was turned to the side; it stared off with terrible focus. The other was fixed on Jud.

Gage was grinning at him.

“Hello, Jud,” Gage said in a childish but perfectly clear voice. “I’ve come to send your rotten, stinking old soul straight to hell. You messed with me once. Did you think I wouldn’t come back sooner or later and mess with you?”

Jud raised the cleaver. “Come on and show yourself, whatever you are. We’ll see who messes with who.”

“Norma’s dead, and no one will miss you,” Gage said. “She was such a cheap slut. She slept with all your friends, Jud. She let them do it every way. That’s what she liked best. She’s burning in hell, arthritis and all. I saw her there, Jud. I saw her there.”

It took two steps toward him, shoes leaving muddy marks on the worn floor. It held one hand out as if to shake; the other was hidden behind its back.

“Listen, Jud,” it whispered—and then its mouth opened, showing small teeth, and though the lips didn’t move, Norma’s voice came out.

“I laughed at you! We all laughed at you! How we laaaaaauuughed—”

“Stop it!” The cleaver shook in his hand.

“We did it in our bed, Herk and I did it, I did it with George, I did it with all of them, I knew about your whores but you never knew you married a whore and how we laughed, Jud! We screwed together and we laaaaaaaaaughed at—”

“STOP IT!” Jud screamed. He jumped at the small, swaying figure in its dirty burial suit, and that was when the cat shot out of the darkness under the butcher block where it had been hiding. It was hissing, its ears flat, and it tripped Jud as easily as could be. The cleaver flew out of his hand. It skittered across the bumpy and faded floor, blade and handle quickly switching places as it spun. It hit the wall with a light clang and slid under the refrigerator.

Jud knew he’d been tricked again, and the only comfort was that it was the last time. The cat was on his legs, mouth open, eyes blazing, hissing like a kettle. And then Gage was on him, grinning a happy, dark grin, eyes round, rimmed with red, and his right hand came out from behind his back, and Jud saw that what he was holding was a scalpel from Louis’s black bag.

“Oh my dear Jesus,” Jud said, and put his right hand up to block the blow. And then he saw something strange; he must be losing his mind because it looked like the scalpel was on both sides of his palm at the same time. Then something warm started to drip down on his face, and he understood.

“I’m gonna mess with you, old man!” the Gage-thing chuckled, blowing its poisoned breath in his face. “I’m gonna mess with you! I’m gonna mess with you all… I… want!”

Jud struggled and grabbed Gage’s wrist. Skin peeled off like paper in his hand.

The scalpel was yanked out of his hand, leaving a vertical cut.

“All…I…WANT!”

The scalpel came down again.

And again.

And again.
