Pet Sematary

Chapter 31

Their winter passed. Ellie believed in Santa Claus again—at least for a while—because of the footprints by the fireplace. Gage loved opening his presents, stopping now and then to chew on a piece of wrapping paper that looked especially good. And that year, both kids decided by the afternoon that the boxes were more fun than the toys.

The Crandalls came over on New Year’s Eve for Rachel’s eggnog, and Louis found himself looking closely at Norma. She looked pale and somehow see-through, a look he had seen before. His grandmother would have said Norma was starting to “fail,” and that was maybe a good way to put it. Suddenly her hands, so swollen and twisted by arthritis, seemed covered with age spots. Her hair looked thinner. The Crandalls went home around ten, and the Creeds celebrated the New Year together in front of the TV. It was the last time Norma was in their house.

Most of Louis’s winter break was wet and gloomy. He was glad for the warmer weather because it saved on heating costs, but the weather was still depressing. He did chores around the house, building bookshelves and cabinets for his wife, and a model Porsche in his study for himself. By the time classes started again on January 23, Louis was happy to be back at the university.

The flu finally arrived—a bad outbreak hit the campus less than a week after the semester started, and he was very busy. He found himself working ten or twelve hours a day and going home exhausted… but not really unhappy.

The warm weather ended suddenly on January 29. There was a blizzard, followed by a week of freezing temperatures. Louis was checking the healing broken arm of a young man who was hoping—but probably wouldn’t, Louis thought—to play baseball that spring when a volunteer told him his wife was on the phone.

Louis went to his office to answer the call. Rachel was crying, and he was immediately worried. Ellie, he thought. She fell off her sled and broke her arm. Or hit her head. He worried about the crazy college kids and their sleds.

“It’s not one of the kids, is it?” he asked. “Rachel?”

“No, no,” she said, crying harder. “Not one of the kids. It’s Norma, Lou. Norma Crandall. She died this morning. Around eight o’clock, right after breakfast, Jud said. He came over to see if you were here and I told him you’d left a half hour ago. He… oh Lou, he just seemed so lost and confused… so old… thank God Ellie was gone and Gage is too young to understand…”

Louis frowned, and despite the bad news, he realized he was thinking about Rachel, trying to understand her. Because it was happening again. Something he couldn’t quite explain, because it was a general feeling. That death was a secret, something scary, and it had to be kept from the children, especially from the children, like Victorian people who thought the truth about sex had to be kept from children.

“Jesus,” he said. “Was it her heart?”

“I don’t know,” she said. She wasn't crying anymore, but her voice was tight and rough. “Could you come, Louis? You’re his friend, and I think he needs you.”

You’re his friend.

Well, I am, Louis thought, a little surprised. I never thought I'd have an eighty-year-old man as a friend, but I guess I do. And then he realized they had to be friends, because of what they shared. And knowing that, he figured Jud had known they were friends long before Louis did. Jud had supported him then, and even with everything that had happened since, even with the mice, even with the birds, Louis felt that Jud’s choice had probably been right… or, if not right, at least kind. He would do what he could for Jud now, and if it meant being there as his wife died, he would be there.

“On my way,” he said and hung up.
