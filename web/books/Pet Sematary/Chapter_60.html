Pet Sematary

Chapter 60

Louis Creed woke up with the sun shining right in his eyes. He tried to get up and winced at the sharp pain in his back. It was awful. He fell back on the pillow and looked down at himself. Still fully dressed. Jesus.

He lay there for a long moment, getting ready to face the stiffness that had settled into every muscle, and then he sat up.

“Oh, shit,” he whispered. For a few seconds the room swayed gently but noticeably. His back throbbed like a bad tooth, and when he moved his head, it felt as if the muscles in his neck had been replaced by rusty saw blades. But his knee was really the worst. The Ben-Gay hadn’t done anything for it. He should have given himself a shot of cortisone. His pants were pulled tightly against the knee by the swelling; it looked like there was a balloon under there.

“Really messed it up,” he muttered. “Boy, oh boy, did I ever.”

He bent it very slowly so he could sit on the edge of the bed, lips pressed so tightly together that they were white. Then he began to flex it a bit, listening to the pain talk, trying to decide just how bad it really was, if it might be—

Gage! Is Gage back?

That got him on his feet in spite of the pain. He stumbled across the room like Matt Dillon’s old sidekick Chester. He went through the door and across the hall into Gage’s room. He looked around wildly, his son’s name shaking on his lips. But the room was empty. He limped down to Ellie’s room, which was also empty, and then into the spare room. That room, which faced the highway, was also empty. But—

There was a strange car across the road. Parked behind Jud’s truck.

So what?

So a strange car over there could mean trouble, that was obvious.

Louis moved the curtain and looked at the car more closely. It was a small blue car, a Chevette. And curled up on top of it, apparently sleeping, was Church.

He looked for a long time before dropping the curtain. Jud had company, that was all—so what? And it was maybe too early to worry about what might happen with Gage; Church hadn’t come back until almost one o’clock, and it was only nine o’clock now. Nine o’clock on a beautiful May morning. He would just go downstairs and make some coffee, get the heating pad and wrap it around his knee, and—

—and what’s Church doing on top of that car?

“Oh, come on,” he said aloud and started to limp back down the hall. Cats slept anywhere; it was typical.

Except Church doesn’t cross the road anymore, remember?

“Forget it,” he muttered and stopped halfway down the stairs (which he was going down almost sideways). Talking to himself, that was bad. That was—

What was that thing in the woods last night?

The thought came to him suddenly, making him tighten his lips the way the pain in his knee had done when he swung it out of bed. He had dreamed about the thing in the woods last night. His dreams of Disney World had seemed to blend easily into dreams of that thing. He dreamed that it had touched him, ruining all good dreams forever, rotting all good intentions. It was the Wendigo, and it had turned him into not just a cannibal but the father of cannibals. In his dream he had been in the Pet Sematary again but not alone. Bill and Timmy Baterman had been there. Jud had been there, looking ghostly and dead, holding his dog Spot on a clothesrope leash. Lester Morgan was there with Hanratty the bull on a length of car-towing chain. Hanratty was lying on his side, looking around with a stupid, drugged fury. And for some reason Rachel was there too, and she’d had some sort of accident at the dinner table—spilled a bottle of catsup or maybe dropped a dish of cranberry jelly, maybe, because her dress was splattered with red stains.

And then, rising behind the deadfall to a huge height, its skin a cracked reptilian yellow, its eyes great hooded foglamps, its ears not ears at all but massive curling horns, was the Wendigo, a beast that looked like a lizard born of a woman. It pointed its horny, nailed finger at all of them as they craned their necks up and up to watch it…

“Stop,” he whispered and shuddered at the sound of his own voice. He would go out into the kitchen, he decided, and make himself breakfast just as if it were any ordinary day. A bachelor breakfast, full of comforting cholesterol. A couple of fried-egg sandwiches with mayo and a slice of Bermuda onion on each one. He felt sweaty and dirty and gross, but he would save the shower for later; right now getting undressed seemed like too much work, and he was afraid he might have to get the scalpel out of his bag and actually cut the leg of the pants open in order to let his swollen knee out. A terrible way to treat good tools, but none of the knives in the house would cut the heavy jeans fabric, and Rachel’s sewing scissors certainly would not work.

But first, breakfast.

So he crossed the living room and then detoured into the front entry and looked out at the small blue car in Jud’s driveway. It was covered with dew, which meant it had been there for some time. Church was still on the roof but not sleeping. He seemed to be staring right at Louis with his ugly yellow-green eyes.

Louis stepped back quickly, as if someone had caught him peeking.

He went into the kitchen, rattled out a frying pan, put it on the stove, got eggs from the fridge. The kitchen was bright and crisp and clear. He tried to whistle—a whistle would bring the morning into its proper focus—but he could not. Things looked right, but they weren’t right. The house seemed dreadfully empty, and last night’s work weighed on him. Things were wrong, out of place; he felt a shadow hovering, and he was afraid.

He limped into the bathroom and took a couple of aspirin with a glass of orange juice. He was working his way back to the stove when the telephone rang.

He did not answer it immediately but turned and looked at it, feeling slow and stupid, a sucker in some game which he was only now realizing he did not understand in the least.

Don’t answer that, you don’t want to answer that because that’s the bad news, that’s the end of the leash that leads around the corner and into the darkness, and I don’t think you want to see what’s on the other end of that leash, Louis, I really don’t think you do, so don’t answer that phone, run, run now, the car’s in the garage, get in it and take off, but don’t answer that phone—

He crossed the room and picked it up, standing there with one hand on the dryer as he had so many times before, and it was Irwin Goldman, and even as Irwin said hello Louis saw the tracks crossing the kitchen—small, muddy tracks—and his heart seemed to freeze in his chest, and he believed he could feel his eyeballs swelling in his head, starting from their sockets; he believed that if he could have seen himself in a mirror at that moment he would have seen a face from a seventeenth-century painting of a mental hospital. They were Gage’s tracks, Gage had been here, he had been here in the night, and so where was he now?

“It’s Irwin, Louis… Louis? Are you there? Hello?”

“Hello, Irwin,” he said, and he already knew what Irwin would say. He understood the blue car. He understood everything. The leash… the leash going into the darkness… he was moving fast along it now, hand over hand. Oh, if he could let go before he saw what was at the end! But it was his leash. He had bought it.

“For a moment, I thought we’d lost connection,” Goldman was saying.

“No, the phone slipped out of my hand,” Louis said. His voice was calm.

“Did Rachel get home okay last night?”

“Oh yes,” Louis said, thinking of the blue car, Church on top of it, the blue car that was so still. His eye followed the muddy footprints on the floor.

“I should talk to her,” Goldman said. “Right away. It’s about Eileen.”

“Ellie? What about Ellie?”

“I really think Rachel—”

“Rachel’s not here right now,” Louis said sharply. “She’s gone to the store for bread and milk. What about Ellie? Come on, Irwin!”

“We had to take her to the hospital,” Goldman said slowly. “She had a bad dream, or many of them. She was very upset and wouldn’t calm down. She—”

“Did they sedate her?”

“What?”

“Sedation,” Louis said impatiently, “did they give her something to calm her down?”

“Yes, oh yes. They gave her a pill, and she went back to sleep.”

“Did she say anything? What scared her so badly?” He was gripping the phone tightly now.

Silence from Irwin Goldman’s end—a long silence. This time Louis did not interrupt, as much as he wanted to.

“That was what scared Dory so badly,” Irwin said finally. “She talked a lot before she got… before she was crying too hard to understand. Dory herself was almost… you know.”

“What did she say?”

“She said Oz the Great and Terrible had killed her mother. Only she didn’t say it that way. She said… she said ‘Oz the Gweat and Tewwible,’ which was the way our other daughter always used to say it. Our daughter Zelda. Louis, believe me when I say I would much rather have asked Rachel this question, but how much have you and she told Eileen about Zelda and how she died?”

Louis had closed his eyes; the world seemed to be rocking gently under his feet, and Goldman’s voice sounded distant, as if coming through thick mists.

You might hear sounds like voices, but it's just the loons down south near Prospect. The sound travels.

“Louis, are you there?”

“Will she be okay?” Louis asked, his voice far away. “Will Ellie be okay? Did you find out what's wrong?”

“She's in shock from the funeral,” Goldman said. “My doctor came. Lathrop. A good man. He said she had a bit of a fever and might not even remember anything when she wakes up this afternoon. But I think Rachel should come back. Louis, I’m scared. I think you should come back too.”

Louis didn't answer. God watches over everyone, or so they say. But Louis was just a man, and he was focused on those muddy footprints.

“Louis, Gage is dead,” Goldman said. “I know that's hard to accept—for you and Rachel—but your daughter is alive, and she needs you.”

Yes, I accept that. You might be an old fool, Irwin, but maybe what happened between your daughters on that day in April 1965 taught you something about understanding. She needs me, but I can't come, because I’m afraid—so afraid—that my hands are covered in her mother’s blood.

Louis looked at his hands. Louis looked at the dirt under his nails, which was just like the dirt that made those footprints on the kitchen floor.

“Okay,” he said, “I understand. We’ll be there as soon as we can, Irwin. Tonight, if possible. Thank you.”

“We did our best,” Goldman said. “Maybe we’re too old. Maybe, Louis, we always were.”

“Did she say anything else?” Louis asked.

Goldman’s reply hit him like a death knell. “A lot, but I only caught one other thing: ‘Paxcow says it’s too late.’ ”

He hung up and wandered back toward the stove, vaguely planning to finish breakfast or clean up, he wasn't sure which. Halfway across the kitchen, a wave of dizziness washed over him, his vision went gray, and he fainted—“swoon” felt like the right word because it seemed to take so long. He fell down and down through blurry depths, twisting and turning, looping and spinning, then crashed onto his bad knee. A jolt of pain shot through his head, snapping him back with a scream. For a moment, he could only crouch there, tears welling up.

Finally, he struggled to his feet, swaying. But his head was clear again. That was something, wasn’t it?

The urge to run hit him again, stronger than ever. He actually felt the comforting shape of his car keys in his pocket. He would get in the Civic and drive to Chicago. He would get Ellie and leave from there. Of course, Goldman would know something was wrong, terribly wrong, but he would get her anyway… snatch her, if he had to.

Then his hand dropped from his keys. It wasn't futility, guilt, despair, or his deep exhaustion that stopped him. It was the sight of those muddy footprints on the kitchen floor. He pictured them tracing a path across the entire country—first to Illinois, then to Florida—across the entire world, if necessary. What you bought, you owned, and what you owned eventually came home.

The day would come when he opened a door and there would be Gage, a twisted version of himself, grinning a hollow grin, his clear blue eyes turned yellow and stupidly clever. Or Ellie would open the bathroom door for her morning shower, and there would be Gage in the tub, his body covered in faded scars and bumps from his accident, clean but smelling of the grave.

Oh yes, that day would come—he had no doubt.

“How could I have been so stupid?” he said to the empty room, talking to himself again, not caring. “How?”

Grief, not stupidity, Louis. There is a difference… small, but vital. The battery that burying ground lives on. Growing in power, Jud said, and he was right—and you’re part of its power now. It has fed on your grief… no, more than that. It’s doubled it, tripled it, magnified it. And it isn’t just grief it feeds on. Sanity. It’s eaten your sanity. The flaw is just the inability to accept, not uncommon. It’s cost you your wife, and it’s almost surely cost you your best friend as well as your son. This is it. What comes when you’re too slow wishing away the thing that knocks on your door in the middle of the night is simple enough: total darkness.

I could kill myself now, he thought, and I guess that's possible, isn't it? I have the means in my bag. It has controlled everything, controlled it from the start. The burial ground, the Wendigo, whatever it is. It forced our cat into the road, and maybe it forced Gage into the road, too. It brought Rachel home, but only when it wanted to. Surely I’m meant to do that… and I want to.

But things have to be fixed, right?

Yes. They did.

He had to think about Gage. Gage was still out there. Somewhere.

He followed the footprints through the dining room and the living room and back up the stairs. They were blurry there because he had walked over them on his way down without seeing them. They led into the bedroom. He was here, Louis thought in amazement, he was right here, and then he saw that his medical bag was unzipped.

The things inside, which he always arranged so carefully, were now a mess. But it didn't take Louis long to see that his scalpel was gone, and he put his hands over his face and sat like that for a while, a soft, sad sound coming from his throat.

Finally, he opened the bag again and started to look through it.

Downstairs again.

The sound of the pantry door opening. The sound of a cupboard opening, then slamming shut. The loud hum of the can opener. Lastly, the sound of the garage door opening and closing. And then the house stood empty in the May sunshine, as it had stood empty on that August day the year before, waiting for the new people to arrive… as it would wait for other new people to arrive sometime in the future. Maybe a young married couple, with no children (but with hopes and dreams). Bright young marrieds who liked Mondavi wine and Löwenbräu beer—he might be in charge of the Northeast Bank’s credit department, she with a dental hygienist’s license or maybe three years of working as an optometrist’s assistant. He would chop half a cord of wood for the fireplace, she would wear high-waisted corduroy pants and walk in Mrs. Vinton’s field, collecting fall grasses in November for a table centerpiece, her hair in a ponytail, the brightest thing under the gray skies, completely unaware that an invisible Vulture rode the air currents overhead. They would be proud of not being superstitious, of being practical in getting the house despite its history—they would tell their friends that it was a bargain and joke about the ghost in the attic, and they would all have another Löwenbräu or another glass of Mondavi, and they would play backgammon or Mille Bourne.

And maybe they would have a dog.
