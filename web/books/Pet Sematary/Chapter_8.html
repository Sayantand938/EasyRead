Pet Sematary

Chapter 8

That Saturday, after Ellie finished her first week of school and before the college students returned, Jud Crandall crossed the road and walked over to the Creed family on their lawn. Ellie had stopped her bike and was drinking iced tea. Gage crawled in the grass, looking at bugs, maybe even eating some; Gage wasn't picky about where he got his protein.

“Jud,” Louis said, standing up. “Let me get a chair.”

“No need.” Jud wore jeans, an open work shirt, and green boots. He looked at Ellie. “Still want to see where that path goes, Ellie?”

“Yes!” Ellie said, jumping up. Her eyes sparkled. “George Buck at school said it was the pet cemetery, and I told Mommy, but she said to wait for you because you knew where it was.”

“I do,” Jud said. “If your folks say it’s okay, we’ll walk up there. You’ll need boots, though. The ground is a bit wet in places.”

Ellie hurried into the house.

Jud watched her with fond amusement. “Maybe you’d like to come too, Louis.”

“I would,” Louis said. He looked at Rachel. “Want to come, honey?”

“What about Gage? I thought it was a mile.”

“I’ll put him in the Gerrypack.”

Rachel laughed. “Okay… but it’s your back, mister.”

They started off ten minutes later, all of them but Gage wearing boots. Gage was sitting up in the Gerrypack and looking at everything over Louis’s shoulder, wide-eyed. Ellie ran ahead constantly, chasing butterflies and picking flowers.

The grass in the back field was almost waist high, and now there was goldenrod, that late-summer sign that tells on autumn every year. But there was no autumn in the air today; today the sun was still all August, even though August was almost two weeks gone. By the time they had reached the top of the first hill, walking in a line along the cut path, there were big patches of sweat under Louis’s arms.

Jud paused. At first Louis thought it might be because the old man was out of breath—then he saw the view that had opened up behind them.

“Pretty up here,” Jud said, putting a piece of timothy grass between his teeth. Louis thought he had just heard the perfect Yankee understatement.

“It’s gorgeous,” Rachel breathed and then turned to Louis, almost accusingly. “How come you didn’t tell me about this?”

“Because I didn’t know it was here,” Louis said, and was a little ashamed. They were still on their own property; he had just never found time to climb the hill in back of the house until today.

Ellie had been a good way ahead. Now she came back also looking with honest wonder. Church padded at her heels.

The hill was not a high one, but it didn’t need to be. To the east, thick woods blocked any view, but looking west, the land sloped away in a golden and sleepy late summer dream. Everything was still, hazy, silent. There was not even an Orinco tanker on the highway to break the quiet.

It was the river valley they were looking at, the Penobscot, where loggers once floated logs from the northeast to Bangor and Derry. They were south of Bangor and a bit north of Derry. The river flowed wide and peacefully, as if in a deep dream. Louis could see Hampden and Winterport on the other side, and he thought he could trace Route 15, a black line running by the river, almost all the way to Bucksport. They looked over the river, its thick border of trees, the roads, the fields. The spire of the North Ludlow Baptist Church stuck out from the old elms, and to the right, he saw Ellie’s sturdy brick school.

White clouds moved slowly toward a faded denim horizon. Everywhere were the late-summer fields, worn out, dormant but not dead, a striking tan color.

“Gorgeous is the right word,” Louis said.

“They used to call it Prospect Hill,” Jud said. He put a cigarette in his mouth but didn’t light it. “Some still do, but younger people have moved in, and it’s mostly forgotten. Not many people come up here. It doesn’t seem like you can see much because the hill isn’t high. But you can see—” He gestured and stopped talking.

“You can see everything,” Rachel said softly, in awe. She turned to Louis. “Honey, do we own this?”

Before Louis could answer, Jud said: “It’s part of the property, yes.”

Which wasn’t quite the same thing, Louis thought.

It was cooler in the woods, maybe eight or ten degrees cooler. The path, still wide and marked with flowers in pots or coffee cans (mostly wilted), was now covered with dry pine needles. They had walked about a quarter of a mile, downhill, when Jud called Ellie back.

“This is a good walk for a little girl,” Jud said kindly, “but promise your mom and dad that if you come here, you’ll stay on the path.”

“I promise,” Ellie said quickly. “Why?”

He glanced at Louis, who had stopped to rest. Carrying Gage, even in the shade of the pines and spruces, was hard work. “Do you know where you are?” Jud asked Louis.

Louis thought about possible answers but rejected them: Ludlow, North Ludlow, behind my house, between Route 15 and Middle Drive. He shook his head.

Jud pointed back with his thumb. “Town's that way,” he said. “This way, nothing but woods for fifty miles or more. They call it the North Ludlow Woods, but it touches a bit of Orrington, then goes to Rockford. Ends up on those state lands I told you about, the ones the Indians want back. It sounds funny that your nice little house on the main road, with its phone, lights, and cable TV, is on the edge of a wilderness, but it is.” He looked back at Ellie. “Just don’t go wandering around in these woods, Ellie. You might lose the path, and who knows where you'd end up.”

“I won’t, Mr. Crandall.” Ellie seemed impressed, even awed, but not scared, Louis saw. Rachel, however, looked at Jud uneasily, and Louis felt a little uneasy himself. He figured it was a city person's fear of the woods. Louis hadn’t held a compass since Boy Scouts, twenty years before, and his memories of finding your way by the North Star or which side of trees moss grew on were vague, like his memories of tying certain knots.

Jud looked them over and smiled. “We haven’t lost anyone in these woods since 1934,” he said. “At least, nobody local. The last one was Will Jeppson—no great loss. Except for Stanny Bouchard, Will was the biggest drunk around here.”

“You said nobody local,” Rachel said, not quite casually, and Louis could almost read her mind: We’re not local. At least, not yet.

Jud paused and nodded. “We lose a tourist every two or three years because they think you can't get lost right off the main road. But we've never lost one for good, missus. Don’t worry.”

“Are there moose?” Rachel asked, sounding worried, and Louis smiled. If Rachel wanted to worry, she would.

“Well, you might see a moose,” Jud said, “but it won’t bother you, Rachel. During mating season they get a little grumpy, but usually they just look. The only people they chase when they're not mating are people from Massachusetts. I don’t know why, but it’s true.” Louis wasn't sure if the man was joking; Jud looked completely serious. “I’ve seen it many times. Some guy from Saugus or Milton or Weston up a tree, yelling about a herd of moose, all of them as big as a motorhome. Maybe moose can smell Massachusetts on a person. Or maybe it’s just all those new clothes from L. L. Bean’s they smell—I don’t know. I wish a college student would write a paper about it, but I guess none ever will.”

“What’s rutting time?” Ellie asked.

“Never mind,” Rachel said. “I don’t want you up here unless you’re with a grown-up, Ellie.” Rachel moved a step closer to Louis.

Jud looked sad. “I didn’t want to scare you, Rachel—you or your daughter. No need to be scared in these woods. This is a good path; it gets a little buggy in the spring and it’s a little muddy all the time—except for ‘55, which was the driest summer I can remember—but hell, there isn’t even any poison ivy or poison oak, which there is at the back of the schoolyard, and you want to stay away from it, Ellie, if you don’t want to spend three weeks of your life taking starch baths.”

Ellie covered her mouth and giggled.

“It’s a safe path,” Jud said earnestly to Rachel, who still didn’t look convinced. “Why, I bet even Gage could follow it, and the town kids come up here a lot, I already told you that. They keep it nice. Nobody tells them to; they just do it. I wouldn’t want to spoil that for Ellie.” He bent over her and winked. “It’s like many other things in life, Ellie. You keep on the path and all’s well. You get off it and the next thing you know you’re lost if you’re not lucky. And then someone has to send out a search party.”

They walked on. Louis began to get a dull cramp of pain in his back, from the baby carrier. Every now and then Gage would grab a double handful of his hair and tug hard or give a cheerful kick to Louis’s kidneys. Late mosquitoes buzzed around his face and neck, making their eyes water.

The path curved down, bending in and out between very old fir trees, and then cut widely through a prickly, tangled patch of undergrowth. The walking was muddy here, and Louis’s boots squelched in mud and some standing water. At one point they stepped over a marshy spot using a pair of good-sized clumps of grass as stepping stones. That was the worst of it. They started to climb again and the trees took over again. Gage seemed to have magically put on ten pounds, and the day had, with some similar magic, warmed up ten degrees. Sweat poured down Louis’s face.

“How you doing, hon?” Rachel asked. “Want me to carry him for a while?”

“No, I’m fine,” he said, and it was true, although his heart was beating fast in his chest. He was more used to telling people to exercise than he was to doing it.

Jud was walking with Ellie by his side; her lemon-yellow slacks and red blouse were bright splashes of color in the shady brown-green gloom.

“Lou, does he really know where he’s going, do you think?” Rachel asked in a low, slightly worried tone.

“Sure,” Louis said.

Jud called back cheerfully, not turning around: “Almost there… you doing okay, Louis?”

Louis thought, the man is over eighty, but he doesn’t seem tired at all.

“I’m fine,” he answered a little sharply. He would have said the same even if he were having a heart attack, he thought. He smiled, adjusted his backpack, and kept walking.

They reached the top of the second hill, and then the path went through some tall bushes and thick undergrowth. It got narrower, and then Louis saw Ellie and Jud go under an arch made of old, weathered boards. The words PET SEMATARY were written on them in faded black paint, barely readable.

He and Rachel smiled at each other and walked under the arch, automatically reaching for each other’s hands, as if they were getting married.

For the second time that morning, Louis was filled with surprise and awe.

There were no pine needles here. Instead, there was a circle of cut grass, about forty feet across. It was surrounded by thick bushes on three sides and a pile of fallen trees on the fourth, a messy heap that looked both scary and hazardous. Louis thought that anyone trying to get through or over it should wear protective gear. The clearing was full of markers, clearly made by children using whatever they could find—pieces of wood, scrap metal, and so on. Yet, against the background of low bushes and thin trees struggling for light, the fact that they were handmade, and that people had created this place, somehow made their simple design stand out. The surrounding forest gave the place a strange sense of importance, a charm that was not religious but more ancient and wild.

“It’s lovely,” Rachel said, but she didn’t sound like she meant it.

“Wow!” Ellie shouted.

Louis took Gage off his shoulder and set him down to crawl. Louis felt relief in his back.

Ellie ran from one marker to the next, excitedly pointing them out. Louis followed her while Rachel watched the baby. Jud sat down, legs crossed, leaning against a rock, and smoked.

Louis noticed the place seemed more than just orderly; the memorials were in rough circles.

SMUCKY THE CAT, one wooden marker said. The writing was childish but neat. HE WAS OBEDIANT. And below: 1971-1974. A little further around, a piece of slate had a name in faded red paint: BIFFER. And below that, a rhyme: BIFFER, BIFFER. A HELLUVA SNIFFER / UNTIL HE DIED HE MADE US RICHER.

“Biffer was the Desslers’ cocker spaniel,” Jud said. He had scraped a spot in the dirt with his heel and was tapping his ashes into it. “Got run over by a dumpster last year. Ain’t that some poem?”

“It is,” Louis said.

Some graves had flowers, some new, most old, many decayed. Most of the writing Louis tried to read had faded. Others had no mark, and Louis guessed they were written with chalk or crayon.

“Mom!” Ellie shouted. “Here’s a goldfish! Come see!”

“I’m okay,” Rachel said, and Louis looked at her. She stood alone, outside the circle, looking uneasy. Louis thought: She’s upset even here. She was never comfortable around death (not that anyone is), probably because of her sister. Rachel’s sister died young, and it left a scar Louis knew not to touch. Her name was Zelda, and she died from spinal meningitis. Her sickness was long, painful, and ugly, and Rachel was young then. If she wanted to forget, he thought it was okay.

Louis winked at her, and Rachel smiled at him.

Louis looked up. They were in a natural opening in the woods. He guessed that explained why the grass was so healthy; the sun could reach it. Still, it would have taken watering and care. That meant carrying cans of water up here or maybe using Indian pumps, even heavier than Gage in his Gerrypack, carried on small backs. He thought again that it was strange for children to have kept it up for so long. He remembered his own childhood interests, and seeing Ellie's, they usually burned out quickly—fast… hot… and quick to die.

Moving further in, the pet graves were older; fewer and fewer of the inscriptions could be read, but those that could gave a rough timeline going back in time. Here was TRIXIE. KILLED ON THE HIGHWAY SEPT 15, 1968. In the same circle was a wide flat board planted deep in the ground. Freezing and thawing had warped it and tilted it to one side, but Louis could still make out IN MEMORY OF MARTA OUR PET RABBIT DIED MARCH 1 1965. A row further in was: GEN. PATTON (OUR! GOOD! DOG! the inscription emphasized), who had died in 1958; and POLYNESIA (who would have been a parrot, if Louis remembered Doctor Doolittle correctly), who had squawked her last “Polly want a cracker” in the summer of 1953. There was nothing readable in the next two rows, and then, still far from the center, carved roughly on a piece of sandstone, was HANNAH THE BEST DOG THAT EVER LIVED 1929-1939. Sandstone was relatively soft, so the inscription was now just a faint outline, but Louis found it hard to imagine how long some child must have spent carving those nine words on the stone. The dedication of love and grief seemed amazing to him; this was something parents didn't even do for their own parents or for their children if they died young.

“Wow, this really goes back a long time,” he said to Jud, who had wandered over to join him.

Jud nodded. “Come here, Louis. I want to show you something.”

They walked to a row only three back from the center. Here the circular pattern, which seemed almost random in the outer rows, was very clear. Jud stopped before a small piece of slate that had fallen over. Kneeling carefully, the old man stood it up again.

“There used to be words here,” Jud said. “I carved them myself, but they've worn away now. I buried my first dog here. Spot. He died of old age in 1914, the year the Great War started.”

Struck by the thought that this graveyard was older than many graveyards for people, Louis walked toward the center and looked at several of the markers. None of them were readable, and most had been almost taken back by the forest floor. The grass had almost completely grown over one, and when he stood it back up, there was a small tearing sound from the earth. Blind beetles scurried over the area he had uncovered. He felt a small chill and thought, Boot Hill for animals. I’m not sure I really like it.

“How far back do these go?”

“Gee, I don’t know,” Jud said, putting his hands deep in his pockets. “The place was here when Spot died, of course. I had a lot of friends in those days. They helped me dig the hole for Spot. Digging here isn’t easy, either—the ground’s really stony, you know, hard to dig. And I helped them sometimes.” He pointed here and there with a rough finger. “That there was Pete LaVasseur’s dog, if I remember right, and there’s three of Albion Groatley’s barn cats buried right in a row there.

“Old Man Fritchie kept racing pigeons. Me and Al Groatley and Carl Hannah buried one of them that a dog got. He’s right there.” He paused thoughtfully. “I’m the last one left from that group, you know. All dead now, my friends. All gone.”

Louis said nothing, just stood looking at the pet graves with his hands in his pockets.

“Ground’s stony,” Jud repeated. “Couldn’t plant anything here but corpses anyway, I guess.”

Across the way, Gage began to cry softly, and Rachel brought him over, holding him on her hip. “He’s hungry,” she said. “I think we should go back, Lou. Please, okay?” her eyes asked.

“Sure,” he said. He put the Gerrypack on his shoulder again and turned around so Rachel could put Gage in. “Ellie! Hey Ellie, where are you?”

“There she is,” Rachel said and pointed toward the fallen trees. Ellie was climbing as if the fallen trees were like the monkey bars at school.

“Oh, honey, you want to come down from there!” Jud called out, worried. “If you put your foot in the wrong hole and those old trees move, you’ll break your ankle.”

Ellie jumped down. “Ow!” she cried and came toward them, rubbing her hip. The skin wasn’t broken, but a stiff dead branch had torn her pants.

“You see what I mean,” Jud said, tousling her hair. “Fallen trees like this, even someone who knows the woods well won’t try to climb over it if he can go around. Trees that all fall down in a pile get mean. They’ll bite you if they can.”

“Really?” Ellie asked.

“Really. They’re piled up like straws, you see. And if you were to step on the right one, they might all come down in an avalanche.”

Ellie looked at Louis. “Is that true, Daddy?”

“I think so, hon.”

“Yuck!” She looked back at the fallen trees and yelled: “You tore my pants, you stupid trees!”

All three of the adults laughed. The fallen trees did not. It just sat there, getting bleached by the sun as it had for years. To Louis, it looked like the bones of some ancient monster, killed by a truly good and noble knight, maybe. A dragon’s bones, left here in a huge pile of stones.

It struck him even then that the fallen trees were too well-placed, standing as they did between the pet cemetery and the deep woods beyond, woods that Jud Crandall sometimes called “the Indian woods.” Its randomness seemed too deliberate, too perfect to be natural. It—

Then Gage grabbed his ear and twisted it, laughing, and Louis forgot about the fallen trees in the woods beyond the pet cemetery. It was time to go home.
