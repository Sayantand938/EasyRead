Pet Sematary

Introduction

Stephen King

When people ask me (and they often do) what I think is the scariest book I've ever written, I always answer right away: Pet Sematary. It might not be the book that scares readers the most—based on my fan mail, The Shining probably does—but everyone is scared by different things. All I know is that Pet Sematary was the one I put away in a drawer, thinking I had gone too far. Time has shown I hadn’t, at least not in terms of what people would accept. But I had gone too far for my own feelings. Simply put, I was horrified by what I had written and the conclusions I had reached. I’ve told the story of how the book was written before, but I guess I can tell it one more time: the last time covers everything.

In the late seventies, I was asked to spend a year at my old college, the University of Maine, as the writer in residence. I also taught a class in fantasy literature (my notes for that class became the basis for Danse Macabre, which came out a year or two later). My wife and I rented a house in Orrington, about twelve miles from the college. It was a great house in a great small town in Maine. The only problem was the road we lived on. It was very busy, with lots of heavy tanker trucks from the chemical plant down the road.

Julio DeSanctis, who owned the store across the road from us, told me early on that my wife and I should watch our children closely, and any pets they might have. “That road has killed a lot of animals," Julio said, a line that ended up in the story. And the proof of how many animals the road had killed was in the woods behind our rented house. A path led through the field next door to a little pet cemetery in the woods… but the sign on the tree outside this charming little graveyard said PET SEMATARY. This phrase did more than just appear in the book; it became the title. There were dogs and cats buried there, some birds, and even a goat.

Our daughter, who was about eight at the time, had a cat named Smucky. Not long after we moved into the Orrington house, I found Smucky dead on the lawn of a house across the road. The road had claimed another animal, it seemed: my daughter’s beloved pet. We buried Smucky in the pet cemetery. My daughter made the grave marker, which read SMUCKY: HE WAS OBEDIANT. (Smucky wasn’t obedient at all, of course; he was a cat, after all.)

Everything seemed fine until that night, when I heard a thumping from the garage, along with crying and popping sounds like small firecrackers. I went to see what was happening and found my daughter, angry and upset in her grief. She had found some sheets of bubble wrap, the kind used to protect fragile items during shipping. She was jumping on it, popping the bubbles, and yelling, “He was my cat! Let God have his own cat! Smucky was my cat!” I think that kind of anger is the healthiest first reaction to grief that a person can have, and I’ve always admired her for that defiant cry: Let God have his own cat! That’s right, honey; that’s right.

Our youngest son, who was less than two years old, had just learned to walk and was already practicing running. One day not long after Smucky died, while we were in the yard next door playing with a kite, our toddler decided to run toward the road. I ran after him, and I could hear one of those Cianbro trucks coming (Orinco, in the novel). Either I caught him and pulled him down, or he tripped on his own; to this day, I’m not completely sure which. When you’re really scared, you often forget things. All I know for sure is that he is still alive and well and is now a young man. But part of my mind has never forgotten that terrible what if: What if I hadn’t caught him? Or what if he had fallen in the middle of the road instead of on the side?

I think you can see why I found the book that came from these events so disturbing. I simply took what had happened and added that one terrible what if. In other words, I found myself not just thinking the unthinkable, but writing it down.

There was no writing space in the Orrington house, but there was an empty room in Julio’s store, and that’s where I wrote Pet Sematary. Day to day, I enjoyed the work, and I knew I was telling an exciting story, one that held my attention and would hold the attention of readers. But when you’re working day to day, you’re not seeing the big picture; you’re just focusing on the details. When I finished, I let the book sit for six weeks, which is how I work, and then read it again. I found it so shocking and so gruesome that I put it in a drawer, thinking it would never be published. At least not while I was alive.

That it came to be published was just luck. I had ended my relationship with Doubleday, the publisher of my first books, but I still owed them one last novel to settle things completely. I only had one available, and that was Pet Sematary. I talked it over with my wife, who gives me the best advice when I’m unsure what to do, and she told me I should publish it. She thought it was good. Terrible, but too good to keep from readers.

My first editor at Doubleday, Bill Thompson, had left by then (for Everest House; Bill was the one who first suggested, then edited and published Danse Macabre), so I sent the book to Sam Vaughn, who was a major figure in publishing at the time. Sam made the final call—he wanted the book. He edited it himself, focusing on the ending, and his help turned a good book into a better one. I’ve always appreciated his insightful editing, and I’ve never regretted publishing the book, even though I still find it upsetting and troubling in many ways.

I’m especially uneasy about the book’s most memorable line, spoken by Louis Creed’s old neighbor, Jud. “Sometimes, Louis,” Jud says, “dead is better.” I sincerely hope that isn’t true, yet within the nightmare of Pet Sematary, it seems to be. And maybe that’s okay. Maybe “sometimes dead is better” is the final lesson of grief, the one we learn when we finally stop raging and begging God to take his own cat (or his own child) and leave ours alone. That lesson suggests that we can only find peace in our lives by accepting the way the world works. That might sound like cheesy, new-age nonsense, but the alternative seems like a darkness too horrible for us to endure.

September 20, 2000
