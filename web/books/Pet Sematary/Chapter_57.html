Pet Sematary

Chapter 57

Louis tripped and fell flat on the ground. For a moment he didn’t think he could get up—getting up was too much—he would just lie there, listening to the chorus of peepers from Little God Swamp behind him and feeling the chorus of aches and pains in his body. He would lie there until he fell asleep. Or died. Probably the latter.

He remembered slipping the canvas bundle into the hole he had dug, and pushing most of the dirt back into the hole with his bare hands. And he thought he remembered piling the rocks up, building from a wide base to a point…

From then to now he remembered very little. He must have gotten back down the steps or he wouldn’t be here, which was… where? Looking around, he thought he recognized one of the groves of great old pines not far beyond the deadfall. Could he have made it all the way back through Little God Swamp without realizing it? He guessed it was possible. Just barely.

This is far enough. I’ll just sleep here.

But it was that thought, so falsely comforting, that made him get up and move again. Because if he stayed here, that thing might find him… that thing might be in the woods and looking for him right now.

He rubbed his hand up his face, palm first, and was surprised to see blood on his hand… he'd given himself a nosebleed at some point. “Who cares?” he muttered roughly and reached around him until he found the pick and shovel again.

Ten minutes later the deadfall appeared ahead. Louis climbed it, stumbling but somehow not falling until he was almost down. Then he glanced at his feet, a branch snapped (don’t look down, Jud had said), another branch tumbled, making his foot slip, and he fell with a thud on his side, the wind knocked out of him.

I’ll be damned if this isn’t the second graveyard I’ve fallen into tonight… and I’ll be damned if two isn’t enough.

He began to feel around for the pick and shovel again, and finally found them. For a moment he looked around, able to see by starlight. Nearby was the grave of SMUCKY. He was obedient, Louis thought wearily. And TRIXIE, KILT ON THE HIGHWAY. The wind still blew strongly, and he could hear the faint ting-ting-ting of a piece of metal—maybe it had been a Del Monte can, cut by a grieving pet owner with his father’s tin snips and then flattened with a hammer and nailed to a stick—and that brought the fear back again. He was too tired to feel it as more than a sickening pulse. He had done it. That steady ting-ting-ting sound coming out of the darkness made it real to him more than anything else.

He walked through the Pet Sematary, past the grave of MARTA OUR PET RABBIT who had DIED MARCH 1 1965, and near the mound of GEN. PATTON; he stepped over the broken piece of wood that marked the final resting place of POLYNESIA. The tick of metal was louder now, and he paused, looking down. Here on a slightly tilted board stuck in the ground, was a tin rectangle, and by starlight Louis read, RINGO OUR HAMSTER, 1964-1965. It was this piece of tin that was ticking against the boards of the Pet Sematary’s entry arch. Louis reached down to bend the piece of tin back… and then froze, his skin crawling.

Something was moving back there. Something was moving on the other side of the fallen trees.

What he heard was a quiet sound—the soft crackle of pine needles, the dry snap of a twig, the rustle of bushes. They were almost lost under the sound of the wind through the pines.

“Gage?” Louis called weakly.

Just realizing what he was doing—standing here in the dark and calling his dead son—made his skin tighten and his hair stand up. He began to shake uncontrollably, as if he had a bad fever.

“Gage?”

The sounds had stopped.

Not yet; it’s too soon. Don’t ask me how I know, but I do. That isn’t Gage over there. That’s… something else.

He suddenly thought of Ellie telling him, He called “Lazarus, come forth”… because if He hadn’t called for Lazarus by name, everyone in that graveyard would have risen.

On the other side of the fallen trees, those sounds had started again. On the other side of the barrier. Almost—but not quite—hidden by the wind. As if something blind were following him with old instincts. His extremely scared brain created awful, sickening images: a giant mole, a big bat that dragged itself through the bushes instead of flying.

Louis backed out of the Pet Sematary, not turning his back to the deadfall—that ghostlike glimmer, a pale mark on the dark—until he was far down the path. Then he began to hurry, and about a quarter of a mile before the path left the woods and entered the field behind his house, he found the strength to run.

Louis tossed the pick and shovel carelessly inside the garage and stood for a moment at the end of his driveway, looking first back the way he had come and then up at the sky. It was a little after four in the morning, and he figured dawn wasn't far off. Light would already be well across the Atlantic, but for now, here in Ludlow, the night was strong. The wind blew steadily.

He went into the house, feeling his way along the side of the garage and unlocking the back door. He went through the kitchen without turning on a light and stepped into the small bathroom between the kitchen and the dining room. Here he did turn on a light, and the first thing he saw was Church, curled up on top of the toilet tank, staring at him with those dull yellow-green eyes.

“Church,” he said. “I thought someone let you out.”

Church only looked at him from atop the toilet tank. Yes, someone had let Church out; he had done it himself. He remembered that very clearly. Just as he remembered replacing the window pane in the basement that time and then telling himself that that had solved the problem. But who was he kidding? When Church wanted to get in, Church got in. Because Church was different now.

It didn’t matter. In this tired, drained aftermath, nothing seemed to matter. He felt less than human, like one of George Romero’s slow, stumbling movie-zombies, or someone who had escaped from T. S. Eliot’s poem about the empty men. I should have been a pair of rough claws, crawling through Little God Swamp and up to the Micmac burial ground, he thought and gave a dry chuckle.

“Head full of straw, Church,” he said in his hoarse voice. He was unbuttoning his shirt now. “That’s me. You better believe it.”

There was a nice bruise forming on his left side, about halfway up his ribs, and when he took off his pants he saw that the knee he had banged on the gravestone was swelling up like a balloon. It had already turned a nasty purple-black, and he figured that as soon as he stopped moving it, the joint would become stiff and painfully stubborn—as if it had been dipped in cement. It looked like one of those injuries that might talk to him on rainy days for the rest of his life.

He reached out a hand to stroke Church, wanting some comfort, but the cat leaped down from the toilet tank, staggering in that clumsy and strangely unfeline way, and left for some other place. It gave Louis one flat, yellow glance as it went.

There was Ben-Gay in the medicine cabinet. Louis lowered the toilet seat, sat down, and smeared a blob on his bad knee. Then he rubbed some more on the small of his back—a clumsy job.

He left the toilet and walked into the living room. He turned on the hall light and stood there at the foot of the stairs for a moment, looking around, dazed. How strange it all seemed! This was where he had stood on Christmas Eve when he had given Rachel the sapphire. It had been in the pocket of his robe. There was his chair, where he had tried to explain death to Ellie after Norma Crandall’s heart attack—facts he had found he couldn't accept himself. The Christmas tree had been in that corner, Ellie’s paper turkey—the one that had reminded Louis of a futuristic crow—had been taped in that window, and long before that, the room had been empty except for the moving boxes, filled with their things and brought from the Midwest. He remembered thinking that their things looked small and unimportant, boxed up like that—a weak defense between his family and the cold, unknown world where no one knew them.

How strange it all seemed… and how he wished they had never heard of the University of Maine, or Ludlow, or Jud and Norma Crandall, or any of it.

He went upstairs in his underwear, and in the bathroom he got the stool, stood on it, and took down the small black bag from the top of the medicine cabinet. He took this into the bedroom, sat down, and began to rummage through it. Yes, there were syringes in case he needed one, and among the tape and scissors and wrapped surgical thread were several vials of poison.

If needed.

Louis snapped the bag shut and put it by the bed. He turned off the light, then lay down, hands behind his head. To lie here resting was wonderful. His thoughts went to Disney World again. He saw himself in a white uniform, driving a white van with the mouse-ears logo—nothing to show it was a rescue unit, nothing to scare the customers.

Gage was sitting beside him, his skin tan, his eyes bright. Here, was Goofy, shaking hands with a little boy; the kid was amazed. Here was Winnie the Pooh posing with two laughing grandmas in pants so a third grandma could take their pictures; here was a little girl crying, “I love you, Tigger! I love you, Tigger!”

He and his son were on patrol. He and his son were the guards in this magic land, and they drove in their white van with the covered red light. They were not looking for trouble, but they were ready for it. It could be here, in a place of happiness; some man buying film could clutch his chest, a pregnant woman might feel labor pains as she walked down the steps, a teenage girl might collapse in a fit, her body jerking as her brain failed. There was sunstroke and heatstroke and stroke, and maybe a lightning strike; there was, even, Oz himself—he might be seen near the monorail or looking down from one of the flying Dumbos—Louis and Gage knew him as just another park figure like Goofy or Mickey or Tigger or Donald Duck. He was the one no one wanted to meet, the one no one wanted to introduce to his kids. Louis and Gage knew him; they had met him in New England. He was waiting to choke you, to smother you, to kill you with electricity—Available at Any Outlet. There was death in peanuts, steak, cigarettes. He was always around, he watched all the paths between life and death. Needles, poison, wires, fires. Skates that sent kids into traffic. When you showered, Oz was there—Shower with a Friend. When you got on a plane, Oz took your ticket. He was in the water, the food. Who’s out there? you cried in the dark, and it was his answer: Don’t be afraid, it’s just me. Hi! You got cancer, too bad! Blood in acid on the road. Pills, eat them up. That blue color of the fingernails—the brain takes all the oxygen, even under the nails. Hi, folks, I’m Oz, call me Oz—we’re old friends. Just here to give you heart failure or a blood clot; got to see a woman give birth, then I’ve got a fire to do in Omaha.

And that thin voice is crying, “I love you, Tigger! I love you! I believe in you, Tigger! I will always love you and believe in you, and I will stay young, and the only Oz in my heart will be that faker! I love you…

We drive… my son and I… because it’s not about war or sex but that fight against Oz. He and I, in our white van under this sky, we drive. And the red light is covered, but it is there… and only we know because a man’s heart is hard; a man grows what he can… and cares for it.

Thinking these thoughts, Louis Creed drifted away, losing touch with reality, until all thoughts stopped and sleep took him.

Just before the first light of dawn appeared in the east, footsteps sounded on the stairs. They were slow and heavy, but determined. A shadow moved in the darkness of the hall. A smell came with it—a stench. Louis, even in his deep sleep, mumbled and turned away from that smell. There was the steady sound of breathing.

The figure stood outside the master bedroom door for a while, not moving. Then it came inside. Louis’s face was buried in his pillow. White hands reached out, and there was a click as the black doctor’s bag by the bed was opened.

A quiet clink and shift as the things inside were moved.

The hands searched, pushing aside drugs and vials and syringes without interest. Now they found something and held it up. In the dim early light, there was a glint of silver.

The shadowy thing left the room.
