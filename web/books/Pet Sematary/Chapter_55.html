Pet Sematary

Chapter 55

Louis was lost in his dream again; every so often he glanced down to make sure he was carrying a body in a tarp, not a green trash bag. He recalled how, when he woke up after Jud took him to the woods with Church, he could hardly remember what they had done. But now he also remembered how strong his senses had been, how alive he had felt, how they seemed to reach out and touch the woods as if the woods were alive and communicating with him.

He followed the path, remembering where it widened like a road, where it narrowed so he had to turn sideways to keep his bundle from getting stuck in the bushes, and where it went through tall, majestic trees. He smelled the sharp scent of pine, and he heard the strange crunch of needles under his feet—a feeling as much as a sound.

Finally, the path began to slope downward more steeply. Soon, his foot splashed in shallow water and sank into the mud… the quicksand, if Jud was right. Louis looked down and saw standing water among reeds and ugly, low bushes with broad, almost tropical leaves. He remembered the light seemed brighter that other night, too. More electric.

This part is like a trap—you have to walk carefully and steadily. Just follow me and don’t look down.

Yes, okay… and by the way, have you ever seen plants like these in Maine? In Maine or anywhere else? What are they?

Never mind, Louis. Just… let’s go.

He walked again, glancing at the wet, swampy ground just long enough to find the next clump of grass, then looking ahead. Faith is accepting gravity, he thought; not something from a college class, but something his high school physics teacher had said near the end of class… something Louis had never forgotten.

He accepted that the Micmac burial ground could bring back the dead and walked into Little God Swamp with his son, not looking down or back. The swamp was louder now than in late autumn. Peepers sang constantly in the reeds, a shrill sound Louis found strange and unwelcome. A frog occasionally croaked somewhere in the distance. About twenty steps into the swamp, something buzzed past him… maybe a bat.

The mist began to swirl around him, first covering his shoes, then his legs, finally surrounding him in a glowing white bubble. The light seemed brighter, pulsing like a strange heartbeat. He had never felt nature so strongly as a single, powerful force, a real being… possibly aware. The swamp was alive, but not with pleasant sounds. If asked to explain what he felt, he couldn't. He only knew it was full of potential and strength. Inside it, Louis felt small and mortal.

Then there was a sound, and he remembered it from before: a high, gulping laugh that turned into a sob. There was silence, then the laugh came again, rising to a crazy shriek that froze Louis's blood. The mist drifted around him. The laughter faded, leaving only the wind's drone, heard but not felt. Of course; it had to be a hollow in the earth. If the wind could get in here, it would have ripped the mist to shreds… and Louis wasn’t sure he wanted to see what might be revealed.

You may hear sounds like voices, but it’s just the loons down south toward Prospect. Sound travels. It’s funny.

“Loons,” Louis said, barely recognizing the cracked, ghostly sound of his own voice. But he sounded amused. God help him, he actually sounded amused.

He hesitated, then moved on. As if to punish him for stopping, his foot slipped from the next clump of grass, and he almost lost his shoe, pulling it free from the mud under the shallow water.

The voice—if that’s what it was—came again, from the left. Then from behind him… right behind him, it seemed, as if he could turn and see something bloody less than a foot from his back, all teeth and shining eyes… but this time Louis didn’t slow down. He looked straight ahead and kept walking.

Suddenly the mist darkened, and Louis saw a face hanging in the air, grinning and babbling. Its eyes, tilted up like in a Chinese painting, were yellowish-gray, sunken, gleaming. The mouth was pulled down; the lower lip was inside out, showing teeth stained brown and worn down. But Louis noticed the ears, which were not ears but curving horns… not devil’s horns, but ram’s horns.

This ghostly head seemed to be speaking—laughing. Its mouth moved, but the turned-down lip never went back to normal. Black veins pulsed. Its nostrils flared, as if alive, and blew out white vapor.

As Louis got closer, the head’s tongue lolled out. It was long and pointed, dirty yellow. It was covered in peeling scales, and as Louis watched, one flipped up like a manhole cover and a white worm oozed out. The tongue's tip moved lazily in the air where its Adam’s apple should have been… it was laughing.

He clutched Gage closer, hugging him for protection, and his feet slipped on the grassy clumps where they barely held.

You might see St. Elmo’s fire, what sailors call foo-lights. It can make funny shapes, but it’s nothing. If the shapes bother you, just look away…

Jud's voice in his head gave him some strength. He started moving forward again, stumbling at first, then finding his balance. He didn't look away but noticed that the face—if that's what it was and not just a trick of the mist and his own mind—always seemed to be the same distance away. And after a few seconds or minutes, it simply faded into the drifting mist.

That was not St. Elmo’s fire.

No, of course it wasn’t. This place was full of spirits; it was dark with them. You could look around and see something that would drive you crazy. He would not think about it. There was no need to think about it. There was no need to—

Something was coming.

Louis stopped completely, listening to that sound… that unstoppable, approaching sound. His mouth fell open, every muscle holding his jaw shut simply giving way.

It was a sound like nothing he had ever heard—a living sound, a big sound. Somewhere nearby, growing closer, branches were snapping off. There was a crackling of bushes breaking under huge feet. The soft ground under Louis’s feet began to shake along with it. He realized he was moaning

(oh my God oh my dear God what is that what is coming through this fog?)

and again holding Gage tightly to his chest; he realized that the peepers and frogs had gone silent, he realized that the wet air had taken on a strange, sickening smell like warm, spoiled pork.

Whatever it was, it was huge.

Louis’s wondering, terrified face tilted up and up, like a man watching a rocket go up. The thing thumped toward him, and there was the cracking sound of a tree—not a branch, but a whole tree—falling over somewhere close by.

Louis saw something.

The mist turned a dull gray for a moment, but this blurry, vague mark was more than sixty feet high. It wasn't a shadow or a ghost; he could feel the air move as it passed, could hear its huge feet hit the ground, the mud sucking as it moved on.

For a moment, he thought he saw two yellow-orange sparks high above him. Sparks like eyes.

Then the sound began to fade. As it went away, a peeper called out hesitantly—one. Another answered. A third joined in; a fourth made it a lively discussion; a fifth and sixth turned it into a peeper convention. The sounds of the thing moving (slow but not clumsy; perhaps that was the worst part, the feeling that it was moving with purpose) were fading to the north. Less and less… gone.

Finally, Louis began to move again. His shoulders and back were frozen and aching. He was soaked in sweat from neck to ankles. The first mosquitoes of the season, newly hatched and hungry, found him and started their late-night snack.

The Wendigo, dear God, that was the Wendigo—the creature that roams the north, the creature that can touch you and turn you into a cannibal. That was it. The Wendigo had just passed within sixty yards of him.

He told himself not to be silly, to be like Jud and not think about what might be seen or heard beyond the Pet Sematary—they were loons, they were St. Elmo’s fire, they were the New York Yankees’ bullpen. Let them be anything but the creatures that jump and crawl and slither and stumble in the world between. Let there be God, let there be Sunday morning, let there be smiling ministers in white robes… but please, not these dark and horrible things on the dark side of the universe.

Louis walked on with his son, and the ground began to feel solid again under his feet. Just a few moments later, he came to a fallen tree, its top visible in the fading mist like a gray-green feather duster dropped by a giant's maid.

The tree was broken off—splintered off—and the break was so fresh that the yellowish-white wood still oozed sap that felt warm to Louis as he climbed over… and on the other side was a huge dent that he had to climb out of, and although juniper and low bushes had been crushed into the ground, he wouldn't let himself believe it was a footprint. He could have looked back to see if it looked like one once he was past it, but he wouldn't. He just walked on, skin cold, mouth hot and dry, heart racing.

The squelching of mud under his feet soon stopped. For a while, there was the faint rustling of pine needles again. Then there was rock. He was almost at the end.

The ground began to rise faster. He banged his shin hard on a rock sticking out. But this was not just a rock. Louis reached out awkwardly with one hand (the strap on his elbow, which had gone numb, screamed briefly) and touched it.

Steps here. Cut into the rock. Just follow me. We get to the top and we’re there.

So he began to climb and the excitement came back, once more pushing back the exhaustion… at least a little. His mind counted the steps as he rose into the cold, as he climbed back into that constant river of wind, stronger now, shaking his clothes, making the piece of canvas tarp Gage was wrapped in sound like gunshots like a lifted sail.

He tilted his head back once and saw the wild spread of the stars. There were no star patterns he knew, and he looked away again, uneasy. Beside him was the rock wall, not smooth but broken and chipped and crumbly, taking here the shape of a boat, here the shape of a badger, here the shape of a man’s face with hooded, frowning eyes. Only the steps that had been carved from the rock were smooth.

Louis reached the top and just stood there with his head down, swaying, gasping breath in and out of his lungs. They felt like badly punched bags, and there seemed to be a big splinter stuck in his side.

The wind ran through his hair like a dancer, roared in his ears like a dragon.

The light was brighter tonight; had it been cloudy the other time or had he just not been looking? It didn’t matter. But he could see, and that was enough to start another chill creeping down his back.

It was just like the Pet Sematary.

Of course you knew that, his mind whispered as he looked at the piles of rocks that had once been cairns. You knew that, or should have known it—not circles inside circles but the spiral…

Yes. Here on top of this rock table, its face turned up to cold starlight and to the black spaces between the stars, was a gigantic spiral, made by what the old people would have called Different Hands. But there were no real cairns, Louis saw; every one of them had been broken apart as something buried beneath came back to life… and clawed its way out. Yet the rocks themselves had fallen in such a way that the shape of the spiral was clear.

Has anyone ever seen this from the air? Louis wondered absently, thinking of those desert drawings some Indian tribe made in South America. Has anyone ever seen it from the air, and if they did, what did they think, I wonder?

He kneeled and set Gage’s body on the ground with a relieved groan.

Finally, he started to wake up fully. He used his pocketknife to cut the tape holding the pick and shovel across his back. They fell to the ground with a clink. Louis rolled over and lay down for a moment, spread out, staring blankly at the stars.

What was that thing in the woods? Louis, Louis, do you really think anything good can come from a play where something like that is part of it?

But now it was too late to turn back, and he knew it.

Besides, he babbled to himself, it might still be okay; you have to take risks to get something, maybe you have to risk to love. There’s still my bag, not the one downstairs but the one in our bathroom on the high shelf, the one I sent Jud for the night Norma had her heart attack. There are syringes, and if something happens… something bad… no one has to know but me.

His thoughts turned into a mumbling, droning prayer as his hands reached for the pick… and still on his knees, Louis began to dig into the earth. Each time he swung the pick down, he collapsed over it, like an old Roman falling on his sword. Yet little by little, the hole took shape and got deeper. He clawed the rocks out, and mostly he just pushed them aside along with the growing pile of stony dirt. But some of them he saved.

For the cairn.
