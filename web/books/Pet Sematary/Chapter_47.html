Pet Sematary

Chapter 47

While Jud Crandall sat in the ladder-backed rocker, watching for him from his bay window, Louis ate a large, bland dinner in the Howard Johnson’s dining room.

The food was plentiful and boring—exactly what his body seemed to crave. Outside, it had gotten dark. The headlights of passing cars searched like fingers. He shoveled the food in. A steak. A baked potato. A side of bright green beans, a color nature never intended. A slice of apple pie with a scoop of ice cream melting on top. He ate at a corner table, watching people come and go, wondering if he might see someone he knew. In a way, he hoped that would happen. It would lead to questions—where’s Rachel, what are you doing here, how’s it going?—and maybe the questions would lead to trouble, and maybe trouble was what he really wanted. A way out.

And in fact, a couple he knew came in just as he finished his apple pie and his second cup of coffee. Rob Grinnell, a doctor from Bangor, and his pretty wife Barbara. He waited for them to see him, sitting alone in the corner, but the hostess led them to the booths on the far side of the room, and Louis lost sight of them except for an occasional glimpse of Grinnell’s graying hair.

The waitress brought Louis his check. He signed it, adding his room number under his signature, and left by the side door.

Outside, the wind had risen to near-gale force. It was a steady, droning sound, making the electrical wires hum strangely. He could see no stars but sensed clouds rushing past overhead at high speed. Louis stood on the walk for a moment, hands in pockets, face tilted into the wind. Then he turned back, went up to his room, and turned on the television. It was too early to do anything serious, and the night wind was too full of possibilities. It made him nervous.

He watched four hours of TV, eight half-hour comedy programs in a row. He realized it had been a long time since he had watched so much TV without stopping. He thought that all the female leads on the sitcoms were what he and his friends had called “teases” back in high school.

In Chicago, Dory Goldman was complaining, “Fly back? Honey, why do you want to fly back? You just got here!”

In Ludlow, Jud Crandall sat by his bay window, smoking and drinking beer, motionless, looking back at his own past and waiting for Louis to come home. Sooner or later Louis would come home, like Lassie in that old movie. There were other ways up to the Pet Sematary and the place beyond, but Louis didn’t know them. If he planned to do it, he would start from his own yard.

Unaware of these other events, like slow-moving bullets aimed not at where he was, but at where he would be, Louis sat and watched the HoJo color television. He had never seen any of these programs before, but he had heard about them: a black family, a white family, a little kid who was smarter than the rich adults he lived with, a woman who was single, a woman who was married, a woman who was divorced. He watched it all, sitting in the HoJo chair and glancing out now and then at the windy night.

When the eleven o’clock news ended, he turned off the TV and went to do what he'd decided, maybe when he saw Gage’s bloody baseball cap in the road. The cold feeling was back, stronger than before, but underneath was something else—a spark of eagerness, passion, or maybe desire. It didn't matter. It warmed him against the cold and kept him steady in the wind. As he started the Honda, he thought Jud might be right about that place getting stronger, because he felt it around him now, leading (or pushing) him, and he wondered:

Could I stop? Could I stop even if I wanted to?
