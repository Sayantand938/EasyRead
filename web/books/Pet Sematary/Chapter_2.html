Pet Sematary

Chapter 2

In Louis Creed’s memory, that moment always seemed magical—partly because it might have been, but mostly because the rest of the evening was chaotic. In the next three hours, there was no peace or magic to be found.

Louis had carefully put the house keys (he was a neat and orderly man) in a small manila envelope labeled “Ludlow House—keys received June 29.” He had placed the keys in the Fairlane’s glove compartment. He was certain of it. Now they were missing.

While he searched, becoming more annoyed, Rachel lifted Gage onto her hip and followed Eileen to the tree in the field. He was checking under the seats for the third time when his daughter screamed and then cried.

“Louis!” Rachel called. “She cut herself!”

Eileen had fallen from the tire swing and hit her knee on a rock. The cut wasn't deep, but she was screaming as if she had lost a leg, Louis thought (a little unkindly). He glanced at the house across the road, where a light was on in the living room.

“Okay, Ellie,” he said. “That’s enough. The people over there will think someone is being murdered.”

“But it hurrrrts!”

Louis fought his anger and silently returned to the car. The keys were gone, but the first-aid kit was still in the glove compartment. He got it and came back. When Ellie saw it, she screamed even louder.

“No! Not the stingy stuff! I don’t want the stingy stuff, Daddy! No—”

“Eileen, it’s just Mercurochrome, and it doesn’t sting—”

“Be a big girl,” Rachel said. “It’s just—”

“No-no-no-no-no—”

“You want to stop that or your butt will sting,” Louis said.

“She’s tired, Lou,” Rachel said quietly.

“Yeah, I know the feeling. Hold her leg out.”

Rachel put Gage down and held Eileen’s leg, which Louis painted with Mercurochrome even though she wailed louder and louder.

“Someone just came out on the porch of that house across the street,” Rachel said. She picked Gage up. He had started to crawl away through the grass.

“Great,” Louis muttered.

“Lou, she’s—”

“Tired, I know.” He capped the Mercurochrome and looked sadly at his daughter. “There. And it really didn’t hurt a bit. Admit it, Ellie.

“It does! It does hurt! It hurrrr—”

He wanted to slap her, so he grabbed his leg hard.

“Did you find the keys?” Rachel asked.

“Not yet,” Louis said, snapping the first-aid kit closed and getting up. “I’ll—”

Gage began to scream. He wasn't just fussing or crying, but really screaming, squirming in Rachel’s arms.

“What’s wrong with him?” Rachel cried, pushing him almost blindly at Louis. It was, he thought, one of the good things about marrying a doctor—you could shove the kid at your husband whenever the kid seemed to be dying. “Louis! What’s—”

The baby was grabbing wildly at his neck, screaming. Louis flipped him over and saw an angry white bump rising on the side of Gage’s neck. And there was also something on the strap of his jumper, something fuzzy, squirming weakly.

Eileen, who had quieted down, began to scream again, “Bee! Bee! BEEEEEE!” She jumped back, tripped over the same rock sticking out that she had already fallen on, sat down hard, and began to cry again in pain, surprise, and fear.

I’m going crazy, Louis thought, amazed. Wheeeeee!

“Do something, Louis! Can’t you do something?”

“Got to get the stinger out,” a voice behind them said slowly. “That’s the way. Get the stinger out and put some baking soda on it. The swelling will go down.” But the voice had such a strong Down East accent that for a moment Louis’s tired, confused mind couldn’t understand the words: Got t’get the stinga out ’n put some bakin soda on’t. ’T ’l! go daown.

He turned and saw an old man of perhaps seventy—a strong and healthy seventy—standing there on the grass. He wore overalls over a blue work shirt that showed his thick, wrinkled neck. His face was sunburned, and he was smoking an unfiltered cigarette. As Louis looked at him, the old man pinched the cigarette out between his thumb and forefinger and put it in his pocket neatly. He held out his hands and smiled crookedly… a smile Louis liked right away—and he didn’t usually like people quickly.

“Not trying to tell you what to do, Doc,” he said. And that was how Louis met Judson Crandall, the man who should have been his father.
