Dark Matter

Chapter 2

I feel someone holding my ankles.

As hands slide under my shoulders, a woman says, “How’d he get out of the box?”

A man responds: “No idea. Look, he’s waking up.”

I open my eyes, but all I see is blurry movement and light.

The man barks, “Let’s get him out of here now.”

I try to speak, but the words come out slurred and without shape.

The woman says, “Dr. Dessen? Can you hear me? We’re going to lift you onto a stretcher now.”

I look toward my feet, and the man’s face comes into focus. He’s staring at me through the face shield of a silver hazmat suit with an air tank.

Looking at the woman behind my head, he says, “One, two, three.”

They lift me onto a stretcher and fasten padded straps around my ankles and wrists.

“Only for your safety, Dr. Dessen.”

I watch the ceiling move by, forty or fifty feet above.

Where am I? A hangar?

I remember something—a needle in my neck. I was injected with something. This is a crazy dream.

A radio crackles, “Extraction team, report. Over.”

The woman says, her voice filled with excitement, “We have Dessen. We’re on our way. Over.”

I hear the squeak of rolling wheels.

“Copy that. What’s his condition? Over.”

She reaches down with a gloved hand and turns on a monitoring device attached to my left arm.

"Heart rate: one-fifteen. Blood pressure: one-forty over ninety-two. Temperature: ninety-eight-point-nine. Oxygen: ninety-five percent. Gamma: point-eight seven. Arrival in thirty seconds. Over."

A buzzing sound startles me.

We move through a pair of heavy doors that are slowly opening.

Jesus Christ.

Stay calm. This isn’t real.

The wheels squeak faster, more urgently.

We’re in a corridor lined with plastic, my eyes squinting against the bright light from fluorescent bulbs overhead.

The doors behind us slam shut with a loud clang, like the gates to a fortress.

They wheel me into an operating room toward a large figure in a protective suit, standing under bright surgical lights.

He smiles down at me through his face shield and says, as if he knows me, “Welcome back, Jason. Congratulations. You did it.”

Back?

I can only see his eyes, but they don’t remind me of anyone I know.

“Are you in any pain?” he asks.

I shake my head.

“Do you know how you got the cuts and bruises on your face?”

Shake.

“Do you know who you are?”

I nod.

“Do you know where you are?”

Shake.

“Do you recognize me?”

Shake.

“I’m Leighton Vance, CEO and medical officer. We’re colleagues and friends.” He holds up surgical scissors. “I need to get you out of these clothes.”

He removes the monitor and starts on my jeans and boxers, tossing them into a metal tray. As he cuts off my shirt, I stare up at the bright lights, trying not to panic.

But I’m naked and strapped to a gurney.

No, I remind myself, I’m hallucinating that I’m naked and strapped to a gurney. Because none of this is real.

Leighton lifts the tray with my shoes and clothes and hands it to someone behind me, out of sight. “Test everything.”

Footsteps hurry out of the room.

I smell the sharp alcohol a second before Leighton cleans a patch of skin on my inner arm.

He ties a tourniquet above my elbow.

“Just drawing some blood,” he says, taking a thick needle from the tray.

He’s good. I don’t even feel the prick.

When he’s done, Leighton rolls the gurney to the other side of the OR to a glass door with a touchscreen on the wall next to it.

“Wish I could say this is the fun part,” he says. “If you’re too out of it to remember what’s about to happen, that’s probably for the best.”

I try to ask what’s happening, but I still can’t speak. Leighton taps the touchscreen. The glass door opens, and he pushes me into a room just big enough for the gurney.

“Ninety seconds,” he says. “You’ll be fine. It never killed any of the test subjects.”

There’s a hiss of air, and then the glass door slides shut.

Lights in the ceiling glow a cold blue.

I stretch my neck.

The walls on each side of me are covered with detailed openings.

A fine, very cold mist sprays from the ceiling, covering me completely.

My body tightens, the icy droplets gathering on my skin and freezing hard.

As I shiver, the chamber walls start to hum.

A white vapor leaks from the openings with a steady hiss that gets louder.

It pours out.

Then shoots out.

Opposing streams slam into each other above the gurney, filling the room with a thick fog that hides the light. Where it touches me, the frozen drops burst with sharp pain.

The fans switch direction.

In five seconds, the gas is pulled out of the chamber, which now smells strange, like the air right before a summer storm—dry lightning and ozone.

The gas and supercooled liquid on my skin have made a sizzling foam that burns like acid.

I’m grunting, thrashing against the restraints and wondering how much longer this can continue. My pain tolerance is high, and this is close to the point where I can't take it anymore.

My thoughts race.

Is there even a drug that can do this? Causing hallucinations and pain this real and horrifying?

This is too intense, too real.

What if this is really happening?

Is this some CIA thing? Am I in a secret clinic being experimented on? Have I been kidnapped by these people?

Wonderful, warm water shoots out of the ceiling like a fire hose, washing away the awful foam.

When the water stops, hot air blasts out, hitting my skin like a hot desert wind.

The pain disappears.

I’m wide-awake.

The door behind me opens and the gurney rolls back out.

Leighton looks down at me. “Wasn’t so bad, right?” He pushes me through the OR into the next patient room and unlocks the straps around my ankles and wrists.

With a gloved hand, he pulls me up on the gurney. My head swims, the room spins for a moment before the world feels normal again.

He watches me.

“Better?”

I nod.

There’s a bed and a dresser with a change of clothes folded neatly on top. The walls are padded. There are no sharp edges. As I slide to the edge of the stretcher, Leighton takes my arm above the elbow and helps me stand.

My legs feel like rubber, useless.

He leads me to the bed.

“I’ll let you get dressed and come back when your lab results are in. It won’t take long. Is it okay if I step out for a minute?”

I finally find my voice: “I don’t understand what’s happening. I don’t know where I—”

“The confusion will fade. I’ll be watching you closely. We’ll help you through this.”

He wheels the gurney to the door but stops, looking back at me through his face shield. “It’s really good to see you again, brother. Feels like Mission Control when Apollo Thirteen came back. We’re all really proud of you.”

The door closes after him.

Three deadbolts lock with three loud clicks, like gunshots.

I get out of bed and walk to the dresser, unsteady.

I’m so weak it takes me a while to get dressed—good pants, a linen shirt, no belt.

A surveillance camera watches me from above the door.

I go back to the bed, sit alone in this clean, quiet room, trying to remember my last clear memory. Just trying feels like drowning near the shore. There are pieces of memory on the beach, and I can see them, almost touch them, but my lungs are filling with water. I can’t stay afloat. The harder I try to put the pieces together, the more energy I use, the more I struggle, the more I panic.

All I have as I sit in this white, padded room is—

Thelonious Monk.

The smell of red wine.

Standing in a kitchen chopping an onion.

A teenager drawing.

Wait.

Not just a teenager.

My teenager.

My son.

Not just a kitchen.

My kitchen.

My home.

It was family night. We were cooking together. I can see Daniela’s smile. I can hear her voice and the jazz. Smell the onion, the sweet wine on Daniela’s breath. See the shine in her eyes. What a safe and perfect place, our kitchen on family night.

But I didn’t stay. For some reason, I left. Why?

I’m right there, about to remember….

The deadbolts unlock quickly, and the door to the patient room opens. Leighton has swapped the protective suit for a regular lab coat, and he’s standing in the doorway grinning, like he’s barely holding back his excitement. I can see now that he’s about my age and handsome in a preppy way, his face lightly covered with stubble.

“Good news,” he says. “You’re clear.”

“Clear of what?”

“Radiation, dangerous substances, diseases. We’ll have all the results from your blood test in the morning, but you don’t have to be isolated anymore. Oh. I have this for you.”

He hands me a Ziploc bag with keys and a money clip inside.

“Jason Dessen” is written on a piece of tape stuck to the plastic.

“Shall we? They’re all waiting for you.”

I pocket what I guess are my things and follow Leighton through the OR.

Back in the hall, some workers are pulling the plastic down from the walls.

When they see me, they start clapping.

A woman yells, “You rock, Dessen!”

Glass doors slide open as we get closer.

My strength and balance are coming back.

He leads me into a stairwell, and we go up, the metal steps loud under our feet.

“You okay on these?” Leighton asks.

“Yeah. Where are we going?”

“Debrief.”

“But I don’t even—”

“It’s better if you just keep your thoughts to yourself for the interview. You know—the rules and stuff.”

Two flights up, he opens a thick glass door. We enter another hallway with windows from floor to ceiling on one side. They look out over a hangar, which the hallways seem to surround—four levels in all—like an open courtyard.

I move toward the windows to get a better look, but Leighton guides me instead through the second door on the left, leading me into a dimly lit room, where a woman in a black pantsuit is standing behind a table as if waiting for me.

“Hi, Jason,” she says.

“Hi.”

Her eyes meet mine for a moment as Leighton straps the monitoring device around my left arm.

“You don’t mind, do you?” he asks. “I’d feel better keeping an eye on your vitals a little longer. We’ll be in the clear soon.”

Leighton gently presses his hand into my lower back and guides me the rest of the way inside.

I hear the door close behind me.

The woman is around forty. She has short, black hair with bangs that almost touch her striking eyes, which are both kind and sharp at the same time.

The lighting is soft and gentle, like a movie theater just before the movie starts.

There are two wooden chairs with straight backs, and on the small table is a laptop, a pitcher of water, two glasses, a steel container, and a steaming mug that smells like good coffee.

The walls and ceiling are made of tinted glass.

“Jason, please have a seat, and we can begin.”

I pause for five long seconds, thinking about just walking out, but something tells me that would be a bad, maybe even disastrous, idea.

So I sit in the chair, reach for the pitcher, and pour myself a glass of water.

The woman says, “If you’re hungry, we can order food.”

“No thanks.”

Finally taking her seat across from me, she pushes her glasses up on her nose and types something on the laptop.

“It is—” She checks her watch. “—12:07 a.m., October the second. I’m Amanda Lucas, employee ID number nine-five-six-seven, and I’m joined tonight by…” She gestures to me.

“Um, Jason Dessen.”

“Thank you, Jason. To give some background, and for the record, at about 10:59 p.m. on October first, Technician Chad Hodge, during a normal check of the inside area, found Dr. Dessen lying unconscious on the floor of the hangar. The rescue team was called, and Dr. Dessen was taken to quarantine at 11:24 p.m. After decontamination and initial lab clearance by Dr. Leighton Vance, Dr. Dessen was brought to the conference theater on sublevel two, where our first debriefing interview begins.”

She looks up at me, now smiling.

“Jason, we are so glad to have you back. It’s late, but most of the team came in from the city for this. As you can probably guess, they’re all watching behind the glass.”

Applause erupts all around us, with cheers and several people shouting my name.

The lights brighten just enough for me to see through the walls. Theater seats surround the glassed-in interview room. Fifteen or twenty people are standing, most smiling, a few even wiping their eyes as if I’ve returned from some heroic mission.

I notice that two of them are armed, the handles of their pistols shining under the lights.

These men aren’t smiling or clapping.

Amanda pushes her chair back and, standing up, starts clapping with the others.

She looks deeply moved too.

And all I can think is, What the heck has happened to me?

When the clapping dies down, Amanda sits back down.

She says, “Sorry for the excitement, but you’re the only one who’s come back so far.”

I have no idea what she means. Part of me wants to just say that, but part of me thinks maybe I shouldn’t.

The lights dim again.

I hold my glass of water tightly, like it’s saving me.

“Do you know how long you were gone?” she asks.

Gone where?

“No.”

“Fourteen months.”

Jesus.

“Is that a shock to you, Jason?”

“You could say that.”

“Well, we’ve been on the edge of our seats. For over a year we’ve been waiting to ask: What did you see? Where did you go? How did you get back? Tell us everything, and please start from the beginning.”

I take a sip of water, holding onto my last clear memory like a crumbling handhold on a cliff face—leaving my house on family night.

And then…

I walked down the sidewalk on a cool, autumn night. I could hear the Cubs game in all the bars.

To where?

Where was I going?

“Just take your time, Jason. We’re in no rush.”

Ryan Holder.

That’s who I was going to see.

I walked to Village Tap and had a drink—two drinks, really good Scotch, to be exact—with my old college roommate, Ryan Holder.

Is he somehow behind this?

I wonder again: Is this really happening?

I lift the glass of water. It looks completely real, even the way it sweats and feels cold and wet on my fingers.

I look into Amanda’s eyes.

I examine the walls.

They’re not dissolving.

If this is some drug trip, it’s unlike any I’ve known. No changes to sight or sound. No great happiness. It’s not that this place feels fake. I just shouldn’t be here. Somehow, my being here is the falsehood. I’m not even sure what that means, but I feel it deeply.

No, this isn’t a hallucination. It’s something different.

“Let’s try something else,” Amanda says. “What do you remember last before waking up in the hangar?”

“I was at a bar.”

“What were you doing?”

“Seeing an old friend.”

“And where was the bar?” she asks.

“Logan Square.”

“So you were still in Chicago.”

“Yeah.”

“Okay, can you describe…?”

Her voice trails off.

I see the El.

It’s dark.

It’s quiet.

Too quiet for Chicago.

Someone is coming.

Someone who wants to hurt me.

My heart starts to race.

My hands sweat.

I set the glass down on the table.

“Jason, Leighton is telling me your heart rate is going up.”

Her voice is back but still far away.

Is this a trick?

Are they messing with me?

No, don’t ask her that. Don’t say those words. Be the man they think you are. These people are cool, calm, and two of them are armed. Whatever they need to hear, say it. Because if they realize you aren’t who they think you are, then what?

Then maybe you never leave this place.

My head is starting to throb. Reaching up, I touch the back of my head and feel a knot that’s so sore it makes me wince.

“Jason?”

Was I hurt?

Did someone attack me? What if I was brought here against my will? What if these people, despite seeming nice, are working with the person who hurt me?

I touch the side of my head, feeling the pain from another hit.

“Jason.”

I see a geisha mask.

I’m naked and can’t defend myself.

“Jason.”

Just a few hours ago, I was home, making dinner.

I'm not who they think I am. What will happen when they realize that?

“Leighton, could you come down, please?”

Nothing good.

I need to not be here.

I need to get away from these people.

I need to think.

“Amanda.” I pull myself back, try to push the questions and fear away, but it’s like trying to hold back a failing dam. It won’t last. It won’t hold. “This is embarrassing,” I say. “I’m just so tired, and honestly, decontamination wasn’t fun.”

“Do you want to take a break?”

“Would that be okay? I just need a moment to clear my head.” I point at the laptop. “I also want to sound at least a little smart for this.”

“Of course.” She types something. “We’re not recording now.”

I get up.

She says, “I can take you to a private room—”

“No need.”

I open the door and step into the hall.

Leighton Vance is waiting.

“Jason, please lie down. Your heart rate and other signs aren't good.”

I rip the device off my arm and hand it to the doctor.

“I know you care, but I really need a bathroom.”

“Oh. Of course. I’ll take you.”

We head down the hall.

He pushes the heavy glass door open with his shoulder and leads me back into the stairwell, which is empty right now. The only sound is the ventilation system blowing hot air through a vent nearby. I grab the railing and lean over the open space in the middle.

Two flights down, two flights up.

What did Amanda say at the start of the interview? That we’re on sublevel two? Does that mean this is all underground?

“Jason? You coming?”

I follow Leighton, climbing, struggling with the weakness in my legs and the pain in my head.

At the top of the stairwell, a sign next to a strong steel door reads GROUND. Leighton swipes a keycard, enters a code, and holds the door open.

The words VELOCITY LABORATORIES are on the wall straight ahead in block letters.

Left: a group of elevators.

Right: a security checkpoint, with a tough-looking guard standing between the metal detector and the turnstile, and the exit just beyond.

It seems like the security here is focused on keeping people out, not in.

Leighton leads me past the elevators and down a hallway to a set of double doors at the end, which he opens with his keycard.

As we enter, he turns on the lights, showing a nice office with aviation photos of commercial airliners, military supersonic jets, and their engines on the walls.

A framed photo on the desk catches my eye—an older man holding a boy who looks a lot like Leighton. They’re standing in a hangar in front of a huge jet engine being put together.

“I thought you’d be more comfortable in my private bathroom.” Leighton points to a door in the far corner. “I’ll be right here,” he says, sitting on the edge of his desk and taking a phone out of his pocket. “Call out if you need anything.”

The bathroom is cold and spotless.

There’s a toilet, a urinal, a walk-in shower, and a small window halfway up the back wall.

I sit down on the toilet.

My chest feels so tight I can hardly breathe.

They’ve been waiting for me to come back for fourteen months. There’s no way they’re letting me leave this building. Not tonight. Maybe not for a long time, since I’m not the man they think they’re talking to.

Unless this is all some kind of test or game.

Leighton’s voice comes through the door: “Everything okay in there?”

“Yeah.”

“I don’t know what you saw in there, but I want you to know I’m here for you, brother. If you’re scared, you have to tell me, so I can help.”

I rise.

He continues, “I was watching you from the theater, and I have to say, you looked lost.”

If I walked back into the lobby with him, could I break away, run through security? I picture that huge guard by the metal detector. Probably not.

“Physically, you’ll be fine, but I’m worried about how you're doing mentally.”

I have to step on the edge of the porcelain urinal to reach the window. The glass seems to be locked with a lever on each side.

It’s only two feet by two feet, and I don’t know if I can fit through.

Leighton’s voice echoes through the bathroom, and as I creep back toward the sink, his words become clear again.

“…worst thing you can do is try to handle this alone. Let’s be honest. You’re the kind of guy who thinks he can get through anything.”

I approach the door.

There’s a deadbolt.

With trembling fingers, I slowly turn the lock.

“But no matter what you’re feeling,” his voice close now, inches away, “I want you to share it with me, and if we need to move this meeting to tomorrow or later—”

He stops talking as the bolt slides into place with a soft click.

For a moment, nothing happens.

I take a careful step back.

The door moves slightly, and then shakes wildly in its frame.

Leighton says, “Jason. Jason!” And then: “I need a security team to my office right now. Dessen has locked himself in the bathroom.”

The door shakes as Leighton slams into it, but the lock holds.

I rush to the window, climb onto the urinal, and flip the latches on the sides of the glass.

Leighton is shouting at someone, and though I can’t hear what’s being said, I think I hear footsteps coming closer.

The window opens.

Night air rushes in.

Even on top of the urinal, I’m not sure I can reach it.

I jump off and throw myself toward the open window, but only get one arm through.

Something slams into the bathroom door as my shoes slide on the smooth, straight wall. I can’t get any grip.

I fall to the floor and climb back onto the urinal.

Leighton screams at someone, “Come on!”

I jump again, and this time I get both arms over the windowsill. It’s not a strong hold, but enough to keep me from falling.

I squeeze through as the bathroom door breaks open behind me.

Leighton shouts my name.

I fall for a split second through darkness.

I slam face-first into the ground.

I'm up, confused, dizzy, ears ringing, blood running down my face.

I’m outside, in a dark alley between buildings.

Leighton shows up in the window above me.

“Jason, don’t do this. Let me help.”

I turn and run, not knowing where I’m going, just running toward the alley’s end.

I get there.

I jump down some brick steps.

I’m in an office park.

Plain, short buildings huddle around a small, sad pond with a fountain lit up in the center.

It’s late, so it's no surprise there’s no one outside.

I rush past benches, neat bushes, a gazebo, a sign pointing to the WALKING PATH.

I glance back: the building I just left is a plain, five-story box, utterly forgettable, and people are pouring out like an upset hornet’s nest.

At the pond’s edge, I leave the sidewalk for a gravel path.

Sweat stings my eyes, my lungs burn, but I keep pumping my arms, pushing myself forward.

Each step takes me farther from the office park lights.

Ahead, there’s only inviting darkness, and I’m running toward it, into it, like my life depends on it.

A strong, fresh wind hits my face, and I start to wonder where I’m going, because shouldn’t there be some light in the distance? Even a tiny bit? But I’m running into a huge pit of black.

I hear waves.

I arrive on a beach.

There's no moon, but the stars shine bright enough to hint at Lake Michigan's restless surface.

I look inland toward the office park, catch bits of wind-blown voices, and see a few flashlight beams cutting through the dark.

Turning north, I start to run, my shoes crunching on smooth, wave-worn rocks. Far up the shore, I see the faint, nighttime glow of downtown, where the skyscrapers meet the water.

I look back, see some lights moving south, away from me, others heading north.

Getting closer.

I turn away from the water, cross a bike path, and head for a line of bushes.

The voices are nearer.

I wonder if it's dark enough to hide me.

A three-foot seawall blocks me, and I climb over the concrete, scraping my shins and crawling through the hedge, branches catching my shirt and face, scratching at my eyes.

I burst from the bushes into the middle of a road that runs beside the lake.

From the office park, I hear an engine speeding up.

Bright headlights blind me.

I cross the road, jump a chain-link fence, and suddenly I’m running through someone’s yard, avoiding bikes and skateboards, then running beside the house while a dog barks like crazy inside, lights turning on as I reach the backyard, jump the fence again, and find myself sprinting across an empty baseball field, wondering how much longer I can keep going.

The answer comes quickly.

At the edge of the infield, I fall, sweat pouring off me, every muscle aching.

The dog is still barking far away, but looking back toward the lake, I see no lights, hear no voices.

I lie there for a long time, and it feels like hours before I can breathe normally.

I finally sit up.

The night is cool, and the breeze from the lake blows through the trees, raining autumn leaves onto the baseball field.

I get to my feet, thirsty and tired, trying to understand what has happened in the last four hours, but I can’t focus right now.

I walk out of the baseball field and into a working-class neighborhood on the South Side.

The streets are empty.

It’s just blocks of quiet, peaceful houses.

I walk a mile, maybe more, and then I’m at the empty intersection of a business area, watching the traffic lights change quickly above me in the late night.

The main street is two blocks long, and the only sign of life is the dive bar across the street, with three cheap beer signs glowing in the windows. People stumble out in a cloud of smoke and loud talk as headlights from the first car I’ve seen in twenty minutes appear in the distance.

A cab with the Off-Duty light on.

I walk into the intersection and stand under the traffic light, waving my arms. The taxi slows down and tries to drive around me, but I step aside, keeping its bumper on a collision course, making it stop.

The driver rolls down his window, angry.

“What the hell are you doing?”

“I need a ride.”

The cabbie is Somali, his thin face dotted with bits of a beard, and he’s staring at me through huge, thick glasses.

He says, “It’s two in the morning. I’m done for tonight. No more work.”

“Please.”

“Can you read? Look at the sign.” He hits the top of his car.

“I need to get home.”

The window starts to go up.

I reach into my pocket and pull out the plastic bag with my things, tear it open, show him the money clip.

“I can pay you more than—”

“Get out of the road.”

“I’ll pay you twice as much.”

The window stops six inches from the top of the door.

“Cash.”

“Cash.”

I quickly flip through the wad of bills. It’s probably a $75 ride to the North Side, and I have to pay him double that.

“Get in if we go!” he yells.

Some people from the bar have seen the cab stopped in the intersection, and guessing they need rides, they’re coming over, shouting for me to wait.

I finish counting my money—$332 and three expired credit cards.

I climb into the backseat and tell him I’m going to Logan Square.

“That’s twenty-five miles!”

“And I’m paying you double.”

He glares at me in the rearview mirror.

“Where’s the money?”

I peel off $100 and hand it into the front seat. “The rest when we get there.”

He snatches the money and speeds through the intersection, past the drunks.

I look at the money clip. Under the cash and credit cards, there’s an Illinois driver’s license with a photo of me I don’t recognize, a gym ID for a place I’ve never been, and a health insurance card from a company I’ve never used.

The cabbie steals glances at me in the rearview mirror.

“You have bad night,” he says.

“Looks that way, huh?”

“I thought you were drunk, but no. Your clothes are ripped. Your face is bloody.”

I probably wouldn’t have wanted to pick myself up either, standing in the middle of an intersection at two in the morning, looking homeless and crazy.

“You’re in trouble,” he says.

“Yeah.”

“What happened?”

“I’m not really sure.”

“I’ll take you to a hospital.”

“No. I want to go home.”
