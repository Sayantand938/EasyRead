Dark Matter

Chapter 15

We’re sitting in our bullet-ridden Suburban in the empty parking lot.

I drove all night.

I look at my face in the mirror. My left eye is purple, very swollen, and the skin on my left cheek is black from the blood underneath.

It all hurts a lot when I touch it.

I look back at Charlie, and then at Daniela.

Reaching across the center console, she runs her fingernails down the back of my neck.

She says, “What else can we do?”

“Charlie? You need to decide too.”

“I don’t want to leave.”

“I know.”

“But I guess we have to.”

A strange thought crosses my mind like a quick summer cloud.

We’re so clearly at the end. Everything we’ve built—our house, our jobs, our friends, our life together—it’s all gone. We have nothing left but each other, and yet, right now, I’m happier than ever.

—

Morning sun shines through cracks in the roof, lighting spots along the dark, empty hallway.

“This place is cool,” Charlie says.

“Do you know where you’re going?” Daniela asks.

“Sadly, I could take us where we need to go blindfolded.”

As I lead us through the abandoned paths, I’m beyond tired. Running on caffeine and fear. The gun I took from the cabin is stuck in the back of my pants, and Jason2’s leather bag is tucked under my arm. It hits me that as we drove down to the South Side at dawn, I never even looked at the skyline as we passed just west of downtown.

One last look would’ve been nice.

I feel a pang of regret, but quickly dismiss it.

I think of all the nights I lay in bed, wondering how things might be if I’d made different choices, if I hadn’t become a father and a so-so physics professor instead of a star in my field. I guess it’s just wanting what I didn’t get. What I thought could have been mine if I’d chosen differently.

But the truth is, I did make those different choices.

Because I am not just me.

My idea of who I am has been broken—I am just one part of an endless being called Jason Dessen who has made every choice and lived every life possible.

I can't help but think we're more than just our choices, that all the paths we could have taken somehow add to who we are.

But none of the other Jasons matter.

I don’t want their lives.

I want mine.

Because even though everything is messed up, I wouldn’t want to be anywhere else but with this Daniela, this Charlie. If anything was even a little different, they wouldn’t be the people I love.

We move slowly down the stairs toward the generator room, our steps echoing in the big, open space.

One flight up from the bottom, Daniela says, “Someone’s down there.”

I stop.

My mouth feels dry as I look into the darkness below.

I see a man stand up from where he was sitting on the floor.

Then another next to him.

And another.

All through the darkness between the last generator and the box, versions of me are getting to their feet.

Fuck.

They came early for the lottery.

Dozens of them.

All watching us.

I look back up the stairs, my ears ringing so loudly it drowns everything out in a flood of panic.

Daniela says, “We’re not running.” She pulls the gun from my pants and loops her arm through mine. “Charlie, grab your father’s arm and don’t let go, no matter what.”

“You sure?” I ask.

“Completely.”

With Charlie and Daniela holding onto me, I slowly walk down the last few steps and move across the broken concrete.

My doubles stand between us and the box.

I can’t breathe.

Only the sound of our steps and the wind through the empty window frames above.

I hear Daniela take a shaky breath.

Charlie’s hand is sweaty in mine.

“Just keep walking,” I say.

One of them moves ahead.

He says to me, “This isn’t what you said would happen.”

I say, “Things changed. Some of us tried to kill me last night, and—”

Daniela cuts in, “One of you shot at our car with Charlie in it. It’s over. Done.”

She pulls me forward.

We’re getting closer to them.

They’re not moving out of our way.

Someone says, “You’re here now. Let’s have that lottery.”

Daniela squeezes my arm even tighter.

She says, “Charlie and I are going into the box with this man.” Her voice breaks. “If there were another way…We’re all just doing the best we can.”

I can’t avoid it—I make eye contact with the closest Jason, his envy a living thing. In ragged clothes, he reeks of homelessness and despair.

He says to me in a low growl, “Why should you get her?”

The Jason next to him says, “This isn’t about him. It’s about what she wants. What our son needs. That’s all that matters. Let them pass. All of you.”

The crowd begins to move apart.

We move slowly through the path of Jasons.

Some are crying.

Hot, angry, desperate tears.

I am too.

So is Daniela.

So is Charlie.

Others stand still and tense.

Finally, the last one moves aside.

The box looms right in front.

The door wide open.

Charlie enters first, then Daniela.

I keep waiting for something to happen as my heart hammers in my chest.

At this point, I expect anything.

I step through the doorway, touch the door, and see my world one last time.

It’s a picture I will always remember.

Light from the high windows shines on the old generators as fifty of me all stare at the box in a shocked and strange and heartbroken silence.

—

The door locks.

The bolt slides into place.

I turn on the flashlight and look at my family.

For a second, Daniela looks like she’s going to fall apart, but she stays strong.

I take out the syringes, the needles, the vials.

Set everything up.

Just like old times.

I help Charlie push his sleeve up.

“First time’s a little intense. You ready?”

He nods.

Holding his arm still, I push the needle into the vein, pull back on the plunger, see blood mix in the syringe.

As I shoot all of Ryan’s drug into my son’s blood, Charlie’s eyes roll back and he slumps against the wall.

I tie the band around my arm.

“How long does it last?” Daniela asks.

“About an hour.”

Charlie sits up.

“You okay?” I ask.

“That was weird.”

I inject myself. It’s been a few days since I last used, and the drug hits me harder than usual.

When I feel better, I pick up the last syringe.

“Your turn, my love.”

“I hate needles.”

“Don’t worry. I’m pretty good at this now.”

Soon we’re all feeling the drug.

Daniela takes the light from me and moves away from the door.

As it lights up the hallway, I watch her face. I watch my son’s face. They look afraid. Amazed. I think back to when I first saw the hallway, to the horror and wonder that took over me.

The feeling of being nowhere.

And in between.

“How far does it go?” Charlie asks.

“It never ends.”

—

We walk together down this hallway that stretches forever.

I still can’t believe I’m here again.

That I’m here with them.

I’m not sure what I’m feeling, but it isn’t the pure fear I felt before.

Charlie says, “So each of these doors…”

“Opens into another world.”

“Wow.”

I look at Daniela, ask, “You okay?”

“Yes. I’m with you.”

We’ve been walking for a while, and we don't have much time left.

I say, “The drug will stop working soon. We should probably go.”

And so we stop in front of a door that looks just like the others.

Daniela says, “I was thinking—all these other Jasons made it back to their world. What if one of them comes into wherever we go? They should all think like you, right?”

“Yeah, but I’m not going to open a door, and neither are you.”

I turn to Charlie.

He says, “Me? What if I mess up? What if I take us to some terrible place?”

“I trust you.”

“I do too,” Daniela says.

I say, “Even though you’ll be opening the door, we’re making the way to this next world together. The three of us.” Charlie looks at the door, tense. “Look,” I say, “I’ve tried to explain how the box works, but forget all that for a minute. The box is like life. If you go in with fear, you’ll find fear.”

“But I don’t even know where to start,” he says.

“It’s a blank canvas.”

I embrace my son.

I tell him I love him.

Tell him I’m so proud.

Then Daniela and I sit on the floor, our backs against the wall, facing Charlie and the door. She rests her head on my shoulder and holds my hand.

Driving here last night, I thought I’d be terrified to enter a new world, but I’m not scared at all.

I’m filled with a child's excitement to see what happens next.

As long as my people are with me, I’m ready for anything.

Charlie walks to the door and grabs the handle.

Just before he opens it, he takes a breath and looks back at us, as brave and strong as I’ve ever seen him.

A man.

I nod.

He turns the handle, and I hear the lock click open.

A line of light cuts into the corridor, so bright I have to shield my eyes for a moment. When I can see again, Charlie is standing in the doorway of the box, lit from behind.

Getting up, I help Daniela to stand, and we go to our son as the cold, empty hall fills with warmth and light.

A wind from the door brings the smell of wet ground and strange flowers.

A world right after a storm.

I put my hand on Charlie’s shoulder.

“Ready?” he asks.

“We’re right behind you.”
