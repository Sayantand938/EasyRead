In the study of beauty, there’s an idea called the uncanny valley. It says that when something looks almost human—like a dummy or a robot that seems human—it makes people feel disgusted. This is because it looks so much like a person, but it’s just different enough to make you feel uneasy, like something is both known and foreign.

I feel something similar as I walk around this Chicago that’s almost my own. I’d rather have a nightmare about the end of the world. Broken buildings and empty lands are nothing compared to standing on a corner I know well and seeing that the street names are wrong. Or the coffee shop where I always get my strong coffee with soy milk is now a fancy wine store. Or my house at 44 Eleanor Street is a brownstone where strangers live.

—

This is the fourth Chicago we’ve found since leaving that world of sickness and death. Each one has been like this—almost home.

Night is coming, and because we’ve taken four doses of the drug quickly without stopping to recover, we decide not to go back to the box for the first time.

It’s the same hotel in Logan Square where I stayed in Amanda’s world.

The neon sign is red instead of green, but the name is the same—HOTEL ROYALE—and it’s just as odd, just as stuck in the past, but in many small, different ways.

Our room has two double beds, and like the last room I had here, it faces the street.

I put our plastic bags with toiletries and used clothes on the dresser next to the TV.

Normally, I might have hated this old room that smells like cleaning spray can’t hide the mold and worse.

Tonight it feels great.

Taking off my hoodie and undershirt, I say, “I’m too dirty to care about this place.”

I throw them in the trash can.

Amanda laughs. “You don’t want to see who’s nastier, me or you.”

“I’m surprised they gave us a room at all.”

“That should tell you how good this place is.”

I go to the window and open the curtains.

It’s early evening.

Raining.

The hotel sign outside spills red neon light into the room.

I couldn’t guess the day or date if I tried.

I say, “The bathroom’s all yours.”

Amanda grabs her things from the plastic bag.

Soon, I hear the clear sound of running water bouncing off the tile.

She calls out, “Oh my God, you have to take a bath, Jason! You have no idea!”

I’m too dirty to lie down on the bed, so I sit on the carpet next to the radiator, letting waves of heat wash over me and watching the sky darken through the window.

—

I take Amanda’s advice and draw a bath.

Water trickles down the walls.

The heat really helps my sore back, which has been hurting for days from sleeping in the box.

As I shave, I keep wondering who I really am.

There’s no Jason Dessen working as a physics professor at Lakemont College or any nearby schools, but I still wonder if I'm out there.

In another city.

Another country.

Maybe living under a different name, with a different woman, a different job.

If I am, if I spend my days fixing cars or filling teeth instead of teaching physics, am I still the same person deep down?

And what is that deep down part?

If you take away everything about my personality and life, what's left that makes me who I am?

“You look good too.”

We walk for twenty minutes in the rain because I want to see if one of my favorite restaurants is really here.

It is, and it feels like seeing a friend while traveling.

This comfy, trendy place is inspired by an old Chicago neighborhood bar.

There’s a long wait for a table, so we watch the bar until two stools are free, and we sit at the end near a wet window.

We order cocktails.

Then wine.

Lots of small plates that keep arriving.

The drinks give us a nice buzz, and we only talk about what’s happening now.

How the food tastes.

How good it feels to be inside and warm.

Amanda says I look like a lumberjack.

I tell her she looks like a biker chick.

We both laugh too much, too loud, but we need it.

As she gets up to go to the bathroom, she says, “You’ll be right here?”

“I will not move from this spot.”

But she keeps looking back.

I watch her walk down the bar and disappear around the corner.

Alone, the normalness of the moment is almost too much. I look around the restaurant, taking in the faces of the waiters, the customers. Two dozen loud talks mixing into a kind of meaningless noise.

I think, What if you knew what I know?

The walk back is colder and wetter.

Near the hotel, I see the sign for my bar, Village Tap, flashing across the street.

I say, “Want a nightcap?”

It’s late, so most of the evening crowd is gone.

We get seats at the bar, and I watch the bartender finish someone’s bill on the touchscreen.

He turns and comes over, looks at Amanda first, then me.

Just blank, disinterested politeness.

“This is my regular bar.”

“Matt.”

“Yep.”

He doesn’t seem to care at all what I think of his bar, and it makes me feel sad inside. Amanda notices. When Matt leaves us, she raises her wineglass and taps it against my beer.

Says, “To a good meal, a warm bed, and still being alive.”

Back in the hotel room, we turn off the lights and undress in the dark. I know I can’t judge our room fairly anymore, because the bed feels amazing.

Amanda asks from her side of the room, “You locked the door?”

“I did.”

I close my eyes. I can hear the rain tapping against the window. Now and then a car goes by on the wet street below.

“It was a nice night,” Amanda says.

“It was. I don’t miss the box, but it feels odd being away from it.”

“I don’t know about you, but my old life feels more and more like a ghost. You know how a dream feels as you wake up? It loses its color and strength and sense. You feel less and less connected to it.”

I ask, “Do you think you’d ever forget it completely? Your world?”

“I don’t know. I think it could get to the point where it didn’t feel real anymore. Because it isn’t. The only thing that’s real right now is this city. This room. This bed. You and me.”

—

In the middle of the night, I realize Amanda is next to me.

It’s not exactly new. We’ve slept like this in the box many times. Holding each other in darkness, as lost as two people could be.

The only difference now is that we’re only in our underwear, and her skin is so soft against mine it's hard to ignore.

Flashes of neon light peek through the curtains.

Reaching over in the dark, she grabs my hand and puts it on her.

Then she turns, faces me.

“You’re better than he ever was.”

“Who?”

“The Jason I knew.”

“I hope so. Jesus.” I smile to show it's a joke. She just stares at me with those dark eyes. We’ve looked at each other a lot lately, but there’s something different in her eyes now.

There’s a connection here, and it’s getting stronger every day.

If I moved even a tiny bit closer to her, we would do this.

I was sure of it.

And if I kissed her, if we slept together, maybe I’d feel guilty and regret it, or maybe I’d realize she could make me happy.

A version of me definitely kissed her right then.

A version knows the answer.

But it won’t be me.

She says, “If you want me to go back over there, just tell me.”

I say, “I don’t want you to, but I have to ask you to.”
