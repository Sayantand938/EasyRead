Dark Matter

Chapter 1

I love Thursday nights.

They feel timeless.

It’s our tradition, just the three of us—family night.

My son, Charlie, is at the table, drawing. He’s almost fifteen. He grew two inches over the summer and is as tall as me now.

I stop cutting the onion and ask, “Can I see?”

He holds up the pad, showing me mountains that look like they're from another planet.

I say, “Love it. Just for fun?”

“Class project. Due tomorrow.”

“Then get back to it, Mr. Last Minute.”

Standing happy and a little drunk in my kitchen, I don’t know that tonight it all ends. The end of everything I know, everything I love.

No one tells you it’s all about to change, to be taken away. There’s no warning, no hint that you’re standing on the edge. And maybe that’s what makes tragedy so sad. Not just what happens, but how it happens: a surprise attack that comes out of nowhere, when you’re not expecting it. No time to react or prepare.

The lights shine on my wine, and the onion is starting to sting my eyes. Thelonious Monk plays on the old record player in the den. I love the full sound of the old recording, especially the static between songs. The den is full of piles of rare records that I keep meaning to organize one day.

My wife, Daniela, sits on the kitchen island, swirling her almost-empty wineglass in one hand and holding her phone in the other. She feels me looking at her and smiles without looking up from the screen.

“I know,” she says. “I’m breaking the most important rule of family night.”

“What’s so important?” I ask.

She looks up at me with her dark eyes. “Nothing.”

I walk over to her, gently take the phone from her hand, and put it on the counter.

“You could start the pasta,” I say.

“I like watching you cook better.”

“Yeah?” Quieter: “Turns you on, huh?”

“No, it’s just more fun to drink and do nothing.”

Her breath is wine-sweet, and she has one of those smiles that seem impossible. It still amazes me.

I finish my glass. “We should open more wine, right?”

“It would be stupid not to.”

As I pull the cork from a new bottle, she picks her phone back up and shows me the screen. “I was reading Chicago Magazine’s review of Marsha Altman’s show.”

“Were they nice?”

“Yeah, it’s basically a love letter.”

“Good for her.”

“I always thought…” She stops talking, but I know what she was going to say. Fifteen years ago, before we met, Daniela was up-and-coming in Chicago’s art scene. She had a studio in Bucktown, showed her work in several galleries, and had just scheduled her first solo show in New York. Then came life. Me. Charlie. A bad case of postpartum depression.

Derailment.

Now she teaches private art lessons to middle schoolers.

“It’s not that I’m not happy for her. I mean, she’s brilliant, she deserves it all.”

I say, “If it makes you feel any better, Ryan Holder just won the Pavia Prize.”

“What’s that?”

“An award for different fields given for achievements in science. Ryan won for his work studying the brain.”

“Is it a big deal?”

“Million dollars. Praise. Makes it easier to get grant money.”

“Hotter TAs?”

“Obviously that’s the real prize. He invited me to a small, casual party tonight, but I didn’t go.”

“Why?”

“Because it’s our night.”

“You should go.”

“I’d rather not.”

Daniela lifts her empty glass. “So, we both have a good reason to drink a lot of wine tonight.”

I kiss her, and then pour a lot from the new bottle.

“You could’ve won that prize,” Daniela says.

“You could’ve run this city’s art world.”

“But we did this.” She points to our brownstone with its high ceilings. I bought it before Daniela with money I inherited. “And we did that,” she says, pointing to Charlie as he sketches with an intensity that reminds me of Daniela when she’s deep in a painting.

It’s strange to be the parent of a teenager. Raising a little boy is one thing, but it’s different when someone about to be an adult looks to you for wisdom. I feel like I have little to offer. I know some fathers see the world clearly and confidently, and know what to say to their sons and daughters. But I’m not one of them. The older I get, the less I understand. I love my son. He means everything to me. Yet, I can’t shake the feeling that I’m failing him. Sending him out into the world with only my uncertain views.

I go to the cabinet by the sink, open it, and start looking for a box of fettuccine.

Daniela turns to Charlie, says, “Your father could have won the Nobel Prize.”

I laugh. “That’s probably an exaggeration.”

“Charlie, don’t be fooled. He’s a genius.”

“You’re sweet,” I say. “And a little drunk.”

“It’s true, and you know it. Science is behind because you love your family.”

I can only smile. When Daniela drinks, three things happen: her accent comes out, she becomes aggressively kind, and she exaggerates things.

“Your father said to me one night—I’ll never forget it—that research takes over your life. He said…” For a moment, and surprisingly, she gets emotional. Her eyes water, and she shakes her head like she always does when she’s about to cry. At the last second, she recovers, pushes through. “He said, ‘Daniela, when I die, I’d rather have memories of you than of a cold, empty lab.’ ”

I look at Charlie, see him rolling his eyes as he sketches.

He’s probably embarrassed by our dramatic display.

I stare into the cabinet and wait for the pain in my throat to ease.

When it does, I grab the pasta and close the door.

Daniela drinks her wine.

Charlie draws.

The moment passes.

“Where’s Ryan’s party?” Daniela asks.

“Village Tap.”

“That’s your bar, Jason.”

“So?”

She comes over, takes the box of pasta out of my hand.

“Go grab a drink with your old college friend. Tell him you’re proud of him. Be confident. Tell him I said congrats.”

“I will not tell him you said congrats.”

“Why?”

“He likes you.”

“Stop.”

“It’s true. From a long time ago. Back when we were roommates. Remember the last Christmas party? He kept trying to get you to stand under the mistletoe with him?”

She just laughs, says, “Dinner will be ready when you get home.”

“Which means I should be back here in…”

“Forty-five minutes.”

“What would I do without you?”

She kisses me.

“Let’s not even think about it.”

I grab my keys and wallet from the ceramic dish by the microwave and go into the dining room, looking up at the geometric chandelier above the table. Daniela gave it to me for our tenth anniversary. Best gift ever.

As I reach the front door, Daniela shouts, “Come back with ice cream!”

“Mint chocolate chip!” Charlie says.

I lift my arm, give a thumbs-up.

I don’t look back.

I don’t say goodbye.

And this moment passes without notice.

The end of everything I know, everything I love.

I’ve lived in Logan Square for twenty years, and it doesn’t get any better than the first week of October. It always reminds me of that F. Scott Fitzgerald line: Life starts all over again when it gets crisp in the fall.

The evening is cool, and the skies are clear enough to see a few stars. The bars are more lively than usual, packed with disappointed Cubs fans.

I stop on the sidewalk in the glow of a flashy sign that blinks VILLAGE TAP and stare through the open doorway of the ever-present corner bar you’ll find in any Chicago neighborhood that values itself. This one happens to be my regular spot. It’s the closest to home—a few blocks from my brownstone.

I pass through the glow of the blue neon sign in the front window and step through the doorway.

Matt, the bartender and owner, nods to me as I move down the bar, making my way through the crowd that surrounds Ryan Holder.

I say to Ryan, “I was just telling Daniela about you.”

He smiles, looking perfectly put-together for giving talks—fit and tan in a black turtleneck, his beard and mustache carefully trimmed.

“Damn, it’s good to see you. I’m touched that you came. Darling?” He touches the bare shoulder of the young woman sitting next to him. “Would you mind letting my dear old friend have your chair for a minute?”

The woman does as she’s asked, and I climb onto the stool next to Ryan.

He calls the bartender over. “Give us two of your most expensive drinks.”

“Ryan, you don’t have to do that.”

He grabs my arm. “Tonight, we drink the best.”

Matt says, “I have Macallan Twenty-Five.”

“Make them doubles. Put it on my tab.”

As the bartender leaves, Ryan punches my arm. Hard. You wouldn’t think he was a scientist at first. He played lacrosse in college and still has the build and easy movements of an athlete.

“How are Charlie and Daniela?”

“They’re doing great.”

“You should have brought her. I haven’t seen her since Christmas.”

“She says congratulations.”

“You’ve got a good woman there, but that’s no surprise.”

“Think you’ll settle down anytime soon?”

“Not likely. I like being single, and all the good things that come with it. You still at Lakemont College?”

“Yeah.”

“Good school. Physics for undergrads, right?”

“Exactly.”

“So you’re teaching…”

“Quantum mechanics. Basic stuff, mostly. Nothing too exciting.”

Matt comes back with our drinks, and Ryan takes them and puts mine in front of me.

“So, this celebration…,” I say.

“Just a little something my grad students put together. They love getting me drunk and listening to me talk.”

“Big year for you, Ryan. I still remember you almost failing differential equations.”

“And you saved me. More than once.”

For a second, behind the confidence, I see the goofy grad student I lived with in a gross apartment for a year and a half.

I ask, “Was the Pavia Prize for your work in—”

“Figuring out that the prefrontal cortex creates consciousness.”

“Right. Of course. I read your paper.”

“What’d you think?”

“Amazing.”

He looks happy to hear that.

“If I’m honest, Jason, and I’m not just being humble, I always thought you’d be the one to publish the important papers.”

“Really?”

He looks at me over his black plastic glasses.

“Of course. You’re smarter than me. Everyone knew it.”

I drink my whisky. I try not to think about how good it tastes.

He says, “Just a question, but do you think of yourself more as a researcher or a teacher these days?”

“I—”

“Because I see myself mainly as someone looking for answers to basic questions. Now, if the people around me”—he points to his students who are starting to gather—“are smart enough to learn just by being near me…that’s fine. But teaching, as such, doesn’t interest me. All that matters is the science. The research.”

I see a hint of annoyance, or anger, in his voice, and it’s growing, like he’s getting himself ready for something.

I try to laugh it off. “Are you upset with me, Ryan? It almost sounds like you think I disappointed you.”

“Look, I’ve taught at MIT, Harvard, Johns Hopkins, the best schools in the world. I’ve met the smartest people around, and Jason, you could've changed the world if you’d chosen that road. If you’d stayed with it. Instead, you’re teaching college physics to future doctors and lawyers.”

“We can’t all be superstars like you, Ryan.”

“Not if you quit.”

I finish my whisky.

“Well, I’m glad I stopped by for this.” I get off the barstool.

“Don’t be like that, Jason. I was giving you a compliment.”

“I’m proud of you, man. I mean it.”

“Jason.”

“Thanks for the drink.”

Back outside, I walk fast down the sidewalk. The farther I get from Ryan, the angrier I become.

And I’m not even sure who I'm mad at.

My face is hot.

Sweat runs down my sides.

Without thinking, I step into the street against the light and immediately hear tires screech and rubber squealing.

I turn and stare in disbelief as a yellow cab speeds toward me.

Through the windshield, I see the cabbie clearly—a mustached man, wide-eyed and terrified, bracing for impact.

Then my hands are flat against the warm, yellow metal of the hood, and the cabbie is leaning out his window, yelling at me, “You dipshit, you almost died! Get your head out of your ass!”

Horns start honking behind the cab.

I go back to the sidewalk and watch the traffic start moving again.

The people in three different cars slow down just long enough to flip me off.

Whole Foods smells like the hippie I dated before Daniela—a mix of fresh fruits and vegetables, coffee, and essential oils.

The near miss with the cab has ruined my good mood, and I look through the freezers in a daze, feeling tired and slow.

It feels colder when I’m outside again, a sharp wind coming off the lake, hinting at the awful winter coming soon.

With my canvas bag full of ice cream, I walk home a different way. It’s six blocks longer, but I get some alone time, and after the cab and Ryan, I need to calm down.

I walk past a construction site, empty for the night, and a few blocks later, the playground of the elementary school my son went to, the metal slide shining under a streetlamp and the swings moving in the wind.

There’s something about these autumn nights that stirs something deep inside me. Something from long ago. From when I was a kid in western Iowa. I think of high school football games and the bright stadium lights shining on the players. I smell ripe apples, and the stinky smell of beer from parties in the cornfields. I feel the wind on my face as I ride in the back of an old pickup truck down a country road at night, red dust flying in the taillights and my whole life stretching out in front of me.

That’s the wonderful thing about being young.

There’s a feeling of freedom in everything because no serious mistakes have been made, no choices set in stone, and the future is full of endless possibilities.

I love my life, but I haven’t felt that carefree in a long time. Autumn nights like this are the closest I get.

The cold is starting to clear my head.

It will be good to be home again. I’m thinking of turning on the gas logs. We’ve never had a fire before Halloween, but tonight is so unusually cold that after walking a mile in this wind, all I want is to sit by the fireplace with Daniela and Charlie and a glass of wine.

The street runs under the El.

I pass under the rusting iron of the railway.

For me, even more than the skyline, the El represents the city.

This is my favorite part of the walk home, because it’s the darkest and quietest.

Right now…

No trains coming in.

No headlights either way.

No sound from the bars.

Only the distant roar of a jet overhead, landing at O’Hare.

Wait…

Something’s coming—footsteps on the sidewalk.

I look back.

A shadow runs toward me, getting closer faster than I can understand.

The first thing I see is a face.

Ghost white.

High, arched eyebrows that seem painted on.

Red, tight lips—too thin, too perfect.

And scary eyes—big and black, without pupils or color.

The second thing I see is the barrel of a gun, inches from my nose.

The low, rough voice behind the geisha mask says, “Turn around.”

I hesitate, too shocked to move.

He shoves the gun into my face.

I turn around.

Before I can tell him my wallet's in my pocket, he says, “I’m not here for your money. Start walking.”

I start walking.

“Faster.”

I walk faster.

“What do you want?” I ask.

“Keep your mouth shut.”

A train roars overhead, and we come out of the dark under the El, my heart pounding. I look around, suddenly very interested in everything. Across the street is a gated group of townhouses, and this side of the block has a few stores that close at five.

A nail salon.

A law office.

An appliance repair shop.

A tire store.

This neighborhood is empty, nobody around.

“See that SUV?” he asks. There’s a black Lincoln Navigator parked on the street up ahead. The alarm beeps. “Get in the driver’s seat.”

“Whatever you’re planning to do—”

“Or you can bleed to death right here on the sidewalk.”

I open the driver’s-side door and slide behind the wheel.

“My grocery bag,” I say.

“Bring it.” He gets in behind me. “Start the car.”

I shut the door and put the canvas Whole Foods bag on the front passenger floor. It’s so quiet in the car I can hear my pulse—a fast beat against my eardrum.

“What are you waiting for?” he asks.

I press the start button.

“Turn on the navigation.”

I turn it on.

“Click ‘previous destinations.’ ”

I’ve never had a car with built-in GPS, and it takes me a second to find the right spot on the touchscreen.

Three locations show up.

One is my home. One is the university where I work.

“You’ve been following me?” I ask.

“Choose Pulaski Drive.”

I pick 1400 Pulaski Drive, Chicago, Illinois 60616, not even knowing where it is. The female voice on the GPS tells me: Make a legal U-turn when possible and go point-eight miles.

I shift into gear and turn onto the dark street.

The man behind me says, “Buckle your seat belt.”

I strap myself in as he does too.

“Jason, to be clear, if you don’t follow these directions exactly, I’m going to shoot you through the seat. Do you understand?”

“Yes.”

I drive us through my neighborhood, wondering if this is the last time I’ll see it.

At a red light, I stop in front of my corner bar. Through the dark window, I see the door is still open. I see Matt, and through the crowd, Ryan, turned around on his stool, his back to the bar, his elbows on the worn wood, talking to his students. Probably telling them a scary story about failure starring his old roommate.

I want to call out to him. To make him understand I’m in trouble. That I need—

“Green light, Jason.”

I drive through the intersection.

The GPS guides us east through Logan Square to the Kennedy Expressway, where the calm female voice tells me, Turn right in one hundred feet and proceed for nineteen-point-eight miles.

Traffic is light enough that I can keep the speedometer at seventy. My hands sweat on the leather steering wheel, and I keep wondering, Am I going to die tonight?

It hits me that if I live, I’ll always know this: we leave this life the way we enter it—completely alone, with nothing. I’m afraid, and Daniela or Charlie or anyone can’t help me now when I need them most. They don’t even know what I’m going through.

The highway goes along the west side of downtown. The Willis Tower and the smaller skyscrapers around it glow warmly in the night.

Through the fear, my mind races, trying to understand what’s happening.

My address is in the GPS. This wasn’t a chance meeting. This man has been following me. He knows me. So, something I did led to this.

But what?

I’m not wealthy.

My life only matters to me and my family.

I’ve never been arrested or committed a crime.

Never slept with a married woman.

I might flip people off while driving, but that’s normal in Chicago.

The last time I fought was in sixth grade when I punched a kid for pouring milk on me.

I haven’t truly wronged anyone. Not in a way that would lead to this, driving in a Lincoln Navigator with a gun to my head.

I’m a physics professor at a small college.

I treat my students, even the worst ones, with respect. Those who failed my classes did so because they didn’t care, and none of them could blame me for their failures. I try my best to help my students pass.

The skyline shrinks in the side mirror, fading away like a comforting coastline.

I ask, “Did I do something to you before? Or someone you work for? I don’t understand what you want from—”

“The more you talk, the worse it will be for you.”

I realize his voice sounds familiar. I can’t place when or where, but we’ve met. I’m sure of it.

I feel my phone vibrate with a text message.

Then another.

And another.

He forgot to take my phone.

I look at the time: 9:05 p.m.

I left my house a little over an hour ago. It’s Daniela, for sure, wondering where I am. I’m fifteen minutes late, and I’m never late.

I glance in the rearview mirror, but it’s too dark to see anything except a bit of the ghost-white mask. I try something. Taking my left hand off the steering wheel, I put it in my lap and count to ten.

He says nothing.

I put my hand back on the wheel.

That computerized voice breaks the silence: Merge right onto the Eighty-Seventh Street exit in four-point-three miles.

Again, I take my left hand slowly off the wheel.

This time, I slide it into the pocket of my khaki pants. My phone is buried deep, and I just barely touch it with my index and pointer fingers, somehow managing to grab it between them.

Little by little, I pull it out, the rubber case catching on every fold of fabric, and now a constant vibration shaking between my fingertips—a call coming in.

When I finally get it free, I place my phone faceup in my lap and return my hand to the steering wheel.

As the navigation voice updates the distance from our upcoming turn, I glance down at the phone.

There’s a missed call from “Dani” and three texts:

DANI 2m ago

Dinner’s on the table

DANI 2m ago

Hurry home we are STARVING!

DANI 1m ago

You get lost? :)

I look back at the road, wondering if the light from my phone can be seen from the backseat.

The touchscreen goes dark.

I reach down, press the ON/OFF button, and swipe the screen. I type in my four-digit code and tap the green “Messages” icon. Daniela’s messages are at the top, and as I open our conversation, my abductor moves behind me.

I grip the wheel with both hands again.

Merge right onto the Eighty-Seventh Street exit in one-point-nine miles.

The screensaver turns on, the phone locks, and the screen goes black.

Shit.

I slide my hand back down, retype the code, and start typing the most important text of my life, my finger clumsy on the screen, each word taking several tries as auto-correct messes things up.

The gun barrel presses into the back of my head.

I react, swerving into the fast lane.

“What are you doing, Jason?”

I straighten the wheel with one hand, pulling us back into the slow lane as my other hand reaches for the phone, about to hit Send.

He jumps between the front seats, his gloved hand reaching around my waist, grabbing the phone.

Merge right onto the Eighty-Seventh Street exit in five hundred feet.

“What’s your passcode, Jason?” When I don’t answer, he says, “Wait. I bet I know it. Month and year of your birthday backwards? Let’s see…three-seven-two-one. There we go.”

In the rearview mirror, I see the phone light up his mask.

He reads the text he stopped me from sending: “ ‘1400 Pulaski call 91…’ Bad boy.”

I turn onto the interstate off-ramp.

The GPS says, Turn left onto Eighty-Seventh Street and go east for three-point-eight miles.

We drive into South Chicago, through a neighborhood we don't belong in.

Past rows of factory houses.

Apartment buildings.

Empty parks with rusty swing sets and basketball hoops without nets.

Storefronts locked up for the night behind security gates.

Gang graffiti everywhere.

He asks, “So do you call her Dani or Daniela?”

My throat tightens.

Rage and fear and helplessness growing inside me.

“Jason, I asked you a question.”

“Go to hell.”

He leans close, his words hot in my ear. “You don't want to do this with me. I will hurt you more than you've ever been hurt. Pain you didn’t even know could exist. What do you call her?”

I grit my teeth. “Daniela.”

“Never Dani? Even though that’s what’s on your phone?”

I want to flip the car at high speed and just kill us both.

I say, “Rarely. She doesn’t like it.”

“What’s in the grocery bag?”

“Why do you want to know what I call her?”

“What’s in the bag?”

“Ice cream.”

“It’s family night, right?”

“Yeah.”

In the rearview mirror, I see him typing on my phone.

“What are you writing?” I ask.

He doesn’t respond.

We’re out of the ghetto now, driving through a strange, empty place that doesn’t even feel like Chicago anymore, with the skyline just a blur of light far away. The houses are falling apart, dark, and empty. Everything left behind long ago.

We cross a river, and straight ahead is Lake Michigan, its blackness a perfect ending to this city wasteland.

As if the world ends right here.

And maybe mine does.

Turn right and go south on Pulaski Drive for half a mile to your destination.

He chuckles to himself. “Wow, you’re in trouble with your wife.” I grip the steering wheel tightly. “Who was that man you had whisky with tonight, Jason? I couldn’t see from outside.”

It’s so dark out here on the edge between Chicago and Indiana.

We’re driving past the remains of railroad yards and factories.

“Jason.”

“His name is Ryan Holder. He used to be—”

“Your old roommate.”

“How did you know that?”

“Are you two close? I don’t see him in your contacts.”

“Not really. How do you—?”

“I know almost everything about you, Jason. You could say I’ve made it my mission to know your life inside and out.”

“Who are you?”

You will arrive in five hundred feet.

“Who are you?”

He doesn’t answer, but I start to pay less attention to him as I focus on how remote we are.

The road slides under the SUV’s headlights.

Empty behind us.

Empty ahead.

There’s the lake to my left, and empty warehouses to my right.

You have arrived.

I stop the Navigator in the middle of the road.

He says, “The entrance is up ahead on the left.”

The headlights shine on a wobbly section of twelve-foot fencing, topped with rusty barbed wire. The gate is partly open, and a chain that once locked it is cut and lying in the weeds by the road.

“Just push the gate with the front bumper.”

Even inside the soundproof SUV, the screech of the gate opening is loud. The headlights shine on what's left of a road, the pavement cracked from years of Chicago winters.

I turn on the high beams.

Light floods a parking lot, where streetlamps have fallen like scattered matchsticks.

Beyond, a large building appears.

The brick front of the old building is next to huge round tanks and two hundred-foot smokestacks that reach into the sky.

“What is this place?” I ask.

“Put it in PARK and turn it off.”

I stop the car, shift to PARK, and kill the engine.

It gets very quiet.

“What is this place?” I ask again.

“What are you doing Friday?”

“What?”

A hard hit to the side of my head makes me fall against the steering wheel, dazed and wondering for a second if this is what it feels like to be shot.

But no, he just hit me with his gun.

I touch where he hit me.

My fingers are wet with blood.

“Tomorrow,” he says. “What are you doing tomorrow?”

Tomorrow. It feels like a strange idea.

“I’m…giving a test to my PHYS 3316 class.”

“What else?”

“That’s it.”

“Take off all your clothes.”

I look in the rearview mirror.

Why does he want me naked?

He says, “If you wanted to try something, you should have done it when you were driving. From now on, you’re mine. Now, take off your clothes, and if I have to tell you again, I’m going to make you bleed. A lot.”

I unbuckle my seat belt.

As I unzip my gray hoodie and shrug my arms out of the sleeves, I cling to a single shred of hope—he’s still wearing a mask, which means he doesn’t want me to see his face. If he planned to kill me, he wouldn’t care if I could identify him.

Right?

I unbutton my shirt.

“Shoes too?” I ask.

“ Everything.”

I slip off my running shoes, my socks.

I slide my pants and boxer shorts down my legs.

Then my clothes—every last bit—sit in a pile in the front passenger seat.

I feel vulnerable.

Exposed.

Weirdly ashamed.

What if he tries to rape me? Is that what this is about?

He puts a flashlight on the console between the seats.

“Out of the car, Jason.”

I see the inside of the Navigator as a kind of safe space. As long as I stay inside, he can’t really hurt me.

He won’t make a mess in here.

“Jason.”

My chest is pounding, I’m starting to breathe too fast, black spots flashing in my eyes.

“I know what you’re thinking,” he says, “and I can hurt you just as easily inside this car.”

I’m not getting enough air. I’m starting to panic.

But I manage to say, breathlessly, “That’s bullshit. You don’t want my blood in here.”

—

When I wake up, he’s dragging me out of the front seat by my arms. He drops me in the gravel, where I sit confused, waiting for my head to clear.

It’s always colder near the lake, and tonight is no different. The wind feels sharp and rough on my bare skin, which is covered in goosebumps.

It’s so dark out here I can see five times as many stars as in the city.

My head is throbbing, and a new line of blood runs down the side of my face. But with a rush of adrenaline pumping through me, the pain is dull.

He drops a flashlight in the dirt beside me and shines his at the falling-apart building I saw as we drove in. “After you.”

I grab the light and struggle to stand. Stumbling toward the building, my bare feet step on wet newspaper. I avoid crushed beer cans and shards of glass that shine under the light.

Getting closer to the entrance, I imagine this empty parking lot on another night. A night in the future. It’s early winter, and through falling snow, the darkness is lit with flashing blues and reds. Detectives and search dogs fill the ruins, and as they look at my body somewhere inside, naked and rotting and cut up, a police car parks in front of my brownstone in Logan Square. It’s two in the morning, and Daniela answers the door in a nightgown. I’ve been missing for weeks and she knows I’m not coming back, thinks she’s accepted that harsh truth, but seeing these young police officers with their serious, steady eyes and a bit of snow on their shoulders and hats, which they take off politely…it finally breaks something in her she didn’t know was still there. She feels her knees weaken, her strength leaving her, and as she falls onto the doormat, Charlie comes down the creaky stairs behind her, sleepy and messy-haired, asking, “Is it about Dad?”

As we get closer to the building, two words appear on the faded brick above the entrance. The only letters I can make out spell CAGO POWER.

He pushes me through a hole in the brick.

Our lights shine across a front office.

Furniture rusted down to the metal frames.

An old water cooler.

The remains of someone’s campfire.

A torn sleeping bag.

Used condoms on moldy carpet.

We enter a long hallway.

Without the flashlights, it would be pitch black.

I stop to shine my light ahead, but it disappears into the darkness. There’s less trash on the warped linoleum floor beneath my feet, and no sound at all, except for the low, distant moan of wind outside these walls.

I’m growing colder by the second.

He shoves the gun into my kidney, making me move.

When did I get on the radar of a psychopath who decided to learn all about me before killing me? I talk to strangers a lot. Maybe we spoke in that coffee shop near campus. Or on the train. Or at my local bar.

Does he have plans for Charlie and Daniela?

“Do you want me to beg?” I ask, my voice cracking. “Because I will. I’ll do anything.”

And the awful thing is it’s true. I would do anything shameful. Hurt someone else, almost anything if he would just take me back home and let tonight go back to normal—with me walking home to my family, bringing them the ice cream I promised.

“If what?” he asks. “If I let you go?”

“Yes.”

His laughter echoes down the hall. “I’m afraid to see what you'd be willing to do to get out of this.”

“Out of what, exactly?”

But he doesn’t answer.

I fall to my knees.

My light slides across the floor.

“Please,” I beg. “You don’t have to do this.” I barely know my own voice. “You can just leave. I don’t know why you want to hurt me, but just think about it for a minute. I—”

“Jason.”

“—love my family. I love my wife. I love—”

“Jason.”

“—my son.”

“Jason!”

“I will do anything.”

I’m shaking hard now—from cold, from fear.

He kicks me in the stomach, and as I gasp for air, I roll onto my back. He forces the gun into my mouth, all the way back until I taste old oil and dirt. It’s more than I can handle.

Just before I throw up the wine and Scotch, he pulls the gun out.

He yells, “Get up!”

He grabs my arm and yanks me up.

He points the gun at my face and shoves my flashlight back into my hands.

I stare at the mask, my light shining on the gun.

It’s the first time I really see the gun. I don’t know much about guns, just that it’s a handgun with a hammer, a cylinder, and a big hole at the end that looks like it could kill me. The flashlight makes the bullet pointed at my face look a little copper. I imagine him in a small apartment, loading bullets, getting ready to do this.

I’m going to die here, maybe right now.

Every moment feels like it could be the last.

“Move,” he growls.

I start walking.

We reach a crossing and turn down another hallway, wider, taller, and arched. The air feels heavy with moisture. I hear water dripping in the distance. The walls are concrete, and instead of linoleum, the floor is covered in damp moss that grows thicker and wetter as we walk.

The taste of the gun stays in my mouth, mixed with the sour taste of bile.

Parts of my face are going numb from the cold.

A small voice in my head is screaming at me to do something, try anything. Don’t just be led like a lamb to slaughter, following him obediently. Why make it so easy for him?

Easy.

Because I’m afraid.

So afraid I can barely stand.

And my thoughts are broken and swarming.

I understand now why victims don’t fight back. I can’t imagine trying to beat this man. Trying to run.

And here’s the most shameful truth: part of me would rather it all be over, because the dead don’t feel fear or pain. Does this mean I’m a coward? Is that the final truth I have to face before I die?

No.

I have to do something.

We step out of the tunnel onto a metal surface that’s freezing to my feet. I grab a rusted iron railing around a platform. It’s colder here, and I clearly feel open space.

As if timed, a yellow moon slowly rises over Lake Michigan.

Its light streams through the upper windows of a large room, and it’s bright enough to see everything without the flashlight.

My stomach churns.

We’re standing at the top of an open staircase that drops fifty feet.

It looks like an oil painting, the way the old light falls on a row of still generators below and the I-beams overhead.

It’s as quiet as a cathedral.

“We’re going down,” he says. “Watch your step.”

We go down.

Two steps up from the second-to-highest landing, I spin around, clutching the flashlight tightly in my right hand, aiming for his head…

…and hitting nothing. The force of the spin carries me right back to where I started and then some.

I’m off balance, falling.

I hit the landing hard. The flashlight flies out of my hand and disappears over the edge.

A second later, I hear it smash on the floor forty feet below.

My captor stares down at me behind that blank mask, head tilted, gun pointed at my face.

He pulls back the hammer and steps toward me.

I groan as his knee slams into my chest, holding me to the floor.

The gun touches my head.

He says, “I have to admit, I’m proud you tried. It was pathetic. I saw it coming a mile away, but at least you went down swinging.”

I jerk back from a sharp prick in my neck.

“Don’t fight it,” he says.

“What did you give me?”

Before he can answer, something crashes through my mind like a truck. I feel incredibly heavy and light at the same time, the world spinning and flipping.

And then, as quickly as it hit me, it's gone.

Another needle jabs into my leg.

As I cry out, he throws both syringes over the edge. “Let’s go.”

“What did you give me?”

“Get up!”

I use the railing to pull myself up. My knee is bleeding from the fall. My head is still bleeding. I’m cold, dirty, and wet, my teeth chattering so hard I think they might break.

We go down, the shaky metal stairs trembling with our weight. At the bottom, we step off and walk down a row of old generators.

From the floor, this room seems even bigger.

Halfway there, he stops and shines his flashlight on a duffel bag next to one of the generators.

“New clothes. Hurry up.”

“New clothes? I don’t—”

“You don’t need to understand. Just get dressed.”

Despite all the fear, I feel a flicker of hope. Is he going to let me live? Why else would he want me to get dressed? Do I have a chance to survive this?

“Who are you?” I ask.

“Hurry up. You don’t have much time left.”

I squat by the duffel bag.

“Clean yourself up first.”

There’s a towel on top, which I use to wipe the mud off my feet, the blood off my knee and face. I pull on a pair of boxer shorts and jeans that fit perfectly. Whatever he injected me with, I think I can feel it in my fingers now—a loss of nimbleness as I struggle with the buttons on a plaid shirt. My feet slide easily into a pair of expensive leather slip-ons. They fit as comfortably as the jeans.

I’m not cold anymore. It’s like there’s a center of heat in my chest, spreading out through my arms and legs.

“The jacket too.”

I lift a black leather jacket from the bottom of the bag, push my arms through the sleeves.

“Perfect,” he says. “Now, have a seat.”

I ease down against the iron base of the generator. It’s a massive piece of machinery the size of a train engine.

He sits across from me, the gun pointed easily in my direction.

Moonlight is filling this place, bouncing off the broken windows above and sending light that hits—

Tangled cables.

Gears.

Pipes.

Levers and pulleys.

Control panels covered with cracked gauges and switches.

Technology from another time.

I ask, “What happens now?”

“We wait.”

“For what?”

He waves my question away.

A strange calm settles over me. A misplaced sense of peace.

“Did you bring me here to kill me?” I ask.

“I did not.”

I feel so comfortable leaning against the old machine, like I’m sinking into it.

“But you let me believe it.”

“There was no other way.”

“No other way to what?”

“To get you here.”

“And why are we here?”

But he just shakes his head, snaking his left hand up under the geisha mask to scratch.

I feel strange.

Like I’m watching and acting in a movie at the same time.

A heavy drowsiness settles onto me.

My head nods.

“Just let it take you,” he says.

But I don’t. I fight it, struck by how fast his voice has changed. He’s like someone else, and the difference between who he is now and the violence from minutes ago should scare me. I shouldn’t be this calm, but my body is humming too peacefully.

I feel deeply serene and far away.

He says to me, almost confessing, “It’s been a long road. I can hardly believe I’m sitting here actually looking at you. Talking to you. I know you don’t understand, but there’s so much I want to ask.”

“About what?”

“What it’s like to be you.”

“What do you mean?”

He hesitates, then: “How do you feel about where you are in life, Jason?”

I say slowly, carefully, “That’s an interesting question considering what you’ve put me through tonight.”

“Are you happy with your life?”

In this dark moment, my life feels incredibly beautiful.

“I have an amazing family. A job I love. We’re doing well. Everyone’s healthy.”

My tongue feels heavy. My words are starting to sound unclear.

“But?”

I say, “My life is good. It’s just not amazing. And there was a time when it could have been.”

“You killed your ambition, didn’t you?”

“It died on its own. From being ignored.”

“And do you know exactly how that happened? Was there a moment when—?”

“My son. I was twenty-seven, and Daniela and I had been together a few months. She told me she was pregnant. We were having fun, but it wasn’t love. Or maybe it was. I don’t know. We definitely weren’t trying to start a family.”

“But you did.”

“When you’re a scientist, your late twenties are so important. If you don’t publish something important by thirty, they give up on you.”

Maybe it’s just the drug, but it feels so good to be talking. A normal break after two of the craziest hours of my life. I know it isn’t true, but it feels like as long as we keep talking, nothing bad can happen. As if the words protect me.

“Did you have something important in progress?” he asks.

Now I’m having to focus on keeping my eyes open.

“Yes.”

“And what was it?”

His voice sounds distant.

“I was trying to make something big enough to see act like it was in two places at once, like in quantum physics.”

“Why did you stop your research?”

“When Charlie was born, he had serious health problems for the first year. I needed to be in a sterile lab for a thousand hours, but I couldn’t get there fast enough. Daniela needed me. My son needed me. I lost my funding. Lost my drive. I was the hot new genius for a while, but when I messed up, someone else took over.”

“Do you regret staying with Daniela and building a life with her?”

“No.”

“Never?”

I think of Daniela, and the feeling rushes back, along with the real terror of that time. Fear comes back, and with it a longing for home that hurts deep inside. I need her right now more than I’ve ever needed anything.

“Never.”

And then I’m on the floor, my face against the cold concrete, and the drug is carrying me away.

He’s kneeling next to me now, turning me onto my back, and I’m looking up at all that moonlight coming in through the high windows of this forgotten place, the darkness flickering with bits of light and color as swirling, empty spaces open and close by the generators.

“Will I see her again?” I ask.

“I don’t know.”

I want to ask him again what he wants from me, but I can’t find the words.

My eyes keep closing, and I try to keep them open, but I can’t.

He takes off a glove and touches my face with his bare hand.

Strangely.

Gently.

He says, “Listen to me. You’re going to be scared, but you can make it yours. You can have everything you never had. I’m sorry I had to scare you earlier, but I had to get you here. I’m so sorry, Jason. I’m doing this for both of us.”

I mouth the words, Who are you?

Instead of answering, he reaches into his pocket and takes out a new syringe and a tiny glass vial filled with a clear liquid that in the moonlight shines like mercury.

He uncaps the needle and draws the liquid from the vial into the syringe.

As my eyelids slowly lower, I watch him slide the sleeve up his left arm and inject himself.

Then he drops the vial and the syringe on the concrete between us, and the last thing I see before my eyes close is that glass vial rolling toward my face.

I whisper, “Now what?”

And he says, “You wouldn’t believe me if I told you.”
