Dark Matter

Chapter 5

“Mr. Dessen?”

I jolt awake.

“Hi. Sorry to startle you.”

A doctor is looking at me—a short, green-eyed redhead in a white coat holding a cup of coffee and a tablet.

I sit up.

It’s daytime outside the window by my bed, and for a few seconds, I have no idea where I am.

Through the glass: low clouds cover the city, hiding the skyline above one thousand feet. From here, I can see the lake and two miles of Chicago, everything softened by a sad, midwestern gray.

“Mr. Dessen, do you know where you are?”

“Mercy Hospital.”

“That’s right. You came to the ER last night, quite confused. Dr. Randolph, one of my coworkers, checked you in, and when he left this morning, he gave me your file. I’m Julianne Springer.”

I look down at the IV in my wrist and follow the tube up to the bag hanging above me on a metal stand.

“What’s in that?” I ask.

“Just plain water. You were really dehydrated. How do you feel now?”

I quickly check how I’m doing.

Nauseous.

Head throbbing.

Mouth completely dry.

I point through the window. “Like that,” I say. “Like a bad hangover.”

More than just feeling sick, I feel incredibly empty, like my soul is getting rained on.

Like I’ve been hollowed out.

“I have your MRI results,” she says, turning on her tablet. “Your scan was normal. There was some light bruising, but nothing serious. Your tox screen results are more telling. We found traces of alcohol, as you told Dr. Randolph, but also something else.”

“What?”

“Ketamine.”

“Don’t know it.”

“It’s a surgical painkiller. One side effect is short-term memory loss. That could explain some of your confusion. The tox screen also showed something I’ve never seen. A mind-altering substance. A really strange mix.” She sips her coffee. “I have to ask—you didn’t take these drugs, did you?”

“Of course not.”

“Last night, you gave Dr. Randolph your wife’s name and a couple of phone numbers.”

“Her cell and our home phone.”

“I’ve been trying to find her all morning, but her cell number belongs to a guy named Ralph, and your home phone just goes to voicemail.”

“Can you read her number back to me?”

Springer reads off Daniela’s cell-phone number.

“That’s right,” I say.

“You’re sure about that?”

“Hundred percent.” As she looks back at the tablet, I ask, “Could these drugs you found in my system cause lasting mental changes?”

“You mean delusions? Hallucinations?”

“Exactly.”

“To be honest, I don’t know what this chemical is, so I can’t say for sure what effect it might have had on your nervous system.”

“So it could still be affecting me?”

“Again, I don’t know how long it stays in your body. But you don’t seem to be under the influence of anything right now.”

Memories from last night are coming back.

I see myself walking naked into an empty building, with someone pointing a gun at me.

A shot in my neck.

In my leg.

Bits of a weird talk with a man in a geisha mask.

A room full of old machines and moonlight.

And while thinking about last night feels like a real memory, it also feels like a dream, or a nightmare.

What happened to me in that old building?

Springer pulls a chair over and sits next to my bed. Up close, I see freckles all over her face like light sand.

“Let’s talk about what you told Dr. Randolph. He wrote down…” She sighs. “Sorry, his writing is terrible. ‘Patient says: It was my house, but not my house.’ You also said you got the cuts and bruises on your face because people were chasing you, but when we asked why, you couldn’t answer.” She looks up from the screen. “You’re a professor?”

“Correct.”

“At…”

“Lakemont College.”

“Listen, Jason. While you were asleep, and after we couldn’t find any sign of your wife—”

“What do you mean you couldn’t find any sign of her?”

“Her name is Daniela Dessen, right?”

“Yes.”

“Thirty-nine years old?”

“Yeah.”

“We couldn’t find anyone with that name and age in Chicago.”

That knocks me flat. I look away from Springer, back out the window. It’s so gray that even the time of day is hidden. Morning, noon, evening—it’s impossible to tell. Tiny drops of rain stick to the other side of the glass.

Now, I’m not even sure what scares me more—this reality that might be true, or the chance that my mind is falling apart. I preferred thinking a brain tumor caused everything. At least that was an explanation.

“Jason, we looked you up. Your name. Job. Everything we could find. I want you to answer carefully. Do you really think you’re a physics professor at Lakemont College?”

“I don’t think it. It’s what I am.”

“We checked the faculty pages for science departments at every university and college in Chicago. Including Lakemont. You weren’t listed as a professor at any of them.”

“That’s impossible. I’ve been teaching there since—”

“Let me finish, because we did find some information about you.” She types something on her tablet. “Jason Ashley Dessen, born 1973 in Denison, Iowa, to Randall and Ellie Dessen. It says here your mother died when you were eight. How? If you don’t mind me asking.”

“She had a heart problem, caught a bad flu, which became pneumonia.”

“Sorry to hear that.” She keeps reading. “Bachelor’s degree from University of Chicago, 1995. PhD from the same university, 2002. So far so good?”

I nod.

"You won the Pavia Prize in 2004, and that same year, Science magazine put you on the cover, calling your work the ‘breakthrough of the year.’ You’ve been a guest speaker at Harvard, Princeton, and UC Berkeley.” She looks up, sees I’m confused, and turns the tablet so I can see she’s reading from Jason A. Dessen’s Wikipedia page.

The heart monitor I’m hooked up to shows my heart is beating faster.

Springer says, “You haven’t put out any new papers or taken any teaching jobs since 2005, when you became chief science officer at Velocity Laboratories, a jet propulsion lab. It also says your brother reported you missing eight months ago, and you haven’t been seen in public for over a year.”

This hits me so hard I can hardly breathe.

My blood pressure sets off an alarm on the heart monitor, and it starts beeping loudly.

A large nurse comes to the door.

“We’re okay,” Springer says. “Can you turn that off?”

The nurse goes to the monitor and stops the alarm.

After he leaves, the doctor reaches over the railing and touches my hand.

“I want to help you, Jason. I can tell you’re scared. I don’t know what’s happened to you, and I think you don’t either.”

The wind off the lake is strong enough to push the rain sideways. I watch the drops race across the glass, turning the world outside into a blurry, gray cityscape with distant taillights and headlights.

Springer says, “I’ve called the police. They’re sending a detective to take your statement and start figuring out what happened last night. That’s our first step. I couldn’t reach Daniela, but I found your brother Michael’s contact information in Iowa City. I’d like your permission to call him, let him know you’re here, and talk about how you’re doing.”

I don’t know how to respond. I haven’t talked to my brother in two years.

“I’m not sure I want you to call him,” I say.

“That’s fine, but understand that under HIPAA, if I think a patient can’t agree or disagree to sharing information because they’re unable to or in an emergency, I can decide if telling a family member or friend is best. I believe your current mental state counts as an inability to decide, and I think talking to someone who knows you and your past is in your best interest. So, I will call Michael.”

She looks at the floor, like she doesn’t want to say what’s next.

“Third thing, last thing,” she says. “We need a psychiatrist’s help to understand what’s going on with you. I’m transferring you to Chicago-Read, a mental health center further up on the North Side.”

“I know I don’t fully understand what’s happening, but I’m not crazy. I’d be glad to talk to a psychiatrist. I’d even like that. But I’m not agreeing to be locked up, if that’s what you’re asking.”

“That’s not what I’m asking. With all respect, Jason, you don’t have a choice.”

“Excuse me?”

“It’s called an M1 hold, and by law, if I think you’re a danger to yourself or others, I can force you to stay here for seventy-two hours. Look, this is what’s best for you. You’re in no shape—”

“I walked into this hospital on my own, because I wanted to know what was wrong with me.”

“And that was the right thing to do, and that’s exactly what we’re going to do: find out why you’re losing touch with reality, and get you the treatment you need to get better.”

I watch my blood pressure go up on the monitor.

I don’t want to set off the alarm again.

Closing my eyes, I breathe in.

Let it out.

Take another breath of oxygen.

My levels go down.

I say, “So you’re going to put me in a padded room, no belt, no sharp objects, and drug me until I’m senseless?”

“It’s not like that. You came to this hospital because you wanted to get better, right? Well, this is the first step. I need you to trust me.”

Springer gets up from the chair and pulls it back across the room under the television. “Just keep resting, Jason. Police will be here soon, and then we’ll get you moved to Chicago-Read tonight.”

I watch her leave, feeling like I’m about to fall apart, the feeling pressing down on me.

What if all the things I believe and remember that make me who I am—my job, Daniela, my son—are just a sad mistake in my brain? Will I keep trying to be the man I think I am? Or will I reject him and everything he loves, and become the person this world wants me to be?

And if I have lost my mind, what then?

What if everything I know is wrong?

No. Stop.

I am not losing my mind.

There were drugs in my blood from last night and bruises on my body. My key opened the door to that house that wasn’t mine. I don’t have a brain tumor. There’s a mark from a wedding band on my finger. I am in this hospital room right now, and all of this is really happening.

I am not allowed to think I’m crazy.

I'm only allowed to solve this problem.

—

When the elevator doors open to the hospital lobby, I push past two men in cheap suits and wet coats. They look like cops, and when they step into the elevator and we make eye contact, I wonder if they’re coming to see me.

I walk past a waiting area, toward the automatic doors. Since I wasn’t in a locked ward, getting out was easier than I thought. I just got dressed, waited for the hall to be empty, and walked past the nurses’ station without anyone even noticing.

As I get to the exit, I keep expecting alarms to go off, someone to call my name, guards to run after me through the lobby.

Soon I’m outside in the rain, and it feels like early evening, the busy traffic suggesting it’s around six p.m.

I rush down the steps, reach the sidewalk, and don’t slow down until I’m on the next block.

I look back.

No one is following me, as far as I can see.

Just a lot of umbrellas.

I’m getting wet.

I have no idea where I’m going.

At a bank, I step off the sidewalk and take shelter under the entrance roof. Leaning against a limestone column, I watch people walk by as rain pounds the pavement.

I dig my money clip out of my pants. Last night’s cab ride took a big chunk out of my small savings. I’m down to $182, and my credit cards are useless.

Going home is impossible, but there’s a cheap hotel in my neighborhood a few blocks from my brownstone, and it’s just dirty enough to make me think I might be able to pay for a room there.

I step back out into the rain.

It’s getting darker every minute.

Colder.

Without a coat or jacket, I’m soaked to the bone within two blocks.

The Days Inn should be across the street from Village Tap. But it isn’t. The awning is the wrong color, and the whole front of the building looks strangely fancy. These are high-end apartments. I even see a doorman under an umbrella, trying to get a taxi for a woman in a black coat.

Am I on the right street?

I look back at my corner bar.

VILLAGE TAP should be shining neon in the front window, but instead there’s a heavy wooden sign with brass letters hanging from a pole that’s swinging over the entrance, creaking in the wind.

I keep walking, faster now, the rain hitting my eyes.

Past—

Noisy bars.

Restaurants getting ready for dinner—shiny wineglasses and silverware quickly set on white tablecloths as servers learn the specials.

A coffee shop I don’t know, filled with the sound of an espresso machine grinding beans.

Daniela’s and my favorite Italian place looking just as it should, reminding me I haven’t eaten in almost a day.

But I keep walking.

Until my socks are soaked.

Until I'm shaking hard.

Until night falls and I’m standing outside a three-story hotel with bars on the windows and a huge, annoying sign above the entrance:

HOTEL ROYALE

I step inside, leaving a puddle on the cracked checkerboard floor.

It isn’t what I expected. Not sleazy or dirty in a shocking way. Just forgotten. Past its best days. Like my great-grandparents’ living room in their shaky Iowa farmhouse. As if the worn furniture has been here forever, stuck in time while the world moved forward. The air smells old, and big-band music plays softly from somewhere hidden. Something from the 1940s.

At the front desk, the old clerk in a tuxedo doesn’t even blink at how wet I am. He just takes $95 in wet money and gives me a key to a room on the third floor.

The elevator is small, and I stare at my weird reflection in the bronze doors as it struggles to the third floor, making noise and moving like a heavy man climbing stairs.

Down a dim hallway, barely wide enough for two people to walk side by side, I find my room number and struggle to unlock the old lock with the key.

It isn’t much.

A single bed with a thin metal frame and a bumpy mattress.

A bathroom the size of a closet.

A dresser.

An old television.

And a chair next to a window, where something glows on the other side of the glass.

Walking around the end of the bed, I pull back the curtain and look outside, finding myself level with the top of the hotel sign and close enough to see the rain falling through the green light.

Down on the sidewalk, I see a man leaning against a lamppost, smoke rising into the rain, the ash of his cigarette glowing and fading in the darkness under his hat.

Is he waiting there for me?

Maybe I’m just being paranoid, but I go to the door and check the lock and hook the chain.

Then I kick off my shoes, take off my clothes, and dry myself with the bathroom’s only towel.

The best thing about the room is the old cast-iron radiator under the window. I turn it up high and hold my hands in the waves of heat.

I hang my wet clothes over the chair and push it close to the radiator.

In the bedside table drawer, I find a Gideon Bible and a large Chicago Metro phone book.

Stretching out on the creaky bed, I flip to the D’s and start looking for my last name.

I quickly find my listing.

Jason A. Dessen.

Right address.

Right number.

I pick up the phone on the bedside table and call my home phone.

It rings four times, then I hear my voice: “Hi, you’ve reached Jason, but not really, because I can’t take your call. This is a recording. You know what to do.”

I hang up before the beep.

That isn’t our home voicemail message.

I feel like I'm going crazy again, like I'm about to curl up in a ball and break into a million pieces.

But I stop it, and go back to my new rule.

I'm not allowed to think I'm crazy.

I'm only allowed to solve this problem.

Experimental physics—all of science—is about solving problems. But you can’t solve them all at once. There’s always a bigger question—the main goal. But if you worry too much about how big it is, you lose focus.

The key is to start small. Focus on problems you can solve. Find some solid ground to stand on. And after you’ve worked hard, and if you’re lucky, the big question becomes answerable. Like slowly stepping back from a photomontage to see the whole picture.

I have to ignore the fear, the paranoia, the terror, and just work on this problem like I’m in a lab—one small question at a time.

Find solid footing.

The main question bothering me right now: What's become of me? There's no answer to that. Not yet. I have some ideas, of course, but ideas lead to prejudice, and prejudice doesn't lead to truth.

Why weren't Daniela and Charlie at our place last night? Why did it seem like I live by myself?

No, that's still too broad, too complicated. Focus the information.

Where are Daniela and Charlie?

Better, but make it even simpler. Daniela will know where my son is.

So, this is where I'll begin: Where is Daniela?

The drawings I saw last night on the walls of the house that isn't mine—Daniela Vargas made them. She signed them with her maiden name. Why?

I hold up my ring finger to the neon light from the window.

The mark from my wedding ring is gone.

Was it ever there?

I pull a loose thread from the curtain and tie it around my ring finger to feel connected to the world and my life.

Then I go back to the phone book, flipping to the V's and stopping at the only Daniela Vargas listed. I tear out the page and dial her number.

Hearing her voice on the recording touches me, even though the message makes me uneasy.

"You've reached Daniela. I'm away painting. Leave a message. Ciao."

—

In an hour, my clothes are warm and almost dry. I wash, dress, and take the stairs to the lobby.

Outside, the wind is blowing, but the rain has stopped.

The smoking man by the streetlamp is gone.

I feel faint from hunger.

I pass six restaurants before finding one I can afford—a bright, dirty pizza place selling huge, deep-dish slices. There’s nowhere to sit inside, so I stand on the sidewalk, stuffing my face and wondering if this pizza is as amazing as I think, or if I’m just too hungry to judge.

Daniela’s address is in Bucktown. I have about $75 left, so I could take a cab, but I want to walk.

The number of people and cars suggests it’s Friday night, and the air feels lively.

I head east to find my wife.

—

Daniela’s building is yellow brick with ivy growing on the front that’s turning red with the recent cold. The buzzer system is an old brass panel, and I find her maiden name near the bottom of the first column.

I press the buzzer three times, but she doesn’t answer.

Through the tall windowpanes around the door, I see a woman in an evening gown and coat, her heels clicking down the hallway as she comes closer. I step away from the window and turn as the door opens.

She’s on her phone, and the smell of alcohol tells me she’s already started celebrating the night. She doesn’t see me as she rushes down the steps.

I grab the door before it closes and take the stairs to the fourth floor.

Daniela’s door is at the end of the hall.

I knock and wait.

No answer.

I head back down to the lobby, wondering if I should just wait here for her to come back. But what if she’s away? What would she think if she returned to her apartment and found me hanging around outside like some stalker?

As I walk toward the entrance, I see a bulletin board full of flyers for things like art shows, book readings, and poetry nights.

The biggest notice in the middle of the board grabs my attention. It’s a poster for a show by Daniela Vargas at a gallery called Oomph.

I stop and look for the opening date.

Friday, October 2.

Tonight.

—

Back down on the street, it’s raining again.

I flag a cab.

The gallery is a dozen blocks away, and I feel my nerves stretch tight as we crawl down Damen Avenue, stuck in a sea of cabs at rush hour.

I get out of the cab and join the crowd of hipsters walking through the freezing drizzle.

Oomph is an old packing plant that’s been turned into an art gallery, and the line to get in stretches halfway down the block.

Forty-five miserable, shivering minutes later, I’m finally out of the rain, paying my $15 to get in, and being ushered with a group of ten people into a small room with Daniela’s full name in huge, graffiti-style letters on the wall all around us.

In our fifteen years together, I’ve been to many shows and openings with Daniela, but I’ve never seen anything like this.

A thin, bearded man comes out of a hidden door in the wall.

The lights go down.

He says, “I’m Steve Konkoly, the producer of what you’re about to see.” He tears a plastic bag off a dispenser by the door. “Phones go in the bag. You get them back on the other side.”

The bag of gathering phones is passed around.

“A quick note about what’s coming. The artist wants you to stop thinking so much and try to feel her art. Welcome to ‘Entanglement.’ ”

Konkoly takes the bag of phones and opens the door.

I’m the last to enter.

For a moment, our group is squeezed in a dark, small space that goes completely black as the loud slam of the door shows a huge, warehouse-like room.

I look up as lights appear above us.

Stars.

They look very real, each one glowing softly.

Some are near, some are far, and now and then one shoots across the darkness.

I see what’s coming.

Someone in our group whispers, “Oh my God.”

It’s a maze made of Plexiglas, which looks like it goes on forever under the starry sky.

Waves of light move through the panels.

Our group slowly moves ahead.

There are five ways into the maze, and I’m standing where they all meet, watching the others go their own ways.

A quiet sound that’s been there the whole time gets my attention—it’s more like TV static than music, a hiss over a long, deep note.

I pick a path, and as I enter the maze, the clear walls disappear.

The Plexiglas glows with bright light, even under my feet.

After a minute, some of the panels start showing repeating images.

Birth—baby crying, mother crying with happiness.

A man about to die, struggling at the end of a rope.

A snowstorm.

The ocean.

A desert going by.

I keep going.

Into paths that lead nowhere.

Around turns where I can't see.

The images coming more often, faster and faster.

The wrecked remains of a car crash.

A couple having intense sex.

The cross.

The Buddha.

The pentagram.

The peace sign.

A nuclear explosion.

The lights go out.

The stars return.

I can see through the Plexiglas again, but now there’s a digital filter on the clear surface—like static, swarming bugs, and falling snow.

It makes the others in the maze look like shadows moving through a huge wasteland.

And despite the confusion and fear of the last twenty-four hours, or perhaps precisely because of all I’ve experienced, what I’m seeing now breaks through and hits me hard.

While I can see the others in the labyrinth, it doesn’t feel like we’re in the same room, or even the same space.

They seem far away and lost in their own paths.

I’m struck for a moment by the overwhelming sense of loss.

Not sadness or pain, but something more basic.

A realization and the terror that comes with it—terror of the vast indifference around us.

I don’t know if that’s what Daniela intended her installation to mean, but it’s certainly what it means to me.

We’re all just wandering through the emptiness of our lives, giving value to things that have none, when all that we love and hate, all we believe in and fight for and kill for and die for is as meaningless as images projected onto Plexiglas.

At the labyrinth’s exit, there’s one last loop— a man and a woman each hold the tiny hand of their child as they run together up a grassy hill under a clear, blue sky —with the following words slowly appearing on the panel—

Nothing exists.

All is a dream.

God—man—the world—the sun, the moon, the wilderness of stars—a dream, all a dream; they have no existence.

Nothing exists save empty space—and you….

And you are not you—you have no body, no blood, no bones, you are but a thought.

MARK TWAIN

I step into another small room, where the rest of my group huddles around the plastic bag, getting their phones.

Onward, into a large, bright gallery with shiny wood floors, art on the walls, a violin trio…and a woman in a beautiful black dress, standing on a platform, speaking to the crowd.

It takes me five seconds to realize this is Daniela.

She’s glowing, holding a glass of red wine and gesturing with her other hand.

“—just the most amazing night, and I’m so grateful to all of you for coming to support my new project. It means so much.”

Daniela raises her wineglass.

“¡Salud!”

The crowd responds, and as everyone drinks, I move toward her.

Up close, she’s electric, so full of life that I have to stop myself from calling out to her. This is Daniela with the energy of our first meeting fifteen years ago, before life—the ordinary, the joy, the sadness, the give-and-take—changed her into the woman who shares my bed: amazing mother, amazing wife, but always fighting the thoughts of what could have been.

My Daniela carries a weight and a distance in her eyes that sometimes scare me.

This Daniela is so happy she's practically floating.

I’m now standing less than ten feet away, my heart thumping, wondering if she’ll spot me, and then—

Eye contact.

Her eyes widen and her mouth opens, and I can’t tell if she’s horrified or delighted or just surprised to see my face.

She pushes through the crowd, throws her arms around my neck, and pulls me in tight with, “Oh my God, I can’t believe you came. Is everything all right? I’d heard you left the country for a while or were missing or something.”

I’m not sure how to respond to that, so I just say, “Well, here I am.”

Daniela hasn’t worn perfume in years, but she’s wearing it tonight, and she smells like Daniela without me, like Daniela before our separate scents merged into us.

I don’t want to let go—I need her touch—but she pulls away.

I ask, “Where’s Charlie?”

“Who?”

“Charlie.”

“Who are you talking about?”

Something twists inside me.

“Jason?”

She doesn’t know who our son is.

Do we even have a son?

Does Charlie exist?

Of course, he exists. I was there when he was born. I held him right after he came squirming and crying into the world.

“Everything okay?” she asks.

“Yeah. I just came through the maze.”

“What did you think?”

“It almost made me cry.”

“It was all you,” she says.

“What do you mean?”

“That talk we had a year and a half ago? When you came to see me? You inspired me, Jason. I thought of you every day I was building it. I thought about what you said. Didn’t you see the dedication?”

“No, where was it?”

“At the entrance to the maze. It’s for you. I dedicated it to you, and I’ve been trying to reach you. I wanted you to be my special guest tonight, but no one could find you.” She smiles. “You’re here now. That’s all that matters.”

My heart is racing, the room feels like it’s spinning, and then Ryan Holder is standing next to Daniela with his arm around her. He’s wearing a tweed jacket, his hair is turning gray, and he’s paler and less fit than the last time I saw him, which was impossibly at Village Tap last night at his party for winning the Pavia Prize.

“Well, well,” Ryan says, shaking my hand. “Mr. Pavia. The man himself.”

Daniela says, “Guys, I have to go be polite and talk to people, but, Jason, I’m having a secret get-together at my apartment after this. You’ll come?”

“I’d love to.”

As I watch Daniela disappear into the crowd, Ryan says, “Want to get a drink?”

God yes.

The gallery has gone all out—waiters in tuxedos carrying trays of appetizers and Champagne, and a cash bar on the other side of the room under three Daniela self-portraits.

As the bartender pours our whiskies—Macallan 12s—into plastic cups, Ryan says, “I know you’re doing fine, but I got these.”

It’s so strange—he doesn’t have any of the arrogance and swagger of the man I saw holding court last night at my bar.

We take our Scotches and find a quiet corner away from the crowd around Daniela.

As we stand there watching the room fill with more and more people coming from everywhere, I ask, “So what have you been up to? I feel like I don’t know what you’re doing anymore.”

“I moved to U Chicago.”

“Congrats. So you’re teaching?”

“Cellular and molecular neuroscience. I’ve been doing some really cool research, too, involving the prefrontal cortex.”

“Sounds exciting.”

Ryan leans in close. “Seriously, the gossip has been wild. Everyone’s talking. People are saying”—he lowers his voice—“that you lost it and went crazy. That you’re in a padded cell somewhere. That you’re dead.”

“Here I am. Clear-headed, warm, and breathing.”

“So that thing I made for you…it worked, I guess?”

I just stare at him, having no idea what he's talking about, and when I don’t answer right away, he says, “Right, I get it. They’ve got you covered in NDAs.”

I sip my drink. I’m still hungry, and the alcohol is hitting me fast. When the next waiter walks by, I grab three mini-quiches off the silver tray.

Whatever is bothering him, Ryan can’t drop it.

“Look, I don’t want to complain,” he says, “but I feel like I did a lot of work for you and Velocity without knowing what was going on. You and I have known each other for a long time, and I know you’re at a different point in your career, but I don’t know…I think you got what you needed from me and…”

“What?”

“Forget it.”

“No, please.”

“You could’ve been a little nicer to your old college roommate, is all I’m saying.”

“What are you talking about?”

He looks at me, barely hiding his anger. “Fuck you.”

We stand quietly at the edge of the room as it fills up with people.

“So, are you two together?” I ask. “You and Daniela?”

“Kind of,” he says.

“What does that mean?”

“We’ve been seeing each other for a bit.”

“You always liked her, didn’t you?”

He just smirks.

Looking around, I find Daniela. She’s calm and present, surrounded by reporters with notepads open, writing quickly as she speaks.

“So, how is it?” I ask, though I’m not sure I really want to know. “You and my…and Daniela.”

“Amazing. She’s the woman I’ve always dreamed of.”
