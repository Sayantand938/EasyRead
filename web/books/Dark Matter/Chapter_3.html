Dark Matter

Chapter 3

We drive north toward the city on the empty highway, the skyline getting closer. Each mile makes me feel a bit more normal, mostly because I'll be home soon.

Daniela will help me understand what's going on.

The taxi driver parks across from my building, and I pay him the rest of the money I owe.

I rush across the street and up the steps, grabbing keys from my pocket that aren't mine. As I try to find the right key, I realize this isn't my door. Well, it is my door. It’s my street. My number on the mailbox. But the handle isn't right, the wood is too fancy, and the hinges are these iron, gothic things that look like they belong on an old tavern.

I turn the deadbolt.

The door opens.

Something is wrong.

Very, very wrong.

I step inside, into the dining room.

This doesn’t smell like my house. It smells like almost nothing, just a faint dusty scent. Like nobody’s been living here for a while. The lights are off, every single one.

I shut the door and feel around in the dark until I touch a dimmer switch. An antler chandelier lights up the room above a simple glass table and chairs that aren’t mine.

I call out, “Hello?”

The house is so quiet.

Disgustingly quiet.

In my home, there’s a big, casual photo on the mantel behind the dining table. It’s Daniela, Charlie, and me at Inspiration Point in Yellowstone.

In this house, there’s a black-and-white photo of the same canyon. It’s more artistic, but there’s no one in it.

I go into the kitchen, and as I walk in, a sensor turns on the lights.

It’s beautiful.

Costly.

And lifeless.

In my house, there’s a macaroni art piece Charlie made in first grade stuck to our white refrigerator with magnets. It makes me smile every time I see it. In this kitchen, there’s not even a smudge on the steel front of the Gaggenau refrigerator.

“Daniela!”

Even how my voice sounds is different here.

“Charlie!”

There’s less clutter, more echo.

As I walk through the living room, I see my old record player next to a high-tech sound system, my jazz records carefully stored in alphabetical order on custom shelves built into the wall.

I go up the stairs to the second floor.

The hallway is dark and the light switch isn’t where it should be, but it doesn’t matter. Most of the lights turn on when they sense movement, and more lights set into the ceiling come on above me.

This isn’t my hardwood floor. It’s nicer, the boards wider, a little rougher.

Between the hall bathroom and the guest room, the picture of my family at the Wisconsin Dells has been replaced with a sketch of Navy Pier. Charcoal on butcher paper. The artist’s signature in the bottom right corner catches my eye—Daniela Vargas.

I step into the next room on the left.

My son’s room.

Except it’s not. There’s none of his strange artwork. No bed, no manga posters, no desk with homework all over it, no lava lamps, no backpack, no clothes scattered on the floor.

Instead, just a monitor sitting on a large desk covered in books and loose paper.

I walk in shock to the end of the hallway. Sliding a frosted pocket door into the wall, I enter a master bedroom that is fancy, cold, and, like everything else in this brownstone, not mine.

The walls have more charcoal sketches like the one in the hall, but the main thing in the room is a glass display case built into an acacia wood stand. Light from the base shines up dramatically to show off a certificate in a padded leather folder that leans against a soft velvet pillar. Hanging from a thin chain on the pillar is a gold coin with Julian Pavia’s picture on it.

The certificate reads:

The Pavia Prize is awarded to

JASON ASHLEY DESSEN for outstanding achievement in helping us know more about the origin, evolution, and properties of the universe by putting a large object into a state of

quantum superposition.

I sit on the end of the bed.

I am not well.

I am so not well.

My home should be my safe place, a spot of comfort with my family around. But it’s not even mine.

My stomach flips.

I rush to the bathroom, yank up the toilet seat, and throw up into the clean bowl.

I'm very thirsty.

I turn on the tap and put my mouth under the water.

Splash water on my face.

I walk back to the bedroom.

I don’t know where my phone is, but there’s a phone on the nightstand.

I don’t ever call Daniela’s cell, so I have to think for a second, but I finally type it in.

Four rings.

A man answers, his voice low and thick with sleep.

“Hello?”

“Where’s Daniela?”

“You have the wrong number.”

I say Daniela’s cell number, and he says, “Yeah, that’s the number, but it’s my number.”

“How can that be?”

He hangs up.

I call her number again, and he answers right away, “It’s three in the morning. Don’t call again, jerk.”

My third try goes right to the man’s voicemail. I don’t leave a message.

Getting out of bed, I go back to the bathroom and look at myself in the mirror above the sink.

My face is bruised, scraped, bloody, and covered in mud. I need a shave, my eyes are red, but I’m still me.

A wave of exhaustion hits me hard.

My knees buckle, but I catch myself on the countertop.

And then, downstairs—a noise.

A door closing quietly?

I stand up straight.

Alert again.

Back in the bedroom, I quietly walk to the doorway and look down the hall.

I hear whispered voices.

The static of a handheld radio.

The hollow creak of someone’s foot on a wooden step.

The voices become clearer, echoing in the stairwell and spilling out the top and down the hall.

I can see their shadows on the walls now, moving up the staircase like ghosts ahead of them.

As I take a hesitant step into the hallway, a man’s voice—calm, careful—slides out of the stairwell: “Jason?”

Five steps and I reach the hall bathroom.

“We’re not here to hurt you.”

Their footsteps are in the hallway now.

Stepping slowly, carefully.

“I know you’re confused and lost. I wish you had said something back at the lab. I didn’t realize how hard it was for you. I’m sorry I missed that.”

I carefully close the door behind me and push in the lock.

“We just want to bring you in so you don’t hurt yourself or anyone else.”

The bathroom is twice the size of mine, with a granite shower and a double sink with a marble top.

Across from the toilet, I see what I’m looking for: a large shelf built into the wall with a door to the laundry chute.

“Jason.”

Through the bathroom door, I hear the radio crackling.

“Jason, please. Talk to me.” Out of nowhere, his voice bursts with frustration. “We have all given up our lives working toward tonight. Come out here! This is crazy!”

One rainy Sunday when Charlie was nine or ten, we spent an afternoon pretending we were cave explorers. I would lower him down the laundry chute again and again, as if it were the entrance to a cave. He even wore a little backpack and a fake headlamp—a flashlight tied to the top of his head.

I open the hatch and climb up onto the shelf.

Leighton says, “Take the bedroom.”

Footsteps patter down the hall.

Squeezing into the laundry chute looks hard. Maybe too hard.

I hear the bathroom door start to rattle, the knob jiggling, and then a woman’s voice: “Hey, this one’s locked.”

I look down the chute.

Complete darkness.

The bathroom door is so strong that their first try to break it only makes a crack.

I might not even fit down this thing, but as they smash into the door again and it breaks apart and slams against the tile, I know I have no choice.

They burst into the bathroom, and in the mirror I see Leighton Vance and one of the security guys from the lab, holding what looks like a Taser.

Leighton and I stare at each other in the glass for a moment, and then the man with the Taser turns, lifting his weapon.

I fold my arms and jump into the opening.

The shouting from the bathroom above fades as I crash into an empty laundry bin. The plastic breaks, and I spill out between the washer and dryer.

Their footsteps are already coming, pounding down the stairs.

A sharp pain shoots up my right leg from the fall. I get to my feet and run for the French doors to the backyard.

The brass handles are locked.

Footsteps get closer, voices louder, radios squawking with instructions.

I unlock the doors, pull them open, and dash across a redwood deck with a grill that’s nicer than mine and a hot tub I’ve never had.

Down the steps into the backyard, past a rose garden.

I try the garage door, but it’s locked.

With all the movement inside, every light in the house has turned on. There must be four or five people running around on the first floor trying to find me, shouting at each other.

An eight-foot privacy fence surrounds the backyard, and as I unlatch the door, someone bursts onto the deck, yelling my name.

The alley is empty, and I don’t pause to consider which way to turn.

I just run.

At the next street, I look back, see two people chasing me.

In the distance, a car engine starts loudly, then tires screech on the road.

I turn left and run hard until I reach the next alley.

Nearly every backyard is shielded by tall privacy fencing, but the fifth one down has a waist-high, wrought-iron fence.

An SUV spins around and speeds into the alley.

I head for the low fence.

Too weak to jump over it, I awkwardly pull myself over the sharp metal points and fall into the backyard. I crawl through the grass to a small shed next to the garage, with no lock on the door.

It creaks open, and I slip inside as someone runs across the backyard.

I shut the door to quiet my heavy breathing.

I can’t catch my breath.

It’s pitch-black inside the shed and smells strongly of gasoline and old grass. My chest pushes hard against the back of the door.

Sweat drips off my chin.

I pull a cobweb off my face.

In the dark, my hands feel the plywood walls, fingers touching different tools—pruning shears, a saw, a rake, the ax blade.

I take the ax from the wall and hold the wooden handle, scraping my finger across the head. Can’t see anything, but it feels like it hasn’t been sharpened in years—deep chips in the blade, which is no longer sharp.

Blinking to clear the sweat, I carefully open the door.

Not a sound comes in.

I push it open a bit more, until I can see the backyard again.

It’s empty.

In this moment of quiet, Occam’s razor whispers to me—the simplest answer is usually the right one. Does the idea that I was drugged and kidnapped by a secret group for mind control fit that? Not really. They would’ve had to brainwash me to believe my house wasn't mine, or in a few hours, get rid of my family and change the inside so I didn't recognize it.

Or—is it more likely a brain tumor has turned my world upside down?

That it’s been growing silently in my head for months or years and is now messing with my mind, changing how I see everything.

The idea feels true.

What else could have hit me so hard and fast?

What else could make me lose myself and reality in hours, making me question everything?

I wait.

And wait.

And wait.

Finally, I step outside onto the grass.

No more voices.

No more footsteps.

No shadows.

No car sounds.

The night feels strong and real again.

I already know where I’m going next.

—

Chicago Mercy is a ten-block walk from my house, and I limp into the bright light of the ER at 4:05 a.m.

I hate hospitals.

I watched my mother die in one.

Charlie spent the first weeks of his life in a NICU.

The waiting room is almost empty. Besides me, there’s a construction worker holding his bleeding arm, and a worried family of three, the father holding a crying baby.

The woman at the front desk looks up from her paperwork, surprisingly awake for this hour.

She asks through the glass, “How can I help you?”

I haven’t thought of what to say, or how to explain what I need.

When I don’t answer right away, she says, “Were you in an accident?”

“No.”

“You have cuts all over your face.”

“I’m not feeling good,” I say.

“What do you mean?”

“I think I need to speak to someone.”

“Are you homeless?”

“No.”

“Where’s your family?”

“I don’t know.”

She looks me up and down—a quick, practiced look.

“Your name, sir?”

“Jason.”

“One moment.”

Rising from her chair, she disappears around the corner.

Thirty seconds later, a buzzing sound signals the door beside her station unlocks and opens.

The nurse smiles. “Come on back.”

She leads me to a patient room.

“Someone will be right with you.”

As the door closes behind her, I sit on the examination table and shut my eyes to block the bright lights. I have never been so tired.

My chin dips.

I straighten up.

I almost fell asleep sitting up.

The door opens.

A stout young doctor walks in with a clipboard. Behind him is a different nurse—a blonde in blue scrubs, looking as tired as someone up since four in the morning.

“You’re Jason?” the doctor asks, not shaking my hand or pretending to care, like most people on the night shift.

I nod.

“Last name?”

I don’t want to tell him my full name, but maybe that’s just the tumor talking, or whatever is wrong in my head.

“Dessen.”

I spell it out as he writes on what looks like an admission form.

“I’m Dr. Randolph, the main doctor here. Why did you come to the ER tonight?”

“I think something is wrong with my mind. Like a tumor or something.”

“What makes you say that?”

“Things aren’t right.”

“Okay. Can you explain more?”

“I…okay, this will sound crazy. I know that.”

He looks up from the clipboard.

“My house isn’t my house.”

“I don’t understand.”

“It’s just what I said. My house isn’t my house. My family isn’t there. Everything’s much…nicer. It’s all been fixed up and—”

“But it’s still your address?”

“Right.”

"So you're saying the inside is different, but the outside is the same?" He says it like he's talking to a child.

"Yeah."

"Jason, how did you get the cuts on your face? The mud on your clothes?"

"People were chasing me."

I shouldn't have said that, but I'm too tired to think straight. I must sound completely crazy.

"Chasing you."

"Yes."

"Who was chasing you?"

"I don't know."

"Do you know why they were chasing you?"

“Because…it’s complicated.”

His careful, doubtful look is much more subtle and practiced than the front-desk nurse’s. I almost don’t notice it.

“Have you taken any drugs or alcohol tonight?” he asks.

“Some wine earlier, then whisky, but that was hours ago.”

“Again, I’m sorry—it’s been a very long shift—but what makes you think something is wrong with your mind?”

“Because the last eight hours of my life don’t make sense. It all feels real, but it can’t be.”

“Have you suffered a recent head injury?”

“No. Well. I mean, I think someone hit me in the back of the head. It hurts when I touch it.”

“Who hit you?”

“I’m not sure. I’m not really sure of anything right now.”

“Okay. Do you use drugs? Now or ever?”

“I smoke weed a few times a year. But not recently.”

The doctor turns to the nurse. “I’m going to have Barbara take some blood.”

He drops the clipboard on a table and pulls a penlight from his lab coat pocket.

“Mind if I check you out?”

“No.”

Randolph moves in until our faces are very close, close enough for me to smell the old coffee on his breath, to see the fresh cut on his chin. He shines the light straight into my right eye. For a moment, there’s only a bright spot in the middle of what I see, which briefly wipes out everything else.

“Jason, are you thinking about hurting yourself?”

“I’m not suicidal.”

The light hits my left eye.

“Have you been in a mental hospital before?”

“No.”

He gently takes my wrist in his soft, cool hands, measures my pulse.

“What’s your job?” he asks.

“I teach at Lakemont College.”

“Married?”

“Yes.” I automatically reach down to touch my wedding band.

Gone.

Jesus.

The nurse begins to roll up the left sleeve of my shirt.

“What’s your wife’s name?” the doctor asks.

“Daniela.”

“Are you two doing okay?”

“Yes.”

“Don’t you think she’s wondering where you are? I think we should call her.”

“I tried.”

“When?”

“An hour ago, at my house. Someone else answered. It was the wrong number.”

“Maybe you dialed wrong.”

“I know my wife’s phone number.”

The nurse asks, “Are needles okay with you, Mr. Dessen?”

“Yes.”

As she cleans the underside of my arm, she says, “Dr. Randolph, look.” She points to the needle mark from a few hours ago when Leighton took my blood.

“When did this happen?” he asks.

“I don’t know.” It’s probably better not to mention the lab I think I just got away from.

“You don’t remember someone giving you a shot?”

“No.”

Randolph nods to the nurse, and she warns me, “This will sting a little.”

He asks, “Do you have your cell phone?”

“I don’t know where it is.”

He grabs the clipboard. “Tell me your wife’s name and phone number again. We’ll try to reach her for you.”

I spell Daniela’s name and quickly say her cell number and our home number as my blood goes into a plastic vial.

“You’re going to scan my head?” I ask. “See what’s happening?”

“Absolutely.”

—

They give me a private room on the eighth floor.

I wash my face in the bathroom, take off my shoes, and get into bed.

I feel sleepy, but I can't turn off my brain.

I can’t stop thinking.

Making guesses and proving them wrong.

Struggling to make sense of everything that’s happened.

Right now, I can’t tell what’s real and what’s not. I can’t even be sure I was ever married.

No. Wait.

I lift my left hand and look closely at my ring finger.

The ring is gone, but the faint mark it left is still there around my finger. It was there. It left a mark. That means someone took it.

I touch the mark, feeling both the horror and the comfort of what it means—the last bit of my reality.

I wonder—

What will happen when this last bit of my marriage is gone?

When there’s nothing left to hold on to?

As the sky over Chicago slowly turns to dawn—a dull, cloudy purple—I fall asleep.
