Dark Matter

Chapter 6

Daniela sits in the bleachers under the scoreboard, above the ivy-covered outfield wall. It’s Saturday afternoon, the last home game of the regular season, and she’s with Jason and Charlie, watching the Cubs get beat badly in their sold-out ballpark.

The warm autumn day is cloudless.

Windless.

Timeless.

The air smells of—

Roasted peanuts.

Popcorn.

Plastic cups filled to the brim with beer.

Daniela finds the roar of the crowd strangely comforting, and they’re far enough back from home plate to notice a delay between swing and bat-crack—speed of light versus speed of sound—when a player sends a pitch flying beyond the wall.

They used to come to games when Charlie was a boy, but it’s been ages since they last came to Wrigley Field. When Jason suggested it yesterday, she didn’t think Charlie would want to, but it must be stirring up some old memories for him, because he actually wanted to come, and now he seems relaxed and happy. They’re all happy, a trio of near-perfect contentment in the sun, eating Chicago-style hot dogs, watching the players run around on the bright grass.

As Daniela sits between the two most important men in her life, a little tipsy from her lukewarm beer, it strikes her that this afternoon feels different. She doesn't know if it's Charlie or Jason or her. Charlie is in the moment, not checking his phone all the time. And Jason looks as happy as she’s seen him in years. Weightless is the word that comes to mind. His smile seems wider, brighter, more freely given.

And he can’t keep his hands off her.

Then again, maybe the difference is her.

Maybe it’s this beer and the clear autumn light and the shared energy of the crowd.

Which is to say maybe it’s just being alive at a baseball game on a fall day in her city.

—

Charlie has plans after the game, so they drop him at a friend’s house in Logan Square, stop at the brownstone to change clothes, and then head out into the evening, just the two of them—going downtown, no plans, no place in mind.

A Saturday-night walkabout.

Driving in slow evening traffic down Lakeshore Drive, Daniela looks across the center console of the old Suburban, says, “I think I know what I want to do first.”

Thirty minutes later, they’re in a gondola on a Ferris wheel covered in lights.

Rising slowly above the sights of Navy Pier, Daniela watches the beautiful skyline of their city as Jason holds her close.

At the top of their ride—one hundred and fifty feet above the carnival—Daniela feels Jason touch her chin and turn her face to him.

They have the gondola all to themselves.

Even up here, the night air smells sweet with funnel cakes and cotton candy.

The laughter of children riding the carousel.

A woman screaming with joy at getting a hole-in-one on the miniature golf course below.

Jason’s strong feelings cut through all of it.

When he kisses her, she can feel his heart through his jacket, pounding in his chest.

—

They have dinner in the city at a fancy restaurant they can't really afford and spend the whole time talking like they haven’t talked in years.

Not about people or old times, but ideas.

They finish a bottle of Tempranillo.

Order another.

Thinking they might stay in the city for the night.

It’s been a long time since Daniela has seen her husband this excited, this confident.

He’s full of energy, in love with his life again.

Partway through their second bottle of wine, he sees her looking out the window and asks, “What are you thinking about?”

“That’s a loaded question.”

“I know.”

“I’m thinking about you.”

“What about me?”

“It feels like you’re trying to sleep with me.” She laughs. “I mean, it feels like you’re trying when you don’t need to. We’re like an old married couple, and I feel like you’re, um…”

“Romancing you?”

“Exactly. Don’t get me wrong, I’m not complaining. Not at all. It’s amazing. I guess I just don’t know why. Are you okay? Is something wrong that you’re not telling me?”

“I’m fine.”

“So this is all because you almost got hit by a cab two nights ago?”

He says, “I don’t know if it was my life flashing before my eyes or what, but when I came home, everything felt different. More real. Especially you. Even now, it’s like I’m seeing you for the first time, and I have this nervous feeling in my stomach. I think about you all the time. I think about all the choices we made that led to this moment. Us sitting here together at this beautiful table. Then I think of all the things that could have stopped this moment from happening, and it all feels, I don’t know…”

“What?”

“So fragile.” Now he thinks for a moment. He says finally, “It’s scary when you think that every thought we have, every choice we could make, creates a new world. After the baseball game today, we went to Navy Pier and then came here for dinner, right? But that’s just one thing that could have happened. In another world, instead of the pier, we went to the symphony. In one, we stayed home. In another, we got into a bad accident on Lakeshore Drive and didn't make it anywhere.”

“But those other realities aren’t really there.”

“Actually, they’re just as real as what you and I are going through right now.”

“How can that be?”

“It’s a mystery. But there are hints. Most space scientists think that the force that keeps stars and galaxies together—the thing that makes our whole universe work —comes from a theoretical substance we can’t measure or see directly. Something they call dark matter. And this dark matter makes up most of the universe we know.”

“But what is it, really?”

“No one’s really sure. Physicists have been trying to come up with new ideas to explain where it comes from and what it is. We know it has gravity, like normal matter, but it must be made of something completely new.”

“A new kind of matter.”

“Exactly. Some string theorists think it might be a hint to the existence of the multiverse.”

She looks thoughtful for a moment, then asks, “So all these other realities…where are they?”

“Imagine you’re a fish, swimming in a pond. You can move forward and back, side to side, but never up out of the water. If someone were standing beside the pond, watching you, you’d have no idea they were there. To you, that little pond is an entire universe. Now imagine that someone reaches down and lifts you out of the pond. You see that what you thought was the entire world is only a small pool. You see other ponds. Trees. The sky above. You realize you’re a part of a much larger and more mysterious reality than you had ever dreamed of.”

Daniela leans back in her chair and takes a sip of wine. “So all these other thousands of ponds are all around us, right now—but we just can’t see them?”

“Exactly.”

Jason used to talk like this all the time. He would keep her up late into the night sharing wild theories, sometimes testing things out, but mostly just trying to impress her.

It worked then.

It’s working now.

She looks away for a moment, staring through the window beside their table, watching the water flow by as the light from the nearby buildings swirls in a constant shimmer on the river's surface.

She finally looks back at him over the rim of her wineglass, their eyes meeting, the candlelight flickering between them.

She says, “In one of those ponds out there, do you think there’s another you who stuck with the research? Who achieved all the goals you had in your twenties, before life got in the way?”

He smiles. “It’s crossed my mind.”

“And maybe there’s a version of me that’s a famous artist? Who gave all this up for that?”

Jason leans forward, pushing their plates aside so he can hold both her hands across the table.

“If there are a million ponds out there, with versions of you and me living lives that are alike and different, none is better than this one, right here, right now. I’m more sure of that than anything else.”
