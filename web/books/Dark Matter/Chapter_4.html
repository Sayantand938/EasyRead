Dark Matter

Chapter 4

Daniela’s hands are deep in the warm, soapy water when she hears the front door slam. She stops scrubbing the saucepan she’s been working on for the last thirty seconds and looks up from the sink, glancing back as footsteps come closer.

Jason appears in the doorway between the kitchen and the dining room, grinning like an idiot, as her mother would say.

Turning back to the dishes, Daniela says, “There’s a plate for you in the fridge.”

In the blurry reflection of the window above the sink, she watches her husband set the canvas grocery bag on the island and walk toward her.

He puts his arms around her waist.

She says, half joking, “If you think a couple pints of ice cream are going to get you out of this, I don’t know what to tell you.”

He presses against her and whispers in her ear, his breath hot with the taste of whatever whisky he’s been drinking, “Life’s short. Don’t be mad. It’s a waste of time.”

“How did forty-five minutes become almost three hours?”

“The same way one drink becomes two, then three, and so on. I feel awful.”

His lips on the back of her neck sent a soft shiver down her spine.

She says, “You’re not getting out of this.”

Now he kisses the side of her neck. It’s been a while since he touched her like this.

His hands slide into the water.

He weaves their fingers together.

“You should eat something,” she says. “I’ll warm up your plate.”

She tries to walk past him to the fridge, but he stops her.

Facing him now, she looks up into his eyes, and maybe it’s because they’ve both been drinking, but the air between them feels electric, as if every tiny part has been charged.

He says, “My God, I’ve missed you.”

“Exactly how much did you drink to—?”

He kisses her suddenly, pushing her against the cabinets, the counter pressing into her back as he slides his hands over her hips and untucks her shirt, his hands burning against her skin.

She pushes him toward the island.

“Jesus, Jason.”

She looks at him in the dim kitchen light, trying to understand the energy he’s brought back home.

“Something happened while you were out,” she says.

“Nothing happened, other than I lost track of time.”

“So you didn’t flirt with some young woman at Ryan’s party who made you feel young again? And now you’re back here wanting sex, pretending—”

He laughs. A lovely sound.

“What?” she says.

“That’s what you think?” He steps closer. “When I left the bar, I was distracted. I wasn’t paying attention. I walked into traffic and a cab almost hit me. It terrified me. I can’t explain it, but ever since—in the store, walking home, here in our kitchen—I’ve felt so alive. Like I truly see my life. All the things I’m thankful for. You. Charlie.”

She feels her anger toward him starting to fade.

He says, “It’s like we get so stuck in our routines, so deep in those patterns, we stop seeing our loved ones as they really are. But tonight, right now, I see you again, like when we first met, when your voice and your scent were a whole new world to me. I’m talking too much.”

Daniela goes to him, holds his face in her hands, and kisses him.

Then she takes his hand and leads him upstairs.

The hallway is dark, and she can’t remember the last time her husband did something that made her heart race like this.

At Charlie’s room, she pauses and listens at the closed door, noting the faint sound of music blasting through headphones.

“All clear,” she whispers.

They move down the squeaky hallway as quietly as they can.

In their bedroom, Daniela locks the door and opens the top drawer of her dresser, looking for a candle to light, but Jason doesn’t wait.

He pulls her to the bed and pulls her down onto the mattress, and then he’s on top of her, kissing her, his hands moving under her clothes, exploring her body.

She feels wetness on her cheek, her lips.

Tears.

His.

Holding his face between her hands, she asks, “Why are you crying?”

“I felt like I’d lost you.”

“You have me, Jason,” she says. “I’m right here, baby. You have me.”

As he undresses her in the darkness of their bedroom, she has never wanted anyone so desperately. The anger is gone. The wine-sleepiness has vanished. He has taken her back to the first time they made love, in her Bucktown loft with the downtown lights glowing through the giant windows that she’d cracked open so the cool October air could drift in, carrying the late-night sounds of people walking home from bars and distant sirens and the hum of the huge city at rest—not completely silent, never off, just a comforting, steady background.

As she climaxes, she tries not to cry out in their bedroom, but she can’t stop it, and neither can Jason.

Not tonight.

Because something is different; something is better.

They haven't been unhappy these last few years, not at all. But it's been a long, long time since she felt that giddy love that bubbles in your stomach and completely turns the world upside down.
