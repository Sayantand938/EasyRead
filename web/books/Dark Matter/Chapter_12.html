In the morning, Amanda is not next to me. I lie on my side, watching the sunlight come through the blinds, listening to the traffic sounds through the walls. The clock is behind me on the bedside table. I can’t see what time it is, but it feels late. We’ve slept in.

I sit up, throw back the covers, look over at Amanda’s bed.

It’s empty.

“Amanda?”

I quickly walk toward the bathroom to see if she’s in there, but I stop when I see something on top of the dresser.

Some cash.

A few coins.

Eight ampoules.

And a piece of paper torn out of a notebook, covered in Amanda’s handwriting.

Jason. After last night, it was clear you've decided on a road I can't take. I wrestled with this all night. As your friend, as a therapist, I want to help you. I want to fix you. But I can’t. And I can’t keep watching you fall. Especially if I’m partly why you keep falling. How much are our shared hidden thoughts pushing us to connect to these worlds? It’s not that I don’t want you to get back to your wife. I want that more than anything. But we’ve been together for weeks now. It’s hard not to get close, especially when you’re all I have.

I read your notebooks yesterday, when I was worried you’d left me, and honey, you’re missing the point. You’re writing down all these things about your Chicago, but not how you feel.

I’ve left you the backpack, half the ampoules, and half the money (a huge $161 and some change). I don’t know where I’ll end up. I’m curious and scared, but excited too. Part of me really wants to stay, but you need to pick your own next door to open. So do I.

Jason, I wish you only happiness. Be safe. Amanda

They shout at me.

Taunts and insults.

I ignore them.

Walk faster.

But I know I’m in trouble when they cross the street, heading straight for me.

For a moment, I think about running, but they’re young and definitely faster. Besides, it hits me as my mouth goes dry and fear floods me with adrenaline that I might need my strength.

On the edge of a neighborhood, where the houses end and a train yard starts, they reach me.

There’s no one else out at this hour.

No help in sight.

They’re even younger than I thought, and the smell of cheap beer hangs around them like bad cologne. The wild look in their eyes tells me they’ve been out all night, maybe looking for something like this to happen.

The beating really starts.

They don’t even bother to trash talk.

I’m too tired, too worn out to fight back.

Before I know it, I’m on the ground getting kicked in the stomach, the back, the face.

I black out for a second, and when I wake up, I feel their hands all over me, searching—I guess—for a wallet that I don't have.

They finally grab my backpack, and as I bleed on the ground, they run off down the street, laughing.

I lie there for a long time, listening to the traffic get louder and louder.

The day gets brighter.

People walk past me on the sidewalk without stopping.

Each breath sends a stab of pain through my bruised ribs, and my left eye is swollen shut.

After a while, I manage to sit up.

Shit.

The ampoules.

Using a chain-link fence, I pull myself onto my feet.

Please.

I slide my hand up inside my shirt, my fingers touching the piece of duct tape stuck to my side.

It hurts like hell to peel it back slowly, but everything hurts like hell.

The ampoules are still there.

Three crushed.

Three intact.

—

I stumble back into the box and shut myself inside.

My money is gone.

My notebooks are gone.

My syringes and needles.

I have nothing but my broken body and three more chances to fix this.

I spend the first half of the day begging on a South Side street corner to get enough money for a train into the city.

I spend the rest of it four blocks from my brownstone, sitting on the pavement behind a cardboard sign that reads:

HOMELESS. DESPERATE. ANYTHING HELPS.

My bruised face must really get people's attention, because I collect $28.15 by the time the sun goes down.

I’m hungry, thirsty, and in pain.

I pick a diner that looks run-down enough to let me in, and as I pay for my meal, I feel completely exhausted.

I have nowhere to go.

No money for a motel room.

Outside, the night has turned cold and rainy.

I walk to my house and go around the block to the alley, trying to find a place where I can sleep without being bothered or seen.

There’s a space between my garage and the neighbor’s, hidden behind the trash and recycling bins. I squeeze between them, carrying a flattened box, which I prop against my garage wall.

Underneath it, I listen to the rain hitting the cardboard above, hoping my flimsy shelter will hold up through the night.

From where I am, I can see over the tall fence around my backyard, through a window, into the second floor of my house.

It’s the master bedroom.

Jason walks by.

On my eighth day living on the streets of Logan Square, Jason Dessen himself puts a $5 bill in my collection box.

There’s no risk.

I’m impossible to identify.

Sunburned, with a beard, and smelling like utter poverty.

The people around here are kind. Every day, I earn enough to buy a cheap dinner and save a little.

Every night, I sleep in the alley behind 44 Eleanor Street.

It turns into a sort of game. When the lights go off in the main bedroom, I close my eyes and pretend I’m him.

With her.

Some days, I feel like I'm losing my mind.

Amanda once said her old life felt like a ghost, and I think I get it. We link reality to what we can touch—everything we sense. I keep telling myself there’s a box in Chicago that can take me to a world where I have it all, but I don’t believe it anymore. My reality—more and more—is this world. Where I have nothing. Where I’m a dirty, homeless thing that just makes people feel sorry, sad, and sick.

A homeless man is standing on the sidewalk, loudly talking to no one.

I wonder, Am I any different? Aren’t we both lost in worlds that, through no fault of our own, don't fit who we are anymore?

The scariest times seem to be happening more often. Times when the idea of a magic box sounds crazy, even to me.

I'm standing in the main bedroom of 44 Eleanor Street, looking down at Jason and Daniela, asleep under the blankets.

The clock says 3:38 a.m. The house is silent, but I’m so drunk I can feel my heart in my ears.

I can’t understand how I got here.

All I can think is that I had this.

Once.

This beautiful dream of a life.

As I stumble out of the bedroom and down the hall, I realize that even thinking about this meant giving up on finding my Daniela.

Like I’m letting her go.

I go to a thrift store and buy new clothes—jeans, flannel shirt, a black peacoat.

I check into a motel, throw my old clothes away, and take the longest shower of my life.

The water running off my body is gray.

Standing in front of the mirror, I look almost like myself again, though my cheekbones stick out more because I haven't been eating well.

I sleep until the afternoon and then take the train down to the South Side.

The power plant is quiet, sunlight coming in at an angle through the windows of the generator room.

Sitting in the doorway of the box, I open the notebook.

Ever since I woke up, I’ve been thinking about what Amanda said in her goodbye note, how I haven’t really written about what I feel.

I’m twenty-seven years old. I’ve worked all morning at the lab, and things are going so well I almost ignore the party. I’ve been doing that a lot lately—neglecting friends and social events to steal just a few more hours in the cleanroom.

I first notice you in the far corner of the small backyard as I stand on the deck, sipping a Corona-and-lime, my thoughts still back at the lab. I think it’s the way you’re standing that catches my attention—stuck with a tall, lanky guy in tight black jeans who I recognize from this circle of friends. He’s an artist or something. I don’t even know his name, only that my friend Kyle has said to me recently, Oh, that guy sleeps with everyone.

I can’t explain it, even to this day, but as I watch him chatting up this dark-haired, dark-eyed woman in a cobalt-blue dress—you—a flash of jealousy hits me. For some reason, insanely, I want to hit him. Something in your body language suggests you're not comfortable. You aren’t smiling, your arms are crossed, and it seems like you’re trapped in a bad conversation, and that for some reason, I care. You hold an empty wineglass, streaked with the remains of a red. Part of me urges, Go talk to her, save her. The other half screams, You know nothing about this woman, not even her name. You are not that guy.

I find myself moving toward you through the grass, carrying a new glass of wine, and when your eyes look at mine, it feels like something has just broken in my chest. Like worlds colliding. As I get closer, you take the glass out of my hand as if you had previously sent me off to get it and smile with an easy familiarity, like we’ve known each other forever. You try to introduce me to Dillon, but the skinny-jeaned artist, now blocked, makes his excuses and leaves.

Then it’s just the two of us standing in the shade of the hedgerow, and my heart is going like mad. I say, “I’m sorry to interrupt, but it looked like you might need rescuing,” and you say, “Good instincts. He’s pretty, but awful.” I introduce myself. You tell me your name. Daniela. Daniela.

I only remember pieces of what was said in our first moments together. Mainly how you laugh when I tell you I’m an atomic physicist, but not in a mean way. As if the news truly delights you. I remember how the wine had stained your lips. I’ve always known, on a purely intellectual level, that our separateness and isolation are an illusion. We’re all made of the same thing—the blown-out pieces of matter formed in the fires of dead stars. I’ve just never felt that knowledge in my bones until that moment, there, with you. And it’s because of you.

Yes, maybe I just want to get laid, but I also wonder if this sense of connection might be evidence of something deeper. This thought I wisely keep to myself. I remember the pleasant buzz from the beer and the warmth of the sun, and then, as it begins to drop, realizing how badly I want to leave this party with you but not having the guts to ask. And then you say, “I have a friend whose gallery opening is tonight. Want to come?”

And I think: I will go anywhere with you.
