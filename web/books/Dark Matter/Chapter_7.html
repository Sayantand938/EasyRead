Dark Matter

Chapter 7

The single lightbulb hanging from the ceiling casts a stark, flickering light on the small cell. I’m strapped to a steel bed, my ankles and wrists chained and locked to bolts in the concrete wall.

Three locks click open in the door, but I’m too drugged to even flinch.

It opens.

Leighton is wearing a tux.

Wire-rim glasses.

As he gets closer, I smell cologne, and then alcohol. Champagne? I wonder where he’s been. A party? A fundraiser? There’s a pink ribbon still on his jacket.

Leighton sits down gently on the edge of the thin mattress.

He looks serious.

And deeply sad.

“I’m sure you have things to say, Jason, but I hope you’ll let me go first. I blame myself a lot for what happened. You came back, and we weren’t ready for you to be as…sick as you were. As you are. We failed you, and I’m sorry. I don’t know what else to say. I just…I hate everything that’s happened. Your return should have been a celebration.”

Even with the strong drugs, I’m shaking with grief.

With rage.

“The man who came to Daniela’s apartment—did you send him after me?” I ask.

“You left me no choice. Even the chance you had told her about this place—”

“Did you tell him to kill her?”

“Jason—”

“Did you?”

He doesn’t answer, but that’s an answer itself.

I stare into Leighton’s eyes, and all I can think about is ripping his face off down to his skull.

“You fucking…”

I break down.

Sobbing.

I can't get the image of blood running down Daniela’s bare foot out of my head.

“I’m so sorry, brother.” Leighton reaches out, puts his hand on my arm, and I almost pull my shoulder out of its socket trying to get away.

“Don’t touch me!”

“You’ve been in this cell almost twenty-four hours. I don’t like keeping you tied down and drugged, but as long as you’re a danger to yourself or others, this can’t change. You need to eat and drink something. Will you do that?”

I stare at a crack in the wall.

I imagine smashing Leighton’s head against it to make another one.

Pounding it into the concrete over and over until there’s nothing left but red goo.

“Jason, either you let them feed you, or I put a feeding tube in your stomach.”

I want to tell him I’m going to kill him. Him and everyone in this lab. I can feel the words rising in my throat, but I stop myself—I’m completely at this man’s mercy.

“I know what you saw in that apartment was horrible, and I’m sorry for that. I wish it had never happened, but sometimes, things get so out of control…Look, please know that I am so, so sorry you had to see that.”

Leighton rises, moves toward the door, and pulls it open.

Standing in the doorway, he looks back at me, his face half in light, half in shadow.

He says, “Maybe you can’t hear this right now, but this place wouldn’t exist without you. None of us would be here if it weren’t for your work, your brilliance. I’m not going to let anyone forget that, especially you.”

—

I calm down.

I pretend to calm down.

Because staying chained up in this tiny cell isn’t doing a damn thing.

From the bed, I stare up at the surveillance camera over the door and ask for Leighton.

Five minutes later, he’s unlocking my restraints and saying, “I think I’m probably as happy as you are to get you out of these things.”

He gives me a hand up.

My wrists are rubbed raw from the leather straps.

My mouth is dry.

I’m lightheaded with thirst.

He asks, “You feeling any better?”

It hits me that my first thought when I woke up here was right. Act like the man they think I am. The only way to do that is to pretend I’ve lost my memories and who I am. Let them guess the rest. Because if I’m not the man they think I am, then they don’t need me.

Then I never leave this lab alive.

I tell him, “I was scared. That’s why I ran.”

"I understand completely."

"I’m sorry I made you all go through this, but you have to realize—I’m lost here. There’s just this huge void where the last ten years should be."

"And we’re going to do everything we can to help you get those memories back. To make you well again. We’re starting the MRI. We’re going to check you for PTSD. Our psychiatrist, Amanda Lucas, will be talking to you soon. I promise you—we will look at everything until we solve this. Until you are completely yourself again."

"Thank you."

"You’d do the same for me. Look, I have no idea what you’ve been through these last fourteen months, but the man I’ve known for eleven years, my colleague and friend who built this place with me? He’s trapped somewhere in your head, and there is nothing I won’t do to find him."

A terrifying thought—what if he’s right?

I think I know who I am.

But there’s a part of me that wonders…What if the memory I have of my real life—husband, father, professor—isn’t real?

What if it’s a result of brain damage I got while working in this lab?

What if I’m actually the man who everyone in this world believes I am?

No.

I know who I am.

Leighton's been sitting on the edge of the mattress.

Now he puts his feet up and leans back against the footboard.

"I have to ask," he says. "What were you doing at that woman's apartment?"

Lie.

"I'm not really sure."

"How did you know her?"

I try to hide the tears and anger.

"I dated her a long time ago."

“Let’s go back to the start. After you got out through the bathroom window three nights ago, how did you get to your place in Logan Square?”

“A cab.”

“Did you tell the driver anything about where you had just been?”

“Of course not.”

“Okay, and after you got away from us at your house, then where did you go?”

Lie.

“I walked around all night. I was confused, scared. The next day I saw this poster for Daniela’s art show. That’s how I found her.”

“Did you talk to anyone else besides Daniela?”

Ryan.

“No.”

“You’re sure about that?”

“Yes. I went back to her apartment, and it was just the two of us until…”

“You have to understand—we’ve given everything to this place. To your work. We’re all in. Any one of us would die to protect it. Including you.”

The gunshot.

The black hole between her eyes.

“It breaks my heart to see you like this, Jason.”

He says this with real sadness and regret.

I can see it in his eyes.

“We were friends?” I ask.

He nods, his jaw tight, like he’s holding back tears.

I say, “I’m having trouble understanding how murdering someone to protect this lab could be okay with you or anyone here.”

“The Jason Dessen I knew wouldn’t have cared about what happened to Daniela Vargas. I’m not saying he would’ve been happy about it. None of us are. It disgusts me. But he would’ve been willing.”

I shake my head.

He says, “You’ve forgotten what we built together.”

“Then show me.”

They clean me up, give me new clothes, and feed me.

After lunch, Leighton and I take a service elevator down to sublevel four.

Last time I walked this corridor, it was covered in plastic, and I didn’t know where I was.

I haven’t been threatened.

Haven’t been told I can’t leave.

But I’ve noticed Leighton and I are rarely alone. Two men who look like cops are always nearby. I remember these guards from my first night here.

“It’s four levels,” Leighton says. “Gym, rec room, mess hall, and some dorms on one. Labs, cleanrooms, conference rooms on two. Level three is for making things. Four is the infirmary and mission control.”

We’re heading toward vault doors that look strong enough to protect national secrets.

Leighton stops at a touchscreen on the wall beside them.

He takes out a keycard and holds it under the scanner.

A female computer voice says, Name, please.

He leans in. “Leighton Vance.”

Passcode.

“One-one-eight-seven.”

Voice recognition confirmed. Welcome, Dr. Vance.

The sound of a buzzer startles me, its echo fading down the corridor behind us.

The doors open slowly.

I step into a hangar.

From the rafters high above, lights blaze down, lighting up a twelve-foot cube the color of gunmetal.

My pulse rate quickens.

I can’t believe what I’m looking at.

Leighton must sense my awe, because he says, “Beautiful, isn’t it?”

It is exquisitely beautiful.

At first, I think the hum inside the hangar is coming from the lights, but it can’t be. It’s so deep I can feel it at the base of my spine, like the very low vibration of a huge engine.

I drift toward the box, mesmerized.

I never imagined I would see it in person, this big.

Up close, it isn’t smooth but bumpy, and reflects the light in a way that makes it look like it has many sides, almost see-through.

Leighton points to the clean concrete floor shining under the lights. “We found you passed out right there.”

We walk slowly next to the box.

I reach out, letting my fingers brush the surface.

It’s cold to the touch.

Leighton says, “Eleven years ago, after you won the Pavia, we came to you and said we had five billion dollars. We could have built a spaceship, but we gave it all to you. To see what you could do with unlimited money.”

I ask, “Is my work here? My notes?”

“Of course.”

We reach the other side of the box.

He leads me around the next corner.

On this side, a door has been made in the cube.

“What’s inside?” I ask.

“See for yourself.”

The bottom of the door frame is about a foot above the hangar floor.

I lower the handle, push it open, and start to step inside.

Leighton puts a hand on my shoulder.

“No further,” he says. “For your own safety.”

“It’s dangerous?”

“You were the third person to go inside. Two more went in after you. So far, you’re the only one to come back.”

“What happened to them?”

“We don’t know. Recording devices don’t work inside. The only information we can get now has to come from someone who makes it back. Like you.”

The inside of the box is empty, plain, and dark.

Walls, floor, and ceiling are made of the same material as the outside.

Leighton says, “It’s soundproof, radiation-proof, airtight, and, as you might have guessed, puts out a strong magnetic field.”

As I close the door, a deadbolt clicks into place on the other side.

Staring at the box is like seeing a dead dream brought back to life.

In my late twenties, I worked on a box much like this one. But it was a one-inch cube designed to put a visible object into superposition.

Into what we physicists sometimes call, in our version of humor, cat state.

Like in the Schrödinger's cat idea.

Picture a cat, a poison bottle, and something radioactive inside a closed box. If a sensor spots radioactivity, like an atom breaking down, the bottle breaks open, and poison kills the cat. The atom is equally likely to break down or not.

It cleverly connects something in our normal world to something happening at the quantum level.

The Copenhagen idea of quantum mechanics says something strange: before we open the box, before we look, the atom is in superposition—an unsure state of both breaking down and not. This also means the cat is both alive and dead.

Only when we open the box and look do we see just one thing.

We only see one of the possibilities.

Like a dead cat.

And that's what we call reality.

But then it gets really strange.

Is there another world, just as real as ours, where we opened the box and found a happy, living cat?

The Many-Worlds view of quantum mechanics agrees.

That when we open the box, reality splits.

In one world, we find a dead cat.

In another, it’s alive.

And it’s our act of seeing the cat that decides its fate—killing it or letting it live.

Then it gets incredibly strange.

Because these kinds of observations happen constantly.

So, if the world splits every time something is seen, there’s a countless, endless number of worlds—a multiverse—where everything possible happens.

My idea for my small cube was to make a space safe from being watched and from outside distractions. This way, my large object—an aluminum nitride disc, 40 µm long and made of about a trillion atoms—could freely exist in that undecided cat state and not fall apart because of its surroundings.

I never solved that before my money ran out, but it seems another version of me did. And then they blew the whole idea up to a level I can't even imagine. Because if Leighton is right, this box does something that, based on all my physics knowledge, is impossible.

I feel shamed, like I lost a race to a better opponent. A man with incredible ideas built this box.

A smarter, better me.

I look at Leighton.

“Does it work?”

He says, “The fact that you’re standing here beside me seems to suggest that it does.”

“I don’t get it. If you wanted to put a particle in a quantum state in a lab, you’d create an isolated chamber. Remove all light, suck out the air, turn down the temperature to almost absolute zero. It would kill a human being. And the larger you go, the more fragile it all becomes. Even though we’re underground, there are all sorts of particles—neutrinos, cosmic rays—passing through that cube that could disturb a quantum state. The challenge seems impossible.”

“I don’t know what to tell you….You overcame it.”

“How?”

Leighton smiles. “Look, it made sense when you explained it to me, but I can’t really explain it back. You should read your notes. What I can tell you is that box creates and maintains an environment where everyday objects can exist in a quantum superposition.”

“Including us?”

“Including us.”

Okay.

Even though everything I know tells me it’s impossible, I seem to have found a way to make a working quantum environment big enough to see. Maybe I’m using the magnetic field to link things inside to the quantum system at the atomic level.

But what about whoever is inside the box?

People inside also watch what’s happening.

We live in one fixed reality because we’re always watching our surroundings and making our own possibilities settle into one.

There has to be more to it than that.

“Come on,” Leighton says. “I want to show you something.”

He leads me to a row of windows on the hangar wall that faces the box’s door.

He swipes his keycard at another locked door and takes me into a room like a communications center or mission control.

Right now, only one desk has someone working at it. A woman has her feet up, listening to headphones, not noticing us come in.

“Someone is always at that desk. We take turns waiting for someone to come back.”

Leighton sits at a computer, types in codes, and clicks through folders until he finds what he wants.

He opens a video.

It's HD, filmed by a camera facing the box's door, likely above these windows in mission control.

At the bottom, there's a timestamp from fourteen months ago, showing the time down to the hundredth of a second.

A man walks into view and goes to the box.

He has a backpack on over a spacesuit, and he's carrying the helmet under his arm.

At the door, he turns the handle and opens it. Before going in, he looks back at the camera.

It’s me.

I wave, step into the box, and shut myself inside.

Leighton speeds up the playback.

I watch the box sit still as fifty minutes rush by.

He slows the video down when someone new comes into view.

A woman with long brown hair walks toward the box and opens the door.

The camera view changes to a head-mounted GoPro.

It sweeps across the inside of the box, a light shining on the bare walls and floor, reflecting off the bumpy metal.

“And poof,” Leighton says. “You’re gone. Until…” He starts another file. “Three and a half days ago.”

I see myself stumble out of the box and fall to the floor, almost as if I was pushed.

More time passes, and then I watch the hazmat team show up and lift me onto a gurney.

It feels so incredibly strange to watch a recording of the exact moment my life turned into this nightmare.

My first seconds in this messed-up, brave new world.

—

One of the bedrooms on the first floor below us has been readied for me, and it’s much better than the cell.

A comfortable bed.

A full bathroom.

A desk with a vase of flowers that have filled the room with their scent.

Leighton says, “I hope you’ll be more comfortable here. I’ll be blunt: please don’t try to kill yourself. We’re watching for it. People will be right outside this door to stop you, and then you’ll be in a straitjacket in that awful cell downstairs. If you feel overwhelmed, just call and ask whoever answers to find me. Don’t suffer alone.”

He touches the laptop on the desk.

“It has all your work from the last fifteen years, even your research before Velocity Laboratories. No password. Look around. Maybe it’ll remind you of something.” As he leaves, he glances back and says, “This door will stay locked, by the way.” He smiles. “Just for your own good.”

I sit in bed with the laptop, trying to understand the huge amount of information in the thousands of folders.

They're organized by year, going back to before I won the Pavia award, to when I was in grad school and first started to realize what I wanted to do with my life.

The early folders have work I recognize—drafts of a paper that became my first published work, summaries of related articles, everything leading up to my time in that University of Chicago lab and the creation of the first tiny cube.

The cleanroom data is carefully sorted.

I read the files on the laptop until my vision blurs, but I keep going, watching my work improve beyond where I know it ended in my version of my life.

It’s like forgetting who you are and then reading your own biography.

I worked every day.

My notes got better, more detailed, more specific.

But I still struggled to find a way to create the superposition of my large disc, the frustration and sadness seeping into my notes.

I can’t keep my eyes open any longer.

Turning off the bedside lamp, I pull up the blankets.

It’s completely dark in here.

The only light in the room is a green dot high on the wall facing my bed.

It’s a camera, filming in the dark.

Someone is watching everything I do, every breath I take.

I close my eyes, trying to ignore it.

But I see the same thing that always haunts me: the blood running down her ankle, over her bare foot.

The empty space where her eyes should be.

It would be so easy to break.

To fall apart.

In the dark, I touch the thread on my ring finger and remind myself my other life is real, that it’s still out there.

Like standing on a beach as the tide pulls the sand from under my feet back to the sea, I feel my real world, and what holds it up, moving away.

I wonder: If I don’t fight it hard enough, will this world slowly lock in and take me away?

I spend the rest of the morning at the desk, eating breakfast and scrolling through files that tell of scientific achievements I don't remember.

Despite my current situation, it’s exciting to read my notes, seeing them move toward my breakthrough with the miniature cube.

The solution to creating the superposition of my disc?

Superconducting qubits linked with resonators that can register simultaneous states as vibrations. Sounds unbelievably boring, but it’s revolutionary.

It won me the Pavia.

It apparently got me here.

Ten years ago, on my first day at Velocity Laboratories, I wrote an interesting mission statement to the whole team, mainly explaining the ideas of quantum mechanics and the multiverse to them.

One part, a discussion about dimensionality, grabs my attention.

I wrote…

We see our world in three dimensions, but we don’t really live in a 3-D world. 3-D is still. A snapshot. We have to add a fourth dimension to start to describe how we exist.

The 4-D tesseract doesn’t add a space dimension. It adds a time one.

It adds time, a flow of 3-D cubes, showing space as it moves forward in time.

This is best shown by looking up at night at stars whose light took fifty years to reach us. Or five hundred. Or five billion. We’re not just looking into space, we’re looking back in time.

Our path through this 4-D space-time is our life's path (reality), starting with our birth and ending with our death. Four points (x, y, z, and t [time]) mark a spot in the tesseract.

And we think it ends there, but that’s only true if every result is unavoidable, if free will is fake, and our life's path is alone.

What if our life's path is just one of endless paths, some only a little changed from the life we know, others very different?

The Many-Worlds idea of quantum mechanics says that all possible realities exist. That everything that could happen is happening. Everything that might have happened in our past did happen, but in another universe.

What if that’s true?

What if we live in a fifth-dimension of possible realities?

What if we really live in the multiverse, but our brains have grown to give us a wall that limits what we see to one universe? One life's path. The one we pick, moment to moment. It makes sense if you think about it. We couldn’t handle seeing all possible realities at the same time.

So how do we get into this 5-D probability space?

And if we could, where would we end up?

“That’s too bad. I was hoping a trip down memory lane might help.”

He straightens up.

His chair creaks.

Something feels off.

Wrong.

We built jet engines and turbofans, and it was more about keeping the big government and corporate deals than doing advanced scientific exploration. There’s only twenty-three of us now, but one thing hasn’t changed. This company has always been a family, and our lifeblood is complete and total trust.”

He turns away from me and nods.

The lights kick on.

I can see through the tinted glass into the small theater, and it’s full, just like that first night, with fifteen or twenty people.

But no one is standing and clapping.

No one is smiling.

They’re all staring down at me.

Serious.

Stiff.

“I explained—”

“Exactly.”

Leighton opens the laptop on the table and types something.

He stands it up, types something on the touchscreen.

“What is this?” I ask. “What’s happening?”

“We’re going to finish what we started the night you came back. I’m going to ask questions, and this time, you’re going to answer them.”

I rise from the chair, move to the door, try to pull it open.

Locked.

“Sit!”

Leighton’s voice is as loud as a gunshot.

“I want to leave.”

“And I want you to start telling the truth.”

“I told you the truth.”

“No, you told Daniela Vargas the truth.”

On the other side of the glass, a door opens and a man stumbles into the theater, led by one of the guards holding the back of his neck.

The first man’s face is pressed against the glass.

Jesus Christ.

Ryan’s nose looks crooked, and one eye is completely closed.

His bruised and swollen face smears blood on the glass.

“You told Ryan Holder the truth,” Leighton says.

I hurry to Ryan and say his name.

He tries to answer, but I can’t hear him through the glass.

I stare angrily at Leighton.

He says, “Sit down, or I’ll have someone come in and tie you to that chair.”

The anger from before rushes back. This man caused Daniela’s death. Now this. I wonder how much harm I could do before they stopped me.

But I sit.

I ask, “You found him?”

“No, Ryan came to me, upset by what you told him at Daniela’s apartment. Those are the things I want to hear about right now.”

As I watch the guards force Ryan into a chair in the front row, it hits me—Ryan created the missing piece that makes the box work, this “compound” he mentioned at Daniela’s art installation. If our brain is made to stop us from seeing our own quantum state, then maybe there’s a drug that can turn off this block—the “firewall” I wrote about in that mission statement.

The Ryan from my world had been studying the prefrontal cortex and how it creates consciousness. It’s not hard to imagine this Ryan might have created a drug that changes how our brain sees reality. That stops us from losing our environment and collapsing our wave functions.

I snap back to the present.

“Why did you hurt him?” I ask.

“You told Ryan you’re a professor at Lakemont College, that you have a son, and that Daniela Vargas was your wife. You told him you were taken one night while walking home, and then you woke up here. You told him this isn’t your world. Do you admit you said these things?”

I wonder again how much damage I could do before someone dragged me away. Break his nose? Knock out teeth? Kill him?
