Dark Matter

Chapter 13

Snow from a recent storm has lightly covered the concrete and the generators under the open, upper windows.

Even now, snow flurries blow in from the lake, falling like cold confetti.

I walk away from the box, trying not to get my hopes up too high.

This could be any deserted power plant in South Chicago, in countless possible worlds.

As I slowly walk along the line of generators, something shiny on the floor catches my eye.

I go closer.

Lying in a crack in the concrete, six inches from the generator's base: an empty glass vial with its top broken off. In all the deserted power plants I’ve been in during the last month, I’ve never seen one of these.

Maybe the one Jason2 used right before I passed out, the night he stole my life.

—

I hike out of the industrial ghost town.

Hungry, thirsty, exhausted.

The skyline appears to the north, and even though the low winter clouds cut off the top, it’s clearly the one I know.

—

I get on the Red Line heading north at Eighty-Seventh Street as dusk falls.

There are no seat belts, no holograms on this El.

Just a slow, shaky ride through South Chicago.

Then the spread of downtown.

I change trains.

The Blue Line takes me into the updated northern neighborhoods.

For the past month, I've been in similar Chicagos, but this one feels different. It's not just the empty vial. It's something deeper that I can't explain, except to say it feels like where I belong. It feels like mine.

As we pass the jammed rush-hour traffic on the expressway, the snow gets heavier.

I wonder—

Is Daniela, my Daniela, alive and okay under the snowy clouds?

Is my Charlie breathing in this world?

—

I get off the train at the Logan Square El platform and shove my hands into my coat pockets. Snow is sticking to the familiar streets of my neighborhood. To the sidewalks. To the cars parked along the curbs. Headlights from rush-hour traffic cut through the heavy snowfall.

Up and down my block, the houses glow, looking beautiful in the storm.

A thin layer has already gathered on my porch steps, where one set of footprints leads to the door.

Through the front window of the brownstone, I see the lights are on, and from the sidewalk, it looks exactly like home.

I keep expecting to find something small that’s wrong—the wrong front door, the wrong street number, a piece of furniture on the porch I don’t know.

But the door is right.

The street number is right.

There’s even a tesseract chandelier above the dinner table in the front room, and I can almost see the big picture on the mantel—Daniela, Charlie, and me at Inspiration Point in Yellowstone National Park.

Through the open door from the dining room to the kitchen, I see Jason standing at the island, holding a bottle of wine. He reaches over and pours some into someone’s wineglass.

I feel happy all of a sudden, but it doesn’t last.

From where I am, I only see a pretty hand holding the wineglass, and it hits me again what this man did to me.

All that he took.

Everything he stole.

I can’t hear anything out here in the snow, but I see him laugh and take a drink of wine.

What are they talking about?

When was the last time they had sex?

Is Daniela happier now than she was a month ago, with me?

Can I bear to know the answer to that question?

The calm, sensible voice in my head is wisely suggesting that I leave the house right now.

I’m not ready to do this. I have no plan.

Only anger and jealousy.

And I shouldn’t jump to conclusions. I still need more proof that this is my life.

The license plate number is mine.

The paint is the right color.

I clear the back windshield.

The purple Lakemont Lions sticker looks perfect, even though it’s half ripped off. I instantly regretted putting the sticker on the glass the moment I did it. I tried to tear it off, but only managed to remove the top half of the lion’s face, so all that’s left is a growling mouth.

But that was three years ago.

I need something more recent, more definite.

Several weeks before I was abducted, I accidentally backed the Suburban into a parking meter near campus. It didn’t do much damage besides cracking the right rear taillight and denting in the bumper.

I wipe the snow off the red plastic of the taillight and then the bumper.

I touch the crack.

I touch the dent.

No other Suburban in all the Chicagos I’ve visited has these markings.

Standing up, I look across the street at the bench where I once spent a whole day watching another life I could have had play out. It’s empty now, snow quietly gathering on the seat.

Shit.

A few feet behind the bench, someone watches me in the snowy darkness.

I start walking fast down the sidewalk, probably looking like I was stealing the license plate off the Suburban.

I need to be more careful.

I can only afford two nights, which leaves me with just over $120.

Daniela Vargas-Dessen isn’t a famous Chicago artist, and she doesn’t have a graphic-design business. Her simple website shows some of her best art and says she teaches art.

As I walk up the stairs to my room on the third floor, I start to believe it.

This is my world.

I’ve never hit anyone.

Never even tried to.

But if I want my family back, there’s just no other way.

I have to do something awful.

Have to do what Jason2 did to me, but without the easy way out of just putting him back in the box. Even though I have one vial left, I won’t make the same mistake.

He should’ve killed me when he could.

Get rid of the Jason Dessen who’s living in my home and hide him where no one will ever find him.

A car.

A gun.

Something to tie him up.

A shovel.

A safe place to bury his body.

Yes, he took my wife, my son, my life, but the thought of planning and hurting him is horrible.

I could drive Jason there at night, or make him drive, just like he did to me.

Lead him down one of the trails I know on the north side of the river.

I will have been there a day or two before, so his grave will already be dug in some quiet, hidden spot. I’ll have looked up how deep to make it so animals can’t smell the decay. I will fire a bullet into the back of his head.

Then I’ll drag him over to the hole and roll him into it and cover him up with dirt.

The good news is that no one will be looking for him.

I’ll slide back into his life the same way he slid into mine.

Maybe years later, I’ll tell Daniela the truth.

Maybe I’ll never tell her.

A mystery.

Where should I even start?

I look Daniela in the eye and move a strand of hair behind her ear.

I look into Charlie’s eyes.

I tell them how much I love them.

That I've been through so much to be sitting here between them.

I start with when I was taken on a cold October night, forced to drive at gunpoint to an empty power plant in South Chicago.

I tell them about my fear, how I thought I was going to be killed, about waking up in a strange science lab, where people I didn't know seemed to know me and were waiting for me.

They listen closely as I explain how I escaped from Velocity Laboratories that first night, and came back to our house on Eleanor Street, to a home that wasn't my home, where I lived alone as a man who had chosen to focus on his research.

A world where Daniela and I had never married and Charlie was never born.

I tell Daniela about meeting someone who looked just like her at the art show in Bucktown.

Being captured and held in the lab.

Escaping with Amanda into the box.

I describe the multiverse.

Every door I walked through.

Every ruined world.

Every Chicago that was a little off, but got me closer to home.

There are things I leave out.

Things I can’t quite say yet.

The two nights with Daniela after the art show opening.

The two times I saw her die.

I’ll share these moments later, when it feels right.

I try to think how Daniela and Charlie must feel hearing this story.

When Daniela starts to cry, I ask, “Do you believe me?”

“Of course I believe you.”

“Charlie?”

My son nods, but his eyes show he's far away. He’s staring blankly at the shoppers walking by, and I wonder how much of what I’ve said has actually registered.

How can anyone even start to understand something like this?

Daniela wipes her eyes and says, “I just want to make sure I understand exactly what you’re saying. So the night you went to Ryan Holder’s party, this other Jason stole your life? He took you into the box and left you in his world so he could live in this one? With me?”

“That’s what I’m telling you.”

“That means the man I’ve been living with is a stranger.”

“Not completely. I think he and I were the same person until fifteen years ago.”

“What happened fifteen years ago?”

“You told me you were pregnant with Charlie. The multiverse exists because every choice we make creates a split, which leads to another world. That night you told me you were pregnant didn’t just happen the way you and I remember it. It happened in many different ways. In one world, the one we live in now, you and I decided to build a life together. We got married. Had Charlie. Made a home. In another, I decided that becoming a father in my late twenties wasn’t right for me. I worried my work would be lost, that I wouldn't reach my goals.”

So there’s a version of our life where we didn’t keep the baby. Charlie. You went after your art. I went after my science. And eventually, we split up. That man, the version of me you’ve been living with for the last month—he built the box.”

“Which is a big version of that thing you were working on when we first met—the cube?”

“Exactly. And at some point, he realized everything he’d lost by letting his work be his whole life. He regretted the choice he made fifteen years ago. But the box can’t take you back or forward in time. It only connects all possible worlds right now, in the present. So he searched until he found my world. And he traded my life for his.”

The look on Daniela’s face is complete shock and disgust.

She gets up from the bench and runs toward the restrooms.

Charlie starts after her, but I put my hand on his shoulder and say, “Just give her a minute.”

“I knew something was off.”

“What do you mean?” I ask.

“You—well, not you, him —he had this different kind of energy. We talked more, especially at dinner. He was just, I don’t know…”

“What?”

“Different.”

There are things I want to ask my son, questions burning in my mind.

Was he more fun?

A better father?

A better husband?

Was life more exciting with the fake?

But I’m afraid the answers might break me.

Daniela comes back.

So pale.

As she sits down, I ask, “Are you okay?”

"I have a question for you."

"What?"

"This morning, when you got arrested—was it to make me come to you?"

"Yes."

"Why? Why not just come to the house after…God, I don’t even know what to call him."

"Jason2."

"After Jason2 left?"

I say, "This is where it gets really wild."

Charlie asks, "Isn't it already wild?"

"I wasn't the only..." It sounds crazy just saying it.

But I have to tell them.

“What?” Daniela asks.

“I wasn’t the only me to return to this world.”

“What does that mean?” she asks.

“Other Jasons also made it back.”

“What other Jasons?”

“Versions of me who escaped into the box in that lab, but went different ways through the multiverse.”

“How many?” Charlie asks.

“I don’t know. Maybe a lot.”

I explain what happened at the sporting-goods store and in the chat room. I tell them about the Jason who followed me to my room and the one who attacked me with a knife.

My family’s confusion turns into clear fear.

I say, “This is why I got arrested. For all I know, many Jasons have been watching you, following you, tracking everything you do to figure out what to do. I needed you to come to me in a safe place. That’s why I had you call the car service. I know at least one version of me followed you to the police station. I saw him as we drove past your Honda. This is why I wanted you to bring Charlie with you. But it doesn’t matter. We’re here together, and safe, and now you both know the truth.”

It takes Daniela a moment to speak.

She says softly, “These other…Jasons…what are they like?”

“What are you asking?”

“Do they all have your past? Are they basically you?”

“Yes. Until I stepped into the multiverse. Then we all went different ways, had different experiences.”

“But some are just like you? Versions of my husband who have fought hard to get back to this world. Who just want to be with me again. With Charlie.”

“Yeah.”

Her eyes narrow.

What must this be like for her?

I can see her trying to understand how this could even be possible.

“Dani, look at me.”

I stare into her bright eyes.

I say, “I love you.”

“I love you too. But so do the others, right? Just as much as you do.”

It guts me to hear those words.

I have no response.

I look at the people close to us, wondering if we’re being watched.

The balcony has gotten more crowded since we sat down.

I see a woman pushing a stroller.

Young lovers walking slowly through the mall, holding hands and ice-cream cones, lost in their happiness.

An old man shuffling along behind his wife, with a look on his face that says, Take me home, please.

We’re not safe here.

We’re not safe anywhere in this city.

I ask, “Are you with me?”

She hesitates, looks at Charlie.

Then back at me.

“Yeah,” she says. “I’m with you.”

“Good.”

"So, what should we do now?"
