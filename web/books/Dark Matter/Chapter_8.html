Dark Matter

Chapter 8

I sit up.

Clearheaded and alert.

Amanda isn’t lying on the floor anymore. She’s standing a few feet away with her back to me.

I call out to her, ask if she’s okay, but she doesn’t answer.

I struggle to stand.

Amanda is holding the lantern, and as I move toward her, I see that the light isn’t hitting the wall of the box, which should be right in front of us.

I walk past her.

She follows with the lantern.

The light shows another door, just like the one we came through from the hangar.

I keep walking.

Another twelve feet and we're at another door.

And then another.

And another.

The lantern shines like a single, sixty-watt bulb, and beyond seventy or eighty feet, the light fades into eerie patches, reflecting off the cold metal walls on one side, and the evenly spaced doors on the other.

Beyond our circle of light—total darkness.

I stop, amazed and without words.

I think of the thousands of articles and books I've read. Tests taken. Classes taught. Theories learned. Equations written on blackboards. I think of the months I spent in that cleanroom trying to make something that was a weak copy of this place.

For physics and cosmology students, the closest they get to seeing real-world results is ancient galaxies through telescopes. Data showing particle crashes that we know happened but can't see.

There’s always a limit, a wall between the equations and what they mean in reality.

But no more. Not for me anyway.

I can't stop thinking, I am here. I am really in this place. It's real.

For now, at least, I'm not afraid.

I’m filled with awe.

I say, “ ‘The most beautiful thing we can experience is the mysterious.’ ”

Amanda looks at me.

“Einstein said that, not me.”

“Is this place even real?” she asks.

“What do you mean by ‘real’?”

“Are we standing in an actual place?”

“I think it’s our mind trying to picture something our brains aren’t built to understand.”

“Which is?”

“Superposition.”

“So, we’re in a quantum state right now?”

I look back down the corridor. Then into the darkness ahead. Even in the dim light, the space seems to repeat itself, like two mirrors facing each other.

“Yeah. It looks like a corridor, but I think it’s the box repeating in every possible reality that shares this point in space and time.”

“Like a cross-section?”

“Exactly. In some explanations of quantum mechanics, the thing that holds all the information for the system—before it changes when observed—is called a wave function. I think this corridor is our minds’ way of seeing what’s in the wave function, all the possible results of our superposed quantum state.”

“So where does this corridor lead?” she asks. “If we kept walking, where would we end up?”

As I say the words, the wonder fades and the horror returns: “There is no end.”

We keep walking to see what happens, if anything will change, if we will change.

But it’s just door after door after door after door.

After walking a while, I say, “I’ve been counting since we started, and this is the four hundred and fortieth door. Each box is twelve feet, so we’ve already gone a full mile.”

Amanda stops and lets her backpack fall off her shoulders.

She sits against the wall, and I sit beside her, with the lantern between us.

I say, “What if Leighton takes the drug and comes charging in here after us?”

“He’d never do that.”

“Why?”

“Because he’s terrified of the box. We all are. Except for you, no one who went inside ever came out again. That’s why Leighton would do anything to make you tell him how to fly it.”

“What happened to your test pilots?”

“The first one in the box was a guy named Matthew Snell. We didn't know what to expect, so Snell got simple instructions. Go in the box. Close the door. Sit. Inject the drug. No matter what, no matter what he saw, he had to stay put, wait for the drug to fade, and walk back out. Even if he saw everything, he wouldn't leave his box. He wouldn't move.”

“So what happened?”

“An hour went by. He was late. We wanted to open the door, but we didn't want to mess with whatever he was going through inside. After twenty-four hours, we opened it.”

“And the box was empty.”

“Yep.” Amanda looks tired in the blue light. “Going into the box and taking the drug is like going through a door you can't return from. There’s no going back, and no one will risk following us. We’re on our own. So, what do you want to do?”

“Like any good scientist, experiment. Try a door, see what happens.”

“And just so I’m clear, you have no idea what’s behind any of these doors?”

“None.”

I help Amanda up. As I lift the backpack, I feel a bit thirsty and wonder if she brought any water.

We head down the corridor, and the truth is I’m unsure which way to go. If there are endless doors to choose from, then each choice both matters completely and doesn't matter at all. Every choice is right. Every choice is wrong.

I finally stop and say, “This one?”

She shrugs. “Sure.”

Gripping the cold, metal handle, I ask, “We have the ampoules, right? Because that would be—”

“I checked the pack when we stopped a minute ago.”

I push the lever down, hear the lock click open, and pull back.

The door swings open.

She whispers, “What do you see?”

“Nothing yet. It’s too dark. Here, give me that.” As I take the lantern from her, I notice we’re standing in a small space again. “Look,” I say. “The corridor is gone.”

“Does that surprise you?”

"Actually, it makes perfect sense. What's outside is affecting what's inside the box. It messed up the quantum state."

I turn back to the open door and hold the lantern out. I can only see the ground right in front of me.

Cracked pavement.

Oil stains.

When I step down, glass crunches under my feet.

I help Amanda out, and as we take a few steps, the light spreads, hits a concrete column.

A van.

A convertible.

A sedan.

It’s a parking garage.

We move up a small hill with cars on both sides, following what's left of a white paint stripe that separates the lanes.

The box is far behind us now and out of sight, hidden in the dark.

We pass a sign with an arrow pointing left next to the words—

EXIT TO STREET

Turning a corner, we start climbing the next ramp.

All along the right side, pieces have fallen from the ceiling and crushed the windshields, hoods, and roofs of the cars. The farther we go, the worse it gets, until we’re climbing over concrete chunks and going around sharp pieces of rusty metal.

Halfway up the next level, we're stopped by a wall of trash we can't get past.

“Maybe we should just go back,” I say.

“Look…” She grabs the lantern, and I follow her to a stairwell entrance.

The door is partly open, and Amanda pushes it all the way.

Total darkness.

We go up to the door at the top of the stairs.

It takes both of us to pull it open.

Wind blows through the lobby ahead.

Some light comes through the empty steel frames of the huge, two-story windows.

At first, I think it’s snow on the floor, but it isn’t cold.

I kneel and grab a handful. It’s dry and a foot deep over the marble floor. It slides through my fingers.

We walk past a long desk with the hotel’s name still on it in block letters.

At the entrance, we walk between two giant planters holding trees that have withered into twisted branches and dry leaf pieces fluttering in the breeze.

Amanda turns off the lantern.

We step through the empty revolving doors.

Even though it's not very cold, it looks like a huge snowstorm outside.

I walk into the street and look up between the dark buildings at a sky with a hint of red. It glows like a city does when the clouds are low and the lights from the buildings bounce off the dampness in the sky.

But there are no lights.

Not one that I can see.

Though they fall like snow, in heavy sheets, the bits that hit my face don't hurt.

"It's ash," Amanda says.

A snowstorm of ash.

Out here in the street, it's knee-deep, and the air smells like a cold fireplace the next morning, before the ashes are removed.

A dead, burnt smell.

The ash is falling so heavily it hides the tops of the skyscrapers, and the only sound is the wind blowing between and through the buildings, and the whoosh of ash piling into gray drifts against old, abandoned cars and buses.

I can’t believe what I’m seeing.

That I’m really standing in a world that isn’t mine.

We walk up the middle of the street, our backs to the wind.

I can’t get over how wrong the blackness of the skyscrapers looks. They’re skeletons, just scary shapes in the falling ash. They look more like strange mountains than anything made by people. Some are leaning, and some have fallen, and in the strongest gusts, high above us, I can hear the steel frames groan as they bend too far.

I feel a sudden tightening behind my eyes.

It comes and goes quickly, like something shutting off.

Amanda asks, “Did you feel that too?”

“That pressure behind your eyes?”

“Exactly.”

“I did. I bet it’s the drug wearing off.”

After several blocks, the buildings stop. We get to a guardrail along the top of a seawall. The lake stretches out for miles under the glowing sky, and it doesn’t even look like Lake Michigan anymore, but a huge gray desert, the ash gathering on the water’s surface and moving like a waterbed as black foam waves hit the seawall.

The walk back is against the wind.

Ash blowing into our eyes and mouths.

Our footprints already gone.

When we’re a block from the hotel, a sound like never-ending thunder starts nearby.

The ground shakes under us.

Another building collapses.

—

The box is where we left it, in a hidden spot on the parking garage's bottom floor.

We’re both covered in ash, and we pause at the door to brush it off our clothes and out of our hair.

Back inside, the lock clicks shut behind us.

We’re in a simple, small box again.

Four walls.

A door.

A lantern.

A backpack.

And two confused people.

—

Amanda sits hugging her knees to her chest.

“What do you think happened up there?” she asks.

“Supervolcano. Asteroid strike. Nuclear war. Who knows.”

“Are we in the future?”

“No, the box only connects us to other realities at the same point in space and time. But some worlds might seem like the future if they have technologies we never invented.”

“What if they're all destroyed like this one?”

I say, “We should take the drug again. I don’t think we’re safe under this falling skyscraper.”

Amanda takes off her flats and shakes the ash out of them.

I say, “What you did for me back at the lab…You saved my life.”

She looks at me, her bottom lip about to quiver. “I used to dream about the first pilots who went into the box. Nightmares. I can’t believe this is happening.”

I unzip the backpack and start taking things out to see what we have.

I find the leather bag with the ampoules and injection kits.

Three notebooks sealed in plastic.

Box of pens.

A knife in a nylon case.

First-aid kit.

Space blanket.

Rain poncho.

Toiletry kit.

Two rolls of cash.

Geiger counter.

Compass.

Two one-liter water bottles, both full.

Six MREs.

“You packed all this?” I ask.

“No, I just grabbed it from the stockroom. It’s standard issue, what everyone takes into the box. We should be wearing space suits, but I didn’t have time to grab any.”

“Seriously. A world like that? Radiation could be really high, or the air could be completely different. If the pressure is too low, our blood and all the liquids in our bodies will boil.”

The water bottles are calling out to me. I haven’t had anything to drink in hours, since lunch. I’m really thirsty.

I open the leather bag. It looks like it was made just for the ampoules, each glass vial held in its own small sleeve.

I begin to count them.

“Fifty,” Amanda says. “Well, forty-eight now. I would’ve grabbed two backpacks, but…”

“You weren’t planning to come with me.”

“How screwed are we?” she asks. “Be honest.”

“I don’t know. But this is our spaceship. We’d better learn to fly it.”

As I begin to cram everything back into the pack, Amanda reaches for the injection kits.

This time, we break the necks of the ampoules and drink the drug, the liquid sliding across my tongue with a sweet, almost unpleasant sting.

Forty-six ampoules remaining.

I start the timer on Amanda’s watch and ask, “How many times can we take this stuff and not wreck our brains?”

“We did some testing a while back.”

“Pulled some homeless guy off the street?”

She almost smiles. “Nobody died. We learned that using it a lot really messes with your brain and you get used to it. The good news is it leaves your system fast, so as long as we’re not taking one right after another, we should be okay.” She slides her feet back into her flats, looks at me. “Are you impressed with yourself?”

“What do you mean?”

“You built this thing.”

“Yeah, but I still don’t know how. I get the idea, but making a stable quantum state for people is…”

“An impossible breakthrough?”

Of course. The hair on my neck stands up as the unlikelihood of it all clicks.

I say, “It’s a one in a billion chance, but we’re talking about the multiverse. About infinity. Maybe there are a million worlds like yours, where I never figured it out. But all it takes is one where I did.”

At thirty minutes, I feel the first effect of the drug—a flickering, bright sense of joy.

A beautiful detachment.

Though not quite as strong as in the Velocity Laboratories box.

I look at Amanda.

I say, “I think I feel it.”

She says, “Me too.”

And we’re back in the corridor.

I ask, “Is your watch still going?”

Amanda pulls back her sweater sleeve and lights up the watch face, making it glow green.

31:15.

31:16.

31:17.

I say, “So it’s been a little over thirty-one minutes since we took the drug. Do you know how long it’s supposed to change our brain chemistry?”

“I’ve heard about an hour.”

“Let’s time it to be sure.”

I move back to the parking garage door and pull it open.

Now I’m staring into a forest.

But there’s no green.

No life.

Just burnt trees as far as I can see.

The trees look haunted, their thin branches like black spiderwebs against a dark gray sky.

I close the door.

It locks on its own.

I feel dizzy as I watch the box move away from me again, fading into the distance.

I unlock the door, pull it open.

The hallway vanishes again.

The dead forest is still there.

I say, “Okay, so now we know the doors only link to these worlds while we’re on the drug. That’s why none of your pilots ever got back to the lab.”

“So the hallway restarts when the drug starts working?”

“I think so.”

“Then how do we get back home?”

—

Amanda starts walking.

Faster and faster.

Until she's jogging.

Then running.

Into a darkness that never shifts.

Never stops.

The edge of everything.

The effort makes me sweat and my thirst is awful, but I don't speak, figuring maybe she needs this. Needs to use up some energy. Needs to see that no matter how far she runs, this hall won't end.

I guess we're both just trying to deal with how scary forever really is.

—

Finally, she's worn out.

Slows down.

There’s nothing but the sound of our footsteps echoing into the darkness ahead.

I feel dizzy from hunger and thirst, and I keep thinking about the two liters of water in our backpack, wanting them, but knowing we need to save them.

Now we move carefully down the corridor.

I hold the lantern to check every wall of every box.

I don’t know exactly what I’m looking for.

Maybe something different or out of place.

Anything that might give us some control over where we go.

All the while, my thoughts race in the dark—

What will happen when we run out of water?

When we run out of food?

When the batteries in this lantern—our only light—die?

How will I ever get home?

I wonder how long it's been since we entered the box back at Velocity Laboratories.

I can't keep track of time.

I'm weakening.

I'm so tired that sleep seems better than water.

I look at Amanda; her face is cold but beautiful in the blue light.

She looks scared.

"Hungry yet?" she asks.

"Almost."

“I’m really thirsty, but we should save the water, right?”

“I think that’s the smart thing to do.”

She says, “I feel so lost, and it’s getting worse all the time. I grew up in North Dakota, and we used to get these crazy blizzards. Whiteouts. You’d be driving out on the plains, and the snow would start blowing so hard you’d lose all sense of direction. Blowing so hard it’d make you dizzy just looking at it through the windshield. You’d have to pull over on the side of the road, wait it out. And sitting in the cold car, it felt like the world was gone. That’s how I feel right now.”

“I’m scared too. But I’m figuring this out.”

“How?”

“Well, first, we have to find out exactly how much time this drug will give us in the corridor. Down to the minute.”

“How long do you want to push it?”

“If you’re saying we have about an hour, then our deadline is ninety minutes on your watch. That’s thirty minutes for the drug to start working, plus the sixty minutes we’re under it.”

“I weigh less than you. What if it affects me longer?”

“It doesn’t matter. The moment it stops working on one of us, that person will break the quantum state and collapse the corridor. Just to be safe, let’s start opening doors at the eighty-five-minute mark.”

“And hope for what exactly?”

“A world that doesn’t destroy us.”

She stops and looks at me. “I know you didn’t really build this box, but you must have some idea how it works.”

“Look, this is way beyond anything I could’ve—”

“So is that a ‘No, I don’t know’?”

“What are you asking me, Amanda?”

“Are we lost?”

“We’re getting information. We’re trying to solve something.”

“But the problem is that we’re lost. Right?”

“We’re exploring.”

“Jesus Christ.”

“What?”

“I don’t want to spend the rest of my life wandering down this endless tunnel.”

“I won’t let that happen.”

“How?”

“I don’t know yet.”

“But you’re trying to figure it out?”

“Yes. I’m trying to figure it out.”

“And we’re not lost.”

We are so lost. Completely adrift in the empty space between universes.

“We’re not lost.”

“Good.” She smiles. “Then I’ll wait to freak out.”

—

We walk on in silence for a while.

The metal walls are smooth and plain, all the same.

Amanda asks, “What worlds do you think we can actually reach?”

“I’ve been trying to figure that out. Let’s say the multiverse started with one event—the Big Bang. That’s the beginning, the base of the biggest, most detailed tree you can imagine. As time passed and matter formed into stars and planets in every possible way, this tree grew branches, and those branches grew more branches, and so on, until somewhere, fourteen billion years later, my birth started a new branch. And from that moment, every choice I made or didn’t make, and the things others did that affected me—those all created more branches, an endless number of Jason Dessens living in parallel worlds, some very similar to my own, some unbelievably different.

“Everything that can happen will happen. Everything. I mean, somewhere in this corridor, there’s a version of you and me that never got into the box when you tried to help me escape. And now we’re being tortured or already dead.”

“Thanks for making me feel better.”

“It could be worse. I don’t think we can reach the entire multiverse. I mean, if there’s a world where the sun died just as the first life-forms were appearing on Earth, I don’t think any of these doors open to that world.”

“So we can only walk into worlds that…”

“If I had to guess, worlds that are close to ours somehow. Worlds that broke off at some point recently, which are right next to ours. That we exist in, or existed in at some point. I don’t know how far back they split, but I think there’s some kind of specific selection happening. This is just my idea.”

“But we’re still talking about a countless number of worlds, right?”

“Yeah, pretty much.”

I lift her wrist and turn on the light on her watch.

The small green square shows…

84:50.

84:51.

I say, “The drug should stop working in the next five minutes. I guess it’s time.”

I go to the next door, give Amanda the lantern, and grab the handle.

Turning the lever, I pull the door open an inch.

I see a concrete floor.

Two inches.

A familiar glass window right in front of us.

Three.

Amanda says, “It’s the hangar.”

“What do you want to do?”

She pushes past me and steps out of the box.

I follow, the lights shining down on us.

Mission control is empty.

The hangar quiet.

We stop at the corner of the box and peek around the edge toward the vault doors.

I say, “This isn’t safe.” My words echo through the large hangar like whispers in a cathedral.

“And the box is?”

With a loud clang, the vault doors unlock and begin to open.

Panicked voices coming through the opening.

I say, “Let’s go. Right now.”

A woman is struggling to get through the space between the doors.

Amanda says, “Oh my God.”

The vault doors are only fifty feet away, and I know we should go back into the box, but I can’t stop watching.

The woman pushes through the doors into the hangar, then reaches back to help the man behind her.

The woman is Amanda.

The man’s face is so swollen and bruised I wouldn’t have known it was me except he’s wearing the same clothes I am.

As they run toward us, I start to back away toward the door of the box.

But they only get ten feet before Leighton’s men burst through the doors behind them.

A gunshot stops Jason and Amanda.

My Amanda starts toward them, but I pull her back.

“We have to help them,” she whispers.

“We can’t.”

Peeking around the corner of the box, we watch our doubles turn slowly to face Leighton’s men.

We should leave.

I know this.

Part of me is screaming to go.

But I can’t pull myself away.

My first thought is that we’ve gone back in time, but that can’t be. There’s no time travel in the box. This is just a world where Amanda and I escaped later.

Or didn't escape.

Leighton’s men have their guns out, moving carefully into the hangar toward Jason and Amanda.

As Leighton follows them in, I hear the other me say, “It’s not her fault. I threatened her. I made her do it.”

Leighton looks at Amanda.

He asks, “Is that true? He made you? Because I’ve known you for over ten years, and I’ve never seen anyone make you do anything.”

Amanda looks scared, but also unyielding.

Her voice shakes as she says, “I won’t let you hurt people anymore. I’m done.”

“Oh. Well, in that case…”

Leighton puts his hand on the big shoulder of the man to his right.

The gunshot is extremely loud.

The flash from the gun is blinding.

Amanda falls like a light being switched off, and next to me, my Amanda quietly screams.

As the other Jason runs at Leighton, the second guard quickly pulls out a Taser and shocks him, bringing him down screaming and twitching on the hangar floor.

My Amanda’s scream has revealed us.

Leighton is staring right at us, looking completely confused.

He shouts, “Hey!”

They chase after us.

I grab Amanda’s arm and pull her back through the box door, slamming it shut.

The door locks, the corridor rebuilds, but the drug will wear off soon.

Amanda is shaking, and I want to say everything is okay, but it’s not. She just saw herself get killed.

“That’s not you out there,” I say. “You’re right here next to me. Alive and safe. That is not you.”

Even in the dim light, I see she’s crying.

Tears run down her dirty face like smeared eyeliner.

“It’s some part of me,” she says. “Or used to be.”

Carefully, I lift her arm and turn it to see the watch. We’re forty-five seconds away from ninety minutes.

I say, “We have to go.”

I start down the corridor.

“Amanda, come on!”

When she catches up, I open a door.

Total darkness.

No sound, no smell. Just emptiness.

I slam it shut.

Trying not to panic, but I need to open more doors, giving us a chance to find someplace to rest and recover.

I open the next door.

Ten feet away, standing in weeds in front of a shaky chain-link fence, a wolf stares at me through large amber eyes. Lowering its head, it growls.

As it starts toward me, I slam the door shut.

Amanda grabs my hand.

We keep walking.

I should be opening more doors, but I’m really scared. I don’t think we’ll find a safe world.

I blink and we’re trapped in a box again.

The drug has stopped working for one of us.

This time, she opens the door.

Snow pours into the box.

A blast of bitter cold hits my face.

Through falling snow, I see the shapes of trees close by and houses far away.

“What do you think?” I ask.

“I think I don’t want to be in this box for another fucking second.”

Amanda steps down into the snow and sinks to her knees in the soft powder.

She immediately begins to shiver.

I feel the drug wear off, and this time the sensation is like an ice pick through my left eye.

Intense but quick.

I follow Amanda out of the box, and we head toward the neighborhood.

Beyond the top layer of powder, I can feel myself sinking—each step slowly breaking through a deeper, older layer of hard snow.

I catch up with Amanda.

We walk slowly through a clearing toward a neighborhood, which seems to be slowly vanishing before my eyes.

I’m somewhat shielded from the cold in my jeans and hoodie, but Amanda is struggling in her red skirt, black sweater, and flats.

I’ve lived most of my life in the Midwest, and I’ve never felt cold like this. My ears and cheeks feel like they're freezing, and I’m already starting to lose control of my fingers.

A strong wind hits us head-on, and as the snow gets heavier, the world ahead looks like a snow globe someone is shaking hard.

We keep going through the snow, as fast as we can, but it’s getting deeper and almost impossible to move through well.

Amanda’s cheeks have turned blue.

She’s shaking hard.

Her hair is covered in snow.

“We should turn back,” I say, my teeth chattering.

The wind is now very loud.

Amanda looks at me, confused, then nods.

I glance back, but the box is gone.

My fear spikes.

The snow is blowing sideways, and the houses in the distance have disappeared.

Everywhere I look, it all looks the same.

Amanda’s head is nodding, and I keep squeezing my hands into fists, trying to force warm blood to my fingertips, but it’s no use. My thread ring is covered in ice.

My thoughts are starting to spin.

I’m shaking from the cold.

We messed up.

It isn’t just cold. It’s way below zero.

Deadly cold.

I have no idea how far we've walked from the box.

Does it even matter now, when we can barely see?

This cold will kill us in just minutes.

Keep moving.

Amanda looks distant, and I wonder if she's going into shock.

Her bare legs are right on the snow.

"It hurts," she says.

I bend down, pick her up, and stumble into the storm, holding Amanda close as she shakes all over.

We’re in a swirl of wind, snow, and deadly cold, and it all looks the same. If I don’t look at my legs, the movement makes me dizzy.

I realize: we’re going to die.

But I keep going.

One foot in front of the other, my face burning from the cold, my arms hurting from holding Amanda, my feet aching as the snow gets into my shoes.

Minutes pass, the snow falls harder, and the cold keeps biting.

Amanda is mumbling, out of it.

I can’t keep doing this.

Can’t keep walking.

Can’t keep holding her.

Soon—so soon—I’ll have to stop. I’ll sit in the snow and hold this woman I hardly know, and we’ll freeze to death together in this terrible world that isn’t even ours.

I think about my family.

Think about never seeing them again, and I try to understand what that means as I finally lose control of my fear—

There’s a house in front of us.

Or, more accurately, the second story of a house, because the first floor is completely buried in snow, piled up to three dormer windows.

“Amanda.”

Her eyes are closed.

“Amanda!”

She opens them. Just barely.

“Stay with me.”

I set her down in the snow against the roof, struggle to the middle dormer, and kick the window in.

After I’ve kicked out the sharpest pieces of glass, I grab Amanda by the arms and pull her down into a child’s bedroom—a little girl’s, it seems.

Stuffed animals.

A wooden dollhouse.

Princess stuff.

A Barbie flashlight on the bedside table.

I pull Amanda far enough into the room that the snow coming through the window can’t reach her. Then I grab the Barbie flashlight and go through the doorway into an upstairs hall.

I call out, “Hello?”

The house takes my voice, doesn't reply.

All the bedrooms upstairs are empty. In most, the furniture is gone.

Turning on the flashlight, I go down the stairs.

The batteries are weak. The light is dim.

Leaving the stairs, I walk past the front door into what was once a dining room. Boards are nailed across the window frames to protect the glass from the snow, which completely fills the frames. An ax leans on what’s left of a dining-room table that’s been chopped up into firewood.

I walk through a doorway into a smaller room.

The weak light hits a couch.

Two chairs with most of their leather gone.

A television above a fireplace full of ashes.

A box of candles.

A stack of books.

Sleeping bags, blankets, and pillows are spread on the floor near the fireplace, and people are in them.

A man.

A woman.

Two teenage boys.

A young girl.

Eyes closed.

Not moving.

Their faces pale and thin.

A framed photo of the family at the Lincoln Park Conservatory, from happier times, lies on the woman’s chest, her darkened fingers still holding it tight.

By the fireplace, I see matchboxes, stacks of newspaper, and a pile of wood shavings from a knife block.

Another doorway from the family room leads me to the kitchen. The refrigerator is open and empty, and so are the cabinets. The counters are covered with empty metal cans.

Creamed corn.

Kidney beans.

Black beans.

Whole, peeled tomatoes.

Soups.

Peaches.

The things that sit in the back of cabinets and usually just go bad because no one uses them.

Even the condiment jars have been emptied—mustards, mayonnaise, jellies.

Behind the full trash can, I see a frozen puddle of blood and a skeleton—small, cat-like—picked clean.

These people didn’t freeze to death.

They starved.

—

Firelight shines on the walls of the family room. I’m naked in a sleeping bag that’s inside another sleeping bag that’s covered with blankets.

Amanda warms up beside me in two sleeping bags of her own.

Our wet clothes are drying on the brick hearth, and we’re lying so close to the fire that I can feel its warmth on my face.

Outside, the storm keeps raging, the whole house creaking in the strongest wind gusts.

Amanda’s eyes are open.

She’s been awake for a bit, and we’ve already finished the two water bottles, which are now filled with snow and standing on the hearth near the fire.

“What do you think happened to the people who lived here?” she asks.

Truth: I dragged their bodies into an office so she wouldn’t see them.

But I say, “I don’t know. Maybe they went somewhere warm?”

She smiles. “Liar. We’re not doing great with our spaceship.”

“I think this is what they call a hard lesson to learn.”

She breathes in deeply, then out.

Says, “I’m forty-one. It wasn’t the best life, but it was mine. I had a job. An apartment. A dog. Friends. TV shows I liked. This guy, John, I’d seen three times. Wine.” She looks at me. “I’m never going to see any of that again, am I?”

I don’t know what to say.

She goes on, “At least you have somewhere to go. A world you want to get back to. I can’t go back to mine, so where does that leave me?”

She stares at me.

Stiff.

Not blinking.

I have no answer.

—

When I wake up again, the fire is just glowing embers, and the snow near the tops of the windows is lit up and sparkling as sunlight tries to peek through.

Even inside the house, it's unbelievably cold.

Reaching a hand out of the sleeping bag, I touch our clothes by the fireplace, relieved they're dry. I pull my hand back inside and turn to Amanda. She has the sleeping bag over her face, and I see her breath pushing through the down in puffs of steam that have made ice crystals on the bag's surface.

I put on my clothes, build a new fire, and hold my hands in the heat just before my fingers go numb.

Leaving Amanda to sleep, I walk through the dining room. The sun shines through the snow at the top of the windows, giving just enough light to see.

Up the dark stairs.

Down the hall.

Back into the girl’s room, where snow has blown in and covered most of the floor.

I climb through the window frame and squint in the painful light. The glare off the ice is so strong that for five seconds I can't see anything.

The snow is waist-deep.

The sky a perfect blue.

No sound of birds.

No sound of life.

There’s not even a breath of wind and no sign we were ever here. Everything smoothed and covered in snow.

It must be far below zero, because even in the sun, I can’t get warm.

Beyond this neighborhood, I see the Chicago skyline, the towers covered in snow and ice, sparkling in the sun.

A white city.

A world of ice.

Across the street, I look at the open field where we almost froze yesterday.

The box is gone.

—

Back inside, I find Amanda awake, sitting up by the fireplace with the sleeping bags and blankets around her.

I go into the kitchen and find some silverware.

Then I open the backpack and take out a couple of MREs.

They’re cold but filling.

We eat quickly.

Amanda asks, “Did you see the box?”

“No, I think it’s buried under the snow.”

“Great.” She looks at me, then back at the fire, and says, “I don’t know if I should be angry or thankful.”

“What are you talking about?”

“While you were upstairs, I had to go to the bathroom. I ended up in the office.”

“So you saw them.”

“They starved, right? Before they ran out of fuel for the fire.”

“Looks like it.”

As I stare into the flames, I feel something nagging at the back of my brain.

A hint of something.

It started when I was outside a moment ago, looking at the field, thinking about us almost dying in that whiteout.

I say, “Remember what you said about the corridor? How it felt like being trapped in a whiteout?”

She stops eating, looks at me.

“The doors in the corridor connect to endless parallel worlds, right? But what if we’re creating these connections?”

“How?”

“What if it’s like building dreams, where we’re somehow choosing these specific worlds?”

“You’re saying that, out of endless realities, I intentionally picked this dump?”

“Not intentionally. Maybe it’s a reflection of what you were feeling when you opened the door.”

She takes the last bite of food and tosses her empty MRE packet into the fire.

I say, “Think about the first world we saw—that ruined Chicago, with the buildings crumbling all around us. What were we feeling as we walked into that parking garage?”

“Fear. Terror. Despair. Oh my God. Jason.”

“What?”

“Before we opened the door to the hangar and saw the other versions of you and me getting caught, you had mentioned that very thing happening.”

“Did I?”

“You were talking about the idea of the multiverse, and everything that can happen will happen, and you said that somewhere there was a version of you and me that never made it into the box. Moments later, you opened a door and we watched that exact thing happen.”

I feel a sudden, exciting realization wash over me.

I say, “All this time, we’ve been trying to find the controls—”

“But we’re the controls.”

“Right. And if that’s true, then we can go anywhere we want. Even home.”

—

Early the next morning, we stand in the quiet neighborhood, snow up to our waists and shivering, even in all the winter clothes we took from that family’s coat closet.

In the field in front of us, there are no tracks. No box. Just smooth, untouched snow.

The field is huge, and the box is tiny.

We have almost no chance of finding it by accident.

The sun is barely over the trees, and the cold is intense.

“What are we supposed to do, Jason? Guess? Start digging?”

I glance back at the half-buried house, wondering for a terrifying moment how long we could last there. How long before we ran out of firewood? Before our food was gone? Before we gave up and died like all the others?

I feel a dark pressure building in my chest—fear closing in.

I take a deep breath, and the air is so cold it makes me cough.

Panic surrounds me.

Finding the box is hopeless.

It’s too cold out here.

There won’t be enough time, and when the next storm comes, and the next, the box will be buried so deep we’ll never be able to reach it.

Unless…

I let the backpack slide off my shoulders into the snow and unzip it with shaky fingers.

“What are you doing?” Amanda asks.

“Throwing a Hail Mary.”

It takes me a moment to find what I’m looking for.

Grabbing the compass, I leave Amanda and the pack and step into the field.

She follows, calling for me to wait.

Fifty feet out, I stop to let her reach me.

“Look at this,” I say, touching the compass face. “We’re in South Chicago, right?” I point to the distant skyline. “So magnetic north is that way. But this compass says differently. See how the needle points east toward the lake?”

Her face lights up. “Of course. It’s the box’s magnetic field, pulling the compass needle away.”

We sink through the deep snow.

In the middle of the field, the needle swings from east to west.

“We’re taking care of it.”

I begin to dig, my bare hands aching from the snow, but I don’t stop.

Four feet down, I hit the edge of the box, and I keep digging, faster now, my sleeves pulled forward to protect my hands, which are going from painful cold to numbness.

When my half-frozen fingers finally touch the top of the open door, I shout, and it echoes through the frozen world.

—

Ten minutes later, we’re back inside the box, drinking ampoule forty-six and ampoule forty-five.

Amanda starts the timer on her watch, turns off the lantern to save the batteries, and as we sit beside each other in the frigid dark, waiting for the drug to work, she says, “Never thought I’d be glad to see our crummy little lifeboat again.”

“Right?”

She leans her head against my shoulder.

“Thank you, Jason.”

“For what?”

“Not letting me freeze to death out there.”

“Does this mean we’re even?”

She laughs. “Not even close. I mean, let’s not forget, this is still all your fault.”

It’s a strange feeling to sit in the complete darkness and silence of the box. The only things I feel are the cold metal through my clothes and Amanda’s head on my shoulder.

“You’re different from him,” she says.

“Who?”

“My Jason.”

“How?”

“Softer. He was really hard when you got to it. The most driven person I’ve ever met.”

“Were you his therapist?”

“Sometimes.”

“Was he happy?”

I feel her thinking about my question in the dark.

“What?” I ask. “Am I making you break doctor-patient confidentiality?”

“Technically, you two are the same person. This is new for me. But no. I wouldn’t say he was happy. He had a life that was interesting to his mind, but not satisfying overall. All he did was work. In the last five years, he didn’t have a life outside the lab. He almost lived there.”

“You know your Jason is the one who did this to me. I’m here right now because a few nights ago, someone kidnapped me with a gun while I was walking home. He took me to an empty power plant, drugged me, asked me a lot of questions about my life and the choices I’d made. If I was happy. If I would’ve done things differently. I remember it all now. Then I woke up in your lab. In your world. I think your Jason did this to me.”

“You’re saying that he went into the box, somehow found your world, your life, and switched places with you?”

“Do you think he could do that?”

“I don’t know. That’s insane.”

“Who else would’ve done this to me?”

Amanda is quiet for a moment.

She says finally, “Jason was obsessed with the road not taken. He talked about it all the time.”

Now I feel the anger coming back.

I say, “There’s still a part of me that doesn’t want to believe it. I mean, if he wanted my life, he could’ve just killed me. But he went to the trouble of injecting me, not only with a vial, but ketamine, which knocked me out and blurred my memories of the box and what he’d done. Then he actually brought me back to his world. Why?”

“It actually makes a lot of sense.”

“You think?”

“He wasn’t a monster. If he did this to you, he would have explained it to himself somehow. That’s how good people justify bad behavior. In your world, are you a famous physicist?”

“No, I teach at a so-so college.”

“Are you wealthy?”

“In terms of career and money, I’m nowhere near as successful as your Jason.”

“There you go. He tells himself he’s giving you a great opportunity. He wants to try a different life. Why wouldn’t you? I’m not saying it’s right. I’m saying that’s how a good man convinces himself to do something awful. It’s basic human nature.”

She must feel my anger rising, because she says, “Jason, you can’t afford to lose it right now. In a minute, we’re going back into that hallway. We’re in control. Your words. Right?”

“Yeah.”

“If that’s true, if our feelings are somehow choosing these worlds, where will your anger and jealousy take us? You can’t hold on to this energy when you open a new door. You have to find a way to release it.”

I can feel the drug taking effect.

My muscles relax.

For a moment, the anger disappears into a peaceful calm that I would do anything to keep, to let it carry me through.

When Amanda turns on the lantern, the walls beside the door are gone.

I look down at the leather bag holding the remaining vials, thinking, If the jerk who did this to me figured out how to use the box, then I will too.

In the blue light, Amanda watches me.

I say, “We have forty-four ampoules left. Twenty-two chances to get this right. How many did the other Jason take with him into the box?”

“A hundred.”

Shit.

I feel a hint of panic go through me, but I smile anyway.

“I guess it’s lucky for us I’m way smarter than him, right?”

Amanda laughs, gets up, and offers me her hand.

“We have one hour,” she says. “Ready for this?”

“Absolutely.”
