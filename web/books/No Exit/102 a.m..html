No Exit

1:02 a.m.

YOU WIN. I WON’T SAY A WORD.

Ashley smirked — she had no idea how right she was.

This CU-Boulder girl was a surprise, but he already understood her. He’d seen her kind before, though never in person. Darby Elizabeth Thorne was a true hero. She was the person on a gas station security video who grabs for the robber's gun, or helps a bleeding worker. She was the type to jump in front of a train to save a stranger. Protecting others, doing the right thing, was natural for her, whether she knew it or not.

Despite what people think, that’s not a strength.

It’s a weakness, because it makes you easy to predict. Easy to control. And as expected — after just a thirty-minute talk, a short group activity, and a failed card game — Ashley already had her.

Breaking her nose? That was just a fun little celebration.

And he was surprised by how much he enjoyed watching Darby fight tears in front of Ed and Sandi, her nose bleeding heavily. There was something great about it, something he couldn’t quite explain. She was embarrassed, suffering in public, like some of his favorite porn. He loved the ones where the girl was secretly wearing vibrating underwear in a street or restaurant, trying not to show it. Trying to hold it in.

It helped that Darby was definitely pretty, too, in a wild way. She had a fierceness about her; a mean side to go with her bright red hair. She didn’t know how strong she could be, if pushed too far. He’d love to push her there. He’d love to take her up to Rathdrum, drive her to the gravel pit and teach her how to shoot his uncle’s SKS. Have her hold the wooden Soviet stock to her shoulder, guide her painted fingernail to the trigger, smell her nervous sweat as she lined up the iron sights.

Too bad, then, that he’d have to kill her tonight.

He didn’t want to.

Ashley Garver had never actually killed anyone, so tonight would be a first. The closest he could recall was more like manslaughter than murder. And not by doing something — but by not doing something.

He’d been a kid when it happened.

This was a year or two before he almost lost his thumb at Chink’s Drop. So he was five, maybe six. Back then, his parents used to leave him and Lars (who was just a toddler) with Uncle Kenny in the summer. Kenny lived in the dry plains of Idaho. He called himself Fat Kenny (Hey, hey, hey!), which Ashley now realized was a play on Fat Albert. He was a happy man who panted when he climbed stairs, smoked clove cigarettes, and always had a joke ready.

What do you tell a woman with a black eye?

‘Should have listened.’

What do you tell a woman with two black eyes?

Nothing. She’s already been told twice.

Each year, Ashley went back to school with a bunch of great jokes. Every September he was the most popular kid on the playground, sharing them widely. By October, the school always had an emergency meeting about being tolerant.

But Uncle Kenny was more than just funny. He also owned a diesel station on a small highway south of Boise, used by truckers and no one else. Ashley used to climb the apple trees with Lars and watch the trucks come and go. Sometimes they parked on Kenny’s land, making muddy marks in the yellow grass, arriving late and leaving early. They rarely came into Uncle Kenny’s house, though — they went to his storm cellar.

It was like a bomb shelter, a single door sticking out of the weeds twenty yards from the laundry room. This submarine-like door was always, always padlocked. Until one morning when, in a veil of damp fog, he’d found it open.

So he’d gone inside.

Ashley recalled few details about the dark room at the bottom of the long, decaying staircase. Mostly just the smells — a moldy, sweet stuffiness that was both disgusting and strangely attractive. He’d never smelled anything like it since. Cold cement under his feet. Electrical cords on the floor; big lights on stands. Vague shapes, hiding in the dark.

He’d just been leaving, climbing back up the stairs when a woman’s voice called out to him: Hey.

He’d turned, almost tripping. He waited a long moment, half on the stairs, half off, goosebumps rising on his arms, wondering if he’d imagined it, until finally, the female voice spoke again.

Hey, you. Little boy.

This shocked him — he hadn’t known how the woman in the cellar could see him. It was completely dark down there. Only as an adult, could Ashley understand that her eyes had adjusted to the darkness, while his hadn’t. Like Darby’s clever little one-eye trick.

You’re a nice boy, aren’t you?

He’d shrunk back on the steps, covering his ears.

No. Don’t be afraid. You’re not like them. The ghostly voice dropped, like she was telling a secret: Can you . . . hey, can you please help me with something?

He’d been scared to answer.

Can you get me a glass of water?

He wasn’t sure.

Please?

Finally he agreed and hurried back up the broken steps, ran back to his uncle’s ranch house, and filled a blue glass at the kitchen sink. The tap water tasted like iron out here. When he came back outside, Uncle Kenny was standing by the open cellar door, his hands on his wide hips.

Little Ashley froze, spilling some water.

But Uncle Kenny wasn’t angry. No, he was never angry. He’d been all happy smiles, showing yellow teeth, taking the glass from Ashley’s scared little fingers: Thanks, kiddo. It’s alright, I’ll take this down to her. Hey, why don’t you go walk your baby brother to the gas station and get yourselves two chicken flautas, free?

The flautas had been dry as sandpaper, dried out by the heat lamp. Lars didn’t mind, but Ashley couldn’t finish his.

That same year, a month or two later, Ashley had come back to Uncle Kenny’s a second time for Memorial Day weekend, and he remembered finding that same cellar door held wide open, with a loud fan blowing air out. When he went down the steps this time he found the lights on, showing an empty, stripped room, the concrete walls wet with condensation. Scrub marks on the floor. The strong smell of bleach. The woman was gone.

Long gone.

Even at that age, Ashley had known he should talk to his uncle about this, or better yet, tell his parents and let them call the police. And he’d almost done it, holding onto that knowledge all weekend like a loaded gun. But that Saturday night, Fat Kenny made macaroni and cheese with jalapenos and whole slices of bacon in it, and told a joke so incredibly funny it made Ashley spray a mouthful of half-chewed food.

Hey, Ashley. How can you tell a black person has been on your computer?

How?

Your computer’s missing.

In the end, he’d simply liked Fat Kenny too much. He was too much fun. And he was really nice to four-year-old Lars, too — letting him carry tools in the workshop, teaching him how to shoot crows with a BB gun. So, basically, whatever those truckers were doing with the woman in the bunker ultimately didn’t matter to Ashley. He just filed it away in a dark corner of his brain.

That was seventeen years ago.

And now, at the Wanapani rest area in Colorado, on the freezing night of December 23, these roles were switched, like a classic TV show returning with a new cast. Ashley himself was the new Fat Kenny, trying to protect a damaging secret. And Darby was the accidental witness.

History doesn’t exactly repeat itself, but man, it sure can rhyme.

Ed reached behind the noisy security grate, testing the hot water dispenser, and then separated two bags of coffee grounds. “I’ve got a dark French Roast, and a light.”

“Either’s fine,” said Sandi.

“Dark roast, please,” Ashley said. “As dark as it gets.”

He didn’t really care; he just liked the way dark roast sounded. He couldn’t taste much anyway, so all coffee tasted the same. But if any night called for jet-black coffee, this was it. He shoved Darby’s brown napkin into his jeans, seeing a crescent thumbprint of her blood on it.

He realized he couldn’t see her anymore.

He quickly looked around. Ed was by the locked coffee stand, Sandi sat like a fat yellow bumblebee, Lars watched the front door — but Darby was gone. She’d disappeared. She’d used his distraction to escape.

But it was fine. No problem. Ashley Garver would just make a move, too.

Restroom?

Restroom.

He nodded to his brother.

Darby knew she didn't have much time.

She closed the men’s restroom door behind her without stopping, passing the stained sinks, her reflection following her in the mirrors. Scar visible, like a white sickle. Haunted eyes in the glass.

Yes, the Wanapani rest area was a pressure cooker. She’d almost gotten Ed and Sandi killed. She needed to get out. She needed to rethink this fight, to move it somewhere else. Somewhere without the risk of hurting others.

I’ll run, she decided. I’ll run up the highway. As fast, and as hard, as I possibly can. I won’t stop until I find signal and call 9-1-1.

Or I’ve frozen to death.

She checked her iPhone again. The screen must have broken when she fell on the toilet, spreading a spider web of deep cracks. The battery was now two percent.

She looked up at the empty window — a triangular little slice of night sky and treetops. It was almost eight feet off the floor. Getting inside had been easy, thanks to the stacked picnic tables outside. Getting outside would be much harder. Even on her tiptoes, she couldn’t reach the window frame. She’d need one hell of a jump to catch it with her fingertips. She’d need a running start, and every inch of it.

She backed up, past the green stalls, past PEYTON MANNING TAKES IT IN THE ASS, all the way back to the door, her back touching the wall, and the rectangular restroom stretched out before her like a twenty-foot runway. Smooth linoleum under her feet, slippery with moisture. She arched her back, got into a runner’s crouch, and closed her hands into fists.

She took a full breath — the bitter smell of ammonia. She let it half-out.

Go.

She ran.

Mirrors, urinals, stall doors, all flying by. Air rushed in her ears. No time to think too much. No time to be scared. She made her hands like knives, moving her legs fast, and jumped in a wild, rushing leap at the small opening—

In the air, she thought: This is going to hurt—

It did. She slammed into the tile wall knees-first, hitting her chin, knocking the wind out of her, but (yes!) she'd grabbed the window frame with two desperate fingertips. Fingernails in the wet, old wood. She pressed her wet shoes against the wall. Then she bent her back again, locked her elbows, and pulled her body up, gasping through tight teeth, like the worst chin-up bar ever, and pulled and pulled and pulled—

She heard someone breathing heavily. Outside.

No.

No, no, no, please don’t be true—

But yes, it was there. Right outside, on the other side of the wall. That soft wheeze she knew so well, that wet little puff. Lars, Rodent Face, had gone around the building and now waited for her outside. Watching that window, gun in hand, ready to shoot her in the head the second she climbed up and showed her face.

Now what?

She hung there on hurting fingertips, her shoes dangling three feet off the floor, really wishing she’d just heard the wind outside wrong. But she knew she hadn’t. She knew Ashley had sent obedient little Lars out there to block her escape. Which meant a much smarter and more dangerous enemy was still out there somewhere.

Then she heard the restroom door click shut.

He’s in the room with—

A plastic bag was yanked over Darby’s face from behind. She screamed, but the sound was stuck in her mouth.
