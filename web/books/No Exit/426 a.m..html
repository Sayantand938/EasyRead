No Exit

4:26 a.m.

On the way, Highway Patrolman Corporal Ron Hill asked Dispatch twice to make sure about the 207 call, but they had no more info. No name. No story. Just a vehicle (gray van), a license plate (VBH9045), and a general location, sent to 9-1-1 by text. No more contact. No calls. They couldn't get back in touch, probably because of bad cell service and the worst winter storm ever.

It seemed like a joke.

The worst calls always seem like jokes at first.

His cruiser struggled uphill, engine working hard, sand and gravel hitting the bottom of the car. CDOT should clear roads in a certain order at this height — plow, then deice, then sand and salt — but it seemed like their best crew was off for Christmas Eve. The whole thing felt like a mess, costing a lot in overtime. Listening to their CB radios, it reminded him of what his old CO called it when Marines move out of line, risking getting shot: a total screw-up.

Ron was thirty-six, looked young, with a wife who'd studied graphic design but became a wife, and a five-year-old son who wanted to be a cop when he grew up. She hated him for that. He’d been written up twice for sleeping while watching for speeders, and once for what they called ‘unnecessary yelling,’ which Ron thought was ridiculous.

Before his 7 p.m. shift tonight, he’d found his wife’s suitcase in the closet.

Standing up, and partly full.

Thinking about this, he almost didn't see the blue sign on his right, covered in snow, shining in his bright headlights:

REST AREA ONE MILE.

“Hey.” Fingers snapped in Darby’s face. “Lost you for a sec.”

Her right hand felt like it had been plunged into boiling water.

At first, it hadn’t hurt at all — just the rush of air and the loud bang of the door slamming next to her right ear — and then the pain came a moment later. Deafening, shattering. Both heavy and sharp. It threw her out of her body, out of this world. For a black instant she was nowhere, and in another, she was back in her tiny childhood house in Provo, six years old again, running up the creaking stairs, diving into the warm blankets of her mother’s bed, hiding from a late-night nightmare. I’ve got you, her mother whispered, turning on the lamp.

It was just a dream, baby.

You imagined it all.

I’ve got you—

And then the bedroom disappeared like paint and Darby was back in this little Colorado rest stop with bright lights and old coffee, this horrible place she could never escape. She’d slumped down when she passed out, her back to the door. A sour taste in her throat. She was afraid to look up at her right hand. She knew what had happened. She knew the door was shut, that at least two of her fingers were crushed inside it, destroyed between hard brass edges—

I’ve got you, Darby—

“Earth to Darbs.” Ashley snapped his fingers. “I need you awake.”

“Ashley,” Sandi hissed. “You’re insane. You’ve lost your mind—”

Darby found the courage to look at her hand, blinking away watery tears. Her ring and pinkie fingers were gone above the hinge, inside the door’s scissor-jaws. Gone. It gave her a nauseous, shivery jolt. Her body just ended there. It couldn’t possibly be her hand, but it was. She couldn’t imagine what her fingers looked like inside the door — skin burst, tissue torn, bones broken into pieces. Tendons crushed and tangled into red spaghetti noodles. There was somehow less blood than she’d expected; just a long, shiny bead running down the doorframe.

She watched it inch down the dry wood.

“Ashley,” Sandi barked. “Are you even listening?”

Darby reached for the doorknob with her good left hand, swiping, missing it twice, finally closing her numb fingers around it, to open the closet door and free her ruined hand, to reveal the awful, heartbreaking damage — but the doorknob didn’t turn. He’d locked it, the bastard.

Ashley strode across the room, pocketing the key, leaving her pinned there. “Alright, Sandi. It’s time I was honest with you.”

“Oh, now it’s time? After all this?”

“Sandi, let me explain—”

“Oh, sure.” She hurled the plastic first-aid box at him, which he swatted away, clattering off the stone counter. “You promised me, Ashley. No one was supposed to get hurt through this whole thing—”

He edged closer. “I have something to tell you.”

“Yeah? What’s that?”

He spoke slowly, precisely, like a surgeon delivering bad news. “Our meeting here wasn’t just about finding a quiet place for you to give me your storage key. I mean, yes, that was your plan, and maybe I’ll use those steroid shots to keep Jaybird alive as long as I can . . .”

Sandi’s eyes widened with icy terror.

“But, see, I had a plan, too.” He kept approaching. “And, it turns out, your plan was just part of my plan.”

Sandi took another step back, frozen by his wide shoulders, his presence, and the flickering fluorescent lights overhead.

Silence.

“You know . . .” Ashley shrugged. “I really thought you’d try to run by now.”

She tried.

He was too quick.

He grabbed her elbow with that strong grip Darby knew well, and with an aikido spin, threw Sandi to the floor. A shoe flew off. Her other foot kicked the vending machine as she fell, cracking the glass until it was opaque. Ashley was already on top of her, forcing her to lie face-down, his knee on her back.

Ed struggled forward, but Lars aimed his .45. “Ah, nope, nope.”

Now Ashley grabbed the woman by the hair, both fists clenched in her black bowl-shaped haircut, and yanked her head backward, against his braced knee. “You called . . . Sandi, now, you may not remember this, but earlier tonight, you said some very mean things about Lars, about what’s wrong with him. Because of choices our mother made long ago, when he was just a tiny baby. How fair is that? Come on, Sandi. You know I love my baby brother—”

She screamed against his thick grip.

“Take it back, Sandi.” He twisted her neck harder. “Take back what you said.”

She cried out, all vowels.

“Try again. I can’t hear you—”

She gasped: “I . . . I take it back—”

“Okay, well, that’s a nice thing to do.” Ashley glanced to Lars. “So, baby brother? Do you accept Sandi’s apology?”

Lars grinned, enjoying the power, and shook his head twice.

“Please. Please, I—”

Ashley tightened his grip on Sandi’s head, pressed his boot harder between her shoulder blades (for leverage, Darby realized), and pulled hard.

The woman’s neck broke, eventually. It wasn’t fast, or easy. Sandi screamed until she ran out of air, her face turning a bruised purple, her eyes bulging before going blank, her fingers clawing, kicking. Ashley paused once, adjusting his grip before yanking her head harder, harder, harder, ninety degrees backward now, until her vertebrae finally dislocated in an audible, wet crack. Like popping knuckles. If she had still been conscious, she might’ve felt the horror of her body going numb. It was a difficult, awkward, grunting process, and it took a full thirty seconds before the woman was clearly dead.

Then Ashley let go, letting Sandi’s forehead hit the tile with a thud, her neck full of broken bones. He stood up, red-faced.

Lars was clapping his scarred hands now, giggling with excitement, like he’d just seen the best magic trick ever.

I just saw a murder, Darby thought blankly. Just now. Right here. Sandi Schaeffer — San Diego school bus driver, part of this messy ransom plot — was gone forever now. A human life, a soul, gone. Whether it was door hinges or Lars’s fetal alcohol syndrome — say something Ashley Garver doesn't like, even in passing, and he doesn’t forget it. He takes note. And later, he gets revenge.

“Hey, baby brother.” He caught his breath, pointing down at the woman’s warm body. “Wanna hear something funny? Not that it matters now, but did this Jesus-freak tell you what she was planning to spend her share on?”

“What?”

“Women’s shelters. A lot of money to shelters for abused women all over California, like she was Mother Teresa. Can you believe that?”

Lars laughed loudly.

Darby glanced up at the Garfield clock, but her vision blurred with tears. Maybe three minutes until the cops arrived? Two minutes? She couldn’t tell. Her mind was a mess. She closed her eyes again, wishing she was six again. She wished this was just another bad dream she’d woken up from, before high school, before drinking and curfews and weed and birth control, before everything became complicated, wrapped up in her mother’s arms, blinking away tears, breathlessly describing the ghostly lady with the strange legs who’d walked through her bedroom—

No, it was just a dream.

I’ve got you, baby. It was just a dream.

Just inhale, count to five, and—

Ashley rattled the closet door. Like sandpaper on exposed nerves, a jangling, intense pain shot up her wrist like electricity. She screamed in a choked voice she’d never heard before.

“Sorry, Darbs. You were dozing off again.” He wiped sweat from his forehead. “Trust me, this was supposed to be easy. We’d pick up Jaybird from her mansion, drive twelve hours to a storage unit in Moose Head, where Sandi had money, cabin keys, and Jay’s stupid adrenaline shots, all under a fake name and a five-digit combination lock — one-nine-eight-seven-two. We’d grab that, disappear up to Sandi’s family cabin, and spend a week or two negotiating a good ransom. Right?”

He rattled the door again — another violin-screech of pain.

“Wrong. After we grabbed Jaybird, and we were halfway across the Mojave, we learned there was a break-in at Sandi’s stupid Sentry Storage place, and all of the combination locks had been opened. Figures, right? So they were back to using default keys, which only Sandi had, all the way back in California. And problem two — Mr Nissen called the cops, even though we told him not to, and now Sandi was under all kinds of scrutiny since she’s the school bus driver who saw Jay last. Meanwhile we’re out here in the Rockies without a place to stay and a sick kid in the van, throwing up everywhere. What were we supposed to do? Huh?”

He reached forward, as if to rattle the doorknob again — Darby winced — but he showed a flash of compassion and didn’t.

“So, Sandi made up a last-minute family Christmas trip to Denver as a cover story for the police, so she could meet up with us at a public place and give us the storage unit key so we could get Jay’s meds, and our supplies. Which brings me to problem three.” Ashley pointed outside. “This goddamn winter wonderland.”

The pieces clicked together in Darby’s mind: The snowstorm trapped them all here at the meeting point. With poor Ed as Sandi’s prop.

And then I showed up.

The sheer size of it overwhelmed her and made her dizzy. This snake pit she'd wandered into at seven p.m., wired on Red Bull and worn out. She watched her own blood drip slowly. It was almost touching the floor now.

"I'm not stupid," Ashley said. "I've seen enough movies to know everything leaves a digital trace. Since the police are involved now, getting Jaybird's ransom from Mommy and Daddy is pretty much impossible. And the cops are all over Sandi, too. She stole Jay's cortisol shots from the school nurse's office a few months back, so they'll blame that on her pretty quick. And then she'd probably rat us out, which makes her a problem. So we came up here to kill her after she gave us the key. Make it look like a robbery gone wrong, a gunshot to the face. But I wasn't expecting the blizzard, or for her to bring Cousin Ed. And I wasn't expecting you, obviously."

It all fit together and made a twisted kind of sense. Except for one last question, burning in the back of Darby's mind with nervous energy. "Then... if there's no ransom money, what are you going to do with Jay?"

"Hey." Ashley snapped his fingers in her face again. "Answer mine first, okay? Where are my keys?"

"What are you going to do with her?"

He smiled like he was guilty. "It'll just make you not want to help."

"Yeah? What else have I been all night?"

"Trust me, Darbs. Just trust me on this one." He stood up, lifting the orange nail gun, and paced across the room. "Because, finally, I've figured you out. I could slam that door on each of your fingers until the sun comes up, until you have nothing but bloody, mangled hands, and you still won't tell me what I need to know, because you're just not that kind of person. You're a hero, a softie. Your whole night went to hell because you broke into a van to save a stranger. So guess what? Here's your chance to save another one."

He crouched beside Ed and pressed the nail gun to his forehead. The older man's eyelids slid sleepily half-open.

“Now, Darbs,” Ashley said, “I’m going to count down from five. You’re going to tell me where you hid my keychain, or I’ll kill Ed.”

She shook her head, thrashing left to right, a helpless denial. Up on the wall, the Garfield clock now read 5:30 (4:30) a.m.

It’s been thirty-two minutes. The police are late—

Ashley raised his voice. “Five.”

“No. I . . . I can’t—”

“Four.”

“Please, Ashley—”

“Three. Come on, Darbs.” He punched the nail gun’s muzzle against Ed’s forehead in cruel, bruising impacts: “Look. At. Him.”

Ed now stared across the room at her through watering eyes. Poor old Edward Schaeffer, the ex-veterinarian with a family far away in Aurora, Colorado. A human cover story; Sandi’s unwitting casualty. He was moving his lips again, muffled by clammy red gauze, trying to form words with his tongue stuck to the roof of his mouth. She could feel his eyes on her, begging her to tell Ashley what he wanted to know. To just please tell him—

“If I tell Ashley,” Darby whispered to Ed, “they’ll kill us both—”

This was true, but she wished she could tell him another, bigger truth, to calm him: The police are almost here. They’re a few minutes late. Any second now, they’re going to break down that door and shoot Ashley and Lars—

“Two.”

“I . . . I can’t say it.” She looked at Ed, realizing what this meant, and a deep sob shook her. “I’m . . . oh God, I’m so sorry—”

Ed nodded slowly, knowingly, dripping clumps of stringy blood into his lap. Like he somehow, unbelievably, understood.

She wanted to shout it to him: Any second, now, Ed. The cops are coming to save us. Please, God, let them get here in time—

The patience left Ashley’s voice. “One.”

“Ten-twenty-three. Getting closer to the building on foot.”

Corporal Ron Hill grabbed his shoulder radio and tripped on a snow bank, catching himself with a gloved hand. The ice was rock-hard here, like shaped cement. He was just a few steps from the Wanapa visitor center.

He reached the front door, stepping under the saucer-shaped lamp. Again, no more information from Dispatch beyond the first two-zero-seven text message, which was annoying.

He tapped the door with his Maglite. “Highway Patrol.”

He waited for an answer.

Then, a little louder: “Police. Anyone here?”

It was still technically just a public building, but his right hand moved to the back of his Glock 17 as he gripped the doorknob and stepped sideways into the crunchy snow, using the brick wall as cover.

In entry drills, doorways are called fatal funnels because they’re the defender’s natural focus. No way around it, unless you blow down a wall — you’re literally walking into the bad guy’s sights. If there really were a burglar hiding inside this rest stop, he’d be watching the door right now down the barrel of a shotgun, perhaps crouched behind his hostages for cover.

Or, just an empty, harmless room. Not that Dispatch knew.

A sharp wind tugged his Gore-Tex jacket, throwing dry snowflakes against the door, and now Corporal Hill wasn’t sure what he was waiting for. For Sara to finish packing her suitcase? Forget it.

He twisted the doorknob.

The door creaked open.

“Zero,” Ashley said.

But Darby wasn’t listening, because she’d just realized something. She stared past Ashley now, at the Colorado map on the wall behind Ed — and her heart sank with a heavy, sickening dread. State Route Seven was a thick blue line on the map, winding through the mountains, and the rest areas were marked as red circles. Wanapa, Wanapani, Colchuck, Nisqual.

This one was Wanapani. Big Devil.

Not Wanapa.

But she’d typed her text message to 9-1-1 earlier, around 9 p.m., before she’d learned this. Before she’d gone back inside, looked at the map again, and realized her mistake — that she’d mixed up two similar-looking, similar-sounding Native American names, both about devils.

My text sent to the cops to the wrong rest area.

To a completely different one, twenty miles down Backbone Pass. On the other side of that crashed semi-truck. The police weren’t coming after all. They were still miles away, unreachable, misled. No one was coming to arrest Ashley and Lars. No one was coming to save them.

She wanted to scream.

She sagged against the locked door, feeling her fingers twist inside the doorframe. Another jolt of sharp pain. She felt weightless, like falling, plunging to some unknown depth. She wished it would all end.

No one is coming to save us.

We’re all alone.

I got us all killed—

Ashley sighed like a sulky child, then pressed the nail gun to Ed’s head and fired—

“Stop,” Darby choked out. “Stop. I’ll tell you where your keys are, if you . . . if you promise you won’t kill him.”

“I promise,” Ashley said.

She knew it was a lie. Of course it was a lie. Ashley Garver was a sociopath. Words meant nothing to him; you couldn’t bargain with him any more than you could bargain with a virus. But she broke down and told him anyway, the room going silent, her voice a broken whisper: “In the snow . . . outside the bathroom window. That’s where I threw them.”

Ashley nodded. He looked at Lars, then Jay. Then back at her, smiling like a kid. “Thanks, Darbs. I knew you’d help,” he said, raising the nail gun to Ed’s forehead anyway.

THWUMP.
