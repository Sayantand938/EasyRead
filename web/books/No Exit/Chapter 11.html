No Exit

Chapter 11

12:09 a.m.

Ashley moved to the table and sat right across from her. He'd come across the room quietly, and now sat with both palms flat on the table. His hands were big and rough.

Darby re-folded the napkin and put it in her lap.

The radio crackled.

"I'm tired of Go Fish," Ashley said sharply. "How about something else?"

She said nothing.

"How about . . ." he thought. "Oh! How about War?"

She glanced over at Ed and Sandi—

Ashley snapped his fingers. "Hey. I'm right here, Darbo. Don't worry about the rules. War is really simple. Simpler than Go Fish, even. You just cut the deck in two, see, and take turns drawing, one after the other, and we see who has the higher card. Higher card takes both, and adds them to your deck. You know, because every war is fought one battle at a time."

He grinned, happy with himself, and smoothly shuffled the cards in front of her. Then he bent the cards backward with a rough sound.

“The winner has all the cards at the end.” He looked her in the eyes. “And the loser? Well, she’s left with nothing.”

Behind her, Ed pumped the coffee pot to fill his cup, and it made that drowning scream again. Like lungs filling with water. Something about it made her shoulder blades quiver.

“Bad news, friends.” Ed rattled the security shutter. “Coffee’s gone.”

Ashley’s eyes widened with fake horror. “What? No more caffeine?”

“Afraid so.”

“Well, I guess we’re all going to start murdering each other now.” Ashley shuffled the cards one last time. It slowly dawned on Darby that these dirty playing cards probably weren't usually at the Wanapani rest area. The brochure stand was bolted down, and the radio and coffee were caged behind a security grate. Ashley had brought these cards himself. Because he was a playful kind of evil, fascinated by games and tricks. Card tricks, surprises, and misleading people.

I’m a magic man, Lars, my brother.

The clues had all been there. She just hadn’t seen them.

“You should get some rest,” Ashley told her. “You look tired.”

Her throat felt like dry paper. “I’m fine.”

“Yeah?”

“Yeah.”

“No rest for the wicked.” He grinned. “Right?”

“Something like that.”

“How much sleep did you get last night?”

“Enough.”

“Enough, huh? What's that?”

“I . . .” Her voice broke. “An hour, two—”

“Oh, no, that’s not enough.” Ashley leaned forward, his chair creaking, and dealt the cards between them. She marveled; his fingers were so chillingly fast.

“Humans need six to nine hours of sleep each night,” he told her. “I get a full eight every night. That’s not just a suggestion, honey, that’s how our bodies work. Less than that hurts your brain. That’s everything — your reflexes, your emotions, your memory. Even how smart you are.”

“Then we’ll be evenly matched,” Darby said.

Ed chuckled, returning to his seat. “Kick his ass. Please.”

But she didn’t pick up her cards. Neither did Ashley. They quietly looked at each other across the table as the wind growled outside. A gust blew through the broken window in the men’s restroom, rattling the door. The room was getting colder, but no one had noticed yet.

“Luckily for you,” Ashley said, “the card game War is all luck. You know, not like the real thing.”

Darby studied his eyes. They were large, green, with hints of amber. She looked for something familiar, something human to connect with — fear, caution, awareness — but found nothing.

Eyeballs are on stalks, she’d learned randomly, at an art gallery in October. She forgot the artist's name, but he’d been there, drinking a Dos Equis, happily saying he’d used real autopsy photos in his work. To Darby, the shape of the eye nerves looked like insects, like antennae on a slug. It made her skin crawl. Now, she imagined Ashley’s big eyes hanging, sending signals along those stalks into his brain. He was a monster, an alien of nerves and flesh. Not human at all.

And he was still watching her.

“Not like the real thing,” he repeated.

The cards sat between them, ignored. Questions fluttered in her mind like trapped birds, things she wanted to ask but couldn’t. Not while Ed and Sandi were close enough to hear.

Why are you doing this?

Why kidnap a child?

What are you going to do with her?

And those green dragon eyes kept staring back at her, full of secrets. Like jewels, they scanned her body, judging her size, planning possibilities and what-ifs. They were frighteningly smart, in all the ways Lars's had been frighteningly stupid. But it was a cold intelligence.

Other questions popped into her head: How fast are you? How strong are you? If I cut your face with Blue's keys, could I blind you? Right now, if I ran for the building's front door, could I make it?

A door opened. A cold breeze slipped into the room.

Ed glanced over. "Hi, Lars."

Ashley smirked.

Over the sound of the wind, Rodent Face stood by the door, his right hand in his jacket pocket, gripping that black .45. She'd seen it now, twice when he'd chased her. She didn't know much about guns, but she knew this one used a magazine, so it held more bullets than a revolver's five or six. She could just see its shape under his blue coat, a bulge at his right hip — but only because she knew to look for it.

Ed wouldn't notice it.

And Sandi was asleep.

Darby was surrounded again. Ashley at the table, and Lars stood by the door. She’d been surrounded this whole time — they’d been quietly planning their positions all night — though she hoped her dive through the restroom window had been a surprise. It had certainly saved her life, at least for a little while longer—

“Dara,” Ed said, startling her. “You never answered the question, did you?”

“What?”

“You know. The question from earlier. Your biggest fear.” He spun his empty Styrofoam cup on the table. “I told you mine. Ashley told his door story. Sandi hates snakes. So what about you?”

Everyone looked at her.

She swallowed. She still had Ashley’s IF YOU TELL THEM, I KILL THEM BOTH napkin squeezed in her lap.

“Yeah.” Ashley held back a grin. “Tell us. What scares you, Darbs?”

Words stuck in her throat. “I . . . I don’t know.”

“Guns?” he suggested.

“No.”

“Nail guns?”

“No.”

“Getting murdered?”

“No.”

“I don’t know. Getting murdered is pretty scary—”

“Failure,” she said, cutting Ashley off and staring into his green eyes. “My biggest fear is choosing wrong, failing, and letting someone get kidnapped or killed.”

Silence.

On the bench, Sandi stirred in her sleep.

“That’s . . .” Ed shrugged. “Okay, that was a strange one, but thanks.”

“She’s—” Ashley started to say something but stopped. Ed didn’t notice, but Darby did, and it excited her. What was he about to say?

She’s—

She, meaning Jay Nissen. The little girl from San Diego in the van outside, whose life was at risk right now.

It was just a small mistake, only part of a sentence, but it showed Darby she’d surprised her enemy. Maybe Ashley and Lars had underestimated her — this small art student from Boulder who’d walked into their kidnapping plan. They probably didn't expect her to escape through the restroom window. She was proud of that.

She hoped she was annoying them.

They don’t want to kill me here, in front of people.

Because then they’d have to kill Ed and Sandi, too, and that seemed like a last option. One murder was probably easier to deal with than three. They’d wanted to kill or stop her outside — secretly, but she’d outsmarted them: dove headfirst through a tiny window, hurt her back on a toilet, and bought herself another ten minutes to live.

Those ten minutes were almost over.

Inhale, she told herself. Count to five. Exhale. She had to breathe deeply and calmly. She couldn’t panic. Not now.

Inhale. Count to five. Exhale.

Ashley looked at his brother and gave a small, firm nod. He was clearly the leader. If Darby killed one of them tonight, it had to be him.

She wondered how much of what he'd said was true. The buried car outside wasn't really his. Was he really studying accounting in Salt Lake City? Had he really almost died in a coalmine in Oregon with his thumb crushed in a rusty hinge? Ashley seemed thrilled by lying, misleading, trying on different roles, showing different sides of himself. He was a kid putting on a magic show.

It was past midnight now. Darby had to last another six hours until the CDOT snowplows came at dawn and opened the highway so she could leave. That was a lot of ten-minute bits. But she'd try.

She didn't know what Ashley's quick nod to his brother meant—Lars hadn't moved from the front door—but she didn't like it. The two brothers had just made another silent move against her, and she was, again, trying to protect herself.

But as long as Ed and Sandi are here, they won't kill me.

She looked up at the clock on the wall, and for a sad moment, she thought about how far away dawn was. How dark and cold the night was. How outnumbered and outmatched she was. They could kill everyone in this room. Maybe they planned to. Maybe the threat on the napkin was just a game.

Ashley grinned, like he'd read her mind.

This standoff won't last.

"Alright, everyone," he said happily. "Fighting with Darbs isn't working. Who wants to play another round of circle-time?"

Ed shrugged. "Sure."

"Let's do . . . first job? No. Let's do favorite movies." Ashley looked around the stuffy room, like a happy game-show host. "Okay if I go first again?"

“Knock yourself out.”

“Alright. Actually — well, I don’t have one favorite movie, but a favorite type of movie. Is that okay with everyone?”

Ed waved his hand, like he didn’t care.

“Monster movies,” Ashley said, looking back at Darby. “Not small monsters like werewolves. I mean the huge, towering ones, twenty stories tall, like Godzilla and Rodan. Kaiju movies, they’re called in Japan. You know, where something big is terrorizing a city, throwing model cars around?”

Ed nodded, not really listening. He was tipping his coffee cup, trying to get the last drops.

It didn’t matter, because Ashley was only looking at Darby as he spoke, his words clear and calm, showing his white teeth: “See, I just love kaiju movies. And . . . what I find interesting about them is this. The human heroes — Bryan Cranston and that boring Sergeant Vanilla guy, in the 2014 Godzilla movie, for example — they don’t really matter. They're just there for the audience. Do these weak humans change the story?”

He paused for a moment to let his question hang in the air.

“No,” he finally answered. “Not at all. They just react to what’s happening. Godzilla, Mothra, the MUTO’s — they’re the real stars. They’re going to fight and settle things, and the humans can’t stop them. Does that make sense?”

Darby didn’t answer.

“No matter what you do, the monsters will do what they want.” Ashley leaned forward, making his chair creak, and she smelled his breath as his voice got lower and rougher: “The monsters are gonna fight, and destroy buildings and bridges, and you just have to get out of their way, or you’ll get crushed.”

Silence.

She couldn’t look away. Like staring down a wild animal.

His breath was strong. Like boiled egg yolks and dark coffee, mixed with meaty smells. An hour ago, his tongue had been warm in her mouth. But now his boyish smile was back, like putting on a rubber Halloween mask, and in a moment, he was the happy talker she’d first met. “So, what about you, Darbs? What’s your favorite kind of movie? Horror? Ghosts? Really scary stuff?”

“Romcoms,” she answered.

Lars giggled by the door, a rough sound like a chainsaw humming. Ashley looked at his brother, and his lips curled as the snow fell harder outside. “This is . . . this is going to be a fun night.”

Maybe, Darby thought, looking him in the eye.

But I promise, I won’t make it easy.

“But,” Ashley said, rubbing his eyes like he was sleepy. “I would love a cup of coffee right now.”

“Actually. . .” Ed thought. “Hey, we have some in the truck. It’s the cheap kind you use for camping, where you just add hot water. It tastes like dirt, but it’s coffee. Anyone want some?”

“Cowboy coffee?” Ashley smiled, like he’d found gold. He’d planned this. “That would be great.”

“Sandi hates it.”

“Well, good thing she’s asleep.”

“Yeah? Anyone coming? Alright.” Ed put on black winter gloves and headed for the door. “I’ll be right back—”

“No rush.” Ashley grinned wider. “Take your time, buddy.”

Darby wanted to say something — wait, stop, please don’t go — but her mind felt stuck. The moment was gone, and just like that, Ed was gone. The visitor center’s front door closed, but not all the way.

Lars pushed it — click.

The two brothers looked at each other, then at Darby. In a flash, the mood in the room changed. The three of them were now alone. For however long it took Ed to walk to Sandi’s truck, search through his stuff, find his camping coffee, and come back. Maybe sixty seconds?

Now . . . the only thing keeping Darby alive was Sandi.

And she wasn’t even awake. She snored like a cat on the blue bench, her arms crossed over her belly, her book about to fall off her face. Even a little breeze could knock it off. For the first time all night, Darby could read the title: Luck of the Devil. For the next sixty seconds or so, Darby’s life depended on how easily this middle-aged woman woke up.

“Romcoms,” Ashley mumbled. “How sweet.”

“Better than Godzilla.”

“Alright, Darbs, I’m sick of hinting around.” Ashley kept his voice low, steady, watching Sandi out of the corner of his eye. “So here’s what’s going to happen. I’m going to make you an offer.”

She listened, but in her mind she was counting seconds, like a ticking clock: Sixty seconds for Ed to walk to his cousin’s truck and back.

Fifty seconds, now?

“This offer is only good once, Darbs, and then it’s gone for good. No second chances. So think hard before you decide—”

“What are you doing with that little girl?”

He licked his lips. “We’re not talking about Jay right now.”

“Are you going to kill her?”

“That’s not the point.”

“It’s pretty damn important to me—”

“Darby.” He was getting annoyed now, showing his perfect white teeth, his voice a tight whisper: “This isn’t about her. Don’t you understand? This is about you, and me, and my brother, and everyone else stuck in the middle at this rest stop. This is about the choice you’re going to make, right now.”

Forty seconds.

She thought about Lars, guarding the door behind her, and her stomach twisted with queasy dread. His dopey smile, the shiny scars dotting his hands, his blank little eyes. She didn’t think she could say it aloud — but then she did: “Is he . . . is Lars going to rape her?”

“What?” Ashley rolled his eyes. “Ugh. Disgusting. Darbs, you’re not listening—”

“Answer me,” she said, looking over at Sandi. “Or I swear to God, I’ll start screaming as loud as I can right now—”

“Do it.” He leaned back. “See what happens.”

She still had her Honda keys in her hand, on her lap. The sharpest one — her Dryden Hall dorm key — was held between her thumb and pointer finger. But she couldn’t trust herself to move across the table fast enough. Ashley would see her coming; he’d raise a hand to protect his face. It wouldn’t work. She wasn’t strong enough or fast enough.

“I dare you,” he whispered. “Scream.”

She almost took the chance.

Then Ashley looked over Darby’s shoulder. He nodded again, and she realized with a shiver of panic — Lars was now standing right behind her. She hadn’t heard him coming, but now she heard the crinkle of his ski jacket moving, just inches behind her. Like the moment they’d first met. She jumped, half-expecting those scarred hands to grab her throat and squeeze — but Lars knelt instead, grabbing her purse from the floor next to her ankle.

“Yoink.” He carried it back to the door.

Ashley glanced back at her, chewing on his lower lip. “Darbs, so we’re clear, I’m giving you a chance to take it all back. A big, red reset button. It’s easy, too, because all you have to do is nothing. Just keep your mouth shut.”

Twenty seconds.

“See, Darbs, we’ll all agree that this little accident never happened. We — my brother and I — we’ll pretend you never broke into our van. You’ll pretend you never saw Jaybird. We’ll all just . . . just erase the last few hours from our brains, and when the snowplows rumble up here first thing in the morning, we’ll all just hop into our cars and go our separate ways. A peaceful end for everyone.”

Pop-pop. Lars opened her wallet. Credit cards clattered to the floor. He sniffed, checking out her Utah driver’s license, and unfolded a crumpled twenty, which he pocketed.

Ten seconds.

“I’ll be honest.” Ashley leaned forward. “I’m really, really hoping you’ll just ignore this. Get some rest. You’re tired. You look awful. You won’t stand a chance against Lars and me. So just . . . let the monsters do what they do, okay?”

Five seconds.

“Please, Darbs. It’ll be easier on all of us.” He glanced at Sandi as he said this, as if his threat wasn’t already clear enough.

Darby felt her cheeks burn. “I can’t.”

“We won’t hurt Jay, you know.” He tilted his head. “Is that it? Is that what you’re afraid of? Because if so, I can promise you—”

“You’re lying.”

“No one will get hurt tonight, if you cooperate.”

“I know you’re lying.”

“She’ll be fine,” Ashley said, waving his hand. “Hey, by the way. I saw a bunch of papers in the backseat of your car. Black papers. What’s all that?”

“Why do you care?”

His eyes turned cold. “You looked into Garver family business. So I looked into yours. Answer the question.”

“It’s . . . just papers.”

“For what?”

“Gravestone rubbings.”

“What’re those?”

“I . . . I use crayons to, uh . . . to make rubbings of headstones.”

“Why?”

“Because I collect them.”

“Why?”

“I just do.” She hated him looking at her so closely.

“You’re kind of a messed up girl,” Ashley said. “I like it.”

She said nothing.

“And you have a scar over your eyebrow.” He leaned over the table, looking closely in the bright light. “That must have been . . . what, thirty stitches? You only really see it when you frown. Or smile.”

She stared at the floor.

“Is that why you don’t smile much, Darbs?”

She wanted to cry. She wished it would be over.

“Smile,” he whispered. “You’ll live longer.”

It’s been over a minute.

Where the hell was Ed? She imagined what could be happening. Maybe he couldn’t find the camping coffee. Maybe he was sneaking a drink. Or maybe . . . maybe he’d seen some small sign, figured out they were kidnapped, and was trying to get a cell signal to call the police? Or, what if Jay got out of the kennel and ran to him? He’d be another witness. That would force Ashley and Lars to start shooting.

Every second felt dangerous. She looked up at the Garfield clock, and Ashley noticed. “That’s an hour fast, you know.”

“I know.”

“It’s only one o’clock.”

“I know.”

He licked his lips, looking at the clock. The picture on it; of Garfield in love, giving roses to Arlene. “Hey, what’s that cat’s name? The pink one?”

“Arlene.”

“Arlene. That’s a pretty girl’s name. Like yours.”

“Yours, too,” she said.

He smirked, enjoying the back-and-forth, glancing back at her eyebrow. “How’d you get the scar, anyway?”

“A fight,” she lied. “In junior high.”

She’d crashed her bicycle into a garage door. If it could be called a fight, the garage door had won. Twenty-eight stitches and an overnight at Saint Joseph. The other fifth graders had called her Frankengirl.

She couldn’t tell if Ashley believed her. He licked his lips. “I should warn you, Darbs, if you’re . . . you know, planning on fighting us tonight. Are you?”

“Am I what?”

“Planning to fight us?”

“I’m thinking about it.”

“Well, if you are, you’d know. I’ve always been kind of special.”

“I bet you are.”

“See, I’m not just lucky — I’m protected, I think. From what happens after. It’s like magic I have. In the end, things always work out for me.” He leaned in closer, like he was sharing a delicate secret. “You might call it luck, but I really think it’s something else. My toast always lands jelly-side up, you could say.”

She had to ask. “You don’t really have asthma, do you?”

“Nope.”

“Do you even go to Salt Lake Institute of Tech?”

His grin widened. “Made-up school.”

“What about your fear of doors?”

“Door hinges. That one’s true, actually.”

“Really?”

“Yeah. They give me the creeps.” He held his hand over his heart. “I swear. I can’t touch them, and I try not to look at them. Ever since I almost lost my thumb down in Chink’s Drop, they’ve really bothered me.”

“Regular door hinges?”

“Yeah.”

“I was sure you’d made that up, too. It didn’t seem real.”

“Why not?”

“Because,” Darby said calmly, “I didn’t think you’d be such a wimp.”

A floorboard creaked.

Ashley looked back at her coldly, like she’d proven him wrong, and the lights flickered overhead. Then he sighed, swallowed once, and when he finally spoke again, his voice was tightly controlled: “You’re playing with a child’s life. Don’t forget that. Tonight might end well, but you’re risking it.”

“I don’t believe you.”

“It’s not about sex,” Ashley said, frowning with obvious disgust. “It’s about money. If you really need to know.”

Sandi stirred again on the bench. Luck of the Devil slid a few centimeters down her face. Darby wondered if she was really asleep. What if she was only pretending? What if she’d heard the whole conversation?

“I mean, listen.” Ashley suppressed a laugh, loosening up again. His demeanor changed quickly; light to dark and back again. “You should see this house, Darbs. Looks like Mr Burns’ mansion. Daddy owns a tech startup, something about a video player. You know, computer stuff, which I don’t understand. I’m more of a practical guy. Which is why we’re borrowing Jaybird here, taking her out to the Rockies for a few weeks, letting Mommy and Daddy get real worried and offer money, and once we’re paid enough, we’ll cash out and leave her at a bus station in some dumpy town in Kansas. She won’t be harmed. It’ll be like a vacation. Hell, maybe we’ll even teach her how to snowboard while we’re—”

“You’re lying again.”

His friendly grin vanished again. “I already told you, Darbs. Pay attention. We won’t hurt her—”

“You already hurt her,” she snarled, hoping Sandi was really awake under her paperback, really listening. “You shot a nail through her hand. And I swear to God, Ashley, if I get the chance, I’ll do worse to you.”

Silence.

At the door, Lars slipped her wallet back into her purse.

“So . . .” Ashley paused. “You saw Jay’s hand?”

“Yes.”

He thought about this for a few moments, sucking his lower lip again. “Okay. Good.” He hardened, another eerie change. “Good, good. Great, even. Let’s call this a lesson, okay? If it’s in my best interests to keep Jaybird alive — scared, but alive — and yesterday morning I got sick of her whining and shot a nail into her palm . . . well, Darbo, just imagine what I’ll do to someone who I don’t have to keep alive. Imagine what I’ll do to this rest stop. What I’ll do to Ed and Sandi. What I’ll make you watch. And it’ll all be your fault, because you thought you were too good to cooperate. So I’m asking you again, Darby. And I’m warning you, too — think carefully about what you say next, because if it’s the wrong thing, I promise you, you won’t be the only one who dies tonight.”

She stared back at him, afraid to blink.

“Also,” he added, “your nose is bleeding.”

She touched her nose—

He lunged forward, grabbed a fistful of her hair, and slammed her face into the tabletop. Fireworks behind her eyes. Dizzying pain. The cartilage in her nose made a wet crunch, and she jerked backward, nearly falling off her chair, holding both hands to her face.

Across the room, Sandi jolted awake. Her paperback fell to the floor. “What . . . what happened?”

“Nothing, nothing,” Ashley said, looking at Darby. “We’re fine.”

Darby nodded, pinching her nose. Hot blood ran down her wrists, bright red. Her eyes stung, trying not to cry.

Don’t cry.

“Oh, honey, your nose—”

“Yeah. I’m fine.” Darby tasted metallic blood in her teeth. Big drops hit the tabletop. Her fingers stuck together.

“What happened?”

“High altitude,” Ashley said sharply. “Low air pressure. It just gets to you. My nose was bleeding like a faucet back at Elk Pass—”

Sandi ignored him. “Need a tissue?”

Darby shook her head quickly, pinching her nose. Blood ran down her throat in thick gulps. Drops dotted her lap.

Oh, Jesus, don’t cry.

Sandi walked over, her big purse swinging. She grabbed a bunch of brown napkins from the coffee counter and put them in Darby’s lap. She touched her shoulder. “Are you sure? It’s . . . it’s really bleeding.”

Darby felt her face tense up, like her skin was pulled tight. Burning heat on her cheeks. Her vision blurred with tears, her breaths hissing through her teeth, while Ashley calmly watched her from across the table with his hands folded in his lap.

Don’t cry, Darby, or he will kill everyone here.

“I’m fine,” she gasped. “It’s just the altitude—”

“I had my first beer at eight thousand feet,” Ashley said again. “Cut my hand on a fluorescent light, and I bled clear red water for two days straight—”

“Oh, shut up,” Darby snarled.

He froze, startled by her sudden fierceness. This should have been another win for Darby, another small moment of prey catching predator off-guard, but she already knew it was a huge mistake.

Because Sandi had noticed.

“I . . .” The lady hesitated, palms up. She glanced between them, her yellow parka crinkling as she moved. “Wait. What’s really going on here?”

Silence.

Ashley chewed his lip thoughtfully, and then nodded to Lars.

No, no, no—

Lars reached into his coat pocket for his pistol. But the front door banged open beside him, hitting the wall, startling him—

“Finally found the coffee.” Ed came in, boots squeaking, spattered with snowflakes, and slammed a clipped baggie of ground Colombian Roast on the table between them. “The recipe is two tablespoons for every eight ounces of boiling — oh, holy shit, that’s blood.”

“The altitude,” Darby choked.

Sandi said nothing.

“Damn.” Ed looked Darby up and down. “You really got it. Keep pressure on your nose, and lean forward, not backward.”

She tilted her head forward.

“Good. Forward makes it clot. Backward, and it all pours down your throat and you get a stomach full of blood.” He brushed snow from his shoulders. “And use those napkins. They’re free.”

“Thanks.”

As Ed moved past, Darby glanced over to Sandi, sharing a brief, shaky look. Sandi was suspicious now, eyes wide, glancing between the two brothers. The outline of Lar’s hidden pistol was clearly shadowed in the overhead light.

Darby raised a finger to her lips: Shhh.

Sandi nodded once.

At the same time, Ashley must have been signaling to Lars. Darby turned back and only caught the end of it, but it looked like a frantic hand-to-throat gesture: Stop, stop, stop. That was it; the room had almost erupted into violence. Ed had no idea that he might have just saved everyone’s lives by wandering back inside when he did with a bag of instant coffee.

Now, he reached through the security shutter and dispensed hot water. “It’s not quite boiling, but it’s hot enough for tea. Should be okay for some shitty coffee.”

“Mana from heaven,” said Ashley. “Sweet, sweet caffeine.”

“Yep, that’s the idea.”

“You’re my hero, Ed.”

He nodded, clearly losing patience with Ashley’s chatter. “Good to hear.”

Sandi backed up and sat on the corner bench, where she could watch the whole room. Darby saw her pick up her book, but hold it in her lap. Her other hand was hidden inside her purse, behind the embroidered letters of Psalm 100:5. Maybe she was holding pepper spray.

Please, Sandi, don’t say anything.

The Wanapani rest area was ready to explode. All it needed was a spark — and this room was full of tension. Carefully, where no one could see, Darby opened the IF YOU TELL THEM, I KILL THEM BOTH note in her lap, under the table, and wrote another message on her thigh. She put the cap back on her pen and folded the napkin tightly, leaving a bloody thumbprint.

“Who else wants coffee?” Ed asked.

“Me,” said Lars.

Sandi nodded, but didn’t speak.

“Me, too,” Darby said as she stood, holding her hurt nose and giving the note to Ashley, then turning to Ed. “No sugar, no cream. And make it strong, please. Tonight is going to be a very long night.”

Behind her, she heard Ashley eagerly unfold the napkin.

He was reading her message now.
