No Exit

Chapter 8

11:55 p.m.

Lars didn’t come after her.

The door shut. She took a few unsteady steps outside, her Converse shoes sinking into the fresh snow, her heart pounding in her chest. She was sure Lars would come after her. He should have been right behind her, following her, his hunched figure blocking the doorway, his back to the room so Ashley could attack—

He wasn’t.

Darby shivered and watched the door. No need to hide now; she held the Swiss Army knife like an icepick as she stood in the orange light, waiting for the door to open. But it didn’t.

What had gone wrong?

The eye contact. She realized she had looked at Lars too much. She’d gone too far. And now the armed criminal was still inside the building, with Ashley and the others, and the trap hadn’t worked.

Okay.

Okay, fine.

She had a choice now.

Go back inside? Or keep walking to his van?

Another gust of wind threw snow in her face. She couldn't see for a moment. She blinked hard, rubbing her eyes with her thumbs. When she could see again, the world was darker. The sodium lamp above the visitor center's door had gone out. Another bad sign to add to the others.

Seconds count, she told herself.

Make a choice.

So she did — she decided to keep walking to Lars’s van. She’d open the door, check on Jay again, and turn on the inside light. Maybe even the bright headlights. This would give Lars another reason to come outside. And Ashley would have his chance to attack — if he was still ready. If the ambush could still work.

Something else came to her as she walked — what if there was a gun in the van? Her first search had been quick and rushed. Lars had one, for sure, but what if there was another?

Yes, a gun would change things. Her stomach rumbled.

Wading through knee-deep snow, her left shoe loose without its lace, she crossed the fifty feet to Lars’s van. Snow had piled up on the windshield again, frozen into sheets of ice where it had melted. She’d left the Astro’s back door unlocked, and she was glad she had.

She walked around to the back of the van. She passed the old sticker of the cartoon fox — the peeling letters of WE FINISH WHAT WE START — and wondered if Lars had bought the van from a failed business. Or maybe he’d killed someone for it. Or maybe Rodent Face was a handyman himself. Maybe that was how he got inside your house and checked out your kids’ rooms, opening drawers and smelling pillows.

Darby glanced back at the Wanapani visitor center. The front door was still shut. The lamp was still out. No one was standing near the window, which was odd. She thought she’d see Lars watching her, or at least Ashley. She couldn’t even see Ed and Sandi; they were sitting too far back. Except for the faint orange light behind the half-buried glass, you wouldn't know anyone was inside.

What’s happening in there?

Hopefully nothing. Yet.

She thought about jumping in her Honda and honking the horn — that would definitely get some attention. Lars would definitely come outside to see what was going on. But Ed and Sandi might too. Things could fall apart. She could lose the element of surprise. Shots might be fired. Bullets could bounce around.

She pulled at the Astro’s back door. Still unlocked. It scraped open, dropping a shelf of snow, showing a soupy darkness inside as her eyes adjusted.

She whispered: “Hey.”

Silence.

“Jay. It’s okay. It’s me.”

Another tense moment, long enough for Darby to worry — and then the girl finally stirred, her fingers gripping the kennel bars to steady herself. The frame made a twanging noise, like tight cables. Darby reached into her jeans pocket for her phone, to turn on the LED flashlight, but it wasn’t there. She patted her other pocket. Also empty. She’d left her phone in her purse. On the edge of that porcelain sink, inside the men’s restroom.

Stupid, stupid, stupid.

Inside the van, she smelled the same smells — dog blankets, urine, stale sweat — and noticed a nasty new one.

“I threw up,” the girl whispered. Softly.

“It’s . . . it’s okay.”

“Sorry. My stomach hurts.”

Mine too, Darby thought. She leaned back and looked around the Astro’s bright taillight — yes, the building’s door was still closed. “I’m sorry, Jay. We’re both having a bad night. But we’ll get through this. Okay?”

“I didn’t mean to throw up.”

“It’s fine.”

“I never throw up. Ever.”

“Believe me, Jay, that’ll change in college.”

“College makes you throw up?”

“Something like that.”

“I hate throwing up. If that’s what college is like, I’m not going—”

“Alright, Jay, listen.” Darby touched the kennel, and the little girl’s fingers squeezed hers through the bars. “I’m going to help you. And to help you, I need you to help me first. Okay?”

“Okay.”

“I need you to try and remember. The farting man . . . can you tell me about the gun he’s carrying?”

“It’s little. Black. He keeps it in his pocket.”

“Of course.” She leaned out and checked the building’s front door again — still closed — and asked: “Did you see him keep any knives in here? Bats? Machetes?”

“I don’t know.”

“Any other guns?”

“One other.”

Darby’s heart skipped a beat. “Where?”

“No, it’s not a regular gun—”

Her mind raced, and she barely managed to ask, “Why? Is it bigger?”

“It shoots nails.”

“Like a . . .” Darby paused. “Like a nail gun?”

Jay nodded.

“And you’re . . . you’re sure?”

She nodded harder.

A nail gun.

Just like the cartoon fox on the van. Darby remembered the bandage on Jay’s hand, the small smear of blood on her palm, and it all made sense. Punishment for trying to escape, maybe? Or maybe this thing, this yellow card, was just a small taste of the awful things Lars planned for her at his cabin in the Rockies.

Her hands were shaking again. Not with fear — anger.

A freaking nail gun.

That’s the kind of psycho we’re dealing with.

“And the nail gun is here?” she asked. “It’s in the van with us?”

“I think so.”

Darby doubted a power tool could beat Lars’s .45, but it was way better than a two-inch Swiss Army knife. She’d never used a nail gun before, or even seen one outside of a Lowe’s store — but she hoped it would be easy to learn. How far could it shoot a nail? Was it heavy? Loud? Would a nail to the head kill the person, or just hurt them badly? Point and click, right?

She touched Jay’s right hand through the bars, and felt the seven-year-old’s fingers were wet with fresh, cold blood. The scab on her palm must have opened up.

Point and click.

Darby promised herself she’d kill Lars tonight. Maybe when she and Ashley finally trapped this sicko and beat him until he was a crying, broken mess — well, maybe she’d keep stabbing. Maybe she’d cut his throat. Maybe she’d enjoy it.

Maybe.

She leaned back and checked on the building again — still nothing happening. Now she was getting worried about Ashley, Ed and Sandi. Was Lars really just standing around in there, letting Darby look around in the parking lot outside? After finding her Styrofoam cup in the snow? After following her and Ashley into the restroom? After she looked him in the eye on her way out the door?

Jesus — what the hell was going on?

Awful possibilities flashed through her mind. She tensed, half-expecting a gunshot. But there was nothing. Only cold silence. Only the far-off moan of the wind. Only Jay and herself, standing on shaky legs in that empty parking lot.

The nail gun, she decided.

Lars’s nail gun was her new goal. She’d find it, learn how to use it, and then she’d run back inside, kick open the door, and whatever was happening inside, she’d shoot a nail right into Lars’s whiskery little face. Ka-thunk. Asshole dead. Innocent child saved. Nightmare over.

That would work.

She looked back at Jay, her teeth chattering. “Okay. Where do you think Lars keeps his nail gun? Back here, or in the front?”

“The other one keeps it in an orange box.”

“Keeps it where?”

“It used to be back here, but I think they moved it—”

But Darby wasn’t listening. Jay’s little voice faded away, and in a burst of burning panic, the previous sentence stuck in her brain and repeated: The other one keeps it in an orange box.

The other one.

The other one.

The other one—

Slipping, stumbling back outside, her kneecaps hit hard snow. She caught herself on the brake light and looked around it—

The building's door was now open.

Lars stood in the doorway. Next to him, Ashley.

The other one.

They watched her, fifty feet away, lit by the inside light. They seemed to be talking quietly, so Ed and Sandi wouldn't hear from inside. Their faces were dark, unreadable. But Lars had his thin arm bent up inside his jacket, holding his pistol. And Ashley had the rock in a sock out, in his right hand.

He was swinging it.

Hitting it against his palm.
