No Exit

Chapter 5

9:39 p.m.

Run or hide?

As Lars got closer to his van, Darby chose to hide. She quickly got inside the van, pulling her knees up to her chest and quietly closing the back door — but it caught on a towel.

His footsteps crunched closer.

“Shit—”

She pulled the towel in and gently closed the door. It clicked shut. Now she was trapped inside the predator’s van, stuck between the back door and Jay’s dog kennel. She got as low to the floor as she could, twisting to fit in the small space, and covered herself with piles of blankets and rough rugs. Coca Cola bottles clinked underneath her. The stale smell of dog blankets. Her forehead pressed against the cold metal door, her right elbow twisted awkwardly behind her back. She tried to control her breathing, to keep her scared gasps for air quiet: Inhale. Count to five. Exhale.

Inhale. Count to five. Exhale.

Inhale. Count to—

Now she heard Rodent Face’s footsteps walk around the right side of the van, past the cartoon fox with the nail gun, past the WE FINISH WHAT WE START saying, going between his van and her own Honda. She felt a nauseous mix of fear and relief — if she had chosen to run instead of hide, he would have definitely seen her. He kept coming, breathing heavily between his small teeth, and she saw his shadow pass by the back window above her head. He stopped there, looking inside, a foot away from her, his breath clouding the glass.

Darby held her breath.

If he opens that door, I’m dead—

But he didn’t. He kept walking, circling the van, and stopped at the driver’s door. He grabbed the handle. Darby heard the door screech on its hinges, and the van dipped as a third person climbed in. She heard the jingle of car keys on his red lanyard.

With one eye open, careful not to move the glass bottles, Darby looked at Jay in her dog kennel and raised a shaky finger to her lips: Shhh.

Jay nodded.

In the driver’s seat, Lars sniffled, leaned forward, and put a key in the ignition — but he didn’t turn it. Darby heard him take a long, deep breath. Then silence. Too much silence.

Something is wrong.

She waited, her ears ringing. Her stomach tightened. She held her breath. Rodent Face was a dark shape at the wheel, separated by a cage and silhouetted against the snowy windshield. With her one eye, Darby saw that his head was turned to the side. He was looking up and to his right. At the van’s dome light.

The dome light she’d turned off.

Oh, no.

She could imagine what he was thinking. He was wondering why the light didn’t turn on when he opened the door, like it usually did. What did that mean? That someone else was in his van. That, looking closer at the footprints outside, someone was still inside, hidden under a dusty rug, sweating and shaking with fear—

Lars twisted the key.

The engine started easily, and Darby sighed in relief. He leaned forward and adjusted the air vents. He turned the heater up all the way. He put his Deadpool beanie on the dashboard next to his model airplane, wrinkling a fast food wrapper.

Darby heard a noise beside her. It was Jay, quietly re-wrapping the electrical tape around her mouth. Smart girl, she thought.

The next twenty minutes felt like forever, as the van slowly got warm and humid. Lars let the engine run and flipped through radio stations. He found only static, the repeating robot voice of that CDOT message, and again, Bing Crosby’s goddamn White Christmas.

I can’t get away from that song, Darby thought. It’ll probably play at my funeral. She’d always thought they would have flying cars by then. Now, stuck in a kidnapper’s humid van, breathing through her nose, she wasn’t so sure.

Of course, Lars listened to the whole song, which meant Darby had to, too. Listening to the words made her like it a little more. She’d always just thought it was about snow, but it had a homesickness and longing to it. As Bing Crosby sang, she pictured some poor farm boy, fresh out of high school, stuck in frozen foreign dirt, fighting someone else’s war, dreaming of loved ones back home. She could understand that part.

Lars probably wasn’t thinking that hard about it. He ate a Baby Ruth bar, chewing loudly. He picked his nose and looked at what he found in the light of the dashboard. Farted twice. The second one made him giggle, and then he suddenly turned around and grinned at the back of the van with a mouthful of small, pointy teeth, and Darby’s chest tightened, her heart clenched.

“Warmed it up for you,” he said.

He was looking at Jay’s kennel in the darkness, but he didn’t know he was also looking right at Darby. Just a layer of fabric covering her, and one eye showing. All it would take was a little more light.

He’s looking right at me.

Rodent Face’s grin vanished. He kept staring.

Oh God, he can see me, Darby thought, her sides cramping, feeling spiders crawling on her skin. His eyes are getting used to the dark, and now he knows I’m in here, and oh my God, he’s going to kill me—

He farted a third time.

Or that, I guess.

This was a long one, a loud honk, and then he burst into hard laughter. He screamed as he laughed, punching the passenger seat. He was really happy with himself, barely able to say to his captive: “You’re . . . ah, you’re welcome for the butt-rumble. Nice and warm, huh, Jaybird?”

Darby heard Jay’s electrical tape wrinkle as her head tilted slightly. She imagined the girl making a “See what I’ve been dealing with?” eye-roll.

Then Lars’s belly laughs changed into coughs. They were wet, bubbly, like he had a sinus infection. That explained the mouth-breathing.

Darby’s feet were pressed up against the five-gallon gas can she’d seen before, and beside it, she now noticed a second white jug. A Clorox logo, barely visible in the dashboard light. Bleach, probably.

Five gallons of gasoline.

And bleach.

Things for cleaning a crime scene, maybe?

After the radio played a few more holiday songs (Grandma Got Run Over By A Reindeer, which he sang along to, and Silent Night, which he didn’t), Lars turned off the Astro’s engine and put the keys in his jacket. The van was now like a hot, steamy box; the windows were covered in condensation. Little dots of lamplight sparkled on the glass. Trapped under that heavy feeling, the sweat and melted snow had made Darby’s skin feel clammy. Her sleeves stuck to her wrists, and underneath, her Art Walk hoodie was soaked with nervous sweat.

Lars got out, pulled his Deadpool beanie back over his head, and glanced back at the light inside. He was still a little confused by that detail. But then he turned around, let out one last, loud fart into the van, waved it out with the door, trapping Jay (and Darby) inside with it, and left.

Darby listened to his footsteps disappear. Then, far away, she heard the visitor center’s front door open and close with a soft sound.

Silence.

Jay pulled the electrical tape off her mouth. “He farts a lot.”

“I noticed.”

“I think it’s the burgers.”

Darby threw the rough blanket off her shoulders, wiping wet strands of hair from her face. She kicked open the Astro’s back door and climbed back outside. It felt like getting out of a sauna. Her Converse were soaked, her socks felt gross and squishy, and her right shoe was still missing a shoelace.

“He puts ranch sauce on everything,” Jay continued. “He asks for a cup of it at the drive-through to dip his fries in, but that’s a lie. He just pours it on—”

“Right.” Darby wasn’t listening. The subzero chill was invigorating, like shedding fifty pounds of sweaters. She felt agile and alive again. She knew what she had to do — she just didn’t know how the hell she was going to do it. She stepped back, raised her iPhone, and snapped two quick photos.

Jay didn’t blink, her bloodstained fingers on the kennel bars. “Be careful.”

“I will.”

“Promise you’ll be careful—”

“I promise.”

The girl extended her unhurt hand to Darby. At first she thought it was a handshake, or a pinkie-swear, or some other half-remembered thing from her own childhood, but then Jay dropped something into Darby’s palm. Something small, metallic, as cold as an ice cube.

It was a bullet.

“I found it on the floor,” Jay whispered.

It was lighter than Darby would have guessed, like a blunt little torpedo. She rolled it left to right on her skin. Her palm was shaking; she almost dropped it. This wasn’t a surprise, exactly, but just a grim confirmation of what she feared most.

Of course Lars has a gun.

Of course.

She should have known. This was America, where cops and robbers have guns. Where, as the NRA says, the only way to stop a bad guy with a gun is a good guy with a gun. Cheesy, but so true. She’d never even held a gun, much less shot one, but she’d give anything to have one now.

She realized Jay was still looking at her.

Usually, she hated talking to kids. When she was stuck with her nieces or her friends’ younger siblings, she always treated them like smaller, dumber adults. But now, it was easy. She didn’t need to be careful with her words. She meant everything she said, and changing it would only weaken its simple strength:

“Jay, I promise I will get you out of here. I will save you.”
