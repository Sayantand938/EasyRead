No Exit

Chapter 7

11:09 p.m.

In the men’s restroom, she told Ashley everything.

The van. The dog kennel. The little girl named Jay from San Diego. The electrical tape, the bloody hand, the scary unknown of a yellow card. Even the farts. And no matter how quietly she whispered, her words seemed to bounce around the restroom, hitting the tile and porcelain. She was sure the others could hear.

Ashley breathed out, clearly shocked. His eyes looked bruised in the bright light, and for the first time all night, he looked as tired as Darby felt. And, also for the first time — he didn't say anything.

She watched him, trying to see what he thought. “So.”

“So?” he replied.

“So. We have to do something.”

“Obviously, but what?”

“We stop him.”

“Stop him? That’s not clear.” Ashley glanced back, watching the restroom door, moving closer to her. “Do you mean kill him?”

She wasn’t sure.

“Jesus, you’re talking about killing him—”

“If it comes to that.”

“Oh, my God.” He rubbed his eyes. “Right now? With what?”

Darby opened her small Swiss Army knife.

Ashley laughed, almost choking. “He’s going to have a gun, you know.”

“I know.”

“You’re not thinking this through—”

“I said, I know.” Darby held out the .45-caliber bullet in a shaky palm. “I mean, I literally know he has a gun.”

He looked at the bullet. “So what’s your plan, then?”

“We stop him.”

“That’s not a plan.”

“That’s why I’m telling you. And guess what, Ashley? You’re in this now. It’s 11:10 p.m. on a Thursday night, and there’s a child kidnapper in the next room, and a little girl locked in his crappy van outside, and this is what we have to deal with. I’m asking you now—will you help me?”

That seemed to affect him. “You’re…you’re sure?”

“Positive.”

“That Lars kidnapped her?”

“Yeah.” She thought again. “If that’s even his real name.”

Ashley ran a hand through his hair and stepped back, leaning against a stall door. PEYTON MANNING TAKES IT IN THE ASS was scratched on it. Ashley took deep breaths, staring down at his shoes, like he was trying not to pass out.

She touched his arm. “You okay?”

“Just asthma.”

“Don’t you have an inhaler?”

“No.” He smiled awkwardly. “I, uh, don’t have insurance.”

Darby realized she might have misjudged this tall, dark stranger. Maybe Ashley — ex-magician, chatterbox, Salt Lake Institute of Tech student — wasn’t quite as competent as she’d thought. But then she remembered his impressive trick when he’d returned her note. She hadn’t even felt it. The napkin had just appeared between her fingers, like . . . well, magic.

That was something. Right?

He’d caught his breath now, and looked up at her directly. “I need proof.”

“What?”

“Proof. Can you prove any of this?”

Darby scrolled through the photos on her iPhone. Behind her, the restroom door banged open.

It was Lars.

Rodent Face stomped in, wet boots squealing on tile. Just like that, the kidnapper was inside the room with them, breathing the same air. Darby’s mind screamed — we’re trapped in here, we’re both vulnerable, there’s no time to hide in a stall — and the slouching figure of Lars turned to face them, that stubbly, chinless face wheezing through a mouthful of baby teeth—

Then Ashley grabbed her face, his palms to her cheeks—

“Wait—”

—And he smashed his mouth against hers.

What?

Then Darby understood. And after another thrilling second, she joined in, pressing her body against his, clasping her fingers behind his neck. Ashley’s hands explored her back, her hips. His warm breath filled her mouth.

For a few long seconds, Lars watched them. Then she heard his squeaky footsteps again, moving to the sinks. A faucet turned. Water rushed out. The soap dispenser pumped once, twice. He was washing his hands.

Darby and Ashley kept going, eyes tightly shut. For Darby, it hadn’t been this terribly awkward since ninth-grade Tolo, just clumsy touches and misplaced squeezes and shallow breaths. He was either a terrible kisser or he wasn’t trying; his tongue felt like a dead slug in her mouth. After a painful eternity — don’t stop, don’t stop, he’s still watching us — she heard the sink turn off, then a paper towel tore and crumpled. Another long silence, and then finally, Lars left the restroom.

The door clicked shut.

Darby and Ashley separated. “Your breath is awful,” he said.

“Sorry, I drank six Red Bulls today.”

“No shit—”

“Here.” She shoved her phone toward him — a blurry photo of Jay stuck in a kennel. Only the girl’s bloody fingernails were clear. “You wanted proof? This is what could happen. She’s out there, in his van, fifty feet from here, right now.”

Ashley barely glanced at the photo — he already believed her. He nodded, taking a shaky breath. “He . . . he didn’t come in here to wash his hands. He was watching us.”

“And you’re in this now.”

“Okay.”

“Okay?”

“Okay.” He sighed. “Let’s . . . just do it, I guess.”

Darby nodded, focused. But she was suddenly thinking about her mom’s cancer.

All of that — the awful day before this — felt like a lifetime ago; one she’d escaped. Remembering it now punched her in the stomach. She still had no cell service. She still didn’t understand Devon’s dumb, confusing text from hours ago: She’s okay right now—

“Darby?”

Ashley was looking at her.

“Okay, yeah.” She calmed herself, wiping his saliva from her lip, blinking in the bright light. “We need to catch this jerk off guard. And since he thinks we know, he won’t turn his back to us.”

“Even if he does, that butter knife of yours won’t do much.”

“So we’ll hit him on the head.”

“With what?”

“What do you have?”

Ashley thought about it. “I . . . I have a jack in my car, I think—”

Too obvious, she knew. Not easy to hide. But she had a better idea. She reached into her jeans pocket and pulled out the pretty stone she’d taken from the Wanapani coffee counter. “This will work better.”

“A rock?”

“Take off your shoe.”

He hesitated, then leaned against the stall door and slipped off his left shoe.

“Now your sock,” she said. “Please.”

“Why mine?”

“Girl socks are too short.”

He handed her a white, ankle-length sock, warm as a handshake and slightly yellowed. He winced. “My washer’s broken.”

Darby pulled the sock tight, slipped the rock inside it, and sealed it up with a tight square knot. She swung it once, smacking it into her palm. The arc gave the small stone fierce power; even a quick flick of the wrist could break an eye socket. Or at least, that was the idea.

Ashley looked at it, then her. “What’s that?”

“It’s called a rock-in-a-sock.”

“I . . . see why, I guess.”

She’d seen it on a TV survival show. “Rock-in-a-sock,” she repeated.

“The Cat in a Hat’s kind of weapon.”

She smiled, showing the scar over her eyebrow for a moment. “Okay.” She lifted the weapon. “Here’s what I’m thinking. Lars likes to stand by that front door and watch the exit, right?”

“Right.”

“One of us — Person A — will walk past him. Through the front door. Outside, toward his van. He knows we’re here now, so he’ll follow Person A outside. He’ll have to. And to do this, he’ll go through the door, turning his back to Person B.”

She hit the rock-in-a-sock against her palm. It hurt.

“Person B — who is stronger than Person A — will come up behind Lars and hit him in the back of the head. One good hit should be enough to knock him out. But if it isn’t, Person A, who has the knife, will turn around and we’ll both jump him—”

“You mean double-team?”

“Yeah. Tag-team.”

“Those don’t mean the same thing.”

“You know what I mean, then.” She didn’t want to be too specific about this part. Ideally, one hit with the rock-in-a-sock would do it. If they had to fight, it would still be two against one, and both of them now had weapons. Lars might be a violent sociopath, but how ready could he be for a surprise attack from two sides?

More importantly: how quickly could he pull his .45 and shoot?

Ashley was beginning to understand. “So Person B is me, right?”

“It’ll be two against one, using the doorway to narrow the space—”

“Am I Person B?”

Darby put the rock-in-a-sock in Ashley’s hand and folded her fingers around it, one by one. “You’re stronger than I am, aren’t you?”

“I was . . . I was kind of hoping you were Rhonda Rousey or something.”

“I’m not.”

“Then I guess I’m stronger.”

“Two against one,” she repeated, like a mantra.

“What if we kill him?”

“We’ll knock him down and take everything he has. Get his gun. Get the keys on that lanyard. If he fights back, so do we. I was in the van with him. I know what we’re dealing with, and I’ll cut his throat myself if I have to—”

She stopped, surprised by her words.

Surprised she’d meant them, too.

“You didn’t answer my question,” Ashley said, moving closer. “And just so you know, Darbs, this is assault if you’re wrong.”

She did, and she knew she wasn’t. She’d spent thirty minutes lying still in Lars’s sweaty van under an Indian blanket, listening to that cold-eyed thing eat and fart and giggle with a seven-year-old girl locked in a dog kennel. She knew that no matter what, she’d see that creepy smile in her nightmares: Warmed it up for you, Jaybird. But Ashley, she understood why he was unsure. This had all fallen on him suddenly. In about ten minutes.

In her other pocket, she still had the .45 bullet. Pressed tight against her leg. That was her real fear—Lars’s gun. He’d use it if they didn’t stop him fast. Even if he only got off a wild shot or two, there were people nearby—Ed and Sandi—to think about. Darby had never been in a fight before, so she wasn’t sure what to expect, but she knew the movies were wrong.

“If you can,” she added, “try to keep one eye closed.”

“Why?”

“We’re going to fight him outside, maybe in the dark. So try to keep one of your eyes closed now, while you’re inside, in the light, and then you’ll have one eye that can see better in the dark. Make sense?”

He nodded, without much enthusiasm.

“And . . . you said you have asthma?”

“Mild shortness of breath. I’ve had it since I was a kid.”

“Well, when I was little,” Darby said, “I used to have panic attacks. Really bad ones that made me breathe too fast and faint. I’d be curled up on the floor, feeling like I was choking, and my mom would always hold me and say: Inhale. Count to five. Exhale. And it always worked.”

“Inhale. Count to five. Exhale?”

“Yep.”

“So, in other words, breathe? That’s brilliant.”

“Ashley, I’m trying to help.”

“Sorry.” He looked at the door. “I’m just . . . I’m just having trouble with this.”

“You saw him, too.”

“I saw a regular weirdo.” He sighed. “And now we’re about to beat him up.”

“I’m sorry,” she said, touching his wrist. “I’m so sorry to pull you into this. But I was pulled into it, too. And I can’t save her by myself.”

“I know. I’ll help.”

“If we don’t do something right now, Lars could lose it and attack us first. Every second we wait here is a second we give him, to decide what to do with us. If it makes it easier for you, stop thinking about this little girl’s life, who you’ve never even met. Think about yours—”

“I said I’ll do it,” he said, and the ice-tray lights flickered behind him.

“Thank you.”

“Don’t thank me yet.”

“I mean it, Ashley. Thank you—”

“I’ll help,” he said with a nervous grin, “if you give me your phone number.”

Darby grinned too, all teeth: “If you help beat the crap out of a complete stranger with a rock just for me, I might just marry you.”

Lars saw them leave the restroom.

He was back at his post, a few steps to the right of the front door in the lobby's hidden spot. He was struggling to fold a map of Mount Hood, but he tilted his head to watch Darby and Ashley cross the room. Darby kept her head down. Her gray Converse shoes squeaked, her socks still wet from melted snow.

No eye contact.

Leaving the restroom at the same time had been a big mistake, Darby realized. Ed and Sandi probably both noticed, and they’d guess what happened. Behind her, Ashley bumped a chair loudly. Smooth move.

Her heart was pounding so hard she was surprised the others couldn’t hear it. Her cheeks were bright red. She knew she looked nervous, but luckily, it might fit the strange situation. If she’d just met a stranger for a quick fling in the dirtiest restroom in Colorado, she’d feel pretty anxious about this walk of shame.

She held her Swiss Army knife hidden against her wrist. The metal was ice-cold against her skin. She had to be ready — if Ashley’s first hit didn’t take Rodent Face down, she’d stab him in the throat. The face. Those small, dull eyes.

I’ll cut his throat if I have to.

She thought about Jay in the Chevrolet Astro outside, crouched in a dog kennel wet with her urine, her hand bloody and bandaged, with five gallons of gasoline and a jug of Clorox bleach nearby. She wondered what would happen to this kid if they failed.

She was still angry at herself for leaving the restroom at the same time as Ashley. That had been stupid.

Ed definitely noticed. He looked up at them, sipped his coffee, and nodded at the radio. “You missed it.”

Ashley tensed up. “Missed what?”

“The emergency alert changed again. It’s not good. The road east is blocked by a semi-truck that crashed at the bottom of the hill. Several people died.”

“How far away is it?”

“Mile marker ninety-nine. So, seven, eight miles?”

Too far to walk.

Darby sighed, looking back at the large Colorado map on the wall. That would put the wreck near Coal Creek, halfway between the Wanapa (Little Devil) and Wanapani (Big Devil) rest areas marked by blue dots. It felt strange how completely trapped they were — a blizzard moving in from the west, and a crashed semi-truck eight miles downhill to the east, blocking the road behind them. Like a planned trap, just like the one they were about to set. She wondered if the road crews were still expected to arrive at dawn, or if they wouldn't be there until tomorrow afternoon. If that was the case, it would be a very long time to hold a criminal at gunpoint.

Ashley reached through the security bars and adjusted the Sony’s antenna. He looked closely at the coffee stand, at the dark areas under the counters. “And . . . do you think they have a radio back there?”

“What?”

“A two-way radio? Or a regular phone? They must have one.”

Calm down, Ashley.

“Yeah?” Ed grunted. “If they do, it’s state property, locked up—”

Ashley pointed. “Held by a cheap padlock. One good hit with something heavy, and those shutters come right up.”

“I’m not in a felony mood just yet.”

“Maybe you’ll change your mind,” Ashley said, “in the next few minutes.”

Darby knew he would. She stood by the window, trying to look calm, and looked outside into the dark trees. The snowflakes kept coming, some rising, some falling, catching bits of light like sparks from a campfire. A few steps behind her, she heard Ashley exhale through chattering teeth. He had the rock-in-a-sock stuffed up his right sleeve, ready to drop into his palm and swing.

They’d agreed on a secret signal. When Ashley was ready, he’d cough once. This would be Darby’s cue to walk to the front door, pass Lars on her way outside, and start the ambush. Like setting off a bear trap.

Only problem? Ashley wasn’t ready.

He stood there, teeth gritted, taking short breaths. She hoped his shortness of breath wouldn’t be a problem. Typical for her luck — I get help from the youngest, tallest, strongest-looking guy around, and he turns out to have asthma. Just great. And she couldn’t even imagine what was going on in poor Ashley’s mind. An hour ago, he’d been showing this guy a Mexican turnover, and now he’d been asked to sneak up behind him and smash his skull in.

It should be me, she realized.

I’m a coward for being Person A.

Maybe. But Ashley was definitely stronger than her. So her being the bait and Ashley being the trap made perfect sense. It just didn’t feel right.

“Hey.” Lars cleared his throat. “Ah . . . excuse me?”

Darby turned to face him, her stomach in knots, her Swiss Army knife hidden up her sleeve.

So did Ashley.

“Does . . .” The child abductor was still by the door, squinting at another tourism brochure: “Does anyone know what this word means?”

Sandi lowered her paperback. “Say it.”

“Res-plend-ent.”

“Resplendent. It means beautiful.”

“Beautiful.” Lars nodded once, stiffly. “Okay. Thank you, Sandi.” His gaze went back to his brochure — but on the way down, it met Darby’s from across the room, and for a half-second, she was stuck in his stupid, beady eyes.

He mouthed: Beautiful.

She looked away.

It’d been over sixty seconds now. Ashley was still standing beside her, his feet stuck to the floor, and now she was starting to worry. She couldn’t just pull him back into the restroom for another pep talk — the first one had already gotten too much attention. She was stuck waiting for his signal.

Come on, Ashley.

She wished he’d just breathe in some dust and cough by accident, so she’d have a reason to go to the door and start the attack. Under her sleeve she poked her thumb on the Swiss Army blade. It was nicely sharp.

Please cough.

She watched him hesitate there, like a kid on a high dive. He’d been so cool, so smooth and confident before, and now he looked like he’d just seen a murder. Darby felt a nervous tightness rise in her throat. She’d picked the wrong ally, and now the plan was falling apart.

Cough. Or you’re going to give us away—

Ed noticed. “Ashley, you’re quiet all of a sudden.”

“I’m . . . I’m fine.”

“Hey, look, man, I’m sorry about circle time.”

“It’s alright.”

“I was just teasing you—”

“I’m fine. Really.” Ashley adjusted his sleeve as he spoke, keeping the rock-in-a-sock hidden.

Ed smiled, tapping the table’s edge with two fingers. A quiet little heartbeat, and for a moment the room was silent, and Darby could feel that sound in her bones. “Your biggest fear is . . . you said it was door hinges. Right?”

Ashley nodded.

Sandi set her paperback down. “Mine’s snakes.”

“Snakes, huh?”

“Mm-hm.”

Ed sipped his coffee, still tapping. “Mine is . . . well, I didn’t really know how to say it. But I think I can now.”

Another growl of wind, and the lights flickered overhead. The room seemed like it would fall into darkness.

Lars watched like a shadow.

Ashley licked his lips. “Let’s . . . uh, let’s hear it, then.”

“Okay.” Ed took an uneasy breath. “So . . . here’s some wisdom I learned the hard way. You want to know the secret to ruining your life? It’s not one big choice. It’s many small ones, that you make every day. It’s mostly excuses, in my case. Excuses are poison. When I was a vet, I had lots of good ones, like: This is my time. I deserve this. Or: No one can judge me for this drink; I just operated on a golden retriever who ran into a barbed wire fence, with her eyeball hanging out by a thread today. See? Awful. That’s how you fool yourself. And then one day I was at Jan’s — I mean, my wife’s sister’s place — a few years ago for my goddaughter’s wedding party. Wine, homemade beer. I brought champagne. But I also brought a bottle of Rich and Rare for myself, and I hid it in their bathroom, inside their toilet tank.”

“Why?”

“Because I didn’t want anyone to see how much I was drinking.”

Silence.

Darby realized he’d stopped drumming on the table.

Ashley nodded kindly. “My mom had trouble with that, too.”

“But . . .” Ed nudged Sandi’s shoulder. “Well, thank God for my cousin Sandi, because she called me up at two o’clock yesterday and told me she was driving me to Denver for family Christmas. No excuses.”

Sandi sniffled. “We missed you, Eddie.”

“So, yeah.” He straightened. “To answer the question, my biggest fear is this Christmas in Aurora. I’m afraid my wife and sons will be at Jack’s tomorrow night. And I’m even more afraid they won’t.”

For a long moment, no one spoke.

Ashley swallowed. Some color was back in his cheeks. “Uh, thanks, Ed.”

“No problem.”

“That couldn’t have been easy to say.”

“It wasn’t.”

“Been sober long?”

“No,” Ed said. “I drank this morning.”

Silence.

“That, uh . . .” Ashley hesitated. “That sucks.”

“Tell me about it.”

Another tense silence, and the lights flickered again, as five people shared air in this small room with three hidden weapons.

“Excuses are poison,” Ed repeated. “Doing what’s right is hard. Talking yourself out of it is easy. Does that make sense?”

“Yeah,” Ashley said. “More than you know.” Then he looked directly at Darby, and raised a fist to his mouth.

He coughed once.

The trap was set. She walked, feeling the tiny hairs on her skin stand up. She looked Lars in the eye as she walked toward the front door — he looked up from his pamphlet and watched her go by, turning his thin neck to follow her — and then Darby pulled the door open. A rush of freezing air. Cutting wind. Sharp snowflakes hitting her eyes.

She stepped outside, her shoulders tight, the knife gripped in her knuckles.

Follow me, Rodent Face.

Let’s end this.
