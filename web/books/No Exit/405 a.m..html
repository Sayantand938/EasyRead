No Exit

4:05 a.m.

Thirty minutes.

Thirty minutes.

Just survive the next thirty minutes, until the cops get here.

This repeated in her mind as she walked back to the rest area. Ashley had told her to walk in front, with Jay at her side, while he held the nail gun to their backs. He also had her iPhone.

He’d grabbed it from Darby’s hand before she could delete the 9-1-1 text. He swiped through it now, the screen lighting up the snow in a ghostly blue as they walked, and she quietly waited for Ashley Garver’s huge reaction when he found out the truth — that the cops were coming right now.

But nothing happened. They walked in silence. She heard him lick his lips, adjusting how he held the nail gun as he scrolled through her phone, and she realized — he’s not reading my texts.

The idea that Darby might have texted the police hadn’t crossed his mind. He was only looking at her call history, searching for successful calls to 9-1-1. Which, of course, she’d already tried, many times, back at 9 and 10 p.m. He was scrolling through them, checking the times.

“Call failed,” he read. “Call failed. Call failed. Call failed—”

You have no idea. She wanted to laugh, but couldn’t.

You’re holding it in your hand.

“Good, good.” He sounded like he was relaxing.

She squeezed Jay’s good hand, lowering her voice. “Don’t be afraid. He can’t kill me, because I know where his keys are—”

“That’s true, Darbs,” Ashley cut in. “But I can hurt you.”

Yeah? She wanted to say. You have half an hour, jerk.

She really hoped thirty minutes was how long it would actually take the police to get here, and not just a guess from the dispatcher. Between the crashed semi and the blizzard, a lot could go wrong that they couldn't see from a warm desk at the sheriff's station. What if it wasn’t thirty minutes, but forty? An hour? Two hours?

Ashley groped her as he walked. The nail gun poked her back, his fingers searching her pockets, front and back. Her legs. The sleeves of her hoodie. “Just making sure,” he breathed on her neck.

He’d been searching for his keys.

The only thing keeping me alive right now is that dumb keychain. She pictured those keys now, in the snow outside the restroom window where they’d landed. Slowly disappearing, one snowflake at a time.

“You should just tell me now what you did with them,” he whispered. “It’ll be so much easier for both of us.”

For a long moment as they walked, Darby didn’t quite understand what he meant. Then the realization dawned on her slowly, like a huge shape rising from the deep, turning into a monster.

When they got back inside the visitor center, Ashley was going to torture her. This was certain. He would give her a warning, or punish her severely, until she confessed where the keychain was. And as soon as she did, he’d kill her. Her heart jumped in her chest, like a trapped animal. She thought about running, but he’d just shoot her in the back. And he was too strong to fight.

The rest area came closer, appearing in the moonlight. It looked deceptively peaceful, like a model in a snow globe. She saw the cars — their Astro, her Honda, the hidden dumpster she’d once mistaken for Ashley’s car. The icy flagpole, standing like a needle. The bronze group of Nightmare Children. And coming out of the darkness, partly covered in snow, with its broken lamp and boarded-up window, the Wanapani visitor center itself.

Big Devil, the name meant.

Then Ashley turned her quickly — “Turn, turn,” and they followed the path from the parking lot to the front door. The last fifty feet.

I’ve already saved Jay, she reminded herself. I’ve told the police. They have guns. They’ll take care of Ashley and Lars.

All I have to do is stay alive.

This long walk back had taken ten, maybe fifteen minutes, she guessed. So she was already halfway there.

Just fifteen more.

As the building got closer, Darby realized something — she wasn’t even afraid anymore. She was excited, actually, filled with a strange kind of thrill. She’d already been shot at, pepper-sprayed, and choked with a Ziploc bag, and like a cockroach, she’d survived everything Ashley and Lars — and even Sandi — had done to her. Against all odds, Darby was still in this fight. It was too important; this eight-hour mental game with Ashley, all of the night’s twists and turns and wins and losses. And now she had to see his terrible defeat. She wanted to be there when it happened, to see the shock on Ashley’s face when the first police car flashed its lights. It thrilled her, in a dark way she couldn’t explain.

You’ll hurt me, Ashley. You’ll hurt me bad. For these last fifteen minutes or so, I’m all yours. But after that?

You’re mine.

And you have no idea—

“Oh, hey.” Ashley stopped. “You . . . got a text message back at the highway.”

The blue light came back. He was reading her phone again.

Darby panicked. 9-1-1 must have sent a second text message. Of course. The emergency dispatcher meant well but had no way of knowing that Darby was in danger, that her phone was now in the killer’s hand.

“From . . .” Ashley squinted. “From someone named . . . Devon.”

Then he held the cracked iPhone out to her, and when her eyes focused, what was left of Darby’s world fell apart.

It happened. Mom died.

“Ooh,” Ashley said. “Awkward.”

Then he snapped her iPhone in two. “Keep walking.”

The front door slammed like a gunshot.

Jay screamed when she saw Ed. Ashley grinned, showing all her teeth, grabbing her collar and making her look. “Cool, huh?”

Ed Schaeffer was slumped, sitting under the Colorado map, the front of his Carhartt shirt gleaming with dark blood. He tilted his head up at them as they came in, and his lips trembled slightly, like he was trying to talk.

“Don’t move, Eddie.” Sandi knelt beside him, trying to wrap the right amount of gauze around his destroyed jaw. The white first-aid box was open on the floor, its contents spilled out. “Don’t move, I’m trying to help you—”

Above her shaking hands, Ed’s eyes flicked up to Darby — a quick look of recognition — and he tried again to speak, but only made a moaning sound. A mouthful of blood, thick with snakelike clumps, spurted through his closed teeth and landed in his lap.

Jay cried, trying to look away, but Ashley wouldn’t let her. “See?” he said in her ear. “That’s a red card.”

Across the room Lars watched everything like a scarecrow, holding the .45 in one hand and a white jug of bleach in the other, as Ed’s choked scream got louder and louder in the small room.

All of this horror barely affected Darby.

She wasn’t really there. She was somewhere else, and the world felt strange, like looking through oil. Lights blurred into streaks. Her body felt like a cold shell, her heart and breathing slow and robotic. She imagined a tiny creature, maybe her real self, pulling levers and watching screens inside her head. She’d seen that in a movie — Men in Black. She remembered watching the DVD years ago with her mother on the basement sofa, sharing a Snoopy blanket. I like Will Smith, her mother had said, sipping a drink that smelled like peaches. He can rescue me anytime.

She was gone now, Darby realized.

The body of Maya Thorne would stay in some hospital in Provo, Utah, but the tiny being inside her head was lost forever.

Now Ashley squeezed her right hand, his cold fingers linking with hers like young lovers, and he led her through the room. Past Ed and Sandi, past the stone counter, past the coffee machines. She didn’t know where he was taking her, and she didn’t care. She blankly saw her right foot was leaving red footprints — she’d walked through Ed’s blood like a sleepwalker. Like a nightmare, she just wanted it to end.

Please, just let it end.

She turned her head, looking back at the old Garfield clock on the wall. It read 5:19 a.m. For winter daylight savings, she subtracted an hour.

That made it 4:19 a.m.

She’d gotten the 9-1-1 text at 3:58 a.m. The walk back had taken twenty-one minutes. Subtracting from thirty, that left nine minutes until the police arrived. Nine short minutes.

Survive nine more minutes.

That’s all.

Ashley stopped her suddenly — here, at the janitor’s closet door. It was still partly open from when she'd unlocked it. He gently turned her around, like a slow, dizzy tango, and pushed her against the wall.

“Sit here,” he said.

She didn’t.

“Sit, please.”

She shook her head and tears fell to the floor. Her sinuses ached.

“You’re not going to sit?”

She shook her head again.

“You’re not tired?”

Oh, she was exhausted. Her nerves were shot, her muscles like heavy meat. Her thoughts were fuzzy. But somehow, she knew that if she sat down now, it would all be over. She’d lose her strength. She’d never get up again.

For a moment, she thought about just saying it, saying the thing she couldn't take back: Ashley, I threw your keys out the window in the men’s restroom. They landed only ten, maybe twenty feet away in the snow.

You can kill me. I’m done.

Across the room, Jay cried. Rodent Face knelt by her, trying to calm her down. “Don’t look at Ed. Don’t look at him, okay? He’s fine—”

Ed took another painful breath through his nose as Sandi wrapped another bandage around his jaw, and then he made a strange, wet sound. The clean white gauze turned red.

“He’s fine, Jaybird. Wanna, um, play circle time?”

“We’re all . . .” Sandi sighed, wiping Ed’s blood on her pants. “We’re all going to prison for the rest of our lives. You know that, right?”

Ashley ignored her. He was a black shadow, standing over Darby, looking her up and down. Still holding her wrist, trapping her against the half-open closet door. His eyes moved all over her body.

Darby stared at the floor, at her size-eight Converse, ripped by ice and stained with dirt and blood. Ten days ago, they’d been new in a box.

“Were . . .” Ashley cleared his throat. “Were you close to your mom?”

She shook her head.

“No?”

“Not really.”

He leaned closer. “Why not?”

She said nothing. She struggled against his grip on her wrist, and he gently responded by pressing his nail gun against her belly with his other hand. His knuckle was on the trigger. Something about the color — a sickening Crayola-orange — made it look like a giant child’s toy.

He repeated himself, his hot breath tickling her neck: “Why not, Darbs?”

“I was . . . I was a pretty bad daughter.” Her voice trembled but she steadied herself. Then, like a dam breaking, it all poured out: “I took advantage of her. I controlled her. I called her terrible names. I stole her car once, using a shoelace. I’d leave, for days at a time, without telling her where I was going or who I was with. I must have given her ulcers. When I . . . when I left for college, we didn’t even say goodbye. I just got in my Honda and drove to Boulder. I stole a bottle of her gin from the cabinet as I left.”

She remembered drinking it alone in her dorm room. The bitter burn in her throat, under sad wallpaper showing stranger’s graves, with names and birthdates drawn in charcoal shadows of crayon and wax.

Ashley nodded, sniffing her hair. “I’m sorry.”

“You’re not.”

“I am.”

“You’re lying—”

“I mean it,” he said. “I’m truly sorry you’re hurting.”

“I wouldn’t be,” Darby said, her voice tight. “If it was your mom.”

More tears welled, burning her sore eyes, but she held them back. Not yet. That would come later. Later, later, later. After the cops broke down the door and shot Ashley and Lars, after Sandi was cuffed, when Darby and Jay were safe in an ambulance with wool blankets. Only then could she really cry.

Ashley frowned. “How’d you steal a car with a shoelace?”

She didn’t answer. It wasn’t a big deal. Her mom’s Subaru had been broken into before, and the idiot thief had messed up the ignition trying to hotwire it. It needed two keys — one for the door and one for the ignition. Darby had one, but not the other.

You rotten little bitch, her mom had said from the porch, watching her Subaru pull into the driveway at 3 a.m.

You rotten little bitch.

“And . . .” Ashley figured it out. “That’s how you broke into our van, huh?”

She nodded, and another tear fell.

“Wow. It’s like tonight was supposed to happen.” He grinned again. “I’ve always thought things happen for a reason, if that helps.”

It wasn’t.

Death is supposed to change you from a person into an idea. But to Darby, her mother had always been an idea. Somehow, after eighteen years of living in the same small house in Provo, eating the same food, watching the same TV, sitting on the same sofa, she’d never really known who Maya Thorne was. Not as a person. And not as the person she would have been, if Darby hadn't been born. Had she really just been the flu.

Oh God, Mom, I’m sorry.

She almost broke down. But she couldn’t — not in front of him. So it stuck thickly in her chest like a wet, knotted towel, a dull ache in her soul.

I’m so sorry for everything—

Ashley looked her over for another long moment. Another thoughtful breath. The strong smell of his sweat. She heard his tongue move behind his lips, like he was struggling with words he couldn’t say. When he finally did speak again, his voice was different, filled with some emotion she couldn’t name: “I wish you were my girlfriend, Darby.”

She said nothing.

“I wish, so much, that you and I . . . that we’d met differently. This, all this, isn’t me. Okay? I’m not evil. I don’t have a criminal record. I’ve never hurt anyone before tonight. I don’t even drink or smoke. I’m just a business owner who got into a small thing that went wrong, and now I have to fix this to protect my brother. Understand? And you’re stopping me. So I’m asking again, before it gets bad — where are my keys?”

She stared back, hard as a rock, giving nothing.

Over Ashley’s shoulder, she could see the clock. The characters on it. Orange Garfield still offering those roses to pink Arlene. Her blurry eyes focused on the minute hand — almost straight up now. 4:22 a.m.

Five minutes until the cops arrived.

“Did you hear me, Ashley?” Sandi stood up. “Are you losing your mind? Keys or no keys, it’s over. We’re all going to prison.”

“No. We’re not.”

“How do you figure?”

Ashley didn’t answer. Instead, his dark shape turned back to Darby, and his grip on her wrist changed. His fingers crawled over her skin like clammy octopus tentacles, rearranging themselves around her, tightening. And he lifted her hand up, up, sliding against the wall . . .

Sandi raised her voice. “What are you doing to her?”

Darby craned her neck to see — he was holding her right hand against the supply closet door. Right up against the door’s hinge. Pressing her fingertips flat against the golden jaws, where the brass was spotted with old lubricant and brown spots of rust. She saw her pinkie fingernail, painted crackle-blue, her exposed flesh seated in there like a tiny head in a guillotine.

Five minutes.

She looked back at Ashley, her gut twisting with panic.

He had the nail gun tucked in his armpit now, leaning to grab the doorknob with his free hand. “You might not remember this, Darbs, but earlier tonight, you made fun of me for my fear of door hinges. Remember that? Remember what you called me?”

She closed her eyes, squeezing sharp tears, wishing it would all disappear—

“Yeah, oops, huh?”

—But it was real. It was all really happening, right now, and it could never be changed, and her artist’s fingers were about to be crushed by unfeeling metal.

Sandi gasped. “Jesus Christ, Ashley—”

“Don’t do it,” Jay begged, fighting Lars. “Please, don’t—”

But the tall shadow of Ashley Garver wasn’t listening. It leaned in close to Darby, licking its lips, and she smelled something sweetly rotten, foul, like decaying meat. “You’re giving me no choice. If you tell me, I promise I won’t hurt you, okay? You have my word. Where. Are. My. Keys?”

Fiveminutes—fiveminutes—fiveminutes—

She forced herself to open her eyes, to blink away the tears, to steady her quick breaths, to look the monster in its green eyes. She couldn’t fall for it. She couldn’t give in and play this game, because the instant he knew where his keychain was, he’d kill her. There was no other option. Ashley Garver was many, many things, but above all else, he was a compulsive liar.

“Please, Darbs, just tell me, so I don’t have to hurt you. Because if you don’t, you’re forcing me to slam this door.”

He knelt down close so she could see the hurt glimmer in his eyes. She knew this was all an act. Another head of the Hydra. This negotiation was just like any other act she’d witnessed tonight, just another version of Ashley to be worn for a while and then thrown away, the way a python crawls out of its wrinkled gray skin.

The whole room went silent, waiting for her answer.

Breathe in. Count to five. Breathe out.

“If I tell you,” she whispered back, “you’ll just slam the door anyway.”

His eyes grew dark. “Smart girl.”

Then he did.
