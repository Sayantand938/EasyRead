No Exit

Chapter 2

7:39 p.m.

December 23

“Screw you, Bing Crosby.”

Darby Thorne was six miles up Backbone Pass when her windshield wiper broke, and that deep voice was just starting the second chorus. It was official: he’d get his white Christmas. He could stop talking about it now.

She turned the radio dial (nothing but static) and watched her left wiper blade move like a broken wrist. She thought about stopping to tape it, but there was no shoulder — just walls of dirty ice on both sides. She was afraid to stop anyway. The snowflakes had been big and wet when she’d driven through Gypsum ninety minutes ago, but they’d gotten smaller and harder as she climbed higher. Now, they were hypnotic in her headlights, a windshield of stars blurring into light speed.

Chains required, said the last sign she’d seen.

Darby didn’t have snow chains. Not yet, anyway. This was her first year at CU-Boulder, and she’d never planned to go further than Ralphie’s Thriftway. She remembered walking back from there last month, tipsy with a group of almost-friends from her dorm, and when one of them asked her (not really caring) where she’d planned to go for Christmas, Darby had answered simply: it would take a miracle to make her go back home to Utah.

And apparently He’d been listening, because He’d given Darby’s mother late-stage pancreatic cancer.

She’d found this out yesterday.

Via text message.

SCRAPE-SCRAPE. The bent wiper blade hit the glass again, but the snowflakes were dry and the car was moving fast enough that the windshield stayed clear. The real problem was the snow building up on the road. The yellow lane lines were already covered by inches of new snow, and Darby felt her Honda Civic scrape the road. It felt like a wet cough, a little worse each time. The last time, she felt the steering wheel shake in her hands. Another inch of snow and she’d be stuck up here, nine thousand feet high with a quarter tank of gas, no cell service, and only her worries for company.

And Bing Crosby’s loud voice, she guessed. He sang the last chorus as Darby sipped warm Red Bull.

SCRAPE-SCRAPE.

The whole drive had been like this — a blurry, strained rush through miles of hills and flat plains. No time to stop. All she’d eaten today was ibuprofen. She’d left her desk lamp on in her dorm, but she’d only noticed as she left the Dryden parking lot — too far to turn back. Stomach acid burned her throat. Copied songs from Schoolyard Heroes and My Chemical Romance played over and over on her (now dead) iPod. Green signs with old fast food stickers. Boulder had disappeared in her rearview mirror around noon, then the hazy Denver skyline with its parked planes, and finally little Gypsum behind falling snowflakes.

SCRAPE-SCRAPE.

Bing Crosby’s White Christmas ended, and the next holiday song started. She’d heard them all twice now.

Her Honda swerved hard left. Red Bull spilled on her lap. The steering wheel locked up and she fought it for a scary second (turn into the skid, turn into the skid) before steering the car back under control, still moving forward and uphill — but slowing down. Losing grip.

“No, no, no.” She pushed the gas.

The all-weather tires gripped and slipped in the slushy snow, jerking the car roughly. Steam rose from the hood.

“Come on, Blue—”

SCRAPE-SCRAPE.

She’d called this car Blue since high school. Now she gently pressed the gas, trying to feel the road. Two puffs of snow flew up behind her, bright red in her taillights. A loud rattling sound — Blue’s bottom scraping the snow again. The car fought and swerved, like a boat now, and—

SCRAPE—

—The left wiper broke off and spun away.

Her heart dropped. “Oh, shit.”

Now the falling snow stuck to the left side of her windshield, quickly piling up on the bare glass. She’d slowed down too much. In seconds, her view of Route Seven had shrunk to a small opening, and she hit her steering wheel. The horn beeped, unheard.

This is how people die, she thought with a shiver. In blizzards, people get stuck in the country and run out of gas.

They freeze to death.

She sipped her Red Bull — empty.

She clicked off the radio, leaned forward to see the road, and tried to remember — when was the last car she’d seen today? How far back? It had been an orange snowplow with CDOT painted on the door, close to the right lane and spraying ice. At least an hour ago. When the sun was out.

Now it was just a gray light fading behind sharp peaks, and the sky was turning a bruised purple. Frozen fir trees becoming sharp outlines. The lowlands darkening into pools of shadow. The temperature was 4° according to the Shell Station sign she’d passed thirty miles back. Probably colder now.

Then she saw it: a green sign half-buried in a snowdrift to her right. It appeared suddenly, catching the light of her Honda’s dirty headlights: 365 DAYS SINCE THE LAST FATAL ACCIDENT.

The count was probably off by a few days because of the snowstorm, but she still found it creepy. One year, exactly. It made tonight a sad anniversary. It felt strangely personal, like one of her gravestone rubbings.

And behind it, another sign.

REST AREA AHEAD.

If you’ve seen one, you’ve seen them all.

One long building (visitor center, restrooms, maybe a small store or coffee shop run by volunteers) among windswept fir trees and rough rocks. A bare flagpole. A round piece of an old tree. A group of bronze statues buried to their waists; art paid for by taxpayers honoring a local doctor or pioneer. And a small parking lot with a few parked cars — other people stuck like her, waiting for the snowplows to come.

Darby had passed many rest areas since Boulder. Some bigger, most better, all less lonely. But this one, it seemed, was the one fate had chosen for her.

TIRED? Asked a blue sign. FREE COFFEE INSIDE.

And a newer one, stamped with the Bush-era Homeland Security eagle: SEE SOMETHING? SAY SOMETHING.

A final sign, at the end of the off-ramp, was T-shaped. It pointed trucks and campers to the left, and smaller cars to the right.

Darby almost ran it over.

Her windshield was now covered in heavy snow — her right wiper was broken, too — so she'd rolled down her side window and wiped a circle in the glass with her hand. Like looking through a periscope while driving. She didn’t even try to find a parking space — the lines and curbs wouldn’t be visible until March — and she parked Blue next to a gray van with no windows.

She turned off the engine. Killed the headlights.

Silence.

Her hands were still shaking. Adrenaline left over from that first skid. She squeezed them into fists, first the right, then the left (inhale, count to five, exhale), and watched snowflakes gather on her windshield. In ten seconds, the circle she’d wiped was gone. In thirty, she was trapped under a wall of darkening ice, facing the fact that she wouldn’t make it to Provo, Utah, by noon tomorrow. That hopeful arrival time depended on her getting through this blizzard over Backbone Pass before midnight and reaching Vernal in time for a 3 a.m. nap. It was almost 8 p.m. already. Even if she didn’t stop to sleep or pee, she wouldn’t be able to speak to her mom before the first surgery. That time to talk was CLOSED INDEFINITELY, like another mountain pass on her news app.

After the surgery, then.

That’s when.

Now her Honda was completely black. Snow pressed against the glass on all sides like an arctic cave. She checked her iPhone, squinting in the electric light — no service, and nine percent battery. The last text message she’d received was still open. She’d first read it back on the highway near Gypsum, crossing a causeway slippery with ice, speeding at eighty-five with the little screen shaking in her palm: She’s okay right now.

Right now. That was a scary limitation. And it wasn’t even the scariest part.

Darby’s older sister Devon thought in emoticons. Her texts and Twitter posts hated punctuation; often-rushed bursts of words in search of a clear thought. But not this one. Devon had chosen to spell out okay, and end the sentence with a period, and these little details had settled in Darby’s stomach like an ulcer. Nothing she could touch, but a sign that whatever was happening at Utah Valley Hospital was less than okay but couldn’t be said via keypad.

Just four stupid words.

She’s okay right now.

And here Darby was, the less successful younger sister, stuck at a lonely rest stop just below the top of Backbone Pass, because she’d tried to outrun the huge snowstorm over the Rockies and failed. Miles above sea level, snowed in inside a ’94 Honda Civic with broken windshield wipers, a dying phone, and a puzzling text message burning in her mind.

Mom is okay right now. Whatever that meant.

As a girl, she’d been fascinated by death. She hadn’t lost any grandparents, so death was still just an idea, something to be visited and explored like a tourist. She loved gravestone rubbings — when you tape rice paper against a headstone and rub black crayon or wax to take a detailed copy. They’re beautiful. Her private collection included hundreds of them, some framed. Some unknowns. Some celebrities. She’d jumped a fence in Denver last year to capture Buffalo Bill’s. For a long time she’d believed this little habit of hers, this teenage fascination with death, would better prepare her for the real thing when it came into her life.

It hadn’t.

For a few moments, she sat in her darkened car, reading and re-reading Devon’s words. It occurred to her that if she stayed inside this cold box alone with her thoughts, she’d just start to cry, and she’d done enough of that in the last twenty-four hours. She couldn’t lose her drive. She couldn’t sink into that mess again. Like Blue stuck in this heavy snow, miles from help — it’ll bury you if you let it.

Inhale. Count to five. Exhale.

Forward motion.

So she pocketed her iPhone, unbuckled her seatbelt, slipped a windbreaker over her Boulder Art Walk hoodie, and hoped that, along with the promised free coffee, this dingy little rest area would have Wi-Fi.

Inside the visitor center, she asked the first person she saw, and he pointed to a cheaply laminated sign on the wall: Wi-Fi for our guests, thanks to CDOT’s proud partnership with RoadConnect!

He stood behind her. “It . . . uh, it says it’ll charge you.”

“I’ll pay it.”

“It’s a bit much.”

“I’ll pay it anyway.”

“See?” He pointed. “$3.95 every ten minutes—”

“I just need to make a call.”

“For how long?”

“I don’t know.”

“Because if it’s going to be more than twenty minutes, you might want to get their RoadConnect monthly pass. It says it’s only ten dollars for—”

“Holy shit, dude, it’s fine.”

Darby hadn’t meant to snap. She hadn’t really looked at this stranger until now, under the bright lights — late fifties, a yellow jacket, one earring, and a silver goatee. Like a sad pirate. She reminded herself that he was probably stuck here, too, and only trying to help.

Her iPhone couldn’t find the Wi-Fi anyway. She scrolled with her thumb, waiting for it to show up.

Nothing.

The guy went back to his seat. “Karma, huh?”

She ignored him.

This place must have been a coffee shop during the day. But now, it reminded her of an after-hours bus station — too bright and empty. The coffee stand (Espresso Peak) was closed behind a security shutter. Behind it were two big coffee machines with old-fashioned buttons and dirty drip trays. Stale pastries. A blackboard listed a few expensive, fancy drinks.

The visitor center was one room — a long rectangle under the roof, with restrooms in the back. Wooden chairs, a wide table, and benches along the wall. Nearby, a vending machine and racks of tourism brochures. The room felt both small and huge, with a strong Lysol smell.

Where was the promised free coffee? On Espresso Peak’s counter was a stack of Styrofoam cups, napkins, and two carafes on warming plates behind the shutter. One labeled COFEE, one labeled COCO.

Someone paid by the state can’t spell.

She noticed a crack in the mortar at the bottom of the counter, and one of the stones was loose. She could kick it out. This bothered a small, perfectionist part of Darby’s brain. Like the urge to pick at a hangnail.

She heard a low buzzing, like the sound of locust wings, and wondered if the place was on backup power. That could have reset the Wi-Fi, maybe. She turned back to the man with the goatee. “Have you seen any payphones here?”

The man looked up at her — oh, you’re still here? — and shook his head.

“Do you have a cell signal?” she asked.

“Not since White Bend.”

Her heart sank. The map on the wall said this rest stop was called Wanapa (meaning Little Devil, according to the local Paiute tribe). Twenty miles north was another rest area — Wanapani, or Big Devil — and then ten miles further, downhill, was the town of White Bend. And tonight, before the huge snowstorm, White Bend might as well be on the moon.

“I got a signal outside,” said a second male voice.

Behind her.

Darby turned. He was leaning against the front door with one hand on the knob. She’d walked right past him when she first came inside (how did I miss him?). The guy was tall, broad-shouldered, about a year or two older than her. He looked like one of her roommate’s Alpha Sig party friends, with his styled hair, green North Face jacket, and shy smile. “Just one bar, though, and just for a few minutes,” he added. “I use T-Mobile.”

“Me too. Where?”

“Out by the statues.”

She nodded, hoping she still had enough battery to call. “Do you . . . hey, do either of you know when the snowplows are coming?”

Both guys shook their heads. Darby didn’t like standing between them; she had to keep turning her head.

“I think the emergency broadcasts are down,” the older one said, pointing to a nineties-era AM/FM radio buzzing on the counter. It was the source of the static-filled buzzing she’d heard. It was behind the security grate. “When I got here, it was playing traffic and ESA stuff every thirty seconds,” he added. “But now it’s just silent. Maybe snow covered their transmitter.”

She reached through the grate and straightened the antenna, causing the static to change pitch. “Still better than Bing Crosby.”

“Who’s Bing Crosby?” the younger man asked.

“One of the Beatles,” the older one answered.

“Oh.”

Somehow, Darby already liked the older one, and felt bad for snapping at him about the Wi-Fi.

“I don’t know much about music,” the younger one admitted.

“Clearly.”

On the big table, she noticed a deck of worn playing cards. A little Texas hold ’em apparently, to help two strangers connect while stuck in a blizzard.

A toilet flushed in the restrooms.

Three strangers, she counted.

She slipped her phone back into her jeans pocket, realizing both men were still staring at her. One in front, one behind.

“I’m Ed,” said the older one.

“Ashley,” said the younger one.

Darby didn’t give her name. She pushed out the front door, back into the freezing cold, and stuffed her hands into her jacket pockets. She let the door swing shut behind her, hearing the older man ask the younger one: “Wait. Your name is Ashley? Like a girl’s name?”

He groaned. “It’s not just a girl’s name—”

The door closed.

The world outside had grown dark in shadow. The sun was gone. Falling snowflakes shone orange in the visitor center’s single outside light, which hung over the doorway in a big shade. But the heavy snowfall seemed to have lessened for a moment; against the coming night, she could see the shapes of faraway peaks. Jagged pieces of rock, partly hidden in trees.

She pulled her jacket up to her neck and shivered.

The group of statues that the younger guy — Ashley — had mentioned were south of the rest area, past the flagpole and picnic tables. Near the exit ramp she’d taken. From here, she could hardly see them. Just shapes partly covered in snow.

“Hey.”

She turned.

Ashley again. He let the door click shut and caught up to her, stepping high in the snow. “There’s . . . so, there’s a very specific spot I had to stand in. That’s the only place I could get a signal, and it was just one bar. You might only be able to send a text.”

“That’ll still work.”

He zipped up his coat. “I’ll show you.”

They followed his old footprints out there, and Darby saw they were already half-filled with several inches of new snow. She wondered, but didn’t ask, how long he’d been stuck here.

Getting away from the building, she also realized this rest area was on a cliff. Behind the back wall (the restrooms), bare treetops showed a sudden drop-off. She couldn’t even see where the land began to fall, as the snow hid how steep it was. One wrong step could be deadly. The plants up here were just as harsh — Douglas firs bent into strange shapes by strong winds, their branches sharp and stiff.

“Thanks,” Darby said.

Ashley didn’t hear. They kept stumbling through snow up to their waists, arms out to stay steady. It was deeper here, away from the path. Her Converse were already soaked, her toes numb.

“So you go by Ashley?” she asked.

“Yeah.”

“Not, like, Ash?”

“Why would I?”

“Just asking.”

Again, she glanced back at the visitor center and saw someone standing in the yellow light of the building’s only window. Watching them from behind the blurry glass. She couldn’t tell if it was the older man (Ed) or the person she hadn’t seen.

“Ashley isn’t just a girl’s name,” he said as they walked. “It’s a perfectly good name for a man.”

“Oh, definitely.”

“Like Ashley Wilkes in Gone with the Wind.”

“I was just thinking that,” Darby said. It felt good to joke around a little. But the cautious part of her brain that was always on wondered: You know that really old movie, but you don’t know who the Beatles are?

“Or Ashley Johnson,” he said. “The famous rugby player.”

“You made that up.”

“I did not.” He pointed into the distance. “Hey. You can see Melanie’s Peak.”

“What?”

“Melanie’s Peak.” He looked embarrassed. “Sorry, I’ve been here a while, reading everything in the information center. See that big mountain? Someone named it after his wife.”

“That’s sweet.”

“Maybe. Unless he meant she was cold and unwelcoming.”

Darby chuckled.

They were at the icy statues now. Lots of them. There was probably a sign explaining it all, under the snow. The sculptures looked like children. Running, jumping, playing, made of bronze and covered in ice.

Ashley pointed at one holding a baseball bat. “There. By the little leaguer.”

“Here?”

“Yeah. That’s where I got a signal.”

“Thanks.”

“Do you . . .” He paused, his hands in his pockets. “Want me to, uh, stay?”

Silence.

“You know. I mean, if—”

“No.” Darby smiled, a real one. “I’m fine. Thanks.”

“I was hoping you’d say that. It’s freezing out here.” He flashed that easy grin of his and walked back to the orange lights, waving over his shoulder. “Have fun out here with the Nightmare Children.”

“Will do.”

She didn’t realize how disturbing the statues were until she was alone with them. The children were missing pieces. It was an art style she’d seen before — the sculptor used rough chunks of bronze, joining them in strange and unexpected ways that left seams and gaps — but in the darkness, she imagined blood and guts. The boy to her left, the one swinging a baseball bat that Ashley had called the little leaguer, had an exposed ribcage. Others waved thin, damaged arms, missing patches of flesh. Like a crowd of pit-bull attack victims, half-eaten to the bone.

What had Ashley called them? Nightmare children.

He was twenty feet away, almost a shadow against the rest area’s orange light, when she turned and called to him. “Hey. Wait.”

He looked back.

“Darby,” she said. “My name is Darby.”

He smiled.

Thanks for helping me, she wanted to say. Thank you for being nice to me, a complete stranger. The words were there, in her mind, but she couldn’t say them. They looked away, the moment disappearing . . .

Thank you, Ashley—

He kept walking.

Then he stopped again, thinking it over, and said one last thing: “You know Darby is a guy’s name, right?”

She laughed.

She watched him leave, and then she leaned against the statue’s baseball bat, stuck in mid-swing, and held her iPhone up to the falling snowflakes. She squinted, watching the top left corner of the screen.

No service.

She waited, alone in the dark. In the right corner, the battery was down to six percent. She’d left her charger plugged into the wall in her dorm. Two hundred miles away.

“Please,” she whispered. “Please, God . . .”

Still no signal. Breathing through chattering teeth, she re-read her sister’s text: She’s okay right now.

Okay is the worst word. It means nothing. Okay could mean her mother Maya was better, worse, or just . . . okay.

People say pancreatic cancer kills fast, because death often comes within weeks or days of diagnosis — but that’s wrong. It takes years. It just has no symptoms early on, growing unseen, not causing jaundice or pain until it’s too late. It was a chilling thought that the cancer had been in her mother when Darby was in high school. It had been there when she lied about the broken tags in her purse. It had been there when she drove home at 3 a.m. on a Sunday, dizzy from bad ecstasy and wearing a green glow-bracelet, and her mother cried on the porch and called her a rotten little bitch. That unseen thing had been there all along, listening, and she’d been dying slowly, and neither of them knew.

They’d last talked on Thanksgiving. The call had been an hour of arguments, but the last seconds stuck in Darby’s mind.

You’re why Dad left us, she remembered saying. And if I could have chosen him instead of you, I would have. In a heartbeat.

In a fucking heartbeat, Maya.

She wiped tears away, already freezing to her skin. She breathed into the cold air. Her mother was being prepped for surgery, right now, at Utah Valley Hospital, and here Darby was, stuck at a run-down rest area miles into the Rockies.

And she knew she didn’t have enough gas to idle Blue here for long. The visitor center at least had heat and electricity. She’d probably have to make small talk with Ed and Ashley, and whoever had flushed that toilet. She imagined them — strangers in a snowstorm, like miners and settlers must have shared shelter in these mountains long ago — drinking weak coffee, sharing stories, and listening for news about when the snowplows would come. Maybe she’d make Facebook friends and learn poker.

Or maybe she’d sit in her Honda and freeze to death.

Both options were equally appealing.

She glanced at the nearest statue. “This is going to be a long night, kids.” She checked her iPhone one last time, but she'd lost hope in Ashley’s signal trick. She was just wasting battery and freezing.

“One hell of a long night.”

She headed back to the Wanapa building, feeling a migraine start. The heavy snowfall had started again, hiding the mountains with swirling snowflakes. A sharp gust of wind rushed behind her, creaking the fir trees and tightening her jacket. Without thinking, she counted the cars in the parking lot as she walked — three, plus her Honda. A gray van, a red pickup truck, and another vehicle, all partly covered by snow.

She walked around the small group of trapped cars in the parking lot. No real reason. She would remember this thoughtless choice many times tonight, wondering how different things might have been if she'd just followed Ashley’s footprints.

She passed the row of vehicles.

First was the red truck. Sandbags and tire chains. Less snow on it than the others, meaning it hadn’t been here long. She guessed thirty minutes.

The second car was completely buried, just a snow-covered shape. She couldn’t even tell the color — it could have been a dumpster. Something big and square. It had been here the longest.

Third was Blue, her Honda Civic. The car she’d learned to drive in, taken to college, and lost her virginity in (not all at once). The left wiper was still missing, lost in a snow pile a mile down the highway. She knew she was lucky to have made it to a rest area.

Last was the gray van.

Darby decided to cut between parked cars and take the path to the building's front door, about fifty feet away. She planned to go between the van and her Honda, using her car doors for balance.

Printed on the side of the van was an orange cartoon fox, like a fake Nick Wilde from Zootopia. He held a nail gun like a secret agent holds a pistol, advertising some kind of building or repair service. The company’s name was hidden by snow, but the saying read: WE FINISH WHAT WE START. The van had two back windows. The right one was covered by a towel. The left one was clear, catching a flash of reflected lamplight as Darby passed, and in it, she saw something pale inside the van. A hand.

A tiny, doll-like hand.

She stopped walking, holding her breath.

This little hand gripped a grate-like thing behind the icy glass — white fingers gently opening one by one, like a child still learning to control their body — and then suddenly, it pulled back into the darkness. Out of sight. It all happened in three, maybe four seconds, leaving Darby in shocked silence.

No way.

The inside was quiet. Still again.

She moved closer, shielding her eyes with her hands, peering inside. Her eyelashes brushing against cold glass. Just visible in the darkness, near where the tiny hand had disappeared, she saw a small curve, a faint glimmer of dim light. It was a round combination lock. Holding a grid of metal bars, which the child’s hand had been gripping. Like the kid was in a cage.

Then Darby breathed out — a mistake — and the glass fogged up with her breath. But she’d seen it. She couldn't unsee it.

She stepped back, leaving a handprint on the door, feeling her heart pounding in her neck. A growing rhythm.

There’s . . .

There's a kid trapped in this van.
