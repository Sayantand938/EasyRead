No Exit

Chapter 3

8:17 p.m.

She went back inside.

Ashley looked up. “Any luck?”

She didn’t answer.

He was sitting now, at the wood table, playing cards with Ed. A new woman was here, too — Ed’s wife, it seemed — sitting next to him. She was a particular little woman in her forties with a black bowl cut and a wrinkled yellow parka, busy popping cartoon bubbles on her tablet. She’d been the one in the restroom.

As the door clicked shut behind Darby, she counted three possible suspects: Chatty Ashley, sad-eyed Ed, and Ed’s frumpy wife. So who did the gray van belong to?

Oh, my God, there’s a kid outside in that van.

Locked in a cage or something.

It hit her again, all at once. She tasted raw oysters in her mouth. Her legs went weak. She needed to sit down, but was afraid to.

One of these three people did it—

“Make sure the door is shut,” Ed said.

Like nothing had happened, the card game resumed. Ashley checked his hand and glanced sideways at Ed. “Four of hearts?”

“Go fish. Two of spades?”

“Nope.”

Something else was wrong, Darby realized. The numbers didn’t make sense. There were three cars outside besides her own. Three suspects in here. But Ed and his wife had almost certainly come together. Right? So there had to be a fourth person at the rest stop. But where?

She glanced from Ashley, to Ed, to Ed’s wife, scanning the room, her heart gripped with icy terror. Where else could—

Then she felt a warm breath touch the back of her neck. Someone was standing behind her.

“Jack of clubs.”

“Go fish.”

Darby stood still, hairs standing up on her skin. A chill racing down her back. She wanted to turn around, but she couldn’t. Her body wouldn’t move.

He’s right behind me.

His breath warmed the back of her neck. A damp puff, lifting her hair, tickling her bare skin. Gently whistling past her ear. Somehow she already knew this fourth traveler was a man — women just didn’t breathe like that. He was standing less than eighteen inches behind her. Close enough to touch her back, or reach around her throat and put his fingers around her windpipe.

She wished she could turn around and face this fourth person, whoever he was, but the moment felt strange, floaty. Like trying to throw a punch in a nightmare.

Turn around, she told herself. Turn around now.

In front of her, the card game continued: “Queen of hearts?”

“Ah! Here you go.”

“Nine of diamonds?”

“Nope.”

Behind her, the breathing stopped for a few seconds — long enough that she briefly hoped she’d been imagining it, all of it — and then he sucked in a deeper breath. Mouth-breathing. Standing there in stiff silence, Darby realized she’d done it again. She’d entered the room without checking the corner on her left.

Jesus, Darby, just turn around.

Face him.

Finally, she did.

She turned slowly, casually, with one palm up, like she was just doing Ed a favor to make sure the door was closed right. She turned — turned until she was face to face with the man.

Man was a strong word. He was tall but slouching, very thin, nineteen at most. His face was weasel-like with acne, an overbite above a shapeless chin covered with peach-fuzz. He wore a Deadpool beanie and a baby-blue ski jacket. His narrow shoulders were wet with melted snow, like he’d been outside, too. He was staring at her, so she looked back — tiny hazel eyes, like a rodent's in their dullness — and she gave a shy smile.

The moment blurred.

Rodent Face’s breath smelled like milk chocolate mixed with the earthy sourness of Skoal. His right arm lifted suddenly — Darby flinched — but he was reaching past her to push the door shut. It clicked as it locked.

“Thanks,” Ed said, turning back to Ashley. “Ace of hearts?”

“Nope.”

Darby looked away and left the man by the door. Her heart pounded. Her footsteps seemed loud. She squeezed her hands into fists to hide their shaking and sat at the table with the others. She pulled up a chair between Ashley and the older couple, and the wooden legs scraped loudly on the tiles.

Ashley gritted his teeth at the harsh sound. “Uh, nine of hearts.”

“Shit.”

Ed’s wife nudged him. “Language.”

Darby knew Rodent Face was still watching her with those small, dull eyes, studying her. She realized she was sitting stiffly — too stiffly — so she relaxed in her chair a bit and pretended to play with her iPhone. Pulling her knees up to the table. She was acting now, just an over-caffeinated art student with a Honda full of gravestone rubbings and a dead phone battery, stuck here at the edge of nowhere like everyone else. Just a harmless CU-Boulder sophomore.

He stood by the door. Still watching her.

Now Darby started to worry. Could he know? Maybe he’d been looking out the west window and seen her peeking into his van. Maybe he’d seen her footprints. Or maybe her face gave it all away the second she’d stumbled back into the lobby, her nerves shot and her heart racing. She was usually a good liar, but not tonight. Not now.

She tried to think of a normal reason for what she’d seen — like, one of these four people’s kid was just napping in the back of their van. That was possible, right? It must happen all the time. That’s what rest stops are for. Resting.

But that didn’t explain the round padlock she’d seen. Or the wire bars the hand had been holding. Or, when she thought about it, the careful way the towels were placed on the back windows — to hide what was happening inside. Right?

Am I making too much of this?

Maybe. Maybe not. Her thoughts were racing; her caffeine buzz fading. She needed some coffee.

And speaking of making too much of things, she’d already tried calling 9-1-1 outside. Still no signal. She’d tried several more times near the Nightmare Children, in the magic spot Ashley had told her about. Nothing. She’d even tried texting 9-1-1 — she remembered reading that texts use very little data, and are the best way to get help from areas with weak cell service. But that hadn’t worked either: Child abduction gray van license plate VBH9045 state route 7 Wanapa rest stop send police.

This text message, marked UNSENT, was still open. She closed it, just in case Rodent Face glanced at her phone.

She’d also tried opening the van’s back door (which could have set off an alarm if it had one), but it was locked. Of course — why wouldn’t it be? She stood there, peering into the darkness with her hands around her eyes, tapping on the glass, trying to get the small figure to move. No luck. The inside of the van was completely dark, and the back doors were covered with blankets and junk. She’d only seen that little hand for a few seconds. But it was enough. She knew she’d seen it.

Right?

Right.

“Ace of spades.”

“Goddamnit.”

“Language, Eddie—”

“For Christ’s sake, Sandi, we’re stuck in a dump in Colorado that the government pays for, and it’s almost Christmas Eve. I’ll put twenty dollars in my swear jar when I get home, okay?”

The woman with the black bowl cut — Sandi, it seemed — looked across the wide table at Darby and mouthed: Sorry about him. She was missing a front tooth. In her lap, her sparkly purse had Psalm 100:5 stitched on it: FOR THE LORD IS GOOD AND HIS LOVE ENDURES.

Darby smiled back kindly. She didn’t mind a little cursing. Besides, Ashley still thought Bing Crosby was one of the Beatles, and that made Ed a good guy in her mind.

But . . . she knew she was missing something here, just like when she’d entered the building without checking her corners. Her gut said that Rodent Face drove the gray van. But it was only a guess. She knew the kidnapper/child-abuser could be anyone here. Any of the four strangers stuck at this roadside shelter could be — no, were— suspects.

Ashley? He was cleaning up at Go Fish right now. He was funny and nice, the kind of cheerful guy she'd dated but not seriously, but something about him felt wrong. She couldn't say exactly what. Was it a habit? The way he talked? He just seemed fake, his interactions too planned, like a clerk who smiles for customers but complains about them in private.

As for Ed and Sandi? They were nice, but something was off about them, too. They didn’t seem married. They didn’t even seem to like each other much.

And Rodent Face? He looked like a wanted man already.

Everyone here was guilty until proven innocent. Darby would need to match each person to each car outside, and then she could be sure. She couldn't just ask, either — or the real kidnapper/abuser would know she suspected them. She’d need to get this information carefully. She thought about asking Ashley, Ed, and Sandi what time they’d arrived and figuring it out from the snow on the cars outside. But that, too, could draw too much attention.

Then again, what if she waited too long?

The kidnapper wouldn’t stay here. As soon as the blizzard stopped, or the snowplows arrived, he (or she, or they) would leave Colorado fast. Leaving Darby with only a description and a license plate number.

Her phone beeped in her pocket, startling her. Five percent battery.

Ashley looked up at her over some dirty cards. “Signal?”

“What?”

“Any luck getting a signal? By the statues?”

She shook her head, knowing this was her chance. Her phone wouldn’t last the night, so she’d ask now, staying in character: “Does anyone have an iPhone charger, by any chance?”

Ashley shook his head. “Sorry.”

“I don’t,” said Sandi, nudging Ed’s elbow, her voice changing from sweet to sharp. “What about you, Eddie? Still have your charger, or did you sell it, too?”

“You don’t sell things like that anymore,” Ed said. “It’s called Craigslist. And it’s not my fault Apple makes overpriced—”

“Watch it—”

“Garbage. I was going to say overpriced garbage, Sandi.” He tossed his cards down and looked at Ashley, forcing a smile. “I broke an iPhone in my pocket once, just by sitting down. A seven-hundred-dollar thing, ruined by sitting. It bent like a leaf against my—”

“Watch it—”

“—Leg. My leg. You see, Sandi, I can finish a sentence without using—”

Ashley interrupted: “Four of clubs?”

“Fuck.”

Sandi sighed and popped another bubble on her tablet. “Careful, young man. Eddie gets upset when he loses.”

“It was a chessboard,” Ed said, “and it only happened once.”

Ashley grinned, taking his new four of clubs.

“You know, Eddie, you’ll never get another job if you don’t control your swearing.” Sandi pecked at her screen with a thumbnail, and a cartoon failure sound chimed — whomp-whomp.

Ed forced a smile. He started to say something, but changed his mind.

The room cooled.

Darby crossed her arms and let the words sink in — simply put, no white Apple charging cord anywhere. She guessed her phone had about ninety minutes of battery life left. Rodent Face hadn’t answered her question, of course, or even spoken at all. He was still standing by the front door, blocking the exit with his hands in his pockets, his fuzzy chin down, his red-and-black Deadpool beanie covering the upper half of his face.

He’s watching me. Just like I’m watching him.

She had to act normal. Her best friend had once told her that she had RBF (“resting bitch-face”), and yes, it was true that Darby rarely smiled. Not because she was mean, or even sad. Smiling made her uncomfortable. When the muscles in her face tensed, the long, curved scar over her eyebrow became visible, as clear as a white sickle. She’d had it since she was ten. She hated it.

CRACKLE-SNAP.

A rough sound, like ripping cloth, and Darby jumped in her seat. It was the radio behind the security shutter buzzing to life. Everyone looked up.

“Is that—”

“Yep.” Ed stood. “The emergency thing. It’s back.”

Darby knew thing was Army talk for frequency. Another burst of harsh static, growing into a jumbled peak. Like a phone dropped underwater.

She didn’t realize Rodent Face had gotten closer until he was standing right over her left shoulder, still breathing through his mouth, joining the group in frozen attention as the old Sony AM/FM leaked electronic noise from the counter. Under the feedback, she recognized . . . yes, there it was . . . the softest whisper—

“A voice,” she said. “Someone’s talking.”

“I can’t hear anything—”

“Hang on.” Ed reached through the security grate and twisted the volume knob, pulling weak pieces out of the noise. It sounded like the automated voice, broken with strange pauses: “—has issued a w-nter st-rm w-rning -ffecting Backb-ne Pass with bl-zzard conditions and heavy r-in. State Route Sev-n is closed to all tr-ffic between exits f-rty-nine and sixty-eight unt-l f-rther notice—”

Ashley blinked. “What mile marker are we at?”

Ed raised a finger, rattling the shutters. “Ssh.”

“—Em-gency and road maint-nance crews exp-ct big delays of s-x to eight hours because of m-ltiple crashes and heavy sn-owfall. All driv-ers are told to st-y off the roads and r-main inside until c-nditions get b-tter.”

A long, crackly pause. Then a faint beep.

Everyone waited.

“The n-tional weath-r service has issued a w-nter storm w-rning for Backb-ne Pass . . .” The broadcast repeated, and everyone in the room lost hope at once. Ed lowered the volume and huffed.

Silence.

Sandi spoke first. “Six to eight hours?”

Darby’s legs almost gave out. She’d been half-standing, leaning forward to listen, and now she slumped back into her chair like a ragdoll. The rest of the room took in this information in quiet voices, swirling around her:

“Is that right?”

“Six to eight freakin’ hours.”

“All night, basically.”

“Better get comfy.”

Sandi pouted and closed her tablet’s leather case. “Figures. I’m already on the last level of Super Bubble Pop.”

All night. Darby rocked in the cheap chair, her knuckles clasped around her knees. A strange feeling of alarm washed over her, a slow kind of horror, like what her mother might’ve felt when she found that first lump under her arm. No panic, no fight, no flight, just that shivery little moment when daily life turns sour.

It’ll be all night until the snowplows get here—

Rodent Face cleared his throat, a juicy gurgle, and everyone looked at him. He was still standing behind Darby’s chair, still breathing down her neck. He spoke to the entire room, his words slow and thick: “I’m Lars.”

Silence.

“My . . .” He inhaled through his mouth. “My name is . . . Lars.”

No one responded.

Darby tensed, realizing this was likely the first time Ashley, Ed, and Sandi had heard him speak as well. The awkwardness was obvious.

“Uh . . .” Ashley smiled easily. “Thanks, Lars.”

“You know . . .” Lars hesitated, his hands in his jacket pockets. “Since we’ll be . . . here for a while. We should introduce ourselves. So, hello, my name is Lars.”

. . . And I’m probably the one with a kid locked in my van.

Darby’s mind raced, her thoughts spinning, her nerves jumping and sparking like electricity.

And we’re trapped here with you.

In this tiny rest stop.

All night.

“Nice to meet you,” said Ed. “What do you think about Apple products?”

Twenty minutes of careful small talk later, Darby knew which car belonged to each person.

The buried one belonged to Ashley. He was the first to arrive, sometime after 3 p.m. this afternoon, finding an empty rest area with a quiet radio and old coffee. He wasn’t in a rush to cross the pass and decided to be safe. He was a college student, like her — Salt Lake City Institute of Tech or something. Now that they’d started talking, he wouldn't stop, grinning widely with his white teeth. Darby now knew he was going to Vegas with his uncle to see a magic show. She knew he hated mushrooms but loved cilantro. He really could talk: “And Ashley is still a perfectly good male name.”

“Uh-huh,” said Ed.

The two older people were more careful, but Darby now knew the red F150 was Sandi’s — not Ed’s, like she thought. She was also surprised they weren’t married, even though they argued like they were. They were cousins, and Sandi was driving them to Denver to visit family for Christmas. It seemed like a last-minute trip. Ed had been in some kind of trouble recently since he didn’t have a car or a job. Maybe he’d been in prison. He seemed lost; a man in his fifties with an earring and a biker goatee, and Sandi seemed to love taking care of him, even if it just gave her a reason to be mad at him.

So Darby had ruled out three drivers and two vehicles.

This left Lars.

He hadn’t said anything since telling them his name, so Darby didn’t know exactly when he'd gotten here, but she guessed he'd arrived about thirty minutes before Ed and Sandi, based on the snow. She watched Lars fill a Styrofoam cup with COCO and go back to his spot by the door, taking a loud sip. She hadn’t seen him sit down at all.

As she drank her COFEE, Darby tried to plan what to do next. But there was too much she didn’t know. She couldn’t involve Ed, Ashley, or Sandi yet, because she’d lose control. Getting other people involved had to be the last thing she tried. You can’t undo something like that. Right now, she had the advantage of surprise, and she couldn't waste it.

Still, she imagined the worst things that could happen. She imagined telling Ashley (the youngest and strongest) that she thought they were stuck with a child molester, and Ashley turning pale. Lars would notice, pull a gun from his jacket, and kill them both. Ed and Sandi would see it, so they’d die too. Four bodies full of bullet holes in a pool of blood. All because Darby spoke up.

And, what if there wasn’t a child in Lars’s van?

What if I made it up?

What if she’d seen a plastic doll hand? A dog paw? A kid’s empty glove? It wouldn’t explain the bars or the combination lock, but it could’ve all been her troubled imagination, a trick of light, and it had only lasted a few seconds. Her mind felt a little dizzy.

She’d been sure thirty minutes ago, but suddenly her certainty vanished. She could think of many more likely scenarios than this one. What were the chances of finding a kidnapping happening? While stuck overnight in a snowy rest stop? It was too unreal to be happening to Darby.

She tried to picture the scene again. Step by step. The van’s back window had been covered in ice. The inside had been dark. And Darby? She was a mess — worried, sleep-deprived, her blood full of Red Bull, seeing flashes of light behind her dry eyes. What if it was just her active imagination, and Lars was just an innocent traveler like the others? Attacking him would be assault.

If I’m wrong about this . . .

She finished her last sip of coffee, and for some reason, she thought of her older sister. Twenty-three year old Devon, who had her first tattoo on her right shoulder blade. Some Chinese symbols, clear and beautiful. They meant: “Strength in Chinese.”

The lesson? Check everything twice.

She needed to go back outside to the van. She needed to see the child. Really see the child.

And she couldn’t act too fast. She had plenty of time; six to eight hours, in fact. Enough time to think. And she needed to be sure before she did anything.

Right?

Right.

She rubbed the goosebumps on her arms and scanned the room. At the table, they’d finished Go Fish — Ashley was now trying to get Ed to play a new card game called War. Sandi had pulled a yellowed paperback from her purse and raised it like a shield. And Lars, the cause of tonight’s bad dream, was still guarding the front door, sipping his Styrofoam cup of cocoa. She’d been counting; this was his third refill. He’d be going to the restroom soon.

That’s when, she decided. That was when she’d slip outside. Last time she’d walked into the scene, unprepared and scared. This time, she’d be ready.

Ashley shuffled the cards, having given up on Ed, and nodded at Sandi’s paperback. “What’re you reading?”

She grunted. “A murder mystery.”

“I like murder mysteries.” He paused. “Well, actually, to be honest, I don’t read much. I guess I just like the idea of them.”

Sandi forced a polite smile, turning a page. Why’d you ask, then?

It was barely two hours into Darby’s stay at the Wanapa rest area, and she was already getting annoyed at Ashley. He was a talker, alright. And he was still going like a windup toy, his attention now stuck on Sandi: “How far . . . uh, how many chapters are you in?”

“Not many.”

“Has the victim been murdered yet?”

“Yep.”

“I like gore. Was it gory?”

Ed shifted in his seat, which creaked. He watched Sandi, who was turning a page and hadn’t even answered Ashley’s first question when he hit her with another: “Can you guess who the murderer is?”

“Not yet,” she said dryly. “That’s the point.”

“It’s always the nice guy,” Ashley said. “Again, I don’t really read, but I’ve seen a lot of movies, and that’s even better. Whoever seems like the nicest character at first will always turn out to be the jerk in the end.”

Sandi ignored him.

Please stop talking, Darby thought. Just stop.

“That truck,” he continued, glancing out the window. “That’s yours, right?”

“Mm-hmm.”

“Reminds me of a joke. What does Ford stand for?”

“I don’t know.”

“Found on road, dead.”

Sandi grunted and kept reading.

Finally, Ashley took the hint. “Sorry. I’ll let you read.”

Lars watched them from the door. He licked his lips, and Darby noticed how small his teeth were. Just two little rows of tiny kernels, like baby teeth, not fully formed, still in pink gums. He finished his cocoa and threw his empty cup at the garbage can, missing by a lot.

No one said anything about it.

Not even Ashley.

Darby watched the white cup spin on the tile and thought — if she was right — maybe she could sneak into Lars’s van and quietly move the child into her Honda. Hide him or her in the back seat, maybe, under the pile of paper she used for her gravestone rubbings. Or even better, the trunk — if there was enough air and heat. When the snowplows came early tomorrow, everyone could leave, and Lars might drive off without knowing his prey had escaped—

No. That was just a dream. Since they’d be stuck there all night, Lars would have to run his engine now and then to keep the child warm. He would notice his captive was gone.

She took a shaky breath. She counted to five before breathing out, like her mother taught her.

Right now, I have the upper hand.

I can’t waste it.

She wished it could be someone else in this situation. Someone smarter, braver, more stable, more competent. Someone from her college’s ROTC, one of those sweaty girls in camo carrying heavy backpacks around campus. Someone who knew ju-jitsu. Anyone else.

But it was just her.

Just Darby Thorne, the weird girl who hid from parties in her dorm room with walls covered in black crayon rubbings from strangers’ graves, like a spiritual vampire.

As the snowstorm got worse outside, she swiped her iPhone and quickly typed another text. Just a draft. Just a backup, if the worst happened, but it made her cry anyway.

Mom, if you find this on my phone, something happened. I’m stuck overnight at a rest stop as I write this, and one of the people here might be dangerous. I hope I’m just being paranoid. But if I’m not . . . I’m sorry for everything. All the things I said and did. I’m sorry about our phone call on Thanksgiving. You don’t deserve that. Mom, I love you so much. And I’m so sorry.

Love, your daughter.

Fifteen minutes later, Lars went to the restroom.

He passed Darby’s chair, and she noticed something strange. He’d taken off his black ski gloves, showing the pale skin on the back of his left hand. It was covered in tiny, raised bumps. Like mosquito bites. Or maybe scars, though she couldn’t imagine what awful tool could do that to a hand, other than a cheese grater—

Then Lars shuffled past and disappeared into the men’s restroom. The door swished shut, taking forever before finally clicking.

Now.

Darby pushed her chair back and stood up on shaky knees. Ed and Ashley looked up at her. This was her chance, her thirty-second window to sneak outside and confirm the unbelievable. Her phone in her hand, she moved to the front door, her lungs filling with held breath — but on the way she surprised herself. She did something completely senseless.

She went to the second carafe, labeled COCO, and quickly refilled her eight-ounce Styrofoam cup. She didn’t even like hot chocolate.

But kids do. Right?

She heard a urinal flush. Lars was coming back.

Rushing now, she sipped the warm drink while she walked back to the front doorway, and pulled the door open, aware that Ashley was still watching her. “Yo, Darbs, where are you going?”

Darbs. She hadn’t been called that since fifth grade.

“Still trying to get a cell signal. My mom’s got pancreatic cancer and she’s in a hospital in Provo.” Without giving Ashley time to respond, she stepped outside into the howling storm, flinching against a wall of bone-chilling air, and remembered a casual little saying she’d heard once from her mother: The easiest lies to tell are the true ones.
