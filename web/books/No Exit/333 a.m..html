No Exit

3:33 a.m.

Ed screamed: “SANDI—”

But Darby’s world turned bloody red. Like acid splashed on her. She felt her eyes burning, both hot and cold at once. Like bleach in her eyes. It filled her mind.

She fell to her knees, eyes squeezed shut, clawing at her face, rubbing away the chemical burn. Small fingers grabbed her elbow, pulling her. Jay's voice in her ears: “Darby. Don't rub your eyes—”

“Sandi, what the fuck are you doing?”

“Eddie, I’m sorry. I’m so sorry—”

Jay’s voice, louder: “Don't rub your eyes.”

Darby didn't, but squeezed them tight, gasping in pain. Pressing so hard her eyeballs felt crushed. She forced her eyes open, pulling her eyelids back with her fingernails, and saw a blurry mix of red and orange, clouded with burning tears. She could barely make out the floor tiles. The room spun, whirling around her. She coughed, her throat full of snot. She saw dark drops hitting the floor. Her nose was bleeding again.

“Hold still.” Jay lifted something heavy. Darby was about to wonder what it was — but then hot water poured down on her face. The carafe, she realized, rubbing her eyes. Smart girl.

Angry shadows moved above her. Loud footsteps.

“Darby.” Jay pulled her elbow, harder. Twisting it against her shoulder: “Darby, come on. Crawl. Crawl.”

She did. Hands and knees on the cold tile, half-blind, dripping. Jay guiding her with pushes and pulls. Behind her, the voices grew louder, booming inside the room, filling the air:

“Sandi. Just explain to me what’s going on—”

“I can save you.”

“Don’t touch that door—”

“Please, let me save you,” Sandi gasped, begging. “Eddie, honey, I can save your dumb life tonight, but only if you shut your mouth and do exactly what I say—”

Darby heard a hollow, metallic click behind her. It was familiar, but she couldn’t place it. She’d heard it several times tonight, though, enough to feel like she’d heard it before. Then through the fog of pain, lightning struck, and her mind screamed: Deadbolt-deadbolt-deadbolt—

Sandi just unlocked the front door.

The doorknob turned easily in Ashley’s hand, surprising him, and he pressed his fingertips against the door and pushed it open a bit, slowly revealing the visitor center inside. He saw Sandi Schaeffer first, standing in the doorway, her cheeks bright red.

“I have them,” she gasped. “I have both of them, stuck in the bathroom—”

Both of them? Ashley felt relieved. “So Jaybird is here?”

“Why wouldn’t she be?”

“It’s complicated.”

Sandi frowned. “Of course. Of course—”

“It’s okay now.”

“Okay? Really? Because I just pepper-sprayed someone—”

“Yeah, thanks for that.”

“All you had to do tonight was nothing and you still messed up.” Sandi coughed in the pepper spray, rubbing her nose. “I mean . . . God, how did you let this happen? How did you let it get this bad?”

Ashley was sick of talking. He shoved his way inside, his eyes watering in the stinging air. Sandi stumbled backward, suddenly alarmed, all of her harsh words momentarily stuck in her throat. She’d seen the orange Paslode nailer in his hand.

Christ, he loved this thing.

“It’s under control,” he assured her. “It’s fine.”

Lars came inside, too, his baby-blue ski jacket flapping in the wind, the Beretta Cougar in his hand.

“You’re sick,” the lady snarled, taking another shaky step back. “You’re both sick. You weren’t supposed to hurt her—”

“We improvised.”

“I was right about you. About both of you—”

“Oh, yeah?” Ashley tapped Lars’s chest. “Listen. This’ll be good.”

“I knew you were both just hillbilly white trash—”

“Aw, Sandi, you’re hurting my feelings.”

“It’s like you want to get caught.” She spat as she talked, saliva flying from her chin, still stumbling backward as they came at her with guns. “You told me . . . you said you’d give her clean clothes every day. You said you’d watch what she ate. You’d give her books. You promised you wouldn’t hurt a hair on Jay’s head—”

“Technically true. Her hair’s fine.”

“How can you think this is funny? You’re going to rot in prison. You and your little fetal-alcohol-syndrome—”

Brother, she would’ve finished, if Ashley hadn’t shoved her.

He wasn’t angry. It’s all under control, remember?

But it was still a harder push than he’d meant. Sandi slid backward, her shoes screeching, hitting the coffee counter with her big rear. The radio fell over, antennae rattling. Her awful black bowl cut covered her face, and she caught herself on the counter, gasping: “You ruined everything—”

Lars aimed his Beretta. “HEY.”

Ashley hadn’t noticed Ed until now — but yep, there he was. The goateed ex-vet he’d beaten in Go Fish, who hated Apple products, whose biggest fear was seeing his estranged family in Aurora this Christmas, stood now by the restrooms, a cross-shaped tire iron in his raised right hand, ready to swing.

“I can’t let you,” Ed said. “I can’t let you near them.”

“Sandi,” Ashley said quietly, “please tell your cousin to drop that thing.”

“It’s a lug wrench, idiot.”

“Ed, just do what he says.”

But the man didn't move. His back to the restroom doors. Sweat popping up on his forehead. The lug wrench shaking in his hand.

Ashley didn’t look away, taking a small step to the side so his brother could see better. “Sandi,” he said quietly, speaking without moving his lips much, “let me be clear. If Cousin Ed here doesn’t put the lug wrench on the floor right now, he will die.”

“Eddie, please, please, just do what Ashley says.”

Ed wiped sweat from his eyes, looking back at Sandi with growing fear. He had to understand by now, but that seemed to confirm it: “Jesus Christ, how do you . . . how do you know these people? What’s going on?”

Sandi cringed. “Things got messy—”

“What were you doing with that little girl, Sandi?”

“Drop it,” Ashley repeated, taking another step forward. “Drop it now, and I won’t hurt you. I promise.”

To his right, Lars carefully held the Beretta Cougar, just like Ashley had taught him. Two hands gripping, thumbs up, finger on the trigger. But Ashley knew he wouldn’t shoot. Not without being told. He was waiting, so obediently, for a signal to kill Ed, which could come in many ways — even as a baseball joke.

A drop of sweat hit the floor.

“I promise we won’t hurt you,” Ashley repeated. “You have my word.”

“Eddie, please.” Sandi’s voice softened. “You’re drunk. Just put it down, and I’ll explain everything.”

But he didn’t give in. He stood firm, not even looking at Lars’s gun, staring back at Ashley, like he was the only person in the world. Hard eyes, daring him to do it. The lug wrench shook with adrenaline. When he finally spoke, it was a low growl: “I knew I hated you.”

“Really?” Ashley said. “I liked you.”

“The moment I met you tonight, when I shook your hand, I just . . . I knew.” The old animal doctor smiled a strange, sad smile. “I saw a glimpse, I think, of who you really are. Behind the games, behind the bad jokes. You’re everything I’ve ever hated in a person. You’re smug, you’re annoying, you talk too much, you’re not as smart as you think you are, and under it all? You’re pure evil.”

And you’re right on the money, Ashley almost said.

But then Ed sighed, and something broke in his eyes, like he was finally seeing that this standoff was pointless. He raised both hands and opened his right in surrender. The lug wrench dropped and banged on the tile floor. The echo rang in the air, and Ashley grinned.

Lars lowered his Beretta.

“Thank you.” Sandi exhaled, tears in her eyes. “Thank you, Eddie, for—”

THWUMP.

Ed made a goofy face, like a man surprised by a burp. For a confused moment, he kept looking at Ashley, just like before. But his eyes were wide now, panicked, searching—

“You forgot,” Ashley told him. “I’m a liar, too.”

He lowered the nailer.

Ed’s eyes followed it, shining with trapped horror. His lips tightened wetly, pulling in his flesh, like he was trying to speak, but a strange thing happened — his jaw wasn’t moving. Not even a little. His voice came out through his nose, a choked moan. A messy red bubble — spit mixed with blood — swelled through his front teeth and splashed down to the floor.

Ashley stepped back, so it wouldn’t get on his shoes.

Sandi screamed. It was deafening.

“Lars.” Ashley snapped his fingers and pointed. “Calm her down, please.”

Ed slapped both hands to his throat, clearly trying to scream, too, but his body wouldn’t let him. His mouth was nailed shut — really — by a steel framing nail, stuck through his lower jaw at an upward angle, pinning his tongue to the roof of his mouth. Ashley imagined it wiggling in there like a bloody worm. And he wondered how far the nail had gone — could the tip be touching Ed’s brain?

He pushed the man over with his foot. Ed slumped against the map of Colorado and slid down the wall, crying quietly into his hands, blood gathering in his palms and dripping small drops on the floor.

“Have a seat. You should know, Eddie-boy, I hate alcoholics . . .”

Sandi was in hysterics. She cried out again, a hyena scream, another big glob of shiny snot hanging from her chin. Lars pushed the Beretta into her face, and she immediately shut up.

“Change of plan,” Ashley said, tapping Lars’s shoulder, and the fluorescent lights flickered above him. “See, you and me, baby brother, we’ve already filled this little building with evidence, and we don’t have enough bleach, or time, to clean everything. So we’re going to have to get creative, if you understand.”

Lars nodded once. He understood the message.

Ashley continued, stepping over a spreading puddle of Ed’s blood. “And as for Darby and—”

Wait.

He realized something.

“Wait, wait . . .” He grabbed Sandi by the elbow, snapping his fingers in her face. “Hey. Look at me. You said . . . you trapped Darby and Jaybird in the restroom, right? The men’s restroom?”

She sniffled, looking up at him with bloodshot eyes, and nodded.

No.

Lars looked at him, too, not understanding. But Ashley did.

No, no, no—

He threw Sandi to the floor, stomping past her, past Ed, toward the restrooms, and he pushed the MEN door open to see . . . an empty room. Snowflakes drifting in through the triangular window.

Lars watched.

Ashley Garver stepped back out and slammed the door hard. “I’m so sick of that damn window—”

Darby turned Sandi’s key and the truck’s engine roared to life. A diesel sound broke the quiet of the parking lot.

Jay crawled into the passenger seat. “What if Ashley hears us?”

She yanked the gear shift. “He just did.”

She’d already scraped a peephole in the windshield and cleared some space around the back tires. Just enough to make icy ramps, to get moving. Sandi was ready; this F-150 was a tough truck with studded tires, chains, and a huge eighteen inches of lift. If any vehicle parked here could make it down the mountain, it was this one. And if it couldn’t . . . well, Darby remembered Ashley’s dumb Ford joke: Found on road, dead.

Let’s hope not. She rubbed the chemical sting from her eyes. Her face was still wet from the carafe, the hot water quickly cooling on her skin.

“Everyone here is bad,” Jay whispered.

“Not me.”

“Yeah, but everyone else—”

Darby tried not to think about it. Her head was still spinning, too. First Ashley had acted like a friend before betraying her. And now Sandi had shown she was part of the kidnapping plan. She didn’t know where Ed Schaeffer stood in all this chaos, but she hoped he was still alive in there.

If he’s even on our side to begin with.

She hoped he was, but with every second, the Wanapani rest area felt more dangerous. The plastic tightened around her face. She had fewer allies. She had more enemies. The conspiracy was confusing.

“What was my bus driver doing here?” Jay asked.

Darby gripped the steering wheel. “Moment of truth.”

She pressed the gas pedal and the Ford slowly moved forward in the slushy snow, tires spinning, throwing sheets of hard ice. Steady pressure under her toes. Not too hard, not too soft. Grinding, skidding motion — but it was motion.

“Come on. Come on, come on—”

“How far away are the police?” Jay asked.

She remembered the CDOT report Ed told her about. The semi-truck wrecked at the bottom of the pass. “Seven, uh, maybe eight miles.”

“That’s not far, right?”

Darby spun the wheel in a clumsy half-turn, sliding Sandi’s truck into icy ruts, turning south now. Downhill, down the off-ramp, facing oncoming traffic — if there was any. She looked for the Ford’s headlights and turned hers on. Ashley and Lars already knew they were there because of the engine’s rumble, so being quiet was impossible. They were coming, right now.

“You stole her truck,” Jay whispered.

“She pepper-sprayed me. We’re even.”

The girl laughed, a weak little sound, as a bit of orange light appeared on the glass behind her. It was the visitor center’s front door opening. A beam of light, and in it, a thin figure.

It was Lars.

Rodent Face. All black shadow. The silhouette raised its right arm, as easily as a man using a TV remote, and Darby knew at once, grabbing Jay by the shoulder and throwing her down against the cold leather seat—

“Get down—”

CRACK.

The passenger window burst. Sticky bits scattered off the dash. Jay cried out, covering her face.

Darby ducked low as glass rained down. The gunshot sounded like a firecracker in the thin air. Her body wanted her to stay down, hidden from Rodent Face’s aim, but her brain knew better: He’s coming closer. Now.

Go, go, go—

She found the gas pedal and slammed it. The truck shot forward, engine roaring, throwing them back in their seats. The world tilted. Luggage banged in the back. Darby straightened up against the sweaty leather, peeked over the steering wheel — just an eye, an inch — and steered Sandi’s F150 toward the highway.

Jay grabbed her wrist. “Darby—”

“Stay down.”

“Darby, he’s shooting at us—”

“Yes, I noticed—”

CRACK. A bullet hit the truck’s windshield, and Darby jumped. A cold breeze blew to her left; her side window was also shattered. Snowflakes flew in, stinging her cheek.

“He’s chasing us,” Jay said. “Drive faster—”

Darby was trying. She pressed harder on the gas, and the truck swerved but sped up. The tires kicked up ice, spraying it through the windows and scattering cold grit inside. Lars fired again — CRACK — and the truck’s side mirror shattered. Jay screamed.

Darby pulled her down with her free hand. “Keep your head down. It’s fine—”

“No, it’s not.”

“He’s not going to catch us—”

CRACK. Another hole appeared in the windshield, a jagged star above Darby’s head. But Lars’s gunshots sounded different. They were becoming faint, fading in the distance.

“Yes.” Her heart fluttered. “Yes, yes, yes—”

“What’s happening?”

They were rolling down the exit ramp now, picking up speed. Thank God for momentum, for gravity, for the steep slope. Darby pushed the gas pedal again. Another engine roar. The world tilted downward, and bits of broken glass slid around them like gravel.

“See? I told you—”

Lars fired again — CRACK — but missed the truck completely. He was too far behind now. They were disappearing. The orange glow of the Wanapani building was disappearing, too, its familiar shapes sinking into the snowy darkness, and Darby was so glad to see it all go. Like waking up from a terrible nightmare, she never wanted to see it again. Ever. Good riddance to that awful place.

Jay peered around her seat, watching the figure of Rodent Face chasing them shrink through the riddled rear window — “Stay down” — and she raised a shaking fist. Her ring finger up.

It took Darby a moment to understand. “Uh . . . wrong finger.”

“Oh.” Jay corrected. “Better?”

“Better.”

“Thanks,” said the seven-year-old girl flipping the bird through the bullet-riddled back window of a stolen pickup truck, and Darby started to laugh. It was uncontrollable, shaking her lungs like a cough. She couldn’t stop it.

Oh, my God, we actually did it.

We got away.

Just seven or eight miles to go. She dug her iPhone from her pocket and tossed it to Jay. “Hey. Watch the screen, okay? If you see a signal bar, you hand it to me right away—”

“The battery’s almost dead.”

“I know.”

They crept downhill, truck tires kicking up fresh snow like waterwheels. She gently pressed the gas, keeping the Ford moving. Keeping the motion going. That’s all it was now — pure, urgent forward motion. Like driving across two states with a stomach full of Red Bull and ibuprofen, fighting to keep her caffeine rush going with a confusing text from Devon shaking in her palm (She’s okay right now), racing the huge snowstorm over the pass. Forward, forward, forward. Don’t stop.

Don’t-stop-don’t-stop—

Now they reached State Route Seven, high beams shining over frozen piles of snow blown by the wind. Here she planned to move into the lane for traffic coming the other way and pass under the first bright light. Darby felt another small thrill in her stomach. This was really happening. She’d done it. They were really getting away.

Still, she worried — what if the brothers got their van out, got it moving in the snow, and chased them down the highway? Then another happy shiver as she realized: Ashley doesn’t even know where his Astro keys are.

He never saw me throw them out the restroom window.

Yes, yes, yes. It all felt too good to be true.

“Hold my phone up.” She pointed. “Out the window.”

Jay did this, kneeling to lean out the passenger window, and Darby suddenly imagined hitting the brakes hard and throwing this poor girl out like a crash-test dummy. That’d be tough to explain to her parents.

“And buckle your seatbelt,” she added. “Please.”

“Why?”

“Because it’s the law.”

“What if we need to get out and run?”

“Then — Jesus. Then you’ll unbuckle it.”

“Yours isn’t buckled—”

“Hey.” Darby grinned darkly, doing her best angry-dad-voice. “Don’t make me turn this car around.”

Jay buckled her seatbelt with a metallic snick and pointed at the seat behind Darby’s head. “He almost shot you.”

She touched the headrest behind her ponytail. Her fingers found a rough hole, leaking bits of yellow foam. Lars’s bullet had gone just an inch above her scalp before going through the windshield. Saved by luck. She laughed roughly. “Good thing I’m short, huh?”

“Good thing,” Jay said. “I kind of like you.”

Darby steered Sandi’s truck onto the highway, joining the empty lanes. Usually, this would be a crazy thing to do. She almost turned on the right signal, then felt dumb. Her hands were still shaking. A weird quiet filled the air, and she cleared her throat, trying to say something: “So . . . Sandi drives your school bus, huh?”

“Mrs. Schaeffer, I think.”

“Was she nice?”

“She kidnapped me.”

“Besides that.”

“Not really.” Jay shrugged. “She filled in sometimes. I barely remember her.”

But she sure remembered you, Darby thought. She remembered you, your fancy house, and your parents' busy schedules. A school bus driver made sense as someone to pick out a target for a kidnapping, and Ashley and Lars were clearly doing the rough stuff. But why would Sandi risk meeting Beavis and Butthead in person, so far away? In a quiet rest stop, two states away?

She watched the snowy highway stretch out, feeling the blood return to her fingers and toes, fighting the cold air coming through the windows. Only now did she start to see the dark humor in the whole situation, in her own bad luck and poor decisions. She’d unknowingly trusted a kidnapper again tonight. That pot of hot water she’d planned to use as a weapon? Jaybird had poured it on her face, which still stung from the burns. Nothing had gone right. She couldn’t help her teeth chattering: “I swear, Jay, next time you think you recognize someone else here . . . like, if the first Colorado cop we see looks like your dentist from San Diego, please tell me, okay?”

“My orthodontist lives in LA, actually.”

“Los Angeles?”

“Yeah.”

“Do you fly to see your orthodontist?”

Jay winced, embarrassed. “Sometimes.”

“Seriously?”

“It’s . . . my parents really like him—”

“No way. Did your parents invent Google or something?”

“You’re teasing me.”

Darby grinned. “Is it too late to kidnap you myself?”

“Maybe not.” Jay grinned back. “You’re the one driving a stolen tru—”

Brain-jarring halt.

The whole world seemed to stop. The truck plunged into a pile of deep snow, headlights digging in and going out, two tons of metal hitting a wall. An empty Gatorade bottle shot out of the console. Small bits of glass bounced around. Darby smacked her jaw on the steering wheel, biting her tongue, and in a flash, they were stuck again, trapped again, and all the happiness turned bitter and hollow, like the taste of blood in her mouth.

Oh, no.

No, no, no—

Jay looked at her. “Glad you made me buckle up.”
