No Exit

1:09 a.m.

Jay Nissen cut through the last bar on the dog kennel.

She cut them one at a time, sawing with the toothed knife the way the red-haired young woman had told her to. Like a small lumberjack. Her left hand tingled, so it took a long time. Twice, she dropped the knife and had to feel around for it in the dark. Once, she worried it had bounced out of the kennel and been lost forever. But she found it.

And now?

With a push, the grating fell and clattered against the van’s door.

This was the first time the cage had been open since they took her. She didn’t know how many days ago. She hadn’t been counting. Going more than a night without her medicine made her dizzy, and since then she’d fallen into an irregular pattern of sickly, four-hour naps. The sun had been up and down, rising and falling from different windows. The smell of ketchup, ranch dressing, and old sweat on the glass. The crumpled Jack in the Box bags. Their quiet voices, Ashley’s jokes, the hum of the road, the constant clicking of the van’s turn signal. Could it have been a week already? What were her parents doing right now?

Her Wii U controller had been charging when they came to her house.

She was plugging the gray wire into the Nintendo when a single, sharp knock came from the front door. Like a tennis ball. She hurried to the door and opened it a few inches — there’s a small brass chain that stops the door — and that was when she first saw him, the one she now knew as Lars. Back then, he didn’t have a cold yet. He smiled and told her he was here with the Fox Roofing Service, that her father “Mr Pete” had given them permission to come in.

Jay said no.

Lars asked her a few more times, in different ways. He seemed to think “Mr Pete” was at the grocery store, which wasn’t true (her father had called from the office to tell her the babysitter was sick and that there was leftover Mongolian Grill in the fridge). Even then, Jay felt that Lars wasn’t like the other adults she knew. She thought that even at her age, she was already smarter than he might ever be.

Lars asked more rudely, leaning in. His breath smelled like dead leaves.

Jay shut the door.

When she turned around, Ashley was sitting at the kitchen table. His boots had left mud on the floor. He looked up, eating banana chips from a bowl. She still didn’t know how he got in — a window, maybe? The garage?

She ran for the living room, but didn’t make it.

Now, Jamie Nissen — or Jay, as she’d been called since first grade — crawled out of the dog kennel on her hands, over the blankets her rescuer had hidden under. The metal bars clattered; she hoped Ashley and Lars weren’t close enough to hear. She reached the back door of the van, expecting it to be locked. Lars always re-locked the doors, every time he—

The handle clicked in her bloody fingers.

The door swung open.

Jay froze, looking out into the darkness. Snowflakes swirled everywhere. A cold gust of night air. A parking lot of white, untouched snow, sparkling. It was thrilling. She’d never seen this much snow before.

Now what?

“Now what, Darbs?”

She couldn’t breathe or see. Plastic was stretched tight over her face, stuck against her teeth. Hard hands around her throat, twisting the bag, cutting off her air. A slippery, buried-alive panic.

“Shh, shh.”

She struggled, but Ashley was too strong. He had her arms twisted behind her in a wrestling hold. Her shoulder blades felt pulled apart, and her hands were pinned and useless far behind her. Like fighting a straitjacket. She kicked, trying to find the restroom wall to push against, but found only empty space. Her back cracked.

“Don’t fight,” he whispered. “It’s okay.”

Pressure building in her chest. Her lungs burning, swelling against her ribs. She felt her last breath — a half-gasp that was in her throat when the bag came down — trapped against her face, foggy and wet. Warm blood running down her chin. Her nose was bleeding again.

She fought again, twisting, flailing. Her legs kicked out. Her fingers clawed and scratched; she found the loop of the lanyard on his jacket. Keys jingled. But there was no gun, no weapon to grab. She was losing energy. This struggle was weaker than the first.

This is it, she realized. I’m going to die here.

Right here, in a dirty restroom off State Route Seven. Next to the clean toilets, the marked mirrors, the stall doors with peeling paint and graffiti. Right here, right now, with that Lysol taste still in her mouth.

“Shh.” Ashley moved his head, like he was checking behind him. “It’s almost over. Just let it happen—”

She screamed silently inside the Ziploc bag. The plastic made a small bubble. Then her lungs breathed in without thinking—a sharp gulp—but found only empty space, sucking in a tiny bit of used air.

“I know it hurts. I know. I’m sorry.” The bag twisted tighter, clockwise, and now she saw the window. Through one squeezed eye, blurred by cloudy plastic and tears, she saw that little triangular window, eight feet off the floor, dusted with snowflakes. So close. So terribly close. She almost wished it were further away, across the room, hopeless and unreachable. But no, it was right there, and she could almost reach out and touch it, if her hands weren’t pinned.

She thrashed a third time, but it was clumsy and weak. This time Ashley barely had to hold her. She knew this was the last one; that there couldn’t be another try. She was done for now. Ed and Sandi were in the same building, on the other side of a wall, ten feet away, unaware while she suffocated to death in the arms of a killer. She felt time slow down. A heavy and comforting rest settled over her, like a thick wool blanket.

She hated how good it felt.

“Rest now.” Ashley planted a wet kiss on the top of her head, crinkling the plastic. “You tried real hard, Darbs. Get some rest now.”

His disgusting voice was so far away now. It sounded like he was in another room. Talking to someone else. Smothering some other girl to death. The ache in her lungs was already fading. All of these awful feelings were happening to someone else, not Darby Thorne.

Her mind wandered now, disconnecting, drifting, thinking about all the things she hadn’t finished in her life. Her final painting, not done. Her Stafford loans, not paid. Her Gmail password, lost forever. Her bank account with $291 in it. Her dorm room. Her wall of gravestone rubbings. Her mother at Utah Valley Hospital, waking up from surgery, about to learn that her daughter had been randomly murdered at a rest stop two hundred miles away from—

No.

She fought it.

No, no, no—

She held onto this, onto forty-nine-year-old Maya Thorne, stuck in the ICU. Because if Darby died right here, right now, in this restroom, she’d never get to apologize for all the things she’d said to her mother on Thanksgiving. It would all become permanent history. Every awful word of it.

And suddenly she wasn’t afraid. Not anymore. She tasted something far more useful than fear — anger. She was furious. She was absolutely fucking furious at the unfairness of it all, of what Ashley was trying to do to her and her family, raging hard against the darkness closing in. And something else . . .

If I die here, she knew, no one will save Jay.

“. . . Darbs?”

She arched her back, and ordered her tired lungs to do one last thing — to open and inhale as hard as possible. To suck the plastic airtight against her open mouth, so it was squeezed between her front teeth like bubble gum, just a thin, pulled-back centimeter—

She bit down.

Not hard enough. The plastic slipped out of her mouth.

“Pancreatic cancer?” Ashley’s lips slid against her ear, like he’d read her mind. “Your mom has . . . you said pancreatic cancer, right?”

She tried again. She sucked the bag tight with burning lungs.

Bit down.

Nothing.

“Isn’t it funny, then?” His tight grip, his awful voice. “You were so sure you’d bury your mom, but it turns out you had it the wrong way around, you dumb cunt, because she’s going to bury you—”

Darby bit down again, and the plastic tore.

A tiny bit of ice-cold air rushed inside. Racing down her throat fast, like inhaling through a straw.

Ashley paused — “Oh” — and in a quick moment of confusion, his grip loosened and Darby’s shoes touched the floor. A quick moment was all she needed. She found her balance, pushed off the tile, and threw her body backwards into his.

Ashley stumbled, losing balance.

She kept running backward, kept pushing him—

He gasped: “Wait, wait, wait—”

She slammed him, back-first, into a sink. Spine against porcelain. The faucet turned on. He grunted and let go, her arms twisting from his grip. Her hands finally free. She grabbed the wet bag and ripped it off her face, sucking in a full breath. A scream inside out, full of blood, snot, and tears.

She saw color again. Air on her cheeks. Oxygen in her blood. She fell away from him, her knees weak, catching herself on the floor with an outstretched palm. Cold tiles, speckled with her blood.

Behind her, Ashley took something out of his pocket.

He raised his arm—

—And he swung the rock-in-a-sock at the back of Darby’s head, swinging the stone like a fast whip, ready to hear the crunch of the girl’s skull — but she was already hurrying forward, moving away.

It brushed her hair.

He jumped after her, unsteady from his swing, the rock hitting the wall to his left, leaving a bit of broken tile. He fell to his knees and watched her run down the restroom, toward that small triangular window, with the plastic bag flapping behind her. She won’t make it, he thought. But in a moment, she’d jumped up to the window frame, grabbed on with her fingernails, and thrown her body through the tiny opening like a gymnast. Ankles up, then out.

Just like that.

She was gone.

Ashley Garver was suddenly alone in the restroom. He stood up unsteadily, almost slipping on the bloody Ziploc bag.

It didn’t matter, he thought, smoothing back his hair with his hand, catching his breath. He’d put Lars outside the back wall, by the picnic tables, for this reason. His brother, with that trusty Beretta Cougar, was the backup. Darby had escaped his kill zone in the restroom, but in doing so she’d practically landed in Lars’s arms, and now she would be too weak to fight back—

The restroom door banged open behind him. He turned, expecting to see Ed's confused face, here to check out the noise, and he already had a story ready — I slipped on the wet floor, I think I hit my head — but it wasn’t Ed standing there.

It was Lars.

Ashley kicked the plastic bag. “Oh, come on.”

“You sounded like you, uh, needed help—”

“Yes. I needed you out there.”

“Oh—”

“Out there.” Ashley pointed hard. “Outside, not inside.”

Lars’s eyes widened, looking from his big brother to the empty window. He knew what he’d done, what he’d let happen, and his face fell apart and turned red with messy tears: “I’m sorry. I’m so sorry, I didn’t mean to—”

Ashley kissed him on the lips.

“Focus, baby brother.” He slapped his cheek. “The parking lot. That’s where she’s running, right now.”

He hoped he could still run, too. His lower back throbbed where the redhead had slammed him into the porcelain sink. And as he gathered his thoughts, he noticed something else. A sudden lightness in his right jeans pocket.

His lanyard was gone.

“And . . . the bitch took our keys.”

Darby tumbled down the stacked picnic tables, landing hard. She dropped Ashley’s keys in the snow but picked them up, scrambling upright.

The red lanyard had become hooked around her thumb in the fight. Pure luck, really. When she’d charged him into the sink and broken free, the bunch of chattering keys came with her. Now she had them. And he didn’t.

They jingled in her palm. A half-dozen mismatched keys, and a black thumb drive. She stuffed the whole handful into her pocket as a new plan formed.

What’s better than running for help?

Stealing the abductors’ van and driving for help.

With Jay inside.

A desperate gamble. She was still in shock, her fingers still slick with sweat, her breaths still surging. Her mind racing with panicked thoughts. She wasn’t sure if their Astro could drive any farther than Blue could in Snowmageddon, but she’d sure as hell try. She’d stomp the gas, rock the four-wheel drive on its shocks, try everything. She had no other options. If she stayed here at Wanapani, Ashley and Lars would murder her.

She circled the building, wading through snowdrifts, and the night air stung her throat. She passed the crowd of half-buried Nightmare Children to her left. Beaten bronze shapes in the darkness, like pit-bull-mauling victims frozen in playtime. That bare flagpole, wobbling under another gust of razor wind.

Ahead, the parking lot. The cars. Their van.

Just another fifty feet—

The visitor center’s front door squeaked open behind her. A rectangle of light spilled out, and she cast a long shadow on the snow. A pair of loud footsteps followed her. The door shut and her shadow vanished.

“No.” Ashley’s voice, firm, like he was scolding a dog: “Don’t shoot her.”

She slipped, cutting a knee on jagged ice. Kept running. The footsteps chasing her split up, one moving right, one cutting left. Like wolves circling their prey. She recognized them by their breathing — the congested pant of Lars on the left, the controlled huffs of Ashley on the right. She kept running and focused on the Astro. Keys jangling in her hand.

“Lars! Don’t shoot her.”

“She’s trying to steal the van—”

“You want a yellow card?”

She slipped again, catching herself. Her purse bounced off her knee. Just ten steps from the kidnappers’ van now. She could see the cartoon fox on the side, racing closer, still holding that orange nail gun—

“She won’t get far. The snow’s too deep—”

“What if she does?”

“She won’t.”

“What if she does, Ashley?”

Darby skidded, reaching the driver's door, her heart pounding. She wiped snow off the lock and searched for the keychain, but it was too dark to find the right Chevy key. At least three of them felt like they could be car keys. She tried the first one. It didn’t fit. She tried the second one. It fit but didn’t turn—

“She’s unlocking the door—”

She was on the third key, pushing it into the icy lock, when she noticed something to the left. A small detail, but very strange.

The Astro’s back door.

It should have been closed — but it was slightly open, the glass reflecting a streak of lamplight, the top edge covered in snowflakes. Darby hadn’t left it open. It couldn’t have been Lars, or Ashley. That left . . . Jay?

Lars panted. “She’s . . . she’s stopping.”

“I know.”

“Why’s she stopping?”

As both sets of footsteps came closer, Ashley understood. “Oh, hell.”

1:23 a.m.

From where she stood, Darby couldn’t quite see it.

But she knew what Ashley saw — Jay’s dog kennel messily broken from the inside, the Astro’s back door forced open, and small footprints in the snow leading into the darkness.

He stared, dumbfounded, before looking back at Darby: “If she tries to run, shoot her.”

She turned — but Lars had already walked around the van and was behind her with that short handgun held low, pointed at her stomach.

She gasped. Surrounded again.

“I don’t . . . I don’t believe it.” Ashley paced, digging his fingers into his hair, and Darby noticed his hairline was just as far back as his younger brother’s. He just had longer bangs to hide it.

She felt a dark satisfaction. She loved it. Despite Ashley’s arrogance and bragging tonight, she’d still managed to ruin their plan. Little Jaybird was free.

Ashley kicked the side of the Astro, denting the metal. “I don’t fucking believe it—”

Lars stepped back.

But Darby couldn’t resist. Too much adrenaline pumping through her. Just moments ago, he’d been suffocating her with a plastic bag, and she was still angry about it, still full of wild energy. “Hey, Ashley. I'm no expert on kidnappings, but doesn’t it only work if there’s a kid involved?”

He turned to face her.

She shrugged. “Just my guess.”

“You . . .” Lars raised his pistol. “You should stop—”

“And you should use a breath mint.” Darby looked back to Ashley, her words shaky and rough, coming out like a thread unwinding: “Are you sure about that speech? Helpless humans letting the big, scary monsters do what they want? Because I think I just changed the story, motherfucker—”

He stomped toward her.

She flinched — Oh God, this is it, I’m dead — and Ashley raised the rock-in-a-sock as he charged, ready to smash it into her skull, but then at the last second he stepped past her and threw it.

She opened her eyes.

He’d been aiming at a streetlight. Two hundred feet away. After floating through the air, the rock hit the post hard, bouncing off the metal with a ringing sound. It echoed twice.

Most NFL quarterbacks couldn’t do that.

Lars whispered: “Magic.”

I’m a magic man, Lars, my brother.

They’d been playing with her all night, she realized. Using her. Acting like strangers, moving around the room, telling obvious lies and confusing little clues and watching how she acted. Like a rat in their maze.

Can you cut a girl in half?

I can. But you only get gold if the girl lives.

That roomful of nervous laughter echoed in her head, as annoying as microphone feedback. Her headache was back.

Ashley wiped spit from his mouth and turned to Darby, his breath visible in the cold air. “You don’t get it yet, Darbs. It’s okay. You will.”

Get what?

This made her feel sick. Her rush of adrenaline, her crazy fearlessness — it was all fading away, like a weak buzz. Two beers, fun while they last, but gone by dessert.

Lars looked inside the van. “How long ago did she get out?”

Ashley was pacing again. Thinking.

The silence made Darby uneasy. Like any good showman, Ashley was hard to read, only showing his violence when he wanted to. His younger brother still held her at gunpoint, never letting the barrel touch her back. Never letting the weapon get close enough to grab.

Lars asked again. “How long ago did she break out?”

Again, Ashley didn’t answer. He stopped with his hands on his hips, studying Jay’s footprints in the snow. They led north. Away from the rest stop. Up the rising land, past the overpass, along the on-ramp. Toward State Route Seven.

His words simmered in her mind. You don’t get it yet, Darbs.

You will.

She guessed, based on the snow that had gathered on the van’s back door, that Jay had broken out and escaped about twenty minutes ago. Before the attack on the restroom, at least. The girl’s footprints were already fading, filling in with light snowflakes.

“What’s that?” Lars asked.

Ashley knelt to pick something up, like a wrinkled black snakeskin. But Darby recognized it — the electrical tape they’d put over Jay’s mouth. She’d thrown it away here as she ran.

Smartly, Jay had avoided the visitor center, because she knew Ashley and Lars were inside. So she’d gone for the highway, probably hoping to flag down a driver and call the police — but this poor girl didn’t know where she was. She didn’t know they were far beyond the edge of Gypsum, far beyond the edge of anything important, nine thousand feet above sea level. She didn’t know it was six uphill miles to the top and ten downhill to the power station; that this harsh, wind-torn place might as well be Antarctica.

Jay was a rich city kid from San Diego — a land of yucca trees, sandals, and mild winters.

Darby searched her mind, now throbbing like a hangover — what had Jay been wearing inside the kennel? A thin coat. That red Pokeball T-shirt. Light pants. No gloves. No protection from the weather at all.

Finally, in a flash of horror, she understood.

So did Lars. “She’s going to freeze to death out there—”

“We’ll follow her tracks,” Ashley said.

“But she could be a mile down the road—”

“We’ll call out to her.”

“She won’t come to our voices.”

“You’re right.” Ashley nodded to Darby. “But she’ll come to hers.”

Now both brothers were looking at her.

For a moment, the winds died down and the parking lot went silent. Only the soft patter of snowflakes falling around them, as Darby quietly understood why Ashley hadn’t killed her yet.

“Well, here we go.” He shrugged. “I guess that makes us even, huh? Neither of us wants a black-fingered little Jay-cicle.”

Jokes. Everything was a joke to him.

She said nothing.

He clicked on a small flashlight, highlighting the girl’s footprints with a blue-white LED beam. Snowflakes lit up like sparks. Then he shined the light in her face, a blinding brightness. “Start calling her name.”

Darby stared at her feet, tasting stomach acid in her throat. A nasty, greasy heartburn, filled with awful thoughts. I shouldn’t have given her that knife. What if, by getting involved tonight, I made things worse?

What if I got Jay killed?

Lars’s pistol pushed into her back, a rough signal to walk. If she’d been ready, she could’ve turned, grabbed for the gun, and maybe, just maybe, taken control of it. But the chance was gone.

“Her name is Jamie,” Lars said. “But call her Jay.”

“Go on. Follow the tracks and start yelling.” Ashley moved his LED light over the footprints, then looked back at her with eyes that grew darker. “You wanted to save her life so badly? Well, Darbs, here’s your chance.”

The girl’s footprints led them along the on-ramp, to the dirty ice banks of State Route Seven before turning uphill into the woods. Up a rocky slope of snowdrifts and unsteady fir trees. With every step, Darby silently dreaded reaching the end of these tracks and finding a little body in a red Pokeball shirt, collapsed on the ground. Instead, something even worse happened — Jay’s footprints simply disappeared, wiped away by windswept snow.

Darby cupped her hands and shouted again: “Jay.”

It had been thirty minutes now. Her voice was hoarse.

Up here, the only guide was the gloomy shadow of Melanie’s Peak, due east. The land got steeper as they climbed. Big rocks stuck out of the snow, granite faces covered in thin streams of ice. The trees here leaned, their roots shallow, branches drooping. Sticks snapped underfoot, like tiny bones breaking in the snow.

“Jay Nissen.” Darby swept the flashlight, casting sharp shadows. “If you can hear me, come to my voice.”

No answer. Only the stiff creak of the trees.

“It’s safe,” she added. “Ashley and Lars aren’t here.”

She hated lying.

But drawing Jay back was the poor girl’s only chance at survival now. Maybe dying at the hands of the Garver brothers was still better than definitely dying in a freezing blizzard. Right? It made sense, but she still hated herself for lying. It was humiliating. Made her feel exposed. She felt like Ashley’s little pet, speaking obediently for him, her nostrils still caked with dried blood from when he’d recently slammed her face into a table.

The brothers followed her, keeping back a bit, about ten steps behind on either side. They were hidden in the dark, with only Ashley’s LED flashlight lighting the way. It was all part of Ashley’s plan. Jay wouldn’t show himself if he saw her kidnappers following Darby with guns. At least, that’s what she thought would happen.

But it hadn’t.

Jamie Nissen. A rich family's missing daughter from San Diego, with a Christmas tree and presents waiting. Now she was lost in the Rockies, her fingers turning black from the cold, her body shutting down, covered in snow, tears freezing on her face. They could have walked right past her small body without even seeing her.

Hypothermia is a calm way to die, Darby remembered reading. The cold doesn’t hurt for long, then you just feel warm and sleepy. You don’t really die, you just fall asleep, not knowing about the damage to your body. Fingers getting hard, blisters filled with dead flesh that needs to be cut off — but in your mind, you’re somewhere else, wrapped in a warm blanket. Darby hoped it was true. She hoped Jay hadn’t suffered.

She called out again into the dark.

Still no answer.

To her left, Lars whispered, “How much longer?”

To her right, “As long as it takes.”

She knew Ashley wasn’t dumb — he was thinking the same thing. They’d been following the footprints for thirty minutes, and Jay had a twenty-minute head start. That meant Jay probably wouldn’t survive long in the freezing woods, and it was getting worse every minute.

Darby thought about her own options, being held at gunpoint. Fight? Get shot. Run? Get shot in the back. She thought about turning and shining the flashlight in their eyes to blind them, but they’d been looking at it for a while, so their eyes were used to it. That was the first problem. And even if she could blind them for a moment, the snowy ground was too rough to run away quickly — that was the second problem.

To her left, Lars worried. “What if we got Jay killed?”

To her right: “We didn’t.”

“What if we did?”

“We didn’t, baby brother.” A pause. “She might have, though.”

This hit Darby like a twisting dagger in her gut, how painfully right Ashley was. It made a terrible kind of sense. If she hadn’t stepped in tonight, Jay would still be locked in that dog kennel inside their van, captive but still alive. Icy fingers wrapped around her stomach and slowly, so slowly, began to squeeze. Why did I have to get involved?

Why couldn’t I have just called the cops in the morning?

She tried to focus on staying alive, on solving problem one (the light) and problem two (the terrain), but she couldn’t.

She wished she could go back and undo everything she’d done tonight. Every single choice she’d made, ever since she first looked through that frosty window and saw Jay’s hand grabbing that kennel bar. She wished she’d been happy to just play detective and get information. She could have waited quietly until morning, kept her advantage, and maybe after the snowplows came and the people at the rest stop left, she could have secretly followed Ashley and Lars’s van in her Honda. A quarter-mile back, one hand on the steering wheel, her iPhone in the other, giving the Colorado State Police the information they needed to arrest them. She still could have saved Jay.

(And Mom would still have pancreatic cancer)

But no. Instead, Darby Elizabeth Thorne, a college sophomore with no police or military training, tried to handle things herself. And now here she was, walking through the woods with a .45 aimed at her back, searching for a dead child.

To her right, a dark laugh. “Gotta say, Darbs, as far as Good Samaritans go, you’re really on a roll. First you trust one of the kidnappers, and then you get the kidnapped girl killed. Nice work.”

Everything was a joke to Ashley Garver. Even this, somehow.

Christ, she hated him.

But now she wondered — had he been telling her the truth after all? Maybe it really was a simple ransom plot, just like he’d told her, and after payment, the brothers really had planned to return Jay to her family alive. She imagined them dropping her off at some sunny bus stop in the middle of nowhere. Little Jaybird blinking in the Kansas sun after two weeks of darkness, running to the nearest stranger on a bench, begging them to call her parents—

Until Darby stepped in, that is. And gave the little girl a Swiss Army knife so she could escape into a dangerous place she was totally unprepared for. And another awful thought slipped into her mind — she felt guilty for even thinking it, given what had already happened — but it stuck like a splinter and wouldn’t leave.

They’re going to kill me now.

Darby was sure of this.

Now that Jay is lost, now that they don’t need my voice. And now that—

Now that they were far enough away from the rest area, Lars had been waiting to be allowed to shoot Darby in the back of the head, and Ashley had finally allowed it. The phrase “batting a thousand” was the signal.

It meant kill.

It was called Spy Code. Since they were kids, Ashley had hidden dozens of secret messages in everyday talks. “Lucky me” means stay. “Lucky you” means go. “Extra cheese” means run. “Ace of spades” means pretend we’re strangers. If you messed up, you got a yellow card, and Lars had pale scars from past mistakes. Tonight almost had a close call — he almost missed “Ace of spades” at the rest area.

But he knew this was coming.

The gun was ice-cold in his hands. His skin stuck to the metal. It was a Beretta Cougar, a short, thick gun that felt wrong in his hands. Like holding a big jellybean. The Cougar usually used 9-millimeter bullets, but this one was the 8405, so it used bigger .45 ACP bullets. More power, but more kick and fewer bullets in the magazine, Ashley said. Eight shots, single-stacked.

Lars liked it okay. But he wished he had the Beretta 92FS, like the gun Max Payne uses in his Xbox games. He would never tell Ashley that. The gun was a gift. You never question Ashley’s gifts, or his punishments. That’s how big brothers are — one day he brought Lars a stray cat. A lively cat with a loud purr. Lars named her Stripes. Then, the next day, Ashley poured gas on Stripes and threw her into a campfire.

Like any big brother, I give, and I take away.

Lars raised the Beretta Cougar now.

As they walked, he aimed at the back of Darby’s head (aim small, miss small). The night-sights lined up; two green dots went up her back. She was ahead of them, shining Ashley’s flashlight through the trees, her body lit up by her own light. She had no idea.

He squeezed the trigger.

To his right, Ashley covered his ear, ready for the shot. And Darby kept walking through the snow, shining the flashlight, not knowing she was about to die, not knowing Lars was pulling the trigger, close to shooting a .45-caliber bullet through her—

She clicked the flashlight off.

Blackness.

Darby heard their startled voices behind her: “I can’t see—”

“What happened?”

“She turned off the flashlight—”

“Shoot her, Lars—”

She ran like hell. Staggering through deep snow. Hard gasps stinging her throat. She’d made them night-blind. Not by flashing them with the LED beam, which their eyes had already adjusted to — but by taking it away. She’d been shielding her own eyes to keep her night vision. This was her solution to problem one. As for problem two—

Ashley’s voice behind her, calm but urgent: “Give me the gun.”

“Can you see her?”

“Give me the gun, baby brother—”

Even downhill, it was like running in deep water. Stumbling over snowdrifts, avoiding trees, tripping, banging a knee against icy rock, catching herself, her heart pounding in her ears, no time to stop, don’t stop—

Ashley’s voice rose: “I see her.”

“How can you see her?”

He kept one eye shut, she realized with growing panic. Just like I taught him—

He shouted after her: “Thanks for the trick, Darbs—”

He was aiming at her right now, taking a shooter's stance. She felt the pistol’s painted sights burning on her back like a laser. Inescapable. No chance to outrun him. Just a few seconds left, now, as Ashley squeezed the trigger and Darby used her desperate solution to problem two—

What’s faster than running?

Falling.

She threw herself downhill.

The world flipped. She saw a swirl of black sky and frozen branches, falling in a quick free-fall, then a wall of bare granite rushed toward her. A huge crash. Stars flashed in her eyes. She lost the flashlight. She rolled on knees and elbows, kicking up snow in a painful tumble—

“Where is she?”

“I see her—”

Ten rolls down, the ground leveled out and she landed hard, dizzy. She stood up fast. Kept moving. Raced through thorny bushes with her hands out, branches breaking against her palms, cutting her skin. Then the ground dropped again, and again she fell—

Their voices fading: “I . . . I lost her.”

“There, there—”

Sliding on her back now. Fir trees rushing past. Right. Left. Right. No stopping this time. The slope kept going, and so did she, sliding over snow, speeding up fast. She raised her arms, trying to slow down, but hit another rock. Another crash knocked the air out of her, throwing her sideways. Up and down meant nothing. Her world became a violent washing machine, a never-ending, crashing mess.

Then it stopped.

It took her a few seconds to realize she’d stopped rolling. She’d landed on her back, her ears ringing, new bruises throbbing on her body. Time blurred. For a moment, she almost passed out.

To her left, a fir tree shook slightly, dropping snow and covering her with wood chips.

Then she heard an echo from uphill — like a whipcrack — and she knew exactly what had happened, and she stood up and kept running.

Ashley blinked away the Beretta’s flash and aimed for a second shot, but he’d lost her in the bushes and rocks. Too many trees.

He lowered the gun. Smoke rising in the air.

“Did you get her?” Lars asked.

“I don’t think so.”

“She’s . . . she’s getting away—”

“It’s fine.” He followed her down the hill, going carefully, finding places to step on the snowy rocks. “We’ll catch her at the bottom.”

“What if she gets back inside and tells Ed—”

“She ran the wrong way.” Ashley pointed downhill with the gun. “See? Dumb bitch is going north. Farther into the woods.”

“Oh.”

“The rest stop is that way. South.”

“Okay.”

“Come on, baby brother.” He put the pistol in his jacket pocket and held out his arms to steady himself, his boots slipping on the stone. He found his flashlight standing in the snow where she’d dropped it.

As he picked it up, he saw something strange in the distance — the white shadow of Melanie’s Peak. The same eastern landmark, hidden in low clouds, but now it was on his right. Not his left.

Which meant south was really . . .

“Oh.” He understood. “Oh, that bitch.”

“What?”

“She . . . she must have turned us around. She’s running back to the building—”

Darby could see the rest area now.

Like a campfire in the dark, pulling her closer with each painful step. The visitor center's warm, yellow window glow, the parked cars, the flagpole, and the half-buried playground monsters—

In the woods behind her, Ashley howled: “Daaaarby.”

No clear words, no feeling you could read — just her name, a shrieking song from the dark. It chilled her blood.

She’d bought herself some time. Not quite ten minutes, but enough to steal the brothers’ Astro (where the keys were still in the door) and try to escape. Fifty-fifty she’d even get out of the flooded parking lot, but hey, that was better than her own Honda, and probably the best chance she’d had all night. She thought about poor little Jay as she ran, and it hit her again like a crushing wave, a swarm of terrible thoughts chasing her, biting with sharp teeth—

Why did I get mixed up in this?

She couldn’t think about it.

This is my fault—

Not now.

Oh, God, I got a kid killed tonight—

She was nearing the parking lot, passing a green sign, when Ashley shouted at her again from the trees, closer now, his voice cracking into an ugly, young pitch: “We’re going to catch you.”

The Astro was now fifty feet away. The snow was thinner in the parking lot, and it gave her more energy; she started a faster, lighter sprint. She passed a blurry shape buried under swept snow — what she’d first thought was Ashley’s car. From this new view, she saw green metal. Lines of rust. A white label. Under the snow, it wasn’t a parked car at all — it was a dumpster.

I should’ve known. I should’ve looked closer—

She kept running, heavy steps, the air burning her throat, her calves burning, her joints aching. The kidnappers’ Astro van getting closer.

She wished she’d never stopped at this stupid rest stop. She wished she’d never left her hometown for college last year. Why can’t I be like my sister Devon? Who was perfectly happy waiting tables at the Cheesecake Factory in Provo? Who vacuumed Mom’s house every Sunday morning? Who had “Strength in Chinese” tattooed on her shoulder blade? Why am I like this? Why do I always go my own way and push people away? Why am I out here, miles from home, running for my life in a frozen parking lot in Colorado, while Mom gets treated for pancreatic cancer—

The Astro van was now thirty feet away.

Twenty.

Ten.

“And when we catch you, you little bitch, I’ll make you beg for that Ziploc bag—”

She hit the Astro’s driver door with her palms. Snow clumps slid off the bumpy glass. Ashley’s lanyard still dangling in the lock, where she’d left it. She opened the door and glanced at the Wanapani building. She could twist the keys in the ignition, right now, and try to escape. And maybe she’d make it. Maybe she wouldn’t.

But that would kill Ed and Sandi.

Thinking ahead, she knew the brothers would then have to kill them both for the keys to Sandi’s truck, so they could chase Darby and kill her on the highway.

No, I can’t leave Ed and Sandi.

I can’t let anyone else die tonight.

She hesitated there, holding the door for balance. Her knees were weak; she almost fell inside. The ignition was right there, close enough to touch. The steering wheel was sticky, patched with duct tape. A crunchy sea of Taco Bell trash on the floor. Lars’s plastic model airplane. The van’s inside was still as warm and damp as a breath, the seats still smelling of sweaty fear, dog blankets, and the pee and vomit of a dead girl.

The ignition was right there.

No. The snow was too deep. She’d seen the highway herself. State Route Seven was buried, hard to see, all useless snow. Four-wheel drive or not, the Astro would get stuck quickly, trapping her on the on-ramp, and then the brothers would catch her and shoot her through the window—

What if it doesn’t?

What if this, right now, is my only chance to get away?

The keys jingled in her right hand. She closed her hand around them. She really wanted to get into the killers’ van, to start the engine, to put it in gear, to just try to drive it, to please just try—

Coming closer: “Daaaaarby—”

Make a choice.

So she did.

She slammed the door. Took Ashley’s keys. And, with the Garver brothers still chasing her somewhere behind, she went around the car on hurting legs and ran toward the orange light of the visitor center. She had to warn Ed and Sandi. She had to do what was right. They’d all get away from the Wanapani rest area together. No one else would die tonight.

Ed and Sandi, I can still save you both.

She had, at most, sixty seconds before Ashley and Lars caught up. Sixty seconds to make a new plan. She looked back at that cartoon fox, at the nail gun in its furry hand, that dumb saying now a scary promise:

WE FINISH WHAT WE START.

Darby froze in the doorway.

Ed was muttering something (“No signal this far from—”) and stopped when he saw her, halfway to Espresso Peak with his Android in his palm. Sandi was kneeling by the table and turned to face Darby, showing a small figure behind her.

It was . . . it was Jay.

Oh, thank God.

The girl’s dark hair was dotted with snowflakes. Her cheeks were bright red. She was wrapped in Sandi’s yellow parka, its sleeves too long for her. This was the first time Darby had seen the girl in good light, not in that dog kennel, and for a moment, she wanted to run to her, to pick up this little child she hardly knew and give her a tight hug.

You turned around.

Oh, thank God, Jay, we lost your tracks but you turned around.

Sandi stood up, holding a black pepper-spray canister in her fist, her eyes hard as stone. “Not one step closer.”

Jay grabbed her wrist. “No. She saved me—”

“Sandi,” Ed hissed. “For Christ’s sake—”

The door slammed shut behind Darby, snapping her back to reality. She tried to guess — how far behind were the brothers? A hundred yards? Fifty? She gasped, tears welling, and stammered, “They’re coming. They have guns, and they’re right behind me—”

Ed knew who she meant. “Are you sure they’re armed?”

“Yes.” She locked the deadbolt.

“With what?”

“They have a gun.”

“Did you see it?”

“Trust me, they have a gun.” Darby looked from Ed to Sandi, now seeing the deadbolt was useless. “And they won’t quit until we’re dead. We need to take your truck and go. Now.”

“What if they follow us?” Sandi asked.

“They won’t.” Darby held up Ashley’s keys.

Ed stopped pacing behind her, considering this. He seemed to like it.

Darby noticed the former vet held a lug wrench in his right hand, partly hidden under his Carhartt sleeve. A heavy weapon. He walked past her, wiping sweat from his brow. “Okay. Okay, Darby, keep your Honda keys with you, too. We don’t want them stealing your car and following us—”

Jay stood up. “Let’s go, then.”

Darby liked her already.

And she saw a yellow bracelet shining on Jay’s wrist. She hadn’t seen it before in the dim darkness of the kidnappers’ van. It looked a little like a medical alert. She wondered for a second — what is that?

No time to ask. Everyone went to the front door, and Ed quickly unlocked the deadbolt. He gathered the group, like a hesitant coach. “On three, we’ll, uh . . . we’re all going to run to the truck. Okay?”

Darby nodded, smelling vodka on his breath. “Sounds good.”

“Are they out there?”

Sandi looked out the dirty window. “I . . . I don’t see them yet.”

“Alright. Sandi, take Jay to the front seat and start the engine. Step on the gas and drive, reverse, drive, reverse—”

“I know how to drive in the snow, Eddie.”

“And Dara, you’re at the back tires with me, so we can push.”

“Deal.”

He pointed at Jay, snapping his fingers: “And somebody carry her.”

Sandi lifted the girl onto her shoulder, despite her complaints (“No, I can run, too.”) and checked the window again. “They’ll get here any minute—”

“Don’t try to fight them. Just run fast,” Ed whispered, leaning against the door, starting the count: “One.”

Run fast.

Darby lowered into a shaky crouch, ready to run at the back of the group, behind Sandi, feeling her tired calves ache. No weapons — they would only slow her down. She remembered it was fifty feet from the door to the parking lot, over a narrow path in the snow.

“Two.” Ed twisted the doorknob.

She pictured the next minute in her mind. She figured the four of them could run fifty feet in maybe . . . twenty seconds? Thirty? Another ten seconds to get into the truck, for Sandi to get the keys in the ignition. More time for the Ford to start moving, struggling through the thick snow. And that was if Ed and Darby didn’t need to push it. Or clear the tires. Or wipe the windows.

And somehow, deep down, she knew: It’s been too long.

Ashley and Lars were right behind me.

They’re back already—

“Three.” Ed opened the door—

Darby grabbed his wrist hard, digging in her nails. “Stop—”

“What are you doing?”

“Stop-stop-stop,” she said, her chest tightening with panic. “They’re already here. They’re hiding behind the cars. They’re out there waiting for us—”

“How do you know?”

“I just do.”

“I see Lars,” Sandi whispered from the window, her hands cupped against the glass. “He’s . . . he’s crouched out there. Behind my truck.”

Clever bastards.

“I see him, too,” said Ed.

Darby re-locked the deadbolt. “They were going to ambush us.”

It would have been bad. The brothers could’ve shot them all down, catching them single-file on that narrow path with nowhere to run. Target practice. It gave Darby a sickly shot of adrenaline, as sour as tequila — they’d been one bad choice away from being murdered. Her gut feeling had just saved their lives.

Clever, clever, clever.

“How did you know?” Ed asked her again.

“It’s . . . it’s what I would’ve done.” Darby shrugged. “If I were them.”

Jay smiled. “I’m glad you’re not.”

“I think I see Ashley, too,” Sandi said. “Behind the van.”

Darby pictured Ashley Garver outside in the cold, low in the snow, his green eyes fixed on the door. She hoped he was upset. She hoped he was now realizing that his mean little trick had failed, that she had outsmarted him for the third or fourth time tonight. She hoped he was keeping track. She hoped the so-called magic man was getting angry.

Sandi peered through the glass. “I can’t . . . I can’t see what they’re doing—”

“They’re watching the cars,” Darby said.

Ashley’s words came back to her, like bits of a bad dream: We’re going to catch you. And when we do, you little bitch, I’ll make you beg for that Ziploc bag—

At the window, Ed pulled Sandi’s shoulder. “Stay down.”

“I see them. They’re moving—”

“Stay away from the damn window, Sandi. They’re going to shoot you.”

Darby bit her lip, knowing Ed was right — the glass was a weak spot. A bullet, or even a big rock, and the two brothers could climb the snow and get inside.

She stood in the middle of the room, lit up by bright lights, running her fingers along the table’s rough top. She turned all the way around, looking from east, to north, to west, to south. Four walls on a cement base. A front door with a lock. One big window. And two smaller ones, in each bathroom.

We have the building.

But they have the cars.

“It’s a stalemate,” she whispered.

Sandi looked at her. “Then what happens next?”

“They’ll make their move,” Ed said grimly. “Then we’ll make ours.”

Each move would be a calculated risk. If they stepped outside, they’d be shot. If the brothers attacked the building, they’d be leaving the cars unguarded. If one brother attacked, he’d be open to an ambush up close. The possibilities and consequences made Darby’s head spin, like trying to think six moves ahead in chess.

She realized Jay had moved to her side and now held her hoodie sleeve, gripping the fabric in white knuckles. “Don’t believe Ashley. He lies for fun. He’ll say anything to get in here—”

“We won’t fall for it,” Darby said, glancing to Ed and Sandi for support. They offered only weary silence. Maybe stalemate was the wrong word, she realized in the growing tension. Maybe a better one was siege.

And she realized something else — everyone was now looking at her.

She hated it. She wasn’t a leader. She’d never been comfortable as the center of attention — she’d practically had a panic attack last year when the Red Robin servers crowded her table to sing happy birthday. Again, she found herself desperately wishing for someone else in her place. Someone smarter, tougher, braver, who everyone could turn to. But there wasn’t.

There’s only me.

And us.

And the monsters circling outside.

“And never insult Ashley, either,” Jay warned. “He . . . he seems okay with it at first, but he remembers. And he gets even if you hurt his feelings—”

“Trust me, Jay. Tonight, we’re way beyond hurt feelings.” Darby emptied her pockets, putting Ashley’s keychain, her Honda keys, and her iPhone on the counter. Then she unfolded the brown napkin, showing her handwritten message to Ashley, and his message to her: IF YOU TELL THEM I KILL THEM BOTH.

Ed read it and his shoulders dropped.

Sandi gasped, covering her mouth.

“When . . . when they realize we’re not running to the truck,” Darby said to everyone, “they’re going to change their plans and come for us. They have no choice, because we’ve all seen too much, and we have their hostage. So this building is going to be our last stand. For the next four hours.”

She pulled the last thing from her pocket — she’d almost forgotten it — and put it on the fake granite counter with a loud click. It was Lars’s .45-caliber bullet, shining gold in the bright light.

Seeing the bullet made Sandi fall into her seat, hiding her red face in her hands. “Oh, Jesus Christ. We are not going to last four minutes—”

Darby ignored her. “First, we need to block the window.”

“Alright.” Ed pointed. “Help me flip that table.”

Ashley watched the window darken.

A large shape moved against the glass from the inside, turning upward, making the orange light only shine through cracks. He imagined the glass groaning under the pressure.

“Oh, Darbs.” He spat in the snow. “I love you.”

Lars looked over at him. He was carefully crouched in a shooting position by the Ford’s tailgate, his elbow on the bumper, his Beretta aimed at the front door.

“Don’t bother,” Ashley said. “They’re not coming out. She warned them about the ambush.”

“How?”

“She just did.” He stood up and walked a few steps, cracking his sore back, stretching his legs, breathing in the mountain air. “Jesus, isn’t she something? I just . . . I just love that little redhead.”

Set against a vertical world of fir trees, white spruce, and rocky peaks, the Wanapani visitor center looked like a nut to be cracked. The snowfall had stopped; the sky had opened up to a clear emptiness. The clouds thinning out, showing a pale crescent and bright stars, and the world had changed with it, pulled into the sharp shadows of new moonlight. A moon begging for blood.

The fun, as always, was deciding how. He’d gone through dozens of Lars’s pets — turtles, fish, two dogs, more rescue cats than he could count — and whether it was bleach, bullets, fire, or the sharp sound of a knife hitting bone, there’s no dignity in death. Every living thing dies afraid.

For all her cleverness, Darby would learn this, too.

Ashley stood silent for a long moment, chewing on his lower lip. Finally, he decided. “Change of plans,” he said. “We’ll do it inside.”

“All of them?”

“Yes, baby brother. All of them.”

“Weapons,” Darby said. “What do we have?”

“My pepper spray.”

“What else?”

Sandi pointed to Espresso Peak. “Well, there’s a coffee kitchen there, but it’s locked—”

“Hang on.” Ed crossed the room. “Let me try my key.”

“A key? Where’d you get a—”

He smashed the padlock with his lug wrench, sending pieces flying across the floor. Then he grabbed the security shutter by the handle and rolled it up to the ceiling. “Espresso Peak is open for Christmas.”

Darby jumped over the counter, landing hard on her sore ankles, and searched the front area — coffee machines, a bagel toaster, a cash register, syrup bottles. Then she opened the drawers, starting at the bottom and going up. Bagged coffee beans, vanilla, powdered milk, jingling spoons—

“Anything?”

“Nothing useful.”

Ed checked the back. “No landline phone, either.”

“There has to be one.” Darby searched the next set of drawers, pulling off a yellow Post-It note: REMINDER, PLEASE MOP RESTROOMS — TODD.

“Any knives?”

“Spoons, spoons.” She slammed another drawer. “Nothing but spoons.”

“What kind of coffee shop doesn’t have knives?”

“This one, it seems.” Darby wiped sweat from her eyes, looking back at the cash register (too heavy), the pastry case (not a weapon), the toaster (nope), and the coffee machines along the counter. “But . . . okay, these things will pour out really hot water. Someone, please, fill a carafe.”

“As a weapon?” Sandi asked.

“No. For coffee, damn it.”

“We already have coffee.”

“I was being sarcastic.”

Light footsteps behind her — she thought Sandi would come closer — but it was Jay. The little girl held the COFFEE carafe and put it under the spout. She stood on her toes to push the button. The machine rumbled.

“Thanks, Jay.”

“No problem.”

Sandi was still at the front of the room. She was on her knees, looking outside through a small gap between the upside-down table and the window frame. “Ashley and Lars just moved again,” she said. “They’re . . . they’re by their van now.”

“Doing what?”

“I can’t tell you.”

“Keep your head down,” Ed reminded her.

“It’s fine.”

Darby opened the last drawer under the cash register and found something rattling at the bottom with pens and receipt paper — a silver key. She picked it up, peeling off another Post-It note: DON’T COPY — TODD.

The closet, she remembered.

She raced to it, put the key in, and twisted the knob. “Please, please, God, let there be a phone in here—”

Darkness inside. She flipped a light switch — showing a small janitor’s closet, five feet by five, with crooked shelves and stacks of droopy cardboard boxes. The stale smell of mold. A mop bucket in the corner, filled with dirty water. And a white first-aid box on the top shelf, covered in dust.

And, to her left, attached to the wall . . . a beige landline telephone.

“Oh, thank God—”

She grabbed the plastic receiver and mashed it to her ear — no dial tone. She tried pressing buttons. Shook it. Checked the spiral cord. Nothing.

“Any luck?” Ed asked.

She noticed another Post-It note on the wall — FIBER LINE DOWN AGAIN — TODD — and slammed the phone down. “I’m really starting to hate Todd.”

“Hot water’s full,” Jay called out.

Darby backpedaled out of the closet, nearly bumping into Ed, and grabbed the carafe off the drip tray. “Thanks, Jay. Now fill another, please.”

“Okay.”

Then she carried the sloshing carafe to the visitor center’s front door, feeling the steam on her palm. The water was hot enough to burn skin, and to maybe temporarily blind an attacker. But it was also rapidly cooling. In a few minutes, it would just be a harmless jug of warm water.

She was halfway there when she noticed something — a brown napkin crammed under the carafe’s silver carrying handle.

Her napkin.

She halted and unfolded it. On one side, her MEET ME IN THE RESTROOM and Ashley’s probably-false response: I HAVE A GIRLFRIEND. On the other, IF YOU TELL THEM I KILL THEM BOTH. And finally, underneath that, in the loopy handwriting of a child, she found Jay’s message to her.

DON’T TRUST THEM.

What?

She glanced up. Jay was filling the second coffee pot, holding the red button, but watching her expectantly.

Darby whispered, “Don’t . . . don’t trust who?”

Ed and Sandi?

Jay didn’t answer. She just nodded quickly. Hiding the movement from the other two adults in the room.

Darby almost asked out loud, but couldn’t.

Why? Why can’t we trust Ed and—

A rough hand slapped her collarbone, startling her. “Three entrances, so three ways Beavis and Butthead could attack,” Ed said, counting on his fingers. “Front door.”

“Dead-bolted,” Darby said.

“Front window.”

“Barricaded.”

“Restroom windows?”

“There’s two. I broke one earlier to get in.” Her shoulders sagged. “That’s what I’m worried about.”

She wasn’t just worried, she was sure Ashley and Lars would try that way first. The picnic tables outside made steps up to the broken restroom window. It was another weak spot, and Ashley knew it was there. It had saved Darby’s life twice tonight.

Ed was still thinking about this, and she smelled that odor on his breath again—vodka, or gin, maybe. Please, she thought. Please, don’t be drunk.

“Can they get through it?” he asked.

“They’ll try.”

“We don’t have much to block it with—”

“Maybe . . .” Darby thought, looking at the lug wrench in Ed’s hand. She remembered Sandi’s pepper spray and the hot water carafes. She ran to the restrooms, mind racing: “Maybe we’ll use that to help us.”

“How so?”

She pushed the door open with her elbow and pointed down the long room, past the green stalls, at the empty triangular window on the far wall. “Ashley and Lars will have to crawl through, one at a time, to get inside to us. They can’t go feet-first. They’ll have to go head-first, so they can cover the room with their gun, and then they’ll have to twist around and drop down to land on their feet.”

Ed looked at her, impressed. “And you climbed that?”

“Here’s my plan. One of us will . . .” Darby stopped, remembering her conversation in this very same restroom, under the same buzzing lights, with Ashley himself. Two hours ago, they’d argued over who would be Person A (the attacker) and who would be Person B (the backup). From now on tonight, she decided with a held breath, I’m Person A.

No more excuses.

“Dara?”

“I’ll press myself flat against the wall,” she continued, pointing at the furthest stall. “Right in that corner there, and they won’t see me when they climb inside, and—”

Ed grinned. “We can pepper spray him.”

“And take his gun.”

And kill them both.

The brothers had weapons and were stronger, so letting one or both in would be deadly. But this window was a natural choke point, and their only real way in, unless they broke the deadbolt or got through the blocked window. Darby knew if Ashley went in first with the gun, she had a good chance of beating him with pepper spray or hot water. If she stole their .45, things would really change.

Ed opened the stall door. “I’ll guard the window.”

“No. I’m doing this.”

“Dara, I should—”

“I said I’m doing it,” she snapped. “I’m the only one small enough to hide here. And I started this.”

I will never be Person B again.

Not as long as I live.

She thought he’d argue more, but Ed just stared. She almost corrected him about her name, once and for all. But she didn’t, because tonight, Dara was close enough. And she was glad she didn’t have to mention the alcohol on his breath.

Maybe . . . maybe that’s why Jay doesn’t trust you?

He paused. “So, you found Jay?”

“Yeah. I got her out.”

“And they were traveling with her? Parked outside, right under our noses, while I played Go Fish with the dirtbag?”

“Yeah.”

“Jesus Christ. You’re . . . you know you’re a hero, Dara—”

“Not yet.” She winced, looking at the floor, fighting a sick chill. She’d grown to hate that word more and more each hour. “And not even close. Not if I get you and your cousin killed tonight—”

“You won’t,” Ed said. “Hey. Look at me.”

She looked up, unwillingly.

“Some advice for you,” he said. “Do you know what they tell you first in the Clairmont rehab center? When you first walk in, check in your things, sign the papers, and sit down?”

She shook her head.

“Me neither.” He smiled. “But I’ll tell you, okay?”

She laughed.

It didn’t make her feel any better. But she pretended it had, like a quick pep talk in a restroom was all she needed. She smiled, letting her scar show on her eyebrow. “I’ll hold you to it, Ed.”

“You bet.”

As he went back to the lobby, she felt something still stuck in her right pocket — Ashley’s keychain. She pulled it out and looked at it, fanning the keys in her palm. A black USB drive. A key to a storage unit place called Sentry Storage, and, the most important, the key to the kidnappers’ Chevrolet Astro.

Then she closed her hand around them, and before she could change her mind, threw them out the window. A soft thump as they landed outside.

Call it a peace offering.

A chance for Ashley and Lars to give up, take their Astro van and try to escape before the sun came up. Before the snowplows came. Before the cops came in with their guns drawn.

Take your keys, she wanted to shout.

No one has to die tonight.

Please, just take your keys, Ashley, and we’ll all go our separate ways.

It was a nice dream. But she felt sure this fight would end in bloodshed. The Garver brothers had too much to lose to just walk away. She'd already sat with Ashley tonight, looked him in the eye, and seen his cold ruthlessness. Like light through a jewel. A young man who saw people as just things. Nothing more.

And the darkest hour was coming. A time of evil, of demons, of things that crawl in the dark. Just superstition, but Darby shivered as she typed another text.

Hey, Mom. If you find this on my phone . . .

She paused.

. . . I want you to know I didn’t stop trying. I didn’t give in. I’m not helpless. I chose to be here. I’m sorry, but I had to. Please know I always loved you, Mom, and I’ll always be your little girl. And tonight I died fighting to save someone else.

Love, Darby.

On her way back into the lobby, she folded Jay’s puzzling little DON’T TRUST THEM napkin and tucked it in her back pocket.

Why? She wondered, a sore pit growing in her stomach.

Why shouldn’t I trust Ed and Sandi?

She wanted to ask the girl, but Ed was too close. “Jay, did those jerks say where they were driving you?” he asked. “Before they got stuck up here on the pass, I mean?”

“No.” Jay shook her head. “They’re here on purpose.”

“What?”

“They were looking for this rest stop. They were looking at maps today on the road, finding it—”

“Why?”

“I don’t know,” she said. “I just know they wanted to be here.”

Tonight, Darby thought, tying her hair up into a ponytail. Another piece of the puzzle missing. Another unsolved bit. It made her stomach hurt. She couldn’t imagine why Ashley and Lars would want to trap themselves up here with their hostage, at nine thousand feet, among a few travelers.

Unless they’d planned to kill everyone here from the start? The murderous brothers had been traveling with a handgun, five gallons of gasoline, and a jug of bleach. Maybe Ashley had something evil in mind. As she thought about this, Ed asked Jay something else that caught her attention: “Did they take your meds? When they took you?”

Darby’s ears perked up. Meds?

Jay wrinkled her nose. “My shots?”

“Yeah. Meds, shots, pens. Whatever your parents called them.”

“I don’t think so.”

“Okay.” He sighed, pushing his thinning hair back. “Then, tell me, Jay. How . . . how long have you been without them?”

“I keep one in my pocket for emergencies, but I used it.” She counted on her fingers. “So three . . . no, four days.”

Ed exhaled, like he’d been punched in the gut. “Wow. Alright.”

“I’m sorry—”

“No. It’s not your fault.”

Darby grabbed his elbow. “What’s this about?”

“It seems . . . well, she has Addison’s.” Ed lowered his voice and pointed at Jay’s yellow bracelet. “Addison’s disease. It’s a condition where the adrenal glands don't make enough cortisol for your body to work right. It’s rare, only one in about forty thousand people have it. She has to take medicine every day, or her blood sugar drops and you . . .” He stopped himself.

Darby touched Jay’s wrist and read the bracelet: ADDISON’S DISEASE/STEROID DEPENDENT. She turned it over, hoping for more information, like how much medicine to give her, a doctor’s number, or what to do in an emergency — but there was nothing else. Just those four words.

Steroid dependent.

“So, what happened?” Darby asked. “Ashley didn’t know how to give her the medicine?”

“They’ve been giving her the wrong medicine, I think. They probably Googled it, then stole something from a drug store that said steroid on it. It just made her worse—”

“I thought you said you were a veterinarian.”

“I am.” Ed forced a smile. “Dogs get Addison’s, too.”

She remembered the sharp smell of vomit in Lars’s van. Jay’s shaking, her tiredness, her pale skin. It all made sense now. And Darby wondered — if you need a steroid shot every day, how dangerous is it to miss four?

To Ed, she mouthed: How bad is it?

He mouthed back: Later.

“Ashley and Lars are still by their van,” Sandi called from the window. “They’re . . . they’re doing something. I just can’t tell what—”

“Getting ready to attack,” Darby said. No need to soften it.

She paced the room, counting weapons. Two carafes of hot water. Sandi’s pepper spray. Ed’s lug wrench.

It was a quick plan, but it made sense. When they attacked, Sandi would watch the locked front door with Jay, blocking it and calling out where the attackers were. Darby would guard the men’s room window. If the brothers tried to get in there, like she thought, she’d surprise Lars or Ashley with hot water from the corner they couldn’t see. And Ed, with his wrench, would move around to help wherever he was needed.

“What’s . . .” Sandi wiped the glass, looking hard outside. “It’s been ten minutes. Why haven’t they tried to get in yet?”

“To mess with us,” Darby guessed. “To make us nervous.”

“It’s working.”

In the quiet building, her ears started to ring. The air felt heavy. The ceiling felt lower. The floor was bare, marked with napkins and mop streaks. Somehow, moving the table had made the room feel smaller. The air was stuffy, full of old air and sweat.

Darby kept waiting for someone to make a joke to ease the tension.

No one did.

On the long drive from Boulder, she’d hated the quiet moments between songs, because that’s when her mind raced. Remembering things she’d called her mother. New pains. New regrets. And now she rethought Ed’s answer to her question, when she’d asked how serious Jay’s four missed injections were. He hadn’t mouthed later.

No, she realized with a sinking heart. He’d said something different.

He’d said fatal.

Jay would die if she stayed under Ashley and Lars’s care tonight. Even if they hadn’t planned to murder her, they still didn’t know how to handle her adrenal condition. And her time was running out.

But really, it made perfect sense that the Garver brothers would turn out to be tragically unskilled kidnappers. Ashley may have been incredibly cruel, but he clearly wasn’t organized enough to run a ransom operation. He improvised too much, and he played with his victims. And Lars? Just a whiskered man-child, a soft and immature mind Ashley had molded into his own twisted image. These two overgrown kids were unprepared for the difficulty and size of what they were trying to do. They weren’t even close to qualified. They were something far worse.

In a dark Walmart parking lot a few years back, watching a crackhead with a buzz-cut break into their Subaru from the safe lights of the Home and Garden section, she remembered her mother holding her shoulder and telling her: Don’t fear the pros, Darby. The pros know what they’re doing, and do it cleanly.

Fear the amateurs.

“They’re . . .” Sandi cupped her hands against the window. “Okay. Ashley just carried something out of his van. An . . . orange box.”

Ed knelt to Jay. “When they come, go behind the counter. Close your eyes. No matter what, don’t come out. Got it?”

The girl nodded. “Okay.”

Darby mouthed to Ed over Jay’s head: What do we do with her?

“We . . . we get her to a hospital. That’s all we can do,” he whispered, leaning close. “I’ve only seen this in dogs, and not even often. I just know she’s in shock now. Her body isn’t making adrenaline — it’s called an Addisonian crisis — so if she gets scared or stressed, she could have a seizure, go into a coma, or worse. We need to keep her calm. And keep things as quiet and peaceful as we can—”

Sandi gasped from the window. “Ashley’s got a . . . oh God, is that a nail gun?”

“Yeah,” Darby said, turning back to Ed. “Not going to happen.”

Ashley clicked a battery into his Paslode IMCT cordless nailer and waited for the little green light.

Back when his father ran things (the best years of Fox Contracting), you needed an air compressor and a lot of hose to power a nail gun. Now it was all batteries and fuel cells — stuff you can carry in your pocket.

Ashley’s model was bright orange, like something from Sesame Street. Sixteen pounds. The Paslode sticker was worn off. Nails fed from a round magazine, which always made Ashley think of the drum on John Dillinger’s tommy gun. The nails’ lengths are measured in pennies, for some dumb old reason, and these were 16-penny nails, about three-and-a-half inches long. Made to go into 2x4 wood. They can go through human flesh up to ten feet away, and even farther, they’re still spinning bits of sharp metal, flying through the air at nine hundred feet per second.

Cool, right?

Ashley wasn't great at running Fox Contracting day to day, but he sure loved the perks. Luckily, his father was now too busy forgetting who he was and using a colostomy bag to notice what Ashley had done to the family business. Both specialists were abruptly laid off, the website was down, and the phone still rang sometimes but went straight to voicemail. Driving the Fox Contracting van with the faded cartoon on the side sometimes felt like driving a big corpse, a dried-out reminder of his father’s dreams and hard work.

See, when Wall Street messed up, the government stepped in and saved them with taxpayer money. When your small family business fails, you have to take matters into your own hands. It’s the American way.

Ashley picked up the nail gun and pressed the safety tip with his left hand, easily disabling it. Then he squeezed the trigger...

THWUMP.

A big nail went through the front tire of Darby’s Honda. The tire went flat with a hiss.

Lars watched.

Ashley kicked the tire, feeling it get softer. Then he leaned over and fired another — THWUMP — into the Honda’s back tire.

“Don’t be nervous, baby brother. We’ll figure this out.” Ashley walked around the car and punctured the other tires as he spoke — THWUMP, THWUMP. “Just some dirty work tonight, and then we’ll go see Uncle Kenny. Okay?”

“Okay.”

His voice lowered, like he was sharing a dangerous secret: “And something else I forgot to mention. Remember his Xbox One?”

“Yeah?”

“He has the newest Gears of War.”

“Okay.” Lars’s smile grew wider, and Ashley felt a pang of sympathy for his dear baby brother. He wasn’t suited for this, but that wasn’t his fault. How could it be? He’d had no say in whether his mother drank heavily every day while she was pregnant with him. Poor Lars had been dealt a bad hand before he was even born. The worst of bad luck.

Quickly, he double-checked the light on his nail gun — still green. Cold weather was known to drain their batteries, and he only had two. The last thing he’d need would be for his nailer to run out of power when he had it pressed to Darby’s head. How embarrassing would that be.

In terms of pure power, Lars’s .45-caliber Beretta Cougar was the clear winner — you don’t go into a gunfight with a cordless nailer and expect to win. And it would take many three-and-a-half-inch nails to reliably kill someone. Worse, the nails themselves rarely go through anything beyond ten feet. But Ashley Garver loved the nailer, he guessed, for all the things that made it a very impractical weapon for killing. He loved it because it was heavy, awkward, inaccurate, scary, and brutal.

All artists show themselves through their tools, right?

This was Ashley’s.

“Come on, baby brother.” He pointed with his nailer. “Get ready for war.”

The Paslode’s cylinder held thirty-five 16-penny nails, loaded in little five-nail strips. He’d fired four. He still had more than enough to turn a human into a screaming mess. Walking beside him, Lars pulled back the slide on the Beretta like he’d been taught, carefully checking to make sure a bullet was ready.

“Gears of War 4, right?” he asked as they walked. “Not the one from last year?”

“That’s what I said.”

“Okay.”

“And don’t you even think about shooting Darby,” Ashley reminded him. “She’s mine.”

“They’re coming.”

“I know.”

“Now they have a nail gun—”

“I know, Sandi.”

Jay held her head like she was fighting off a headache, rocking against the overturned table legs. “Please, please, don’t argue—”

“Ed, they’re going to kill us—”

He pointed his lug wrench at her. “Shut up.”

Darby took the child by the shoulders and pulled her back, away from the blocked window, toward the middle of the Wanapani lobby. Any stress could cause a seizure. This is truly life and death. I have to keep her calm.

Was that even possible tonight? She tried to recall the exact words Ed had used — an Addisonian crisis? — and she crouched down to Jay. “Hey. Jay. Look at me.”

She did, her eyes full of tears.

“Jaybird, it’ll be okay.”

“No, it won’t—”

“They won’t hurt you,” Darby said. “I promise, I won’t let them.”

By the door, the argument grew louder: “Ed, they’re going to get inside—”

“Then we’ll fight them.”

“You’re just drunk. If we try to fight them, we will die.” Sandi’s voice shook. “I will die, you will die, and she will die—”

“She’s wrong.” Darby pulled Jay further back, behind the coffee counter. She patted the stones — strong enough to stop a bullet. “But stay behind this counter, like Ed said, okay? Just in case.”

“They won’t hurt me,” Jay whispered. “They’ll hurt you.”

“Don’t worry about me.” She remembered the girl’s strange message on the napkin and moved closer, whispering so the others wouldn’t hear: “But tell me. Why don’t you want me to trust Ed and Sandi?”

Jay looked ashamed. “I . . . no, it’s nothing.”

“Why, Jay?”

“I was wrong. It’s nothing—”

“Tell me.”

At the front door, Ed and Sandi’s argument became a screaming fight. He held the wrench at his cousin, waving it like a weapon, his voice now booming: “If we cooperate, they’ll kill us anyway.”

She slapped it away. “It’s our only chance—”

“I thought . . .” Jay paused, pointing at Sandi over the counter, finally answering: “I thought, at first, I knew that woman. She looks just like one of my school bus drivers.”

All the way in San Diego.

Darby’s world stopped.

“But that’s impossible,” Jay said. “Right?”

She didn’t know what to say. How likely was that? How likely was it that two other people came from the same West Coast city as the missing child? Of all places? Here, hundreds of miles inland, stuck at a lonely highway rest stop in the Rockies?

The room felt empty of air.

San Diego.

“But . . . but, it’s not her,” Jay added fast, holding her wrist. “She just looks like her. It’s just a fluke.”

No, it isn’t, Darby wanted to say. Not tonight.

Tonight, nothing is a fluke—

By the front door, Ed and Sandi had stopped arguing. They were both listening now, standing frozen. Then Darby heard it, too — soft footsteps, boots crunching in the snow outside, coming to the door. Two people coming to kill them.

Ed backed away from the door, red-faced. “Oh, Jesus. Everyone get ready—”

“Ed,” Darby said. “Where did you say you guys are from?”

“Not now—”

“Answer the question, please.”

He pointed. “They’re right outside the door—”

“Answer the goddamn question, Ed.”

The brothers’ footsteps stopped outside. They’d heard Darby raise her voice and now they were listening, too. Ashley was close by, waiting on the other side of the thin wooden door. She even heard Rodent Face’s familiar heavy breathing outside, like a machine.

“We . . . we drove from California,” Ed answered. “Why?”

“What city?”

“What?”

“Tell me the city you’re from.”

“Why does that matter?”

“Answer me.” Darby’s voice trembled with adrenaline, with two strangers inside and two killers outside. They were listening, too. Everyone was listening. Everything depended on what this ex-veterinarian said next—

“Carlsbad,” Ed said. “We’re from Carlsbad.”

Not San Diego.

Darby blinked. Oh, thank God.

He threw up his arms. “There, Dara. Are you happy?”

She exhaled, like emptying her lungs after coming up from underwater. It was just a coincidence. Jay had been wrong. It’s easy to mix up faces among strangers you barely remember, and Sandi apparently had a look-alike in San Diego with a morning bus route. California was a huge place, so it wasn’t surprising that Ed and Sandi happened to be from the same state as the kidnapped girl. Everything else was just nerves, just paranoia.

Silence outside. The brothers were still listening through the door.

“I told you,” Jay whispered. “See? I was wrong—”

“Carlsbad,” Ed hissed to Darby, his face shining with sweat. “Carlsbad, USA. What else do you need? State? California. Zip code? 92018. Population? A hundred thousand—”

“Sorry, Ed. I just had to be sure—”

She felt Sandi move up behind her, and she was turning to face the older woman when Ed continued — “County? San Diego county” — and that was the last clear thought in Darby’s mind before a burst of icy liquid shot into her eyes.

Then pain.

Burning pain.
