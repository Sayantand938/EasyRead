No Exit

Chapter 6

10:41 p.m.

Darby hadn’t seen her father in eleven years, but two years ago, as a high school graduation gift, he’d mailed her a multitool. The funny part? The drugstore card congratulated her for graduating college.

Oops, huh?

But as gifts go, it wasn’t bad. It was one of those red Swiss Army types that unfolded in a fan — corkscrew, clippers, nail file. And of course, a two-inch serrated blade. She’d only used it once, to help open the hard plastic package her roommate’s new ear buds came in, and then she’d forgotten about it for the rest of her college days. She kept it in Blue’s glove box.

It was in her back pocket now. Like a prison knife.

She was seated on the stone coffee counter, her back against the security shutter, her knees tucked up to her chest. From here she could watch the entire room — Ed and Ashley finishing their millionth game of Go Fish, Sandi reading her paperback, and Lars guarding the door in his usual spot.

From the back seat of her Honda outside, under her sheets of rice paper for gravestone rubbings, she’d also grabbed a blue pen and one of her college notepads. It was in her lap now.

Page one was doodles. Simple shapes, shaded lines.

Page two — more doodles.

Page three? Carefully hidden, Darby had sketched maybe her best drawing ever of a human face. It was almost perfect. She’d studied Lars, every inch of him. His blonde whiskers, his slight overbite, his soft chin and slanted forehead. The clear V-shape of his hairline. She’d even captured the dull look in his eyes. The police would find it useful; maybe they’d even show it to the media to help the coming manhunt. She also had the van’s make, model, and license plate. Plus a blurry photo of the missing San Diego girl. It would look great on CNN, blown up on big screens across the country.

But was it enough?

Driving was impossible now, but tomorrow morning, when the snowplows came and opened Backbone Pass, Lars would take Jay and leave. Even if Darby called 9-1-1 right away, the police would only know their last location. Maybe he’d get caught, but maybe not. He’d have plenty of time to get away, to disappear back into the world, and that would be a death sentence for seven-year-old Jay Nissen. Jaybird Nissen. Whatever her name was.

The map on the wall showed State Route Seven crossed two other highways near the pass. Plus a major interstate going north. Whether Lars drove east or west, he’d have many ways to escape. She also saw that the Wanapa (Little Devil) rest stop was twenty miles downhill. This one, where they were stuck, was Wanapani. She’d read the map wrong earlier. They were twenty miles farther from civilization.

In Paiute, Wanapani meant Big Devil.

Of course it did.

Darby still had the bullet in her pocket. She’d looked at it under the lights in the women’s room. The bullet’s tip was split with four cuts, which looked like they were made on purpose, for some reason. The bottom, the brass part, had letters: .45 lic. She’d heard of guns called forty-fives in movies. But it was scary to think there was a real one here, in the room with her, under Lars’s jacket. Just a few feet away.

Darby had known this in her gut for an hour, but now her mind agreed. A description and a blurry photo wouldn’t be enough. It would be enough for the news to call her a hero if things went well, but it wasn’t enough to save Jay.

And if the cops never found Lars, what would she tell Jay’s parents? Sorry your kid is dead, but I called the police and wrote down the license plate and followed the rules. I even drew a picture.

No, she needed to do something.

Here. Tonight. In this snowy rest area. Before the plows came at dawn, she needed to stop Lars herself.

Somehow.

That was the extent of her plan.

She sipped her coffee. It was her third cup, bitter and black as night. She had always loved her stimulants — espresso shots, Red Bull, Full Throttle, Rockstar. No-Doze pills. Her roommate’s Adderall. Anything for that addictive little buzz. Pure rocket fuel for her paintings and oil pastels. Depressants — alcohol, weed — were the enemy. Darby preferred to live wide-eyed, tormented, running, because nothing can catch you if you never stop. And thank God for caffeine’s acidic little kick. Tonight, of all nights, she needed to stay sharp.

Above the regional map, she saw an old analog clock. It was Garfield-themed. In the center, Garfield wooed Arlene, the pink female character, with a handful of badly drawn flowers. The clock’s hour hand showed it was almost midnight, but she realized it was an hour fast. Someone had forgotten to change it for daylight savings.

It wasn’t even eleven yet.

She wasn’t sure which was worse — running out of time, or having too much. As she finished her sketch (shading the lumpy slope of his forehead, which reminded her of a human fetus) she noticed Lars was finally getting comfortable with the others. There was more of a group dynamic now. Ashley was showing Lars and Ed a card trick called a Mexican turnover. Darby overheard that you flip over a card using another one in your hand — but really, you’re switching them. Right in front of you. Lars was fascinated, and Ashley seemed happy to have an audience.

“So that’s why you keep winning,” Ed said.

“Don’t worry.” Ashley grinned like a con man, hands up. “I beat you fair and square. But yes, I did take silver in a stage magic competition once.”

Ed snorted. “Yeah?”

“Yeah.”

“That’s a thing?”

“Of course it’s a thing.”

“Second grade?”

“Third, actually.” Ashley shuffled cards. “Thank you very much.”

“Did you wear a little tux?”

“You have to.”

“How’s the job market for second-best magicians?”

“Really bad.” Ashley put away the cards with a rattlesnake sound. “So I went to school for accounting. And let me tell you, that’s where the real magic is.”

Ed laughed.

Lars had been listening, his hairy lips tight, and now he used this pause to jump in: “So, were the . . . ah, were the magic tricks real?”

The blizzard raged outside. The window creaked as the wind hammered it. Ashley looked at Ed, smirking for a second (Is magic real? Really?), and Darby watched him weigh whether to be serious or tease the armed child abductor.

Don’t do it, Ashley.

He turned back to Lars. “Yep.”

“Really?”

Ashley grinned wider. “Absolutely.”

A cold dread grew in her stomach. Like watching a car crash about to happen. The screech of brakes, the unstoppable force of motion: Stop it, Ashley. You don’t know who you’re talking to—

“So it’s real?” Lars whispered.

Stop-stop-stop—

“Oh, it’s all real,” Ashley said, drawing it out now. “I can bend time and space, pull surprises out of nowhere, make people misremember. I can cheat death. I can dodge bullets. I’m a magic man, Lars, my brother, and I can—”

“Do you know how to cut a girl in half?” Lars asked suddenly.

The room went quiet. The window creaked under another howl of wind.

Darby glanced back down and pretended to doodle again with her blue pen, but she realized with a sinking feeling — he was staring across the room at her. Lars, the weak-chinned kidnapper with a Deadpool beanie and a child’s love for magic tricks, was looking directly at her.

Ashley hesitated. His nonsense was wearing thin. “I . . . uh, well . . .”

“Do you know how to cut a girl in half?” Lars asked again, eagerly. Same tone, same inflection. His eyes were still fixed on Darby as he spoke. “You know. You put her in a big wooden box, like a coffin, and then you . . . ah, you cut it with a saw?”

Ed stared at the floor. Sandi lowered her paperback.

Again: “Can you cut a girl in half?”

Darby’s fingers tightened around her pen. Her knees pulled closer to her chest. Rodent Face was standing about ten feet from her. She wondered — if he went for the gun under his jacket, could she yank the Swiss Army knife from her pocket, close the blade, and cross the room quickly enough to stab him in the throat with it?

She rested her right hand on the countertop. Near her hip.

Lars asked again, louder: “Can you cut a girl in—”

“I can,” Ashley answered. “But you only win gold if she survives.”

Silence.

It wasn’t that funny, but Ed forced a chuckle.

Sandi laughed, too. So did Ashley. Lars tilted his head — like he had to push the joke through his brain — and finally joined in, and the room boomed with laughter. It echoed in the tight air, until Darby’s migraine came back and she wanted to squeeze her eyes shut.

“See, I got silver,” Ashley added. “Not gold—”

Under another wave of forced laughter, and still grinning, Lars pulled his coat back and reached for something on his hip. Darby grabbed the knife in her pocket — but he was just fixing his belt.

Jesus. That was close.

He’d moved fast, though. If he was really going for his gun, she realized, he could have killed everyone. Lars seemed clumsy and slow — until he surprises you and hits.

“Gold medal,” he chuckled, tugging his belt around his skinny butt, pointing a thumb at Ashley. “I, uh, like his jokes. He’s funny.”

“Oh, give me time,” Ashley said. “You’ll find me annoying.”

As the fake laughter died down, Darby realized something else. A small thing, but something really disturbing about how the abductor had laughed. He seemed too aware. Normal people blink and relax. But not Lars. His face laughed but his eyes watched. He scanned everyone’s faces, his eyes searching the room, coldly watching while he showed his mouthful of pointy teeth.

That’s the grinning, stupid face of evil, Darby realized.

That’s the face of a man who stole a little girl from her home in California.

The lights flickered. A jolt of icy darkness. Everyone glanced up at the fluorescent lights, but as they buzzed back on and the room filled again with light, Darby was still studying Lars’s whiskery face.

That’s what I’m up against.

There’s a time, late at night, when evil is said to be strongest. The witching hour, Darby’s mom used to call it, with a silly little voodoo tone.

It’s 3 a.m.

This was supposedly the Devil’s mockery of the Holy Trinity. Growing up, Darby had respected this idea but never really believed it — how can one time of day be more evil than another? But still, as a kid, whenever she woke up from a nightmare, her breaths catching and her skin sweaty, she’d glanced at her phone. And strangely, the time had always been close to 3 a.m. Every time she could remember.

The time she dreamt that her throat closed up in seventh grade Social Studies class and she vomited a three-inch maggot, pale and bloated, squirming on her desk?

3:21 a.m.

The time a man followed her to Seven-Eleven, whistling, then trapped her in the restroom, pulled out a small gun, and shot her in the back of the head?

3:33 a.m.

The time that tall ghost — a gray-haired woman in a floral skirt with knees that bent backward like a dog’s legs — lurched through Darby’s bedroom window, half-floating and half-walking, weightless and ghostly, like an underwater creature?

3:00 a.m. exactly.

Just a coincidence, right?

Witching hours, her mother used to say, lighting a jasmine candle. When demons are strongest.

Then she’d snap her Zippo shut to make a point — click.

Here at the Wanapani rest area, it was only 11 p.m., but Darby still felt a darkness gathering with her, with all of them. Something aware, pooling in shadows, eagerly waiting for violence.

She hadn’t decided how she’d attack Lars.

She’d already memorized the visitor center's layout. It was simple, but full of important details. A rectangular lobby with restrooms, old drinking fountains, and a locked supply closet labeled EMPLOYEES ONLY. A stone coffee counter surrounded a closed coffee shop, locked with security shutters. One obvious front door with a squeaky hinge. One large window overlooking the parking lot, partly blocked by snow. And a small, triangular window in each restroom, near the ceiling, ten feet above the floor. Like a jail window, without bars. She'd remembered this because it seemed like a detail others would forget.

Outside felt like another world. The moon hidden by clouds. The thermometer outside read negative two. Piled snow reached the window, still growing. The wind came in sharp bursts, sending dry snowflakes that hit the glass like tiny rocks.

“I could use some global warming right now,” said Ed.

Sandi turned a page. “Global warming is fake.”

“I’m just saying, thank God we’re inside.”

“True,” Ashley murmured, looking at Lars, “until someone gets locked in a wooden box and sawed in half.”

Rodent Face was back by the door, fiddling with the brochures. Darby didn’t know if he’d heard Ashley’s joke. She wished he would stop pushing their luck. This couldn’t last eight more hours. Ashley would say the wrong thing eventually.

It would come down to weapons.

That was what tonight would be about. And this rest stop seemed as safe as a preschool. The coffee bar had only plastic forks and spoons. Paper plates and brown napkins. There was a closet, but it was locked. No tire irons, flare guns, or steak knives. Her best weapon was the small blade on her Swiss Army knife. She patted her pocket, relieved it was there.

Could she stab Lars with it? More importantly, would it stop him? She didn’t know. It was barely a weapon, unlikely to go through a rib. She’d need to surprise Rodent Face and stab him in the throat or eyes. No time to wait. It was possible, but not ideal.

The cracked cement under the counter, she remembered. The loose stone.

That might work.

She stood and went to the coffee area, pretending to refill her cup. When no one was looking, she put her right foot on the shaky stone, and leaned forward. She pressed down a little, then more, then more — fiddling with the coffee pot's lever to hide the sound — until the stone popped loose and hit the floor. Lars, Ed, and Ashley didn’t notice. Sandi glanced up, then went back to her book.

When the woman’s eyes were back in her book, Darby picked it up. It was a little smaller than a hockey puck, smooth and egg-shaped. Big enough to knock out some teeth, or to throw hard. She put the cold rock in her pocket and went back to her seat, taking stock of what she had.

A two-inch knife.

A medium-sized rock.

And one .45-caliber bullet.

I need help, she thought.

She could try to take down Lars herself, of course. Surprise him, hurt him, grab the gun from his jacket and hold him until the snowplows came in the morning. Tie him up with his own tape, maybe. And if things went wrong, she guessed she’d be ready to kill him. But trying it alone right now would be a mistake. She needed to tell someone else here what she found, in case Lars overpowered her and hid her body without the others knowing. She couldn’t save Jay if she died first.

What makes a hero different from a victim?

Timing.

At the table, Ashley fanned out the cards in a smooth rainbow, all facedown except a single, upturned ace of hearts. “And, here’s your card.”

Lars gasped, like a caveman discovering fire.

Ed shrugged. “Not bad.”

From the bench, Darby assessed her potential allies. Ed was almost sixty and had a belly. His cousin Sandi might as well be made of light wood and hairspray. Ashley, though — as annoyingly chatty as he was, he was also broad, muscular, and quick on his feet. The way he moved to pick up dropped cards, the way he confidently moved around chairs — he had the smooth, quick grace of a basketball player. Or a stage magician.

A silver-medal stage magician.

“Do another one,” said Lars.

“That’s the only real trick I remember,” Ashley said. “Everything else was for kids. Fake sleeves, trapdoors in cups, that kind of thing.”

“You missed your chance,” Ed said.

“Yeah?” He smiled, and for a moment, Darby saw pain in his eyes. “Well, accounting is pretty cool, too.”

Lars sulked by the door, disappointed that the show was over.

Darby decided her next move had to be Ashley. He was strong enough to fight, at least. She’d get him alone, maybe in the restroom, and tell him about the girl. She’d make him understand how serious things were; that right now, a child’s life was in danger outside. Then she’d have help when she chose the right moment to attack and hold Lars—

“Oh!” Ashley clapped his hands, making everyone jump. “I know how we can pass the time. We can play circle time.”

Ed blinked. “What?”

“Circle time.”

“Circle time?”

“Yes.”

“What the hell is circle time?”

“My aunt teaches preschool. She uses this to help kids get to know each other. Basically, you sit in a circle, like we are now, and pick a topic, like my favorite pet, or something. Then you take turns, going around the circle, saying your answer.” Ashley paused, looking at everyone. “And that’s . . . that’s why it’s called circle time.”

Silence.

Finally, Ed spoke. “Please, just shoot me.”

Everyone was distracted again, so Darby walked back to the Espresso Peak counter and grabbed a brown napkin. She slipped it inside her notebook, clicked her pen, and wrote a message.

“Guys, we’re all stuck here together, and we’ve got another seven hours to go.” Ashley tried hard. “Come on. We’re going to go stir-crazy if we don’t open up and talk a little more.”

Ed grunted. “We’re talking right now.”

“So, let's go around and share, then—”

“I’m not doing that.”

“I’ll start.”

“So help me God, Ashley, if you make me do this, the snowplows will arrive tomorrow morning and find a rest stop full of bloody corpses.”

Darby clicked her pen. Let’s hope not.

“I like going around and sharing,” Lars chimed in.

Ed sighed. “Yeah, of course he does.”

“Okay. A good way to start is with phobias, or biggest fears,” Ashley said. “So . . . I’ll start, and I’ll tell you my biggest fear. Okay?”

“Nope,” said Ed.

Lars had set down his brochure. He was listening.

“You’re going to think my phobia is weird,” Ashley said. “It’s not a common fear, like needles or spiders . . .”

Darby folded the napkin twice with her message inside. She knew she was about to do something she couldn’t take back. This was the point of no return for tonight. From now on, one wrong look, or word, and the Wanapani rest area could explode.

“So, I grew up in the Blue Mountains,” Ashley told the room. “When I was a kid, I walked the railroads and explored these old, boarded-up coal mines. The hills are full of them. And this mine wasn’t on any map, but it was called Chink’s Drop.”

Sandi frowned. “Okay.”

“You know,” Ashley said. “The offensive word for Chinese people—”

“Yeah, I got it.”

“I guess a miner must have fallen and died, and—”

“I get it—”

“And he must have been Chinese—”

“I get it, Ashley.”

“Sorry.” He paused. “So, uh, I’m seven and really dumb. I crawled under the fence and went alone, without telling anyone, with just a flashlight and some rope. Like a little Indiana Jones. And, I mean, it wasn’t scary at first. I followed the tunnel as it got smaller, going deeper and deeper, past these old mine carts, over these broken train tracks from the 1700s, through blocked doors. Sound travels weird down there, all echoing and distorted. And I’m squeezing past this old wooden door, and I touched the rusty hinge for a second. And . . . something terrible happens.”

Darby noticed Lars was looking at his Colorado Air brochure again, so she took her chance. She got off the bench, and her wet shoes made quiet, wet sounds on the floor.

Ashley made a quick cutting motion. “The door slams shut. The hinge snaps closed, like rusty metal teeth, crushing my thumb, breaking three bones in my hand. Boom. It didn’t hurt at first. Just shock. And this door weighed three hundred pounds and was made of solid oak, impossible to move. And there I was, alone in the dark, a half-mile underground.”

Darby walked toward him.

“Two days without food or water. I slept a little. Scary dreams. Tired, thirsty. I didn’t have a knife, but I really thought about cutting off my thumb. I remember looking at it with my dying flashlight, wondering how hard I’d have to twist against the hinge to . . . you know.”

Ed leaned forward. “You still have both thumbs.”

Darby walked around Ashley’s chair and quietly dropped her folded napkin in his lap. Like kids passing a note in school.

He noticed — but smoothly finished his story, giving Ed an ironic thumbs-up: “Correct-amundo. Turns out all I had to do was wait. Some teenagers from another town broke into Chink’s Drop and walked right into me. Saved by pure, dumb luck.”

“And . . .” Sandi looked at him. “Your phobia is . . . what, being trapped?”

“No. Door hinges.”

“Door hinges?”

“I hate door hinges,” Ashley said, with a fake shiver. “They freak me out, you know?”

“Huh.”

Darby stopped by the window, watching snowflakes hit the glass, and waited for Ashley to read her note. She saw him from the corner of her eye lift the napkin and unfold it under the table to secretly read it on his knee, where Ed and Sandi couldn’t see. In scratchy blue pen, Darby had written: MEET ME IN THE RESTROOM I HAVE SOMETHING YOU NEED TO SEE.

He paused.

Then he took out a black pen from his pocket, thought for a moment, and wrote a reply. Then he stood up and casually walked to the window, too, smoothly slipping the napkin back into Darby’s hand as he passed. He did this as easily as a pickpocket.

She unfolded it and read what he wrote.

I HAVE A GIRLFRIEND.

She sighed. “Jesus Christ.”

He looked at her.

She mouthed: Not what I meant.

He mouthed: What?

Not. What. I. Meant.

Now they were both standing obviously by the window with their backs to the room. Lars was probably watching them, wondering what they were mouthing to each other. Ed and Sandi, too—

Ashley touched her shoulder, mouthing again: What?

Darby felt it, that familiar paralysis locking up her bones. Like climbing onstage and forgetting your lines. If she spoke, they’d overhear. If she didn’t, she risked making a scene. The entire world hung in the balance. She chanced a look over her right shoulder, toward Rodent Face, and as she’d feared, he was watching them. She noticed something else, too, and her blood turned to ice water.

Lars had placed something white on the brochure rack. A Styrofoam cup.

Her cup.

The eight ounces of misspelled COCO that she'd foolishly filled and brought outside an hour ago. She’d set it in the snow by the Astro’s back door, right before she broke in and talked to Jay. Then she’d forgotten about the cup, leaving it out there in the dark for him to find. Near her close footprints.

He knows, she realized. And something even worse struck her — that now, the quiet danger went both ways.

He’s planning to attack me.

The same way I’m planning to attack him.

“Trapped in a coal mine,” Sandi repeated to Ed. “Scary stuff.”

“Eh.” Ed shrugged. “I would’ve just cut off my thumb.”

“I don’t think it’s that simple.”

“Just saying. When you’re facing death, what’s a few little bones and tendons?”

Lars kept quietly watching them, and what scared Darby most was the deep, blank calm in his eyes. A criminal with any sense would have his gun out by now. But Lars was chillingly unconcerned, unworried, his empty little eyes looking at her like nothing more urgent, or dangerous, than a spill on the floor that needed to be mopped up soon. That was all.

A dark thought crept into her mind, and somehow, she felt it was a warning, like one of her mother’s old Tarot cards: That man will kill me tonight.

This is how I die.

She glanced at Ashley and whispered: “Come with me. Now.”
