No Exit

Chapter 9

12:01 a.m.

Two against one.

She was right about that much.

Ashley was part of the kidnapping. He’d lied to her — about driving the other car, about not knowing Lars, about everything. He acted along with her in the restroom. He kissed her. He seemed so real, so genuinely human and scared. She believed it all. She told him everything. Her whole plan, all her choices, her thoughts, her fears.

She gave him everything. Even a new weapon.

She turned to face Jay. “You didn’t say there were two of them.”

“I thought you knew.”

“How could I know?”

“Sorry—”

“Why didn’t you fucking mention it?”

“I’m sorry.” Jay’s voice broke.

Darby realized she was yelling at a seven-year-old girl who’d recently taken a steel nail through the palm. What did it matter? It was Darby’s fault. Her mistake. Her horrible, deadly misjudgment, and now it was two against one, and they were both as good as dead. Or worse.

One of the figures started walking toward them.

Her heart clenched. “Okay. Where’s their nail gun?”

“I don’t know.”

“Front or back?”

“I don’t know,” the girl sniffed.

She needed to find it fast. Under the front seats, maybe? This orange box had to be large; there were only so many places it could fit.

She raced to the driver’s side door, her feet sinking, like running in quicksand. She risked a look over her shoulder — the figure was halfway to them. Twenty feet back, taking high steps on the footpath. She recognized the beanie, the slouching walk. It was Lars. His right hand swung past a slice of light, and she saw a blocky shape.

His .45-caliber pistol.

“Jay,” Darby whispered. “Close your eyes—”

“What’s going to happen?”

“Just close your eyes.” She got to the Astro’s driver door, hitting it hard, her mind screaming: Find their nail gun. Kill this jerk. And then take his gun, and kill that lying snake Ashley—

She pulled the door handle. Locked.

Her stomach dropped.

Because . . . because Lars had locked it again. Of course he had; he was in there last. It was locked, locked, locked.

“You, um . . . you asked my brother to kill me,” Lars’s gargled voice called out, getting closer. “Is . . . is that right?”

They’re brothers.

Shit, shit, shit.

Crunchy footsteps, like breaking eggshells, coming toward her. “He says you . . . you asked him to bash my brains in.” His voice was so frighteningly close. Hoarse, rattling in the crisp air, hot with exhaled fog.

The Astro’s driver door was a no-go. Darby scrambled back to the rear of the van, catching herself on the slightly open door for balance, and looked back inside the dark vehicle. At Jay’s eyes, brimming with panicked tears, full of reflected light. At her rash-red cheeks. Her tiny fingernails.

She pleaded: “Run—”

Lars’s footsteps crunched closer.

Darby pressed her Swiss Army knife into the little girl’s outstretched fingers, almost dropping it. “Use this,” she said, touching the jagged edge on the blade. “Scraping motions, okay? To saw at the kennel bars—”

“He’s coming.”

“Do it, Jay. Promise?”

“I promise.”

“Keep cutting. You’ll get out.”

“What’re you going to do?”

Darby stepped back out and slammed the rear door, dropping a shelf of snow. She hadn’t answered Jay’s question, because she had no answer at all.

I have no goddamn idea.

“Why . . . ah, why are you running?” Lars called.

Darby struggled through the snow. It was waist-deep off the path, like pulling herself out of a pool, over and over with each clumsy step. Hard, gasping breaths. Her throat stung. Her calves burned.

“Hey. I just wanna talk—”

From the clearness of his voice, he was less than ten feet behind her. Chasing her. His mouth-breathing had turned into a steady pant. Low, guttural, like a wolf. Her left shoe — still unlaced — came off in the snow. She grabbed it and kept going, half-barefoot, as his heavy breathing grew behind her. He was gaining, she knew. A few steps closer and he’d grab her ankle—

“I’m . . . ah, I’m gonna catch you anyway—”

A metallic rattle. The gun, moving in his hand.

But she knew the pistol was just to scare her. If Lars really wanted to shoot her, he would’ve done it already. That would alert Ed and Sandi, so Ashley had probably ordered his brother to run her down, to kill her quietly, by suffocation or a broken neck—

His brother.

His fucking brother.

Darby walked past the empty flagpole and looked back. Lars was a shadow chasing her. He’d lost his Deadpool hat. She saw his thin blonde hair, pale in the dim light, and his hairline was receding. She could see the angry cloud of his breath. He’d stopped yelling at her; he was too out of breath now. The deep snow was too tiring. It was a nightmare in slow motion.

He’s going to catch me, Darby knew.

She was already getting tired. Her muscles ached. Her joints felt weak.

He’s going to run me down out here, and wrap his hands around my neck, and choke me until I die—

He was right behind her now. She could smell his salty sweat. She’d lost her head start and given up both of her weapons — the rock in a sock to Ashley, the pocket knife to Jay — and now all she had left was a bullet in her pocket and a size-eight shoe in her hand. She thought about throwing it at Lars, but that would just annoy him. He’d knock it away without slowing down.

There was nowhere to run anyway. Ashley was wisely guarding the building’s front door. She didn’t have her keys, so locking herself inside Blue wasn’t an option. Running wasn’t either — there were only miles of rough Colorado forest in all directions, cold and uncaring. Just noisy mountain trees, little ground cover, and deadly drops hidden by snow. How long would she last before freezing to death?

I can’t keep running.

She thought about stopping, standing her shaky ground, and fighting Lars. Not a good idea.

“Turn around,” Lars huffed behind her. “Let’s . . . ah, let’s talk—”

She needed to decide. If she stopped now, she'd have a few seconds to breathe before the fight. But if she kept running and he tackled her, she’d be out of breath, and her chances would be even worse—

Or . . .

The Wanapani visitor center popped into her head again. Walls, corners, blind spots. Even though Ashley still blocked the front door, there was another way in. The small triangle windows in the restrooms. She’d seen it in the men’s room, about the size of a dog door. She could see it from here, a bit of orange light shining through hanging icicles, above the stacked picnic tables.

Her purse was in that restroom. With her keys and her phone.

Okay.

I’ll climb those tables, break that window, and get inside.

She changed direction.

Lars noticed. “Where . . . where are you going?”

She didn’t have a plan for what to do inside. She just went for it. Because, like Sandi said, inside was much better than out here. Ed and Sandi were in there, and Ashley and Lars wouldn’t risk killing her in front of two people.

Would they?

No time to think about it.

The picnic tables were stacked below that window, covered in snow, so she climbed them like huge stairs. One, two, three tables up, shaking under her weight. But she made it, and she hit the building’s triangular window with her hands. The glass was frosted, glowing with inside light, and rough with ice. Too thick to break with an elbow. But it was a window that opened outward on a rusty hinge, and it seemed to sit unevenly, so she felt for the edges, gripping with numb fingers—

Lars laughed. “What are you doing up there?”

A foot-long icicle fell from the roof and hit the table next to her. She winced, gritting her teeth, still pulling, digging her fingernails into the window’s rubber edge—

“Hey, girlie—”

Pull . . . pull . . .

Another icicle fell and broke apart, showering her with ice bits. Like glass on her cheeks.

“Girlie, I’m coming for you—”

Two more icicles fell to her sides, exploding like gunshots in her ears, and the picnic table wobbled as Rodent Face climbed up toward her, crawling on elbows and knees like a fast, scurrying animal, but she was only focused on that window. On that warm light behind the glass, so close. On her tight fingers, yanking the thing open—

Pull . . .

Pullpullpull—

It broke. The window came loose.

She let it drop, and it smashed on a frozen picnic table. Lars lifted a hand to cover his face from the pieces — Oh, Jesus Christ, he’s right behind me — and Darby was out of time. She dove in, face-first, throwing herself through the small space—

Cold fingers grabbed her ankle. “Gotcha—”

She kicked away.
