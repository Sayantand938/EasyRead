No Exit

Chapter 10

12:04 a.m.

She fell six feet and landed on a toilet.

Back first, hitting the edge of the toilet with her lower back. She rolled off, kicking a toilet paper holder off the wall, pushing a stall door open. Her head hit the floor. Bright flashes in her eyes.

The toilet flushed.

She stood up quickly, hitting the stall door again, turning to face the empty window. Just a triangle of darkness. Snowflakes moved in circles inside. The opening was probably too small for Lars to follow, but she couldn’t be sure. Plus, Ashley was still around.

She moved away from the window, down the long, narrow restroom, past the stalls, past PEYTON MANNING TAKES IT IN THE ASS, past the dirty urinals, until she hit the sink with her sore back. Another sharp pain. She’d left her purse here. She picked it up, feeling inside for the familiar sound of her Honda keys. And her iPhone.

Three percent battery.

She held her breath and listened. She could hear Lars’s footsteps outside the window, and his loud, noisy breaths under the sound of the wind. He was stuck now — not wanting to climb through and risk getting stuck, not wanting to leave the window open and walk around to the front. It was strange. He’d stopped talking to her. Just grunting, making animal sounds now.

Keep moving, Darby.

She heard voices from the visitor center lobby. Quiet because of the door. Ed and Sandi had probably heard her fall. And she recognized the robotic voice of the radio — another CDOT update. How long until help arrived, now? Dawn, right? Six hours? Seven?

Don’t think about that. Keep moving.

Ashley was close but she didn't know where, and this terrified her. Worse, she had no weapon now. She hoped Jay could cut through the kennel bars with her serrated knife, or this was all pointless. She just had to give the little girl enough time to do it (if she could survive the next few minutes so close to two killers) and then drive them both to safety (if Blue could limp through Snowmageddon). All in all, three huge guesses. Unlikely was too mild a word.

No, Blue was stuck in the snow. It was too deep now—

But what about Sandi’s truck?

Tire chains, good height — yeah, that thing had a chance.

She gripped her keys tightly, letting the sharp points stick out between her knuckles. She could hurt someone's face, or poke an eye if she was lucky. Her Dryden Hall dorm key was very sharp, like a little filet knife.

She heard shuffling outside. She froze, listening. Something heavy moved and scraped, followed by a thud of falling snow. A picnic table moving. She knew Lars was trying, again, to climb the wobbly stack of tables and follow her inside. Any second now, that weak-chinned face would appear in the window, grinning with crazy joy—

Time to go.

Darby slipped on her left shoe. Double-knotted the shoelace. Then she slung her purse over her shoulders — car keys still gripped in her knuckles — and pushed out into the Wanapani visitor center lobby.

Ed was fiddling with the radio’s antenna through the security shutter. He looked at her, confused, and she knew why. She’d left the building twenty minutes ago — and now she’d come back through the restrooms. Beyond him, Sandi slept on the bench, her legs bent, her paperback covering her face.

“Find a cell signal?” Ed asked.

Darby didn’t answer. She looked ahead, past Espresso Peak, at the front door. That was where Ashley stood, his broad shoulders blocking her way out. He was staring at her. The nervous, easily scared asthmatic she’d spoken to just an hour ago was gone, just an act he'd dropped. This new Ashley was still and strong, with deep, watchful eyes. He looked her up and down — she had snow on her knees, her cheeks were red, her skin sticky with sweat, her Honda keys in her fist — and then he glanced at the center table, as if telling her to sit down.

She stared back at him, gritting her teeth and trying to look brave. Bold. Like a hero surrounded by bad guys.

Instead, she almost cried.

She was sure now — she’d die tonight.

“Hey.” Ed leaned between them, trying to remember her name. “Are . . . are you okay, Dara?”

For Christ’s sake, it’s Darby.

She swallowed, her voice quiet. “I’m fine.”

She wasn’t. She felt sobs stuck in her chest, shaky spasms trying to get out. Her spine hurt where she’d landed on the toilet. She wanted to jump forward, grab Ed by the shoulders, to scream at this nice old vet and his sleeping cousin: Run. Please, run right now. But where?

Ashley nodded again at the table, harder.

At her chair.

She noticed a brown thing neatly placed in the center of her seat, and knew it was her brown napkin. The same napkin they’d used before, back when she’d thought he was a friend.

She walked to the chair and picked up the napkin. Ashley watched her, her lip curling. It was the start of a smug grin, unseen by Ed and Sandi.

She unfolded the napkin with numb, clumsy fingers.

IF YOU TELL THEM, I KILL THEM BOTH.
