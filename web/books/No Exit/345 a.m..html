No Exit

3:45 a.m.

“Oh, shit.”

Darby shifted into reverse. Tried again. Pushed the gas pedal hard, again and again. No luck; the tires spun until the cab smelled like burnt rubber.

The truck was stuck there, facing the wrong way in the far-right lane heading north on State Route Seven, just before the blue REST AREA sign. She twisted her neck to look back through the cracked rear window — she’d gone less than fifty feet down the highway. Maybe a quarter-mile from the Wanapani building. She could still see the orange parking lot lights through a group of rough Douglas fir trees. It didn’t matter if they found their keys, because Ashley and Lars were still close enough to walk.

“Shit-shit-shit.” She hit the steering wheel, accidentally honking the horn.

Jay looked back, too. “Can they catch up to us?”

Yes, yes, yes, absolutely yes—

“No,” Darby said. “We drove too far. But stay inside.” She opened the driver's side door, scattering bits of glass, and slid out into the deep snow. She felt old and tired. Her bones hurt. Her eyes still burned from the pepper spray.

“What are you doing?”

“Digging us out.” She walked around the Ford’s front bumper, squinting in the partly buried headlights. Her heart sank when she saw the huge pile of pushed snow, rolled into a snowball in front of the truck’s grill. It must have weighed a hundred pounds, maybe more, as heavy and impossible as wet cement.

She almost fell at the sight of it. The hugeness of it.

But then she saw the little girl behind the cracked windshield, close to a dangerous medical crisis. An anxiety bomb; one bad moment from a seizure, coma, or worse.

So Darby dropped to her scraped knees and started to dig.

“Can I help?” Jay asked.

“No. You can’t push yourself too hard. Just watch my cell phone, please. Tell me if it gets a signal.” She lifted a crumbling snow-boulder and threw it aside. Her bare fingers ached with cold.

Seven miles, she thought, looking downhill.

Seven miles to that wrecked semi-truck. Could that really be all? She imagined a busy accident scene down there, full of first responders, busy with lights and movement. The flashing red-and-blue of police lights. Road crews in bright jackets. Paramedics putting tubes into throats. Confused victims being taken away on rattling stretchers.

All of that, just seven miles down the dark road. It didn’t seem real.

Seven miles.

Route Seven was high up here where they’d crashed, at the top of a curve in the road. The pine trees were sparse, the land rocky and steep. In daylight, with good weather, this might have been a beautiful mountain view. But here and now, it was maybe the only place on Backbone Pass that might get a cell signal. Forget Ashley’s Nightmare Children. Now she knew that was almost certainly another of his lies. Another cruel trick, to waste her battery.

Another gust of wind blew up the mountain, branches creaking, tugging her sleeves, lifting strange swirls of snow that slid across the road like passing ghosts.

“Hey, Jay,” she panted as she dug, trying to make conversation to break the eerie silence. Trying to keep things light, pleasant, and relaxed. “What . . . what do you want to be when you grow up?”

“I’m not telling.”

“Why?”

“You’ll make fun of me again.”

Darby leaned around the Ford’s headlights, checking the rest stop’s exit for Ashley and Lars coming. Still no sign of them. “Come on, Jay. You owe me. I got pepper spray in the face for you.”

“It wasn’t for me. It was meant for you.”

“You know what I mean—”

“A paleontologist,” the girl answered.

“A what?”

“A paleontologist.”

“Like . . . like a dinosaur fossil hunter?”

“Yeah,” Jay said. “That’s what a paleontologist does.”

But Darby wasn’t listening. She’d noticed the truck’s tire looked strangely soft, and now her blood froze. She brushed away more snow and saw a steel circle sticking out from the tire’s side. A nail head. She heard it now — the soft, hissing sound like a reptile. Leaking air.

She crawled to the other tire. Two more nails stuck into the treads.

Oh God, this was Ashley’s backup plan all along.

She punched snow. “Shit.”

He broke all of the cars, just in case we managed to escape in one—

But this didn’t make sense — why, then, would Ashley put nails in Sandi’s truck tires, too? If she was a key part of the kidnapping plan? After they’d been so careful to meet here, in the frozen Rockies?

Jay peered over the door. “What is it?”

“Nothing.” Darby rushed back to the front of Sandi’s truck and started digging again, faster this time. Her heart raced, pounding against her ribs, as she tried to seem calm. “Jaybird, tell me. What’s . . . what’s your favorite dinosaur?”

“I like them all.”

“Yeah, but you must have a favorite. T-Rex? Raptor? Triceratops?”

“Eustreptospondylus.”

“I . . . I don’t know what that is.”

“That’s why I like it.”

“Tell me about it, please.” Darby just needed to keep talking, scooping up handfuls of snow, her worried thoughts swirling: He’s coming for us. Right now, he’s catching up, and he’s carrying that nail gun—

“It’s a meat-eater,” the girl said. “It walks on two back legs. Jurassic period. Three fingers on each hand, like a raptor—”

“You could have just said ‘raptor,’ then.”

“No. It’s Eustreptospondylus.”

“Sounds like a lame dinosaur.”

“You couldn’t spell it,” Jay said, stopping. “Oh. Your phone found a signal—”

Darby sat up fast and ran to the passenger door, reaching through the broken window, grabbing her iPhone from Jay’s hand. She didn’t believe it until she saw it — one signal bar. Blinking fast. “Your turn to dig,” she said.

“The battery’s one percent—”

“I know.”

The door creaked, dropping more glass, and Jay jumped out. Darby held the phone with red fingers, pressing 9-1-1 with her thumb — but the phone shook in her hand, startling her. A NEW MESSAGE bubble blocked her screen. She was going to ignore it, until she saw who sent it.

It was 9-1-1.

An answer to her text message; the one she’d tried to send hours ago tonight, and must have just now sent: Child abduction gray van license plate VBH9045 state route 7 Wanapa rest stop send police.

The answer?

Find a safe place. Officer coming in 30 minutes.

Darby almost dropped her phone. ETA, as in estimated time of arrival. 30 must be minutes, right? It couldn’t be hours, or days—

Thirty minutes.

“Is it working?” Jay asked, panting as she dug.

Darby couldn’t believe it. It felt like a dream. She blinked, afraid it would all vanish, but the letters were still there, trembling in her numb hands. Her text had sent at 3:56 a.m. She’d gotten the 9-1-1 dispatcher’s response at 3:58 a.m. Just minutes ago.

Oh, thank God, the cops will be here in thirty minutes—

Her chest swelled with quick breaths. Nervous energy in her bones. She had questions. Lots of them. For starters, she didn’t know how this fit with CDOT’s snowplow situation — were the plows due in thirty minutes, too? Were they due first? Were they all coming up Backbone Pass at once — cops and road crews — as one big group? She didn’t know, and truthfully, she didn’t care, as long as the cops got here and shot Ashley Garver in his smirking face.

“Oh, Jay,” she whispered. “I could kiss you—”

The girl’s voice rose: “Darby, stop.”

“What?”

Jay faced her now, standing in the curved glare of the Ford’s headlights. Staring with snowflakes collecting on her shoulders, alarmingly still.

Darby tried to keep her voice calm. “Jay, I don’t understand—”

“Don’t move.”

“What is it?”

She whispered: “He’s behind you.”

Ashley was just about to pull the Paslode’s trigger, ready to shoot a nail into the back of her head, when Darby turned to face him.

Her auburn bangs brushed her cheek as she turned, her eyes looking up to find him. Her skin, lit by a bit of moonlight, looked soft like a marshmallow. That white scar still hidden — unless she squinted or smiled. Like an actress in a movie, perfectly framed by the camera, like Eva Green greeting Daniel Craig in Casino Royale.

Just a turn.

But God, what a turn.

Under Darby’s jacket and jeans, he could see the beautiful shapes of her body. Her shoulders. Her hips. Her breasts. He wished he could save this moment, this picture of heartbreaking beauty, and keep it forever. Like all real art, you’re never sure how it makes you feel at first, until you figure it out later. And he’d have a lot to figure out. He wished it was just something simple, like lust, because lust can be satisfied easily — but ever since he’d kissed her in that dirty bathroom, his feelings for Darby had become more complicated than that.

“Hi, Darbs.” He forced a smile. “Long night, huh?”

She said nothing.

No fear in those eyes. Not even a tremor.

She just looked him up and down, judging him, like this CU-Boulder redhead had somehow already expected this meeting, hours ago, and had a backup plan ready, which was of course impossible. Tonight had been a chaotic mess of pure chance and unexpected surprises. Not even a pro like Ashley himself could control everything all the time.

But still, he thought, I wish you hadn’t turned around.

It makes this harder.

He raised the cordless nailer again. He pushed the Paslode’s tip down with his left hand, bypassing the safety, squeezing the trigger, carefully aiming at her left eye—

Darby didn’t flinch. “That’d be a mistake.”

“What?”

“You don’t want to kill me.”

“Yeah? Why’s that?”

“I hid your keychain,” she said. “I know where your Astro keys are, and if you kill me now, you’ll never find them. Now that Sandi’s truck is stuck here, and you shot up my Honda, you’ve trapped yourself here. That van is the only way you and your brother will ever get out of this rest stop tonight.”

Silence.

She raised her hands, like a mic drop.

And from the front of Sandi’s truck, Ashley heard a strange, scraping chitter. A sound he’d never heard before.

It was Jay laughing.
