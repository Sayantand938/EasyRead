No Exit

Chapter 4

9:25 p.m.

Darby went to the Nightmare Children first.

This was her plan. It would look strange if she went straight to the cars. She figured Lars would look out the window after he left the restroom and didn't see her. Plus, she was leaving footprints in the snow. She saw her own from an hour before, and Ashley’s, and Lars’s (her size-eight shoes were much smaller than theirs). Snowflakes were already filling them.

Tonight, every choice would leave its mark.

The hot chocolate had been a bad choice. As bad as Devon’s “Strength in Chinese” tattoo. She didn’t know why she’d bothered to make a drink while a possible child predator was in the bathroom. She just did. She’d burned her tongue when she tasted it outside, like a tough guy.

She walked around the damaged statues, then back around the visitor center. The building stood close to the cliff, with just a narrow edge behind a cement wall, made smaller by stacked picnic tables. On the building’s back wall, she saw two more windows, one for each restroom. They were small and rectangular, about ten feet up, under the roof's icicles. She was sure Lars was done in there. She’d heard the urinal flush minutes ago. But she moved quietly, just in case.

She walked uphill, still acting like a Girl Without Cell Service. Her iPhone found nothing, of course. She tried sending her 9-1-1 text again every few steps, but it didn't work. Her battery was now at four percent.

From up here, she could see the whole rest area, like a small scene. Wanapa — Little Devil, in the local language. The short building. The flagpole. The cedar trunk. The Nightmare Children. The group of cars stuck in the snow. She especially watched the visitor center’s front door, waiting for Lars to come out under the orange light. Waiting to see if he’d follow her tracks.

The door didn’t open.

No sign of Rodent Face.

Melanie’s Peak loomed to the left, a sloping shadow. The heavier snowfall had hidden most of it, but it was still the tallest mountain in sight. It would be a good guide for finding her way.

From where she stood, she could also see State Route Seven, lit by circles of lights above. It looked like a huge ski ramp, sparkling with new snow. Totally impossible to drive on for anything except (maybe) Sandi’s truck. Blue wouldn’t make it five feet up — or down — that.

She waited there with snowflakes in her hair, listening to the far-off gusts of wind high up. Between them, a sad silence. And in it, Darby’s own painful thoughts raced around, like in an echo chamber.

You’re the reason Dad left. And if I could have picked him instead of you, I would have, without a second thought.

In a fucking heartbeat, Maya.

Before hanging up, her mother had answered: If he really wanted you, Darby, he would have taken you.

She took another sip of her hot cocoa. Not so hot anymore.

Now that she was sure Lars wasn’t following her, she could finally go to his van. She crossed the exit ramp and came at it from the north, her eyes always on Wanapa’s front. From the inside window, you could see the van’s right side but not the left, and she had to guess Lars would be watching. Walking in the deep snow was tiring; she struggled and breathed hard, spilling her drink. The air felt rough on her throat. Her nose burned. She felt the wetness freeze on her eyelashes, making them stiff.

But oddly, her body itself didn’t feel cold. Her blood was hot with adrenaline. She felt like she was glowing. She didn’t even have gloves, but she felt like she could stay out here all night.

Walking across the part of the parking lot for RVs and trucks, she was close enough to the building to see people sitting inside through the dirty glass. She saw Ashley’s shoulder. The top of Ed’s bald head. But no Lars, which worried her. What if he had followed her outside? What if he'd left the building when she was behind it, and was now following her tracks, sneaking up on her in the dark?

She couldn’t decide what was scarier — seeing Rodent Face, or not seeing him. Her hot chocolate would soon freeze.

She kept walking toward the strange van, and the dumb cartoon fox got closer with each step. That saying: WE FINISH WHAT WE START. The snow on the parking lot was thinner; only ankle-deep under a layer of ice. It had been plowed in the last day, which was good. Coming from the left, she used the van’s long side to hide.

She went to the van’s back doors. A Chevrolet Astro. She guessed AWD meant all-wheel-drive. An older van, by the looks of it. Dirty scratches on the bumper. Dark gray paint, peeling off in flakes. To the right, she saw her own faint footprints from an hour ago, going between the van and her Honda, and stopping right here. This was where it happened. This was where her night changed.

And now, this was it.

She put her Styrofoam cup in the snow and leaned up to the van’s rectangular back windows, partly covered in frost. She cupped her hands to the glass again and looked inside. It was even darker than she remembered. No shapes. No movement. Just dark blackness, like looking into a stranger’s closet.

She tapped the glass with two fingers. “Hey.”

No answer.

“Hey. Is anyone in there?” It felt weird to be talking to a van.

Nothing.

Only Darby Thorne, standing out here like a car thief, feeling more and more awkward as time passed. She thought about using the LED flashlight on her iPhone, but that would drain the battery and, even worse, be as bright as the sun. If Lars was facing the window, he’d definitely see it.

She knocked on the metal door twice with her knuckle, just above the California license plate, and waited for an answer. No movement inside. Nothing at all.

I must have imagined it.

She stepped back from the door, taking a cold breath. “Listen,” she whispered, her voice rough. “If someone’s trapped in there, make a noise now. Or I’m leaving. This is your last chance.”

Still no answer. Darby counted to twenty.

I imagined that little hand. That’s what happened.

Now, looking back, she knew exactly why she’d taken the time to get a cup of hot chocolate back in the visitor center. It was her way of avoiding reality. She’d done the same thing after Devon had texted last night with a message that destroyed her world: Call me mom has cancer.

The first thing she’d done?

She’d put her phone down, put on a jacket, and then walked from Dryden Hall to the student union building and ordered a cheeseburger. She’d watched it come to her, greasy and flat, paid $5.63 with a crumpled ten, found a seat in the empty cafeteria, and took two weak bites before running to the restroom and throwing up. She’d called Devon right there in the stall, her elbows on clean porcelain, her cheeks hot with tears.

There’s safety in normal life — if you can hold onto it.

Outside Lars’s van, she kept counting.

She was at fifty now, and still hadn’t seen this imaginary child. It made sense, right? Like how normal people claim to see strange lights, ghosts, or Bigfoot — Darby Thorne had just imagined a child’s hand in a stranger’s car, and almost reacted violently to that quick illusion. Too much coffee, not enough sleep.

This wasn’t a movie. This was just real life.

It was just a misunderstanding, a mistake, and Darby couldn’t wait to get back to the stuffy visitor center. Now the company didn’t seem so bad. She’d try to play cards with Ashley, maybe talk to Ed and Sandi. Maybe doze off on the bench until CDOT announced more weather updates.

Because Lars wasn’t a kidnapper. He was a creepy guy with a stutter and a skin problem on his hands, but there were lots of creeps. Most were harmless. Since the owner of this van probably was too, she felt braver and pressed her phone to the back window and turned on her flashlight, sending a bright blue-white light inside. Just to be sure, to finally confirm there was nothing—

Behind the glass, she saw a little girl’s face staring back.

Darby dropped her phone.

The light landed sideways at her feet, pointing at the visitor center like a signal, making sharp shadows in the snow. She grabbed for it, covering it with her hands and searching for the button.

The van was still again. The girl had gone back into the darkness.

Again, Darby had only glimpsed her. But in the bright flash, the image was burned into her eyes, like staring at the sun. Details stayed with her. The shape of her face. Maybe six or seven, with messy hair. Wide eyes, squinting at the light. Dark tape stuck around her mouth, wet with snot. She was behind something like a black wire cage. Like she thought. A dog crate.

Oh, my God. Her mouth is duct-taped shut, and she’s stuffed inside a dog crate.

For the first time since she'd stepped outside, Darby shivered. All the heat seemed to leave her body in one sharp instant. It was all confirmed. It was all true. It was all exactly as she'd thought. It was all really happening, right now, in bright color, and a little girl's life was really at risk, and tonight's battle would be between a tired art student and a human predator.

She stood again.

Stupidly, she tried the Astro’s back door again. Still locked. She already knew this. She went for the driver's door next. She wasn’t thinking; she was acting on instinct. Just reflexes, raw nerves. She was going to break into Rodent Face’s van. She was going to get this little girl out of there and hide her in her Honda. The trunk, maybe. She'd be safe in there, right?

Breaking glass would be loud and would leave evidence. Instead, Darby peered through the driver's window. The Astro’s inside was cluttered with receipts on the dashboard and yellow burger wrappers on the seats. The cup holders were full of Lars’s empty Big Gulps. She brushed away fresh snow and looked for the door’s lock pin behind the icy glass — yes, there it was. Thank God for old cars—

Darby, think this through.

She crouched and ripped the white shoelace from her right shoe. Gritting her teeth, she tied a slipknot in the middle. Drew it tight, like a small lasso. She’d only done this once before.

Darby, stop.

No way. She brushed more snow off the top of the door, dropping bits of ice, and pressed her shoelace into the upper corner. With her fingertips, she gripped the metal and pulled, just enough to ease the pressure between the door and its frame. Just a millimeter or two. After thirty seconds of fidgeting, the lace slipped right through and hung behind the glass.

Stop.

She couldn’t. She fed the shoelace in careful inches, lowering the loop to the lock. And something miraculous happened — the loop fell onto the pin and caught it on her first try. This was the hardest part, the part that had taken forty-five frustrating minutes last time, but amazingly, Darby got it here on her very first try. This was a good sign, like God was on her side. She sure hoped He was. Tonight, she’d need all the help she could get.

Her good sense was still arguing: Darby, don’t be rash. After you free her, then what? You can't take her inside. You can't hide her in Blue's trunk all night. First, think about it—

Nope. All she could think about was that girl. That terrified little face, still burned in her mind.

Think this through—

She moved to the left, sliding along the door’s edge, and pulled the shoelace sideways. The knot tightened around the lock, like a noose squeezing a neck. Then she moved up, adjusted her grip, and pulled a little harder (too hard, and she’d lose her grip on the pin and have to start over) and a little harder, and harder still, and the shoelace shook with sweaty tension, and the pin creaked, and now she was committed and couldn’t stop . . .

Darby, you’re going to die tonight.

CLICK.

The door unlocked.

Her heartbeat speeding up, Darby grabbed the door handle and yanked it open, and to her horror, the Astro’s dome light came on. A glaring brightness.

Larson Garver saw a light outside.

He was slouched by the brochures, looking at the Colorado Air paper and trying to figure out if their Robinson helicopter was an R66 or an R44, when he noticed it. It glimmered at the edge of his eye. A silent little flash from the parked cars, reflected on the window. From his van.

He felt a knot of panic tighten in his gut.

The others in the room didn’t notice. Ashley and Ed’s card game went on, their voices a gentle back-and-forth:

“Nine of diamonds?”

“Agh. You got me.”

Lars held his breath. He couldn’t see the light well enough; it could just be a reflection on the glass. So he stuffed the Colorado Air paper into his pocket — where it would join Springs Scenic (a Cessna 172) and Rocky Vistas (a DHC-3 Otter) — and hurried to the window, craning his neck to see better—

Darby found the light button and turned it off.

Darkness again.

Holy shit. She gasped, her heart pounding, her ears ringing, full of blood. That had been stupid. Careless. Dangerous. She’d acted without thinking and let herself be surprised by a door light.

Still, no one had seen. No harm, no foul, right?

. . . Right?

The van smelled like old sweat. Like a gym locker room. The leather seat felt sticky under her fingers. A model plane sat on the dashboard. The floor was full of crumpled yellow Jack in the Box and Taco Bell bags, greasy and see-through with hardened grease. She reached for the center console and opened it — more overflowing trash. She’d been hoping for a gun or something. She wanted to try the glove box, but knew there’d be another light in there, ready to go off like a trap. She couldn’t risk that again.

Inside the door, she found the locks.

CLICK-CLICK.

The van’s back doors were now unlocked. The front was separated from the back by a metal screen, like a confessional. So carefully, she crawled back outside, grabbed her shoelace knot, pushed the lock pin, and gently shut the driver door with her palms. She could see the building’s window over the van’s hood. She didn’t want to see Lars behind the glass — checking the dome light — but the window was still empty. Just the top of Ed’s head, and part of Ashley’s shoulder, as Go Fish went on.

So far, so good.

Darby crept back along the van’s left side, walking past the dumb cartoon fox, struggling through piled snow. She stuffed her shoelace into her jeans pocket; no time to lace her shoe now. She walked around the back of the van, grabbed the left door handle, and pulled it open.

The girl was inside a dog cage. One of those black, wire ones that can be folded flat. This one was sized for a collie, held shut with a padlock and lots of zip-ties. She was crouched on her knees because she couldn’t stand up. Her small fingers gripped the wire bars like a jail cell. Duct tape was wrapped around her mouth in messy loops.

Darby smelled a damp, sour odor. Urine.

For a long moment, she couldn’t speak. What could you even say? There were no words for this. Like swallowing a mouthful of peanut butter, she finally managed to move her lips and say, “Hi.”

The girl stared at her with wide eyes.

“Are . . . are you okay?”

She shook her head.

Well, duh.

“I’m . . .” Darby shivered in a gust of chilling wind, realizing she hadn’t thought this through. “Okay, I’m going to take the duct tape off your face, so you can talk to me. Is that alright?”

The girl nodded.

“It might hurt.”

The girl nodded harder.

Darby knew it would hurt; it was stuck in her hair. Lars had wrapped it carelessly around her head, and it was the black electrical kind. She reached through the spaces in the dog kennel and found the tape’s edges with her fingernails. Carefully, she peeled off the first loop, and then the second, and as the little girl worked on the rest, Darby asked: “What’s your name?”

“Jay.”

“Do you know the man who drives this van?”

“No.”

“Did he take you?”

“Yeah.”

“From your house?” Darby rephrased: “Wait, okay, Jay, where do you live?”

“1145 Fairbridge Way.”

“Where is that?”

“By Costco.”

“No. What’s the name of the city you live in?”

“San Diego.”

This made Darby shudder. She’d never driven to the West Coast before. Lars must have been on the road for days, with this girl stuck in the back. That explained the fast food trash. She saw more of the van’s inside as her eyes got used to the darkness — blankets and rugs piled up to hide the cage. Plywood shelves on the walls, all empty. Coca Cola bottles, the glass kind, clinking on the metal floor. Loose sawdust. Nails. A red gas can with a black spout. Children’s clothing stuffed in white K-Mart bags, though Darby doubted Lars had changed Jay even once since he’d taken her from her hometown. All the way in southern California.

“Right by the Costco,” Jay said.

Darby saw a round logo on the girl’s shirt, and knew it — the ball from the Pokémon games. A Pokeball, she remembered, from the iPhone app that had been popular on CU-Boulder’s campus for a while. “What’s your last name?”

“Nissen.”

“Is . . .” Darby shook the round padlock on the kennel door. “Is Jay short for something?”

“Jaybird.”

“No. A longer name. Like . . . Jessica?”

“Just Jay,” the girl said.

Jay Nissen. Age seven. Reported missing in San Diego.

Darby slowly understood — this would be on the news. She’d just broken into a man’s car (already a crime) and decisions were being made, right now, that would later be read in court. Lawyers would argue over every small detail. If she survived, she would have to explain every choice she’d made, good and bad. And so far, all she’d done was ask the kidnapped girl with her mouth taped shut if she was okay.

Darby had always been bad at talking to children. Even when she babysat, she didn’t have that natural motherly feeling. Kids were just messy, annoying little things that stressed her out. She’d often wondered how her own mother had dealt with her, especially since she’d been unplanned.

Her older sister Devon had been planned, of course. The perfect first child. But then three years later, Darby came along after her parents had a painful split. Divorce papers, unpaid rent, and morning sickness. I thought you were the stomach flu, her mother had once told her with a funny smile. Darby never knew how to feel about this.

I thought you were the flu.

I tried to kill you with Theraflu.

Now this little kidnapped girl raised her other hand to hold the kennel, and Darby saw it was bandaged. Jay’s palm was wrapped and covered with messy electrical tape. It was too dark to see clearly.

Darby touched it — and Jay quickly pulled away.

“Did he . . . did he hurt you?”

“Yeah.”

Her gut stirred with rage. She couldn’t believe it — how much worse this night seemed to get with each passing second — but she steadied her voice and asked through chattering teeth: “What did he do to your hand, Jay?”

“It’s called a yellow card.”

“A yellow card?”

The girl nodded.

Darby’s mind fluttered — like in soccer?

Jay lowered her injured hand and leaned back, creaking the kennel, and Darby felt something crusty coating the wire bars. It flaked off under her fingernails, smelling like metal. Scales of dried blood.

A yellow card.

That’s the kind of psycho I’m up against—

Fifty feet away, the building’s front door opened, and then banged shut.

Jay froze.

Footsteps came quickly. Boots crunched on ice. Darby paused, leaning into the back of the kidnapper's Chevrolet Astro. Half-in, half-out. She was afraid to move, afraid to stay. Frozen by growing fear, she looked into the little girl’s big eyes as the footsteps grew louder in the dark.

And another sound came fast.

Heavy breathing.
