Brave New World

Chapter Six

STRANGE, STRANGE, strange, was Lenina's opinion of Bernard Marx. So strange, in fact, that in the weeks that followed she wondered more than once if she should change her mind about the New Mexico trip, and instead go to the North Pole with Benito Hoover. The problem was she knew the North Pole, having been there with George Edzel just last summer, and found it quite awful. Nothing to do, and the hotel was hopelessly old-fashioned–no TVs in the rooms, no scent organ, only terrible synthetic music, and only twenty-five Escalator-Squash Courts for over two hundred guests. No, she definitely couldn't face the North Pole again. Besides, she had only been to America once before. And even then, how briefly! A cheap weekend in New York–was it with Jean-Jacques Habibullah or Bokanovsky Jones? She couldn't remember. Anyway, it didn't matter at all. The thought of flying West again, for a whole week, was very appealing. Also, for at least three days of that week they would be in the Savage Reservation. Hardly anyone in the whole Centre had ever been inside a Savage Reservation. As an Alpha-Plus psychologist, Bernard was one of the few people she knew allowed to have a permit. For Lenina, the opportunity was special. And yet, Bernard's strangeness was so special that she had hesitated to take it, even considering risking the Pole again with funny old Benito. At least Benito was normal. But Bernard …

"Alcohol in his blood substitute," was Fanny's explanation for every strange thing he did. But Henry, with whom Lenina had rather nervously talked about her new lover one evening in bed, compared poor Bernard to a rhinoceros.

"You can't teach a rhinoceros tricks," he said simply and directly. "Some men are almost like rhinoceroses; they don't respond well to conditioning. Poor guys! Bernard's one of them. Luckily for him, he's good at his job. Otherwise, the Director would never have kept him. But," he added reassuringly, "I think he's pretty harmless."

Pretty harmless, maybe; but also pretty unsettling. That obsession, to begin with, with doing things in private. Which meant, really, not doing anything at all. Because what could you do in private? (Besides going to bed, of course: but you couldn't do that all the time.) Yes, what was there? Almost nothing. The first afternoon they went out together was especially nice. Lenina had suggested a swim at Toquay Country Club followed by dinner at the Oxford Union. But Bernard thought it would be too crowded. Then how about a round of Electro-magnetic Golf at St. Andrew's? But again, no: Bernard thought Electro-magnetic Golf was a waste of time.

"Then what is time for?" Lenina asked, surprised.

Apparently, for going on walks in the Lake District; because that was what he suggested now. Land on top of Skiddaw and walk for a couple of hours in the heather. "Alone with you, Lenina."

"But, Bernard, we'll be alone all night."

Bernard blushed and looked away. "I mean, alone to talk," he mumbled.

"Talking? But about what?" Walking and talking–that seemed like a very strange way to spend an afternoon.

In the end, she convinced him, against his will, to fly over to Amsterdam to see the Semi-Demi-Finals of the Women's Heavyweight Wrestling Championship.

"In a crowd," he grumbled. "As usual." He stayed stubbornly gloomy all afternoon, refusing to talk to Lenina's friends (and they met many at the ice-cream soma bar between wrestling matches). Despite his unhappiness, he completely refused the half-gram of raspberry sundae she offered him. "I'd rather be myself," he said. "Myself and unpleasant. Not someone else, no matter how happy."

"A gram in time saves nine," said Lenina, sharing a piece of sleep-learned wisdom. Bernard pushed away the offered glass impatiently.

"Now don't get angry," she said. "Remember, one cubic centimetre cures ten gloomy thoughts."

"Oh, for Ford's sake, be quiet!" he shouted.

Lenina shrugged. "A gram is always better than a curse," she said with dignity, and drank the sundae herself.

On their way back across the Channel, Bernard insisted on stopping his propeller and hovering on his helicopter blades within a hundred feet of the waves. The weather had gotten worse; a south-westerly wind had started, and the sky was cloudy.

"Look," he ordered.

"But it's horrible," said Lenina, shrinking back from the window. She was terrified by the rushing emptiness of the night, the black foam-covered water rising beneath them, and the pale face of the moon, looking worn and troubled among the hurrying clouds. "Let's turn on the radio. Quick!" She reached for the dial on the dashboard and turned it randomly.

"… skies are blue inside of you," sang sixteen trembling high voices, "the weather's always …"

Then a hiccup and silence. Bernard had turned off the power.

"I want to look at the sea in peace," he said. "You can't even look with that awful noise going on."

"But it's lovely. And I don't want to look."

"But I do," he insisted. "It makes me feel like…" he paused, trying to find the right words, "like I'm more myself, if you understand. More alone, not so much a part of something else. Not just a tiny piece of society. Doesn't it make you feel that way, Lenina?"

But Lenina was crying. "It's horrible, it's horrible," she kept saying. "And how can you talk about not wanting to be a part of society? Everyone works for everyone else. We need everyone. Even Epsilons…"

"Yes, I know," said Bernard mockingly. "'Even Epsilons are useful'! So am I. And I really wish I wasn't!"

Lenina was shocked by his disrespect. "Bernard!" She protested, sounding amazed and upset. "How can you?"

In a different tone, "How can I?" he repeated thoughtfully. "No, the real question is: Why can't I? Or rather—because I know why I can't—what would it be like if I could, if I were free—not controlled by how I was raised."

"But, Bernard, you're saying terrible things."

"Don't you wish you were free, Lenina?"

"I don't know what you mean. I am free. Free to have a wonderful time. Everyone's happy now."

He laughed, "Yes, 'Everybody's happy nowadays.' We start teaching that to the children at five. But wouldn't you rather be free to be happy in your own way, Lenina? Not the same way as everyone else."

"I don't know what you mean," she repeated. Then, turning to him, "Oh, please let's go back, Bernard," she begged; "I really hate it here."

"Don't you like being with me?"

"Of course, Bernard. It's this awful place."

"I thought we'd be closer here–with just the sea and moon. Closer than in that crowd, or even in my apartment. Don't you get it?"

"I don't understand anything," she said firmly, determined to stay confused. "Nothing. Especially," she went on in a different voice, "why you don't take soma when you have these awful thoughts. You'd forget them all. And instead of being miserable, you'd be happy. So happy," she repeated and smiled, despite the worried look in her eyes, trying to be inviting and seductive.

He looked at her silently, his face blank and serious–stared at her. After a moment Lenina looked away; she gave a nervous laugh, tried to think of something to say but couldn't. The silence went on.

When Bernard finally spoke, his voice was quiet and tired. "Okay then," he said, "we'll go back." And pressing down hard on the gas, he sent the vehicle zooming into the sky. At four thousand feet he started his propeller. They flew in silence for a minute or two. Then, suddenly, Bernard began to laugh. Kind of strangely, Lenina thought, but it was still laughter.

"Feeling better?" she asked carefully.

Instead of answering, he took one hand off the controls and, putting his arm around her, began to touch her breasts.

"Thank Ford," she said to herself, "he's okay again."

Half an hour later they were back in his rooms. Bernard swallowed four soma tablets at once, turned on the radio and television, and began to undress.

"Well," Lenina asked, with a sly hint in her voice, when they met the next afternoon on the roof, "did you have fun yesterday?"

Bernard nodded. They climbed into the plane. A small bump, and they were off.

"Everyone says I'm very curvy," said Lenina thoughtfully, patting her own legs.

"Very." But there was a look of pain in Bernard's eyes. "Like meat," he was thinking.

She looked up, a little worried. "But you don't think I'm too fat, do you?"

He shook his head. Like so much meat.

"You think I'm fine." Another nod. "In every way?"

"Perfect," he said out loud. And to himself. "She thinks of herself that way. She doesn't mind being meat."

Lenina smiled, pleased with herself. But she was wrong to be so happy.

"Even so," he continued, after a short pause, "I still wish it had ended another way."

"Another way?" Were there other endings?

"I didn't want us to go to bed right away," he explained.

Lenina was shocked.

"Not right away, not the first day."

"But then what …?"

He started saying a lot of confusing and risky nonsense. Lenina tried to block it out, but now and then a phrase caught her attention. "… to see what happens if I stop my urges," she heard him say. The words seemed to trigger something in her mind.

"Never put off fun you can have today until tomorrow," she said seriously.

"Two hundred repeats, twice a week from fourteen to sixteen and a half," was all he said in response. The crazy talk went on. "I want to know what real feeling is," she heard him say. "I want to feel something strongly."

"When someone feels, everyone suffers," Lenina said.

"Well, why shouldn't they suffer a little?"

"Bernard!"

But Bernard didn't care.

"Adults in their minds and at work," he continued. "Babies when it comes to feelings and wants."

"Our Ford loved babies."

Ignoring her, Bernard went on, "It just hit me the other day that maybe we could be adults all the time."

"I don't get it." Lenina's voice was sharp.

"I know you don't. And that's why we slept together yesterday—like babies—instead of being adults and waiting."

"But it was fun," Lenina said. "Wasn't it?"

"Oh, the best fun," he answered, but his voice was so sad and he looked so deeply unhappy that Lenina's joy disappeared. Maybe he thought she was too fat, after all.

"I told you so," Fanny said when Lenina came to tell her everything. "It's the alcohol in his substitute."

"Still," Lenina said. "I really like him. He has such nice hands. And the way he moves his shoulders–it's very attractive." She sighed. "But I wish he wasn't so strange."

Stopping for a moment outside the Director's room, Bernard took a deep breath and straightened his shoulders, getting ready for the dislike and disapproval he knew he would find inside. He knocked and went in.

"A permit for you to sign, Director," he said, trying to sound casual, and put the paper on the desk.

The Director looked at him unpleasantly. But the World Controller's Office stamp was at the top of the paper, and Mustapha Mond's signature, big and black, was at the bottom. Everything was correct. The director had to sign it. He wrote his initials–two small, weak letters under Mustapha Mond's name–and was about to give the paper back without a word or friendly greeting when he noticed something written on the permit.

"For the New Mexican Reservation?" he said, his voice and face showing a kind of worried surprise.

Surprised by his surprise, Bernard nodded. There was silence.

The Director leaned back in his chair, frowning. "How long ago was it?" he said, talking more to himself than to Bernard. "Twenty years, I guess. Almost twenty-five. I must have been your age…" He sighed and shook his head.

Bernard felt extremely uncomfortable. A man so conventional, so meticulously correct as the Director–and to commit such a blunder! It made him want to hide, to run away. Not that he saw anything inherently wrong with talking about the distant past; that was one of those prejudices learned in his sleep that he had (or so he thought) completely gotten rid of. What made him feel awkward was knowing that the Director disapproved–disapproved and yet had been led into doing the forbidden thing. What inner urge made him do it? Bernard listened intently, despite his discomfort.

"I had the same idea as you," the Director was saying. "Wanted to see the savages. Got a permit for New Mexico and went there for my summer vacation. With the girl I was seeing at the time. She was a Beta-Minus, and I think" (he shut his eyes), "I think she had yellow hair. Anyway she was curvy, very curvy; I remember that. Well, we went there, and we looked at the savages, and we rode horses and all that. And then–it was almost the last day of my leave–then … well, she got lost. We'd gone riding up one of those awful mountains, and it was horribly hot and stuffy, and after lunch we went to sleep. Or at least I did. She must have gone for a walk, alone. At any rate, when I woke up, she wasn't there. And the worst thunderstorm I've ever seen was just starting. And it poured and roared and flashed; and the horses broke loose and ran away; and I fell, trying to catch them, and hurt my knee, so that I could barely walk. Still, I searched and I shouted and I searched. But there was no sign of her. Then I thought she must have gone back to the rest-house by herself. So I crawled down into the valley the way we had come. My knee was incredibly painful, and I'd lost my soma. It took me hours. I didn't get back to the rest-house till after midnight. And she wasn't there; she wasn't there," the Director repeated. There was a silence. "Well," he resumed at last, "the next day there was a search. But we couldn't find her. She must have fallen into a ravine somewhere; or been eaten by a mountain lion. Ford knows. Anyway it was horrible. It upset me very much at the time. More than it should have, I suppose. Because, after all, it's the kind of accident that could have happened to anyone; and, of course, society continues even if individuals die." But this sleep-taught consolation didn't seem to help much. Shaking his head, "I actually dream about it sometimes," the Director went on in a low voice. "Dream of being woken up by that thunder and finding her gone; dream of searching and searching for her under the trees." He became silent, lost in thought.

"You must have had a terrible shock," said Bernard, almost envying him.

Hearing Bernard's voice, the Director suddenly realized where he was; glanced at Bernard, and looking away, blushed deeply; looked at him again with sudden suspicion and, angrily standing on his dignity, "Don't think," he said, "that I had any improper relationship with the girl. Nothing emotional, nothing lasting. It was all perfectly healthy and normal." He handed Bernard the permit. "I really don't know why I bored you with this unimportant story." Furious with himself for revealing a shameful secret, he took his anger out on Bernard. The look in his eyes was now openly hostile. "And I would like to take this opportunity, Mr. Marx," he went on, "to say that I'm not at all happy with the reports I receive of your behavior outside of work. You may say that this is not my concern. But it is. I have the reputation of the Centre to consider. My workers must be beyond reproach, especially those of the highest castes. Alphas are conditioned to not be childish in their emotional behavior. But that is all the more reason for them to make a special effort to conform. It is their duty to be mature, even if they don't feel like it. And so, Mr. Marx, I am giving you fair warning." The Director's voice shook with anger that had now become completely justified and impersonal–it was the expression of society's disapproval itself. "If I ever hear again of any failure to maintain a proper standard of mature behavior, I will ask for your transfer to a Sub-Centre–preferably to Iceland. Good morning." And turning around in his chair, he picked up his pen and began to write.

"That'll teach him," he said to himself. But he was wrong. For Bernard left the room confidently, rejoicing, as he slammed the door behind him, in the thought that he stood alone, fighting against the established order; thrilled by the intoxicating awareness of his individual importance. Even the thought of being punished didn't scare him, it was more invigorating than depressing. He felt strong enough to face and overcome hardship, strong enough to face even Iceland. And this confidence was strengthened by the fact that he didn't really believe he would be punished at all. People weren't transferred for things like that. Iceland was just a threat. A very stimulating and life-giving threat. Walking along the corridor, he actually whistled.

The story he told that evening of his meeting with the D.H.C. was heroic. "Whereupon," it concluded, "I simply told him to go to hell and marched out of the room. And that was that." He looked at Helmholtz Watson expectantly, waiting for his reward of sympathy, encouragement, admiration. But no word came. Helmholtz sat silent, staring at the floor.

He liked Bernard; he was grateful to him for being the only man he knew with whom he could talk about the things he thought were important. Nevertheless, there were things about Bernard that he hated. This boasting, for example. And the outbursts of pathetic self-pity that followed it. And his annoying habit of being brave after the fact, and full of extraordinary composure when the person he was talking about wasn't around. He hated these things–precisely because he liked Bernard. The seconds passed. Helmholtz continued to stare at the floor. And suddenly Bernard blushed and turned away.

THE journey was quite uneventful. The Blue Pacific Rocket was two and a half minutes early at New Orleans, lost four minutes in a tornado over Texas, but flew into a favorable air current at Longitude 95 West, and was able to land at Santa Fé less than forty seconds behind schedule time.

"Forty seconds on a six and a half hour flight. Not so bad," Lenina admitted.

They slept that night at Santa Fé. The hotel was excellent–much better, for instance, than that awful Aurora Bora Palace where Lenina had suffered so much the summer before. Air conditioning, television, vibro-vacuum massage, radio, hot caffeine, contraceptives, and eight different scents were provided in every room. The synthetic music system was playing as they entered and was perfect. A sign in the elevator said there were sixty Escalator-Squash-Racket Courts in the hotel, and that Obstacle and Electro-magnetic Golf could be played in the park.

"But it sounds so wonderful," Lenina cried. "I almost wish we could stay here. Sixty Escalator-Squash Courts…"

"There won't be any in the Reservation," Bernard warned. "And no scent, no television, not even hot water. If you can't handle it, stay here until I return."

Lenina was quite annoyed. "Of course I can handle it. I only said it was wonderful here because…well, because progress is wonderful, isn't it?"

"Five hundred repetitions once a week from thirteen to seventeen," Bernard said tiredly, as if to himself.

"What did you say?"

"I said that progress was wonderful. That's why you shouldn't come to the Reservation unless you really want to."

"But I do want to."

"Very well, then," said Bernard; it almost sounded like a threat.

Their permit needed the signature of the Reservation Warden, so they went to his office the next morning. A low-caste negro porter took in Bernard's card, and they were let in almost right away.

The Warden was a blond, round-headed Alpha-Minus, short, red-faced, moon-like, and broad-shouldered, with a loud, booming voice, perfect for saying simple, sleep-teaching wisdom. He was full of random facts and unwanted advice. Once he started, he went on and on–boomingly.

"… five hundred and sixty thousand square kilometres, divided into four separate areas, each surrounded by a high-voltage fence."

At that moment, for no clear reason, Bernard suddenly remembered he had left the cologne tap in his bathroom wide open.

"… powered by the Grand Canyon hydro-electric station." "It'll cost me a fortune by the time I get back." In his mind, Bernard saw the needle on the scent meter spinning around and around, like a tireless ant. "Need to quickly call Helmholtz Watson."

"… over five thousand kilometres of fencing at sixty thousand volts."

"Really?" said Lenina politely, not knowing what the Warden had said, but following his pause. When the Warden started booming, she had secretly swallowed half a gram of soma, so she could now sit, calmly not listening, thinking of nothing, but with her big blue eyes fixed on the Warden's face, looking very interested.

"Touching the fence means instant death," the Warden said dramatically. "There is no way to leave a Savage Reservation."

The word "leave" was suggestive. "Maybe," said Bernard, starting to stand, "we should think about leaving." The little black needle was hurrying, like an insect, eating away at time, and his money.

"No leaving," repeated the Warden, waving him back into his chair; and because the permit wasn't signed yet, Bernard had to obey. "Those born in the Reservation–and remember, young lady," he added, looking strangely at Lenina and whispering inappropriately, "remember that in the Reservation, children are still born, yes, actually born, disgusting as that may seem…" (He hoped this shameful topic would make Lenina blush, but she only smiled as if she understood and said, "Really?" Disappointed, the Warden started again.) "Those born in the Reservation are destined to die there."

Destined to die… A tenth of a litre of cologne every minute. Six litres an hour. "Maybe," Bernard tried again, "we should…"

Leaning forward, the Warden tapped the table with his finger. "You ask me how many people live in the Reservation. And I reply"–triumphantly–"I reply that we don't know. We can only guess."

"Really?"

"My dear young lady, really."

Six times twenty-four–no, it would be closer to six times thirty-six. Bernard was pale and trembling with impatience. But steadily the booming continued.

"… about sixty thousand Indians and mixed-race people … complete savages … our inspectors sometimes visit … otherwise, no contact at all with the civilized world … still keep their disgusting habits and customs … marriage, if you know what that is, my dear young lady; families … no training … terrible superstitions … Christianity and totemism and ancestor worship … dead languages, like Zuñi and Spanish and Athapascan … pumas, porcupines and other dangerous animals … catching diseases … priests … poisonous lizards …"

"Really?"

They finally got away. Bernard rushed to the telephone. Quick, quick; but it took him almost three minutes to get through to Helmholtz Watson. "We might already be among the savages," he complained. "Damned incompetence!"

"Have a gramme," suggested Lenina.

He refused, preferring his anger. And finally, thank Ford, he was through and, yes, it was Helmholtz; Helmholtz, to whom he explained what had happened, and who promised to come over right away, right away, and turn off the tap, yes, right away, but took this chance to tell him what the D.H.C. had said, in public, yesterday evening … "What? He's looking for someone to replace me?" Bernard's voice was full of pain. "So it's really decided? Did he mention Iceland? You say he did? Ford! Iceland …" He hung up the phone and turned back to Lenina. His face was pale, his expression completely sad.

"What's the matter?" she asked.

"The matter?" He dropped heavily into a chair. "I'm going to be sent to Iceland."

Often in the past he had wondered what it would be like to be put through some great trial, some pain, some persecution, without soma and with only his own inner strength to rely on; he had even longed for trouble. Just a week ago, in the Director's office, he had imagined himself bravely resisting, calmly accepting suffering without a word. The Director's threats had actually thrilled him, made him feel important. But that, he now realized, was because he hadn't taken the threats seriously, he hadn't believed that the D.H.C. would ever really do anything. Now that it seemed the threats would come true, Bernard was horrified. His imagined calmness and courage had completely disappeared.

He raged against himself–what a fool!–against the Director–how unfair not to give him another chance, a chance he now knew he had always meant to take. And Iceland, Iceland …

Lenina shook her head. "Was and will make me ill," she said, "I take a gramme and only am."

Finally, she persuaded him to swallow four soma tablets. Five minutes later, worries were gone; the present moment was beautiful. A message from the porter said that, on the Warden's orders, a Reservation Guard had arrived with a plane and was waiting on the hotel roof. They went up immediately. A light-skinned man in a Gamma-green uniform saluted and began to recite the morning's plan.

A bird's-eye view of ten or a dozen of the main pueblos, then a landing for lunch in the Malpais valley. The rest-house there was comfortable, and the savages at the pueblo would probably be celebrating their summer festival. It would be the best place to spend the night.

They took their seats in the plane and took off. Ten minutes later, they were crossing the border that separated civilization from savagery. Up and down hills, across deserts of salt or sand, through forests, into the deep violet of canyons, over rocks and peaks and flat-topped mesas, the fence stretched endlessly, a straight line, a perfect symbol of human success. And at its base, here and there, a collection of white bones, an unrotted body dark on the tan ground marked the spot where deer or steer, puma or porcupine or coyote, or the vultures drawn by the smell of decay, had been killed as if by fate, had gotten too close to the deadly wires.

"They never learn," said the pilot in green, pointing down at the skeletons below. "And they never will learn," he added, laughing as if he had personally defeated the electrocuted animals.

Bernard laughed too; after two grammes of soma, the joke seemed funny for some reason. He laughed and then quickly fell asleep, and slept as they flew over Taos and Tesuque; over Nambe and Picuris and Pojoaque, over Sia and Cochiti, over Laguna and Acoma and the Enchanted Mesa, over Zuñi and Cibola and Ojo Caliente, and finally woke up to find the plane on the ground, Lenina carrying the suitcases into a small square house, and the light-skinned man in Gamma-green talking in a strange language with a young Indian.

"Malpais," the pilot explained as Bernard got out. "This is the rest-house. And there's a dance this afternoon at the pueblo. He'll take you there." He pointed to the gloomy young savage. "Funny, I bet." He grinned. "Everything they do is funny." With that, he climbed into the plane and started the engines. "Back tomorrow. And remember," he added to Lenina, "they're perfectly safe; savages won't hurt you. They know enough about gas bombs to not cause trouble." Still laughing, he started the helicopter blades, sped up, and left.
