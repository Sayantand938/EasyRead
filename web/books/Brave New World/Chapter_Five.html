Brave New World

Chapter Five

BY EIGHT O'CLOCK the light was fading. The loudspeaker on the Stoke Poges Club House tower began announcing the courses' closing in an unnaturally high voice. Lenina and Henry stopped their game and walked back to the Club. From the Internal and External Secretion Trust grounds came the mooing of thousands of cattle, whose hormones and milk provided the raw materials for the large factory at Farnham Royal.

Helicopters buzzed constantly in the twilight. Every two and a half minutes, a bell and whistles announced the departure of a light monorail train carrying lower-caste golfers from their separate course back to the city.

Lenina and Henry got into their machine and took off. At eight hundred feet, Henry slowed the helicopter's blades, and they hovered for a minute or two above the fading landscape. The Burnham Beeches forest stretched like a dark pool towards the bright western sky. The last of the sunset faded from crimson at the horizon, through orange, to yellow and a pale green. Northward, above the trees, the Internal and External Secretions factory shone brightly from every window of its twenty stories. Below them were the Golf Club buildings: the large Lower Caste barracks and, separated by a wall, the smaller houses for Alpha and Beta members. The monorail station entrances were crowded with lower-caste people moving like ants. A lighted train shot out from under the glass roof. Following its path across the dark plain, they saw the Slough Crematorium's grand buildings. Its four tall chimneys were floodlit and tipped with red warning lights for night-flying planes. It was a landmark.

"Why do the smoke-stacks have those balcony-like things around them?" Lenina asked.

"Phosphorus recovery," Henry said briefly. "The gases go through four treatments on their way up the chimney. P2O5 used to be lost when someone was cremated. Now they recover over ninety-eight percent of it. More than a kilo and a half per adult body, which makes almost four hundred tons of phosphorus every year from England alone." Henry spoke with pride, happy about the achievement as if it were his own. "It's great to think we can still be useful after we die, making plants grow."

Lenina looked away and down at the monorail station. "Fine," she said. "But it's strange that Alphas and Betas won't make any more plants grow than those Gammas, Deltas, and Epsilons down there."

"All men are chemically equal," Henry said importantly. "Besides, even Epsilons do necessary jobs."

"Even an Epsilon…" Lenina suddenly remembered waking up as a little girl at school and hearing the whispering that haunted her sleep. She saw the moonlight, the row of small white beds, and heard the soft voice that said (the words were unforgettable after so many repetitions): "Everyone works for everyone else. We can't do without anyone. Even Epsilons are useful. We couldn't do without Epsilons. Everyone works for everyone else. We can't do without anyone…" Lenina remembered her first shock of fear and surprise, her thoughts for half an hour, and then the gradual calming of her mind under the influence of those endless repetitions, the soothing, smoothing, creeping sleep. …

"I guess Epsilons don't really mind being Epsilons," she said aloud.

"Of course they don't. How can they? They don't know what it's like to be anything else. We'd mind, of course. But we've been conditioned differently. Besides, we start with different genes."

"I'm glad I'm not an Epsilon," said Lenina, with conviction.

"And if you were an Epsilon," said Henry, "your conditioning would have made you just as thankful that you weren't a Beta or an Alpha." He put his forward propeller into gear and headed the machine towards London. Behind them, in the west, the crimson and orange were almost faded; a dark bank of cloud had crept into the zenith. As they flew over the crematorium, the plane shot upwards on the column of hot air rising from the chimneys, only to fall as suddenly when it passed into the descending chill beyond.

"What a marvelous switchback!" Lenina laughed happily. But Henry's tone was almost, for a moment, sad. "Do you know what that switchback was?" he said. "It was some human being finally and completely disappearing. Going up in a squirt of hot gas. It would be curious to know who it was–a man or a woman, an Alpha or an Epsilon. …" He sighed. Then, in a determinedly cheerful voice, "Anyway," he concluded, "there's one thing we can be sure of; whoever he may have been, he was happy when he was alive. Everybody's happy now."

"Yes, everybody's happy now," echoed Lenina. They had heard the words repeated a hundred and fifty times every night for twelve years.

Landing on the roof of Henry's forty-story apartment house in Westminster, they went straight down to the dining-hall. There, in a loud and cheerful company, they ate an excellent meal. Soma was served with the coffee. Lenina took two half-gramme tablets and Henry three. At twenty past nine they walked across the street to the newly opened Westminster Abbey Cabaret. It was a night almost without clouds, moonless and starry; but Lenina and Henry were fortunately unaware of this somewhat depressing fact. The electric sky-signs effectively blocked out the outer darkness. "CALVIN STOPES AND HIS SIXTEEN SEXOPHONISTS." The giant letters glared invitingly from the front of the new Abbey. "LONDON'S FINEST SCENT AND COLOUR ORGAN. ALL THE LATEST SYNTHETIC MUSIC."

They entered. The air seemed hot and somehow breathless with the scent of ambergris and sandalwood. On the domed ceiling of the hall, the colour organ had momentarily painted a tropical sunset. The Sixteen Sexophonists were playing an old favourite: "There ain't no Bottle in all the world like that dear little Bottle of mine." Four hundred couples were five-stepping round the polished floor. Lenina and Henry were soon the four hundred and first. The saxophones wailed like melodious cats under the moon, moaned in the alto and tenor registers as though they were dying. Rich with a wealth of harmonics, their tremulous chorus mounted towards a climax, louder and ever louder–until at last, with a wave of his hand, the conductor let loose the final shattering note of ether-music and blew the sixteen merely human blowers clean out of existence. Thunder in A flat major. And then, in almost silence, in almost darkness, there followed a gradual decrease, a diminuendo sliding gradually, through quarter tones, down, down to a faintly whispered dominant chord that lingered on (while the five-four rhythms still pulsed below) filling the darkened seconds with an intense expectancy. And at last expectancy was fulfilled. There was a sudden explosive sunrise, and simultaneously, the Sixteen burst into song:

"Bottle of mine, it's you I've always wanted! Bottle of mine, why was I ever decanted? Skies are blue inside of you,
The weather's always fine;

For "There's no Bottle in the world Like my dear little Bottle."

Dancing in a circle with hundreds of others around Westminster Abbey, Lenina and Henry felt like they were in another world—the warm, colorful, and incredibly friendly world of a soma-induced vacation. Everyone seemed so kind, attractive, and amusing! "Bottle of mine, you're all I've ever wanted…" And Lenina and Henry had what they desired… They were inside, right here and now—safe inside with the beautiful weather and the always blue sky. And when the band had put down their saxophones and the music machine played the latest slow songs, they might as well have been twin babies gently rocking together on the waves of a bottled ocean of fake blood.

"Good-night, dear friends. Good-night, dear friends." The speakers softened their instructions with friendly and musical politeness. "Good-night, dear friends…"

Obediently, Lenina and Henry left the building with everyone else. The depressing stars had moved quite a bit across the sky. But even though the screen of sky-signs had mostly disappeared, the two young people were still happily unaware of the night. Swallowing their second dose of soma half an hour before closing, they had built a wall between their minds and the real world. Like being in a bottle, they crossed the street; like being in a bottle, they took the elevator to Henry's room on the twenty-eighth floor. And yet, even in her bottled state and despite the second dose of soma, Lenina remembered to take all the birth control measures required by the rules. Years of constant sleep-teaching and, from twelve to seventeen, birth control drills three times a week had made taking these precautions almost as automatic and unavoidable as blinking.

"Oh, that reminds me," she said, returning from the bathroom, "Fanny Crowne wants to know where you got that lovely green fake-leather cartridge belt you gave me."

Bernard's Solidarity Service days were on ALTERNATE Thursdays. After an early dinner at the Aphroditzeum (where Helrnholtz had recently been accepted under Rule Two), he said goodbye to his friend and, grabbing a taxi on the roof, told the driver to fly to the Fordson Community Singery. The vehicle rose a couple of hundred meters, then headed east, and as it turned, the Singery appeared before Bernard's eyes, incredibly beautiful. Lit up by floodlights, its white fake Carrara marble gleamed like snow over Ludgate Hill; at each corner of its helicopter platform, a huge T glowed red against the night, and solemn synthetic music rumbled from the mouths of twenty-four giant golden trumpets.

"Damn, I'm late," Bernard thought as he first saw Big Henry, the Singery clock. And right on cue, Big Henry sounded the hour as he paid his cab fare. "Ford," an enormous bass voice sang from all the golden trumpets. "Ford, Ford, Ford…" Nine times. Bernard ran for the elevator.

The main hall for Ford's Day celebrations and other large Community Sings was at the bottom of the building. Above it, with a hundred rooms on each floor, were the seven thousand rooms used by Solidarity Groups for their bi-weekly services. Bernard went down to floor thirty-three, hurried along the corridor, hesitated for a moment outside Room 3210, then, having gathered his courage, opened the door and walked in.

Thank Ford! He wasn't the last one. Three of the twelve chairs arranged around the circular table were still empty. He slipped into the nearest one as quietly as he could and prepared to frown at the latecomers when they arrived.

Turning to him, "What were you playing this afternoon?" the girl on his left asked. "Obstacle, or Electromagnetic?"

Bernard looked at her (Ford! it was Morgana Rothschild) and with a blush admitted he had been playing neither. Morgana stared at him in surprise. There was an awkward silence.

Then she deliberately turned away and spoke to the more athletic man on her left.

"A good start for a Solidarity Service," thought Bernard miserably, and expected yet another failure to find peace. If only he had taken a moment to look around instead of rushing to the nearest chair! He could have sat between Fifi Bradlaugh and Joanna Diesel. Instead, he had blindly sat next to Morgana. Morgana! Ford! Those dark eyebrows of hers–that eyebrow, really–because they met above her nose. Ford! And on his right was Clara Deterding. It was true that Clara's eyebrows didn't meet. But she was too curvy. Whereas Fifi and Joanna were perfect. Plump, blonde, not too big … And it was that big lout, Tom Kawaguchi, who now took the seat between them. The last to arrive was Sarojini Engels.

"You're late," said the Group's President sternly. "Don't let it happen again."

Sarojini apologized and slid into her place between Jim Bokanovsky and Herbert Bakunin. The group was now complete, the circle of solidarity perfect and whole. Man, woman, man, in a ring of constant change around the table. Twelve of them ready to become one, waiting to join together, to be fused, to lose their twelve separate identities in a larger being.

The President stood up, made the sign of the T and, turning on the synthetic music, released the soft, tireless beating of drums and a choir of instruments–almost wind and super-string–that loudly repeated the short, unforgettable melody of the first Solidarity Hymn. Again, again–and it wasn't the ear that heard the pulsing rhythm, it was the gut; the cry and clash of those repeating harmonies haunted, not the mind, but the longing bowels of compassion.

The President made another sign of the T and sat down. The service had begun. The special soma tablets were placed in the center of the table. The loving cup of strawberry ice-cream soma was passed from hand to hand and, with the words, "I drink to my destruction," drunk twelve times. Then, to the sound of the synthetic orchestra, the First Solidarity Hymn was sung.

"Ford, we are twelve; oh, make us one, Like drops within the Social River, Oh, make us now together run As swiftly as thy shining Flivver."

Twelve longing verses. And then the loving cup was passed a second time. "I drink to the Greater Being" was now the phrase. All drank. The music played on tirelessly. The drums beat. The crying and clashing of the harmonies were an obsession in the melted bowels. The Second Solidarity Hymn was sung.

"Come, Greater Being, Social Friend, Destroyer of All into One! We long to die, for when we end, Our larger life has but begun."

Again twelve stanzas. By this time the soma had begun to work. Eyes shone, cheeks were flushed, the inner light of universal kindness showed on every face in happy, friendly smiles. Even Bernard felt himself a little softened. When Morgana Rothschild turned and smiled at him, he did his best to smile back. But the eyebrow, that black two-in-one–alas, it was still there; he couldn't ignore it, couldn't, however hard he tried. The softening hadn't gone far enough. Perhaps if he had been sitting between Fifi and Joanna … For the third time the loving cup went round; "I drink to the nearness of His Coming," said Morgana Rothschild, whose turn it was to start the circular ritual. Her tone was loud, excited. She drank and passed the cup to Bernard. "I drink to the nearness of His Coming," he repeated, trying to feel that the coming was near; but the eyebrow continued to bother him, and the Coming, so far as he was concerned, was horribly far away. He drank and handed the cup to Clara Deterding. "It'll be a failure again," he said to himself. "I know it will." But he went on doing his best to smile.

The loving cup had made its circuit. Lifting his hand, the President gave a signal; the chorus broke out into the third Solidarity Hymn.

"Feel how the Greater Being comes! Rejoice and, in rejoicings, die! Melt in the music of the drums! For I am you and you are I."

As verse followed verse the voices thrilled with an ever stronger excitement. The sense of the Coming's nearness was like an electric tension in the air. The President switched off the music and, with the final note of the final stanza, there was absolute silence–the silence of stretched expectancy, shaking and alive. The President reached out his hand; and suddenly a Voice, a deep strong Voice, more musical than any human voice, richer, warmer, more full of love and longing and pity, a wonderful, mysterious, supernatural Voice spoke from above their heads. Very slowly, "Oh, Ford, Ford, Ford," it said, getting quieter and lower. A feeling of warmth spread thrillingly out from the stomach to every part of the bodies of those who listened; tears came into their eyes; their hearts, their guts seemed to move within them, as though alive. "Ford!" they were melting, "Ford!" dissolved, dissolved. Then, in another tone, suddenly, startlingly. "Listen!" trumpeted the voice. "Listen!" They listened. After a pause, sunk to a whisper, but a whisper, somehow, more powerful than the loudest cry. "The feet of the Greater Being," it went on, and repeated the words: "The feet of the Greater Being." The whisper almost died. "The feet of the Greater Being are on the stairs." And once more there was silence; and the expectancy, briefly relaxed, was stretched again, tighter, tighter, almost to the breaking point. The feet of the Greater Being–oh, they heard them, they heard them, coming softly down the stairs, coming nearer and nearer down the invisible stairs. The feet of the Greater Being. And suddenly the breaking point was reached. Her eyes staring, her lips parted. Morgana Rothschild sprang to her feet.

"I hear him," she cried. "I hear him."

"He's coming," shouted Sarojini Engels.

"Yes, he's coming, I hear him." Fifi Bradlaugh and Tom Kawaguchi rose at the same time to their feet.

"Oh, oh, oh!" Joanna said, unable to speak clearly.

"He's coming!" yelled Jim Bokanovsky.

The President leaned forward and, with a touch, started a wild burst of cymbals and brass, a frenzy of tom-toms.

"Oh, he's coming!" screamed Clara Deterding. "Aie!" and it sounded like she was being stabbed.

Feeling like he should do something, Bernard also jumped up and shouted: "I hear him; He's coming." But it wasn't true. He heard nothing, and nobody was coming for him. Nobody–despite the music, despite the growing excitement. But he waved his arms, he shouted with the others; and when they started to dance and stomp, he danced and stomped too.

Around they went, a circle of dancers, each with hands on the hips of the person in front, round and round, shouting together, stomping to the rhythm of the music, beating it out with hands on the buttocks in front; twelve pairs of hands beating as one; as one, twelve buttocks loudly echoing. Twelve as one, twelve as one. "I hear Him, I hear Him coming." The music sped up; faster beat the feet, faster, faster fell the rhythmic hands. And suddenly a deep, artificial bass voice boomed out the words that announced the coming unity and final completion of togetherness, the coming of the Twelve-in-One, the embodiment of the Greater Being. "Orgy-porgy," it sang, while the tom-toms continued to beat their feverish rhythm:

"Orgy-porgy, Ford and fun, Kiss the girls and make them One. Boys at One with girls at peace; Orgy-porgy gives release."

"Orgy-porgy," the dancers joined in the repeated chant, "Orgy-porgy, Ford and fun, kiss the girls …" And as they sang, the lights began to slowly fade–to fade and grow warmer, richer, redder, until they were dancing in the crimson light of an Embryo Store. "Orgy-porgy …" In their blood-colored, early-life darkness, the dancers continued to circle for a while, to beat and beat out the tireless rhythm. "Orgy-porgy …" Then the circle faltered, broke apart, and fell in scattered groups on the ring of couches surrounding–circle inside circle–the table and its planetary chairs. "Orgy-porgy …" Tenderly the deep Voice hummed and cooed; in the red twilight it was as though some huge black dove was hovering kindly over the now lying-down dancers.

They were standing on the roof; Big Henry had just rung eleven. The night was calm and warm.

"Wasn't it wonderful?" said Fifi Bradlaugh. "Wasn't it simply wonderful?" She looked at Bernard with a look of joy, but a joy without any agitation or excitement–because being excited still means being unsatisfied. Hers was the calm happiness of achieved fulfillment, the peace, not of empty satisfaction and nothingness, but of a balanced life, of energies at rest and in harmony. A rich and living peace. Because the Solidarity Service had given as well as taken, drawn out only to refill. She was full, she was made whole, she was even more than herself. "Didn't you think it was wonderful?" she insisted, looking into Bernard's face with those supernaturally shining eyes.

"Yes, I thought it was wonderful," he lied and looked away; the sight of her changed face was both an accusation and a mocking reminder of his own separateness. He was as miserably alone now as he had been when the service began–more alone because of his unrefilled emptiness, his dead satisfaction. Separate and not atoned, while the others were being joined into the Greater Being; alone even in Morgana's embrace–much more alone, in fact, more hopelessly himself than he had ever been before in his life. He had come out of that crimson twilight into the ordinary electric light with a self-awareness made agonizingly sharp. He was utterly miserable, and maybe (her shining eyes accused him), maybe it was his own fault. "Quite wonderful," he repeated; but all he could think of was Morgana's eyebrow.
