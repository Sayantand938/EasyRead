Brave New World

Chapter Four

THE ELEVATOR was crowded with men from the Alpha Changing Rooms, and Lenina's entry was met with many friendly nods and smiles. She was well-liked, and had slept with almost all of them at some point.

They were nice guys, she thought, returning their greetings. Charming guys! Still, she did wish that George Edzel's ears weren't quite so big (maybe he'd been given a little too much parathyroid at Meter 328?). And looking at Benito Hoover, she couldn't help remembering that he was really too hairy when he took his clothes off.

Turning, her eyes a little saddened by the thought of Benito's curly black hair, she saw Bernard Marx in a corner, small, thin, and sad.

"Bernard!" she went to him. "I was looking for you." Her voice rang out clearly above the hum of the elevator. The others looked around curiously. "I wanted to talk to you about our New Mexico plan." She could see Benito Hoover staring in surprise. The stare annoyed her. "Surprised I wouldn't be begging to go with him again!" she thought. Then aloud, and more warmly than ever, "I'd really love to come with you for a week in July," she went on. (Anyway, she was publicly showing she wasn't faithful to Henry. Fanny should be pleased, even if it was Bernard.) "That is," Lenina gave him her most inviting smile, "if you still want me to."

Bernard's pale face turned red. "Why on earth?" she wondered, surprised, but also touched by this strange show of her power.

"Shouldn't we talk about this somewhere else?" he stammered, looking very uncomfortable.

"As if I'd said something shocking," Lenina thought. "He couldn't look more upset if I'd told a dirty joke—asked him who his mother was, or something."

"I mean, with all these people around…" He was struggling with his confusion.

Lenina's laugh was open and not at all mean. "You're so funny!" she said, and she really thought he was. "You'll give me a week's notice, right?" she continued in a different tone. "I guess we take the Blue Pacific Rocket? Does it leave from the Charing-T Tower? Or is it from Hampstead?"

Before Bernard could answer, the elevator stopped.

"Roof!" a shaky voice called out.

The elevator operator was a small, ape-like person, wearing the black uniform of an Epsilon-Minus Semi-Moron.

"Roof!"

He opened the doors wide. The bright warmth of the afternoon sun made him jump and squint. "Oh, roof!" he repeated happily. He seemed to have been suddenly and joyfully woken from a dark, blank daze. "Roof!"

He looked up with a kind of eager, dog-like worship at the faces of his passengers. Talking and laughing, they stepped out into the light. The elevator operator watched them go.

"Roof?" he said again, as if asking a question.

Then a bell rang, and from the lift's ceiling, a loudspeaker began to give orders, softly yet authoritatively.

"Go down," it said, "go down. Floor Eighteen. Go down, go down. Floor Eighteen. Go down, go …"

The liftman slammed the gates, pressed a button, and immediately went back into the droning half-light of the shaft, the half-light of his usual daze.

It was warm and bright on the roof. The summer afternoon was sleepy with the buzz of helicopters passing by; and the deeper drone of rocket-planes hurrying, unseen, through the bright sky five or six miles above, felt like a gentle touch on the soft air. Bernard Marx took a deep breath. He looked up at the sky, around the blue horizon, and then down at Lenina's face.

"Isn't it beautiful!" His voice shook a little.

She smiled at him with a look of deep understanding. "Perfect for Obstacle Golf," she answered excitedly. "And now I must fly, Bernard. Henry gets annoyed if I make him wait. Tell me soon about the date." And waving her hand, she ran across the wide, flat roof towards the hangars. Bernard watched the disappearing flash of the white stockings, the tanned knees bending and straightening quickly, again and again, and the softer movement of those well-fitting corduroy shorts under the bottle green jacket. His face looked pained.

"I'd say she was pretty," said a loud, cheerful voice right behind him.

Bernard jumped and turned around. Benito Hoover's round, red face was smiling at him—smiling with obvious friendliness. Benito was known for being good-natured. People said he could have lived his whole life without ever using soma. The anger and bad moods that others needed to escape from never bothered him. For Benito, reality was always bright.

"Bubbly too. Definitely!" Then, in a different voice: "But, hey," he went on, "you look sad! You need a gram of soma." Reaching into his right trouser pocket, Benito pulled out a vial. "One cubic centimeter cures ten sad … But, hey!"

Bernard had suddenly turned and rushed away.

Benito stared after him. "What's wrong with him?" he wondered, and, shaking his head, decided the story about alcohol in the poor guy's blood substitute must be true. "Messed with his brain, I guess."

He put away the soma bottle, took out some sex-hormone chewing gum, popped a piece in his cheek, and walked slowly toward the hangars, thinking.

Henry Foster had his machine rolled out and was already in the cockpit, waiting when Lenina arrived.

"Four minutes late," he said as she climbed in. He started the engines and engaged the helicopter rotors. The machine shot straight up. Henry sped up; the propeller's hum rose from hornet to wasp, then to mosquito; the speedometer showed they were climbing nearly two kilometres a minute. London shrank below. The huge, flat-topped buildings quickly became a bed of geometrical mushrooms sprouting from the green of parks and gardens. Among them, a taller, slender fungus, the Charing-T Tower, lifted a shining concrete disk towards the sky.

Like vague torsos of giant athletes, huge, fleshy clouds lounged in the blue air above. A small scarlet insect suddenly dropped from one, buzzing as it fell.

"There's the Red Rocket," said Henry, "just in from New York." Looking at his watch, he added, "Seven minutes late. These Atlantic services are terribly unreliable."

He eased off the accelerator. The rotors' hum dropped an octave and a half, back through wasp and hornet to bumblebee, cockchafer, and stag-beetle. The machine's upward rush slowed; then they hovered. Henry pushed a lever; there was a click. Slowly at first, then faster, until it was a circular blur, the propeller in front began to spin. The wind of horizontal speed whistled louder. Henry watched the revolution counter; when the needle hit twelve hundred, he disengaged the helicopter rotors. The machine had enough forward speed to fly on its wings.

Lenina looked down through the floor window. They were flying over the six-kilometre zone of parkland separating Central London from its first ring of suburbs. The green was dotted with tiny figures. Forests of Centrifugal Bumble-puppy towers gleamed among the trees. Near Shepherd's Bush, two thousand Beta-Minus mixed doubles were playing Riemann-surface tennis. A double row of Escalator Fives Courts lined the main road from Notting Hill to Willesden. In the Ealing stadium, a Delta gymnastic display and community sing was in progress.

"Khaki is such an awful colour," remarked Lenina, repeating her caste's hypnopaedic prejudices.

The buildings of the Hounslow Feely Studio covered seven and a half hectares. Nearby, a black and khaki army of workers was busy repairing the surface of the Great West Road. One of the huge moving containers was being emptied as they flew over. The melted stone poured out in a bright stream across the road, the asbestos rollers moved back and forth; steam rose in white clouds from the back of an insulated watering cart.

At Brentford, the Television Corporation's factory was like a small town.

"They must be changing shifts," said Lenina.

Like aphids and ants, the leaf-green Gamma girls and the black Semi-Morons swarmed around the entrances or waited in lines to board the monorail trams. Mulberry-coloured Beta-Minuses moved among the crowd. Helicopters were constantly landing on and taking off from the main building's roof.

"Wow," said Lenina, "I'm glad I'm not a Gamma."

Ten minutes later, they were at Stoke Poges and had started their first round of Obstacle Golf.

WITH his eyes mostly downcast, and quickly looking away if he happened to glance at someone, Bernard hurried across the roof. He was like a man being chased, but by enemies he didn't want to see, afraid they might seem even more hostile than he thought, and make him feel guiltier and more alone.

"That horrible Benito Hoover!" And yet, the man had meant well enough. Which, in a way, made it worse. People who meant well acted the same as those who meant harm. Even Lenina was making him suffer. He remembered the weeks of nervous uncertainty, when he had wanted to ask her out but was afraid he wouldn't have the courage. Would he risk being humiliated by her rejection? But if she said yes, what joy! Well, she had said yes, and he was still miserable – miserable that she thought it was a perfect afternoon for Obstacle Golf, that she had gone off to join Henry Foster, that she found it funny he didn't want to talk about their private matters in public. Miserable, in short, because she had acted like any normal, healthy English girl should and not in some other, strange, unusual way.

He opened the door to his private room and called to a couple of lazy Delta-Minus attendants to push his machine out onto the roof. The hangars were staffed by a single Bokanovsky Group, and the men were twins, identically small, black, and ugly. Bernard gave his orders in a sharp, arrogant, and even rude tone, like someone who wasn't sure of their own importance. Dealing with members of the lower castes was always unpleasant for Bernard. Whatever the reason (and the rumors about alcohol in his blood substitute may have been true, since accidents did happen), Bernard's body was only slightly better than the average Gamma. He was eight centimeters shorter than the standard Alpha height and was thin. Contact with lower castes always reminded him of this physical shortcoming. "I am me, and I wish I wasn't"; he was very self-conscious and stressed. Every time he found himself looking into a Delta's face instead of down at it, he felt embarrassed. Would the creature treat him with the respect he deserved? The question bothered him. And for good reason. Gammas, Deltas, and Epsilons had been conditioned to associate physical size with social status. There was even a slight subconscious preference for size. That's why women laughed when he proposed, and why men played jokes on him. The teasing made him feel like an outsider; and feeling like an outsider, he acted like one, which made people dislike him more and increased the contempt and hostility caused by his physical flaws. Which, in turn, made him feel even more alone. A constant fear of being insulted made him avoid people of his own level, and made him act overly dignified around his inferiors. How he envied men like Henry Foster and Benito Hoover! Men who never had to yell at an Epsilon to get an order done; men who took their position for granted; men who moved through the caste system like a fish in water – so comfortable that they didn't even notice themselves or the pleasant environment they were in.

The two attendants slowly and unwillingly wheeled his plane onto the roof, it seemed to him.

"Hurry up!" Bernard said, annoyed. One of them glanced at him. Did he see a kind of animal-like mockery in those blank grey eyes? "Hurry up!" he shouted louder, his voice harsh.

He got into the plane and, a minute later, was flying south toward the river.

The various Propaganda Offices and the College of Emotional Engineering were in one sixty-story building on Fleet Street. In the basement and on the lower floors were the printing presses and offices of the three big London newspapers–The Hourly Radio, a paper for the upper class, the pale green Gamma Gazette, and The Delta Mirror, printed on khaki paper with only one-syllable words. Then came the Television, Feeling Picture, and Synthetic Voice and Music Propaganda Offices–twenty-two floors of them. Above were the research labs and padded rooms where Sound-Track Writers and Synthetic Composers did their careful work. The top eighteen floors were the College of Emotional Engineering.

Bernard landed on the roof of Propaganda House and got out.

"Call Mr. Helmholtz Watson," he told the Gamma-Plus porter, "and tell him Mr. Bernard Marx is waiting for him on the roof."

He sat down and lit a cigarette.

Helmholtz Watson was writing when the message arrived.

"Tell him I'm coming right away," he said and hung up the phone. Then, turning to his secretary, "I'll leave you to put my things away," he said in the same formal and detached way. He ignored her bright smile, got up, and walked quickly to the door.

He was a strong man, with a deep chest, broad shoulders, and a large frame, but he moved quickly, with energy and grace. His strong, round neck held up a well-shaped head. He had dark, curly hair and strong features. He was handsome in a powerful way, and looked, as his secretary always said, every inch an Alpha Plus. He was a lecturer at the College of Emotional Engineering (Writing Department) and, between teaching, he worked as an Emotional Engineer. He wrote often for The Hourly Radio, created feely scenarios, and was very good at making slogans and hypnopaedic rhymes.

"Able," his bosses said. "Maybe," they'd add with headshakes and lowered voices, "a little too able."

They were right; he was a little too able. Helmholtz Watson's mental gifts created issues similar to those Bernard Marx experienced due to a physical flaw. Bernard's lack of size isolated him, and this feeling of being different became a mental burden, causing further separation. Helmholtz's discomfort stemmed from having too much talent, making him feel alone. Both men knew they were unique. But while Bernard had always felt separate, Helmholtz only recently realized his difference as his mental abilities grew. This Escalator-Squash champion, tireless lover (with 640 girls in under four years), and excellent team player had suddenly realized that sports, women, and social events were secondary. He was truly interested in something else. But what? That was what Bernard wanted to discuss—or rather, listen to Helmholtz discuss again.

Three charming women from the Bureau of Propaganda by Synthetic Voice stopped him as he left the elevator.

"Helmholtz, darling, come for a picnic supper on Exmoor." They begged him, clinging to him.

He shook his head and pushed through them. "No, no."

"We're not inviting any other man."

But Helmholtz remained firm. "No," he repeated, "I'm busy." He continued on his way as the girls followed. Only when he was in Bernard's plane and had shut the door did they give up, but not without complaints.

"These women!" he said as the plane took off. He shook his head and frowned. "Awful," Bernard agreed insincerely, wishing he had Helmholtz's success with women and as little effort. He suddenly felt the need to brag. "I'm taking Lenina Crowne to New Mexico," he said casually.

"Are you?" Helmholtz said, showing no interest. After a pause, he added, "I've stopped going to committees and seeing girls these last couple of weeks. The College is making a fuss. But it's worth it, I think. The effects…" He hesitated. "They're strange, very strange."

A physical flaw could create a mental excess. It seemed the reverse was also true: mental excess could cause deliberate isolation, a rejection of the world for its own purposes.

The rest of the short flight was in silence. When they arrived and were relaxing on the air sofas in Bernard's room, Helmholtz began again.

Speaking very slowly, "Did you ever feel," he asked, "like you have something inside you that's just waiting for you to let it out? Some kind of extra power that you're not using–like all the water that goes over the falls instead of through the machines?" He looked at Bernard, waiting for an answer.

"You mean all the feelings you might have if things were different?"

Helmholtz shook his head. "Not exactly. I'm thinking of a strange feeling I sometimes get, like I have something important to say and the ability to say it–but I don't know what it is, and I can't use the power. If there was a different way of writing… Or something else to write about…" He stopped talking; then, "You see," he continued, "I'm good at coming up with phrases–you know, the kind of words that surprise you, almost like you've been poked with a pin, they seem so new and exciting even though they're about something obvious. But that's not enough. It's not enough for the phrases to be good; what you create with them should be good too."

"But your things are good, Helmholtz."

"Oh, as far as they go." Helmholtz shrugged. "But they don't go very far. They're not important enough, somehow. I feel I could do something much more important. Yes, and more intense, more powerful. But what? What is there more important to say? And how can you be powerful about the kind of things you're supposed to write about? Words can be like X-rays, if you use them right–they'll go through anything. You read and you're pierced. That's one of the things I try to teach my students–how to write in a way that pierces. But what good is it to be pierced by an article about a Community Sing, or the newest improvement in scent organs? Besides, can you make words really piercing–like the strongest X-rays–when you're writing about that kind of thing? Can you say something about nothing? That's what it comes down to. I try and I try…"

"Hush!" said Bernard suddenly, and held up a warning finger; they listened. "I think someone's at the door," he whispered.

Helmholtz got up, tiptoed across the room, and quickly threw the door open. Of course, nobody was there.

"I'm sorry," said Bernard, feeling and looking foolish. "I guess I'm a bit on edge. When people are suspicious of you, you start being suspicious of them."

He rubbed his hand across his eyes, sighed, and his voice became whiny. He was trying to justify himself. "If you knew what I've had to deal with lately," he said, almost crying–and his self-pity came rushing out like a fountain. "If you only knew!"

Helmholtz Watson listened with a certain discomfort. "Poor little Bernard!" he thought. But he also felt a bit ashamed for his friend, wishing Bernard would show a little more pride.
