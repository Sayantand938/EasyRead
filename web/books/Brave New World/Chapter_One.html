Brave New World

Chapter One

A short, gray building with only thirty-four floors. Above the main entrance were the words, CENTRAL LONDON HATCHERY AND CONDITIONING CENTRE, and, on a shield, the World State's motto, COMMUNITY, IDENTITY, STABILITY.

The large room on the ground floor faced north. It was cold despite the summer outside the windows, and despite the tropical heat inside. A harsh, weak light shone through the windows, searching for some draped mannequin or pale, scholarly figure, but finding only glass, nickel, and the cold, shiny porcelain of a laboratory. The coldness outside matched the coldness inside. The workers wore white overalls, and their hands were covered with pale, corpse-colored rubber gloves. The light seemed frozen, dead, like a ghost. Only the yellow barrels of the microscopes gave it some rich, living substance, like butter spread along the polished tubes, thick streaks fading into the distance down the work tables.

"And this," said the Director, opening the door, "is the Fertilizing Room."

Bent over their instruments, three hundred Fertilizers were deeply absorbed as the Director of Hatcheries and Conditioning entered. The room was almost silent, filled only with the absent-minded humming or whistling of people completely focused on their work. A group of new students, very young, pink, and inexperienced, followed the Director nervously and humbly. Each carried a notebook, in which they frantically scribbled whenever the great man spoke. They were getting information straight from the source, a rare privilege. The D. H. C. for Central London always made a point of personally showing his new students around the various departments.

"Just to give you a general idea," he would explain. Of course, they needed some general idea to do their work intelligently, but as little as possible if they were to be good and happy members of society. Everyone knows that details lead to virtue and happiness, while general ideas are a necessary evil. Society is built on craftsmen and collectors, not philosophers.

"Tomorrow," he would add, smiling with a slightly threatening friendliness, "you'll start serious work. You won't have time for generalities then. But for now…"

For now, it was a privilege to get information straight from the source and write it down. The boys scribbled like crazy.

Tall, somewhat thin, but standing straight, the Director walked into the room. He had a long chin and large, somewhat prominent teeth, which were covered by his full, red lips when he wasn't talking. Old or young? Thirty? Fifty? Fifty-five? It was hard to tell. And it didn't matter anyway; in this year of stability, A. F. 632, no one thought to ask.

"I shall begin at the beginning," said the D.H.C., and the keenest students wrote down his intention: Begin at the beginning. "These," he gestured, "are the incubators." Opening an insulated door, he showed them and explained, "at blood heat; while the male cells," he opened another door, "must be kept at thirty-five instead of thirty-seven. Full blood heat makes them infertile." Rams in warm wraps have no lambs.

Still leaning against the incubators, he gave them a short description of the modern process of fertilization while the pencils scribbled quickly. He spoke first of its surgical start—"the operation done willingly for the good of society, plus a bonus of six months' pay." He then described how they keep the removed ovary alive and growing. He moved on to the best temperature, salt level, and thickness; mentioned the liquid where the separated, ripe eggs were kept. Leading them to the work tables, he showed how this liquid was drawn from the test tubes, dropped onto warmed microscope slides, and how the eggs were checked for problems, counted, and moved to a porous container. He then showed them how this container was put in a warm broth with free-swimming sperm—at least one hundred thousand per cubic centimeter, he stressed. After ten minutes, the container was lifted out and checked again. If any eggs weren't fertilized, it was put back in, maybe more than once. The fertilized eggs went back to the incubators. The Alphas and Betas stayed there until bottled, while the Gammas, Deltas, and Epsilons were taken out after thirty-six hours for Bokanovsky's Process.

"Bokanovsky's Process," the Director repeated, and the students underlined it.

One egg, one embryo, one normal adult. But a bokanovskified egg will bud, grow, divide. From eight to ninety-six buds, each becoming a perfect embryo, and each embryo a full-sized adult. Making ninety-six people grow where only one did before. Progress.

"Basically," the D.H.C. said, "bokanovskification is stopping development at certain points. We slow the normal growth, and the egg buds in response."

Buds in response. The pencils moved fast.

He pointed. A rack of test tubes moved slowly into a large metal box, and another rack came out. Machines hummed softly. It took eight minutes for the tubes to go through, he said. Eight minutes of strong X-rays is about all an egg can take. A few died; the hardiest split into two; most made four buds; some eight. All went back to the incubators, where the buds grew. After two days, they were suddenly chilled and stopped. Two, four, eight, the buds budded in turn. After budding, they were almost killed with alcohol, so they budded again. Once they had budded—bud out of bud—they were left to grow in peace, since more stopping was usually fatal. By then, the original egg could become anywhere from eight to ninety-six embryos—a huge improvement on nature. Identical twins—not just two or three like in the old days when an egg might split by accident, but dozens, scores at a time.

"Scores," the Director repeated, throwing out his arms as if giving gifts. "Scores."

But one student was foolish enough to ask what the advantage was.

"My good boy!" The Director turned on him. "Can't you see? Can't you see?" He raised a hand, looking serious. "Bokanovsky's Process is a key tool for social stability!"

Key tools for keeping society stable.

Typical men and women; all the same. A whole small factory staffed by people from a single divided egg.

"Ninety-six identical twins running ninety-six identical machines!" The voice shook with excitement. "You really know where you stand. For the first time ever." He said the world's motto. "Community, Identity, Stability." Important words. "If we could keep dividing eggs forever, all our problems would be solved."

Solved by standard Gammas, unchanging Deltas, uniform Epsilons. Millions of identical twins. Mass production finally used in biology.

"But, sadly," the Director said, shaking his head, "we can't divide eggs forever."

Ninety-six seemed to be the most; seventy-two was a good average. To make as many identical twins as possible from the same ovary and sperm–that was the best they could do (though not ideal). And even that was hard.

"Because in nature, it takes thirty years for two hundred eggs to mature. But we need to stabilize the population right now. Producing twins slowly over twenty-five years–what good would that do?"

Clearly, no good at all. But Podsnap's Technique had greatly sped up the maturing process. They could be sure of at least a hundred and fifty mature eggs in two years. Fertilize and divide them–multiply by seventy-two–and you get about eleven thousand brothers and sisters in a hundred and fifty groups of identical twins, all born within two years.

"And sometimes we can get over fifteen thousand adults from one ovary."

He waved over a young, fair-haired man who was walking by. "Mr. Foster," he called. The young man came over. "Can you tell us the record for a single ovary, Mr. Foster?"

"Sixteen thousand and twelve in this Centre," Mr. Foster answered quickly. He spoke fast, had bright blue eyes, and clearly loved quoting numbers. "Sixteen thousand and twelve; in one hundred and eighty-nine groups of identicals. But they've done even better," he continued excitedly, "in some of the tropical Centres. Singapore has often produced over sixteen thousand five hundred; and Mombasa has actually reached seventeen thousand. But they have advantages we don't. You should see how a black woman's ovary responds to pituitary! It's amazing, especially if you're used to working with European material. Still," he added, with a laugh (but his eyes showed he was ready to compete, and he raised his chin as if challenging them), "still, we plan to beat them if we can. I'm working on a great Delta-Minus ovary right now. Only eighteen months old. Over twelve thousand seven hundred children already, either born or in development. And it's still going strong. We'll beat them yet."

"That's the attitude I like!" the Director exclaimed, patting Mr. Foster on the shoulder. "Come with us, and share your knowledge with these students."

Mr. Foster smiled modestly. "Gladly." They left.

In the Bottling Room, everything was a smooth and busy operation. Pieces of fresh pig lining, cut to the right size, shot up in small elevators from the Organ Store in the basement. A quick whirr and then, click! the elevator doors popped open; the bottle-liner simply reached out, took the lining, put it in, smoothed it down, and before the lined bottle had moved too far along the endless belt, whirr, click! another piece of lining had shot up from below, ready to be placed into another bottle, the next in the slow, never-ending line on the belt. Next to the Liners were the Matriculators. The line moved forward; one by one, the eggs were moved from their test tubes to the larger containers; the peritoneal lining was carefully slit, the group of cells dropped in, the salt water poured in… and the bottle had already passed, and it was the labellers' turn. Heredity, date of fertilization, Bokanovsky Group number – details were copied from test tube to bottle. No longer unknown, but named, identified, the line moved slowly on; through an opening in the wall, slowly into the Social Predestination Room.

"Eighty-eight cubic metres of card-index," Mr. Foster said with pleasure, as they entered.

"Containing all the important information," the Director added.

"Updated every morning."

"And put in order every afternoon."

"Based on which they make their calculations."

"So many people, of this or that type," Mr. Foster said.

"Given out in these amounts."

"The best Pouring Speed at any moment."

"Any unexpected losses quickly replaced."

"Quickly," Mr. Foster repeated. "If you knew how much extra time I had to work after the last Japanese earthquake!" He laughed kindly and shook his head.

"The Predestinators send their numbers to the Fertilizers."

"Who give them the embryos they request."

"And the bottles come here to be predestined carefully."

"After that, they're sent down to the Embryo Store."

"Where we're going now."

And opening a door, Mr. Foster led the way down a staircase to the basement.

The temperature was still tropical. They went down into a growing darkness. Two doors and a passage with a double turn kept the cellar safe from any daylight.

"Embryos are like photograph film," said Mr. Foster jokingly, as he pushed open the second door. "They can only handle red light."

And indeed, the hot darkness into which the students now followed him was red, like the darkness you see when you close your eyes on a summer afternoon. The bulging sides of rows and rows and levels of bottles sparkled with countless rubies, and among the rubies moved the faint red figures of men and women with purple eyes and signs of lupus. The faint hum and rattle of machinery stirred the air.

"Give them some numbers, Mr. Foster," said the Director, who was tired of talking.

Mr. Foster was very happy to give them some numbers.

Two hundred and twenty metres long, two hundred wide, ten high. He pointed upwards. Like chickens drinking, the students looked up at the distant ceiling. Three levels of racks: ground floor, first level, second level.

The thin steel framework of level above level disappeared into the dark in every direction. Near them, three red figures were busy unloading large bottles from a moving staircase.

The escalator from the Social Predestination Room.

Each bottle could be put on one of fifteen racks, each rack, though you couldn't see it, was a conveyor moving at thirty-three and a third centimetres an hour. Two hundred and sixty-seven days at eight metres a day. Two thousand one hundred and thirty-six metres in total. One trip around the cellar at ground level, one on the first level, half on the second, and on the two hundred and sixty-seventh morning, daylight in the Decanting Room. Independent existence–so called.

"But in that time," Mr. Foster finished, "we've managed to do a lot to them. Oh, a great deal." He laughed knowingly and triumphantly.

"That's the spirit I like," said the Director again. "Let's walk around. You tell them everything, Mr. Foster."

Mr. Foster did as he was asked.

He told them about the growing embryo on its bed of tissue. He let them taste the rich blood substitute it fed on. He explained why it needed to be stimulated with certain chemicals. He told them about the extract from a part of the ovary. He showed them the jets that automatically injected it every twelve meters from zero to 2040. He spoke of the increasing doses of a hormone given during the final ninety-six meters. He described the artificial mother's blood flow in every bottle at Meter 112; showed them the blood substitute tank, the pump that kept the liquid moving over the placenta and through the artificial lung and filter. He mentioned the embryo's tendency to become anemic, and the large doses of animal stomach extract and young horse liver it needed as a result.

He showed them the simple way that all the embryos were shaken to get them used to movement during the last two meters of every eight. He hinted at the seriousness of being "decanted," or taken out of the bottle, and listed the precautions taken to reduce that shock by training the embryo. He told them about the test for sex near Meter 200. He explained the labeling system–a T for males, a circle for females, and a question mark for those made infertile.

"Because," said Mr. Foster, "fertility is mostly a problem. One fertile ovary in twelve hundred would be enough for us. But we want choices. And we need to be safe. So we allow thirty percent of the female embryos to develop normally. The others get a dose of male hormone every twenty-four meters for the rest of the time. As a result, they are born infertile, structurally normal but sterile. Guaranteed sterile. Which brings us," continued Mr. Foster, "out of simply copying nature and into the more interesting world of human invention."

He rubbed his hands. They didn't just hatch embryos; any cow could do that.

"We also decide their fate and train them. We decant our babies as social beings, as Alphas or Epsilons, future sewage workers or future…" He was going to say "future World controllers," but corrected himself and said "future Directors of Hatcheries" instead. The D.H.C. smiled at the compliment.

They were passing Meter 320 on Rack 11. A young Beta-Minus mechanic was working on the blood substitute pump of a passing bottle with a screwdriver and wrench. The hum of the motor deepened as he turned the nuts. Down, down… A final twist, a look at the counter, and he was done. He moved down the line and began on the next pump.

"Reducing the revolutions per minute," Mr. Foster explained. "The substitute goes around slower, so it passes through the lung less often, giving the embryo less oxygen. A lack of oxygen keeps an embryo from reaching its full potential." He rubbed his hands again.

"But why do you want to keep the embryo below par?" asked a naive student.

"Fool!" said the Director, breaking a long silence. "Hasn't it occurred to you that an Epsilon embryo needs an Epsilon environment as well as Epsilon genes?"

It clearly hadn't occurred to him. He looked completely confused.

"The lower the caste," said Mr. Foster, "the less oxygen." The first organ affected was the brain. After that, the skeleton. At seventy per cent of normal oxygen, you got dwarfs. At less than seventy, eyeless monsters.

"Who are no use at all," Mr. Foster concluded.

But (his voice became confidential and eager), if they could discover a way to shorten the time it takes to mature, what a triumph, what a gift to Society!

"Consider the horse."

They considered it.

Mature at six; the elephant at ten. While at thirteen, a man is not yet sexually mature; and is only fully grown at twenty. Hence, of course, that result of delayed development, human intelligence.

"But in Epsilons," said Mr. Foster very fairly, "we don't need human intelligence."

Didn't need it and didn't get it. But though the Epsilon mind was mature at ten, the Epsilon body was not fit to work till eighteen. Long years of unnecessary and wasted immaturity. If physical development could be sped up until it was as quick as a cow's, what an enormous saving to the Community!

"Amazing!" the students whispered. Mr. Foster's excitement was contagious.

He got quite technical, talking about the unusual hormone balance that made people grow so slowly. He suggested a genetic change to explain it. Could this genetic change be reversed? Could an Epsilon embryo be changed back to the normal growth rate of dogs and cows using a special method? That was the question. And they were close to solving it.

Pilkington, in Mombasa, had created people who were sexually mature at four and fully grown at six and a half. A scientific success. But not useful for society. Six-year-old men and women were too unintelligent to even do Epsilon work. And the process was all or nothing: either you changed nothing, or you changed everything. They were still trying to find the perfect balance between adults of twenty and adults of six. So far, they hadn't succeeded. Mr. Foster sighed and shook his head.

Their walk through the red light had taken them near Metre 170 on Rack 9. From this point, Rack 9 was enclosed, and the bottles continued their journey in a tunnel, broken up by openings every few metres.

"Heat conditioning," said Mr. Foster.

Hot tunnels followed cool tunnels. The coolness was combined with discomfort in the form of strong X-rays. By the time they were born, the embryos hated the cold. They were destined to move to the tropics, to be miners, acetate silk spinners, and steel workers. Later, their minds would be taught to agree with their bodies' preferences. "We train them to enjoy the heat," Mr. Foster concluded. "Our colleagues upstairs will teach them to love it."

"And that," the Director added importantly, "is the secret to happiness and good behavior – enjoying what you have to do. All conditioning aims for that: making people like the social role they can't escape."

In a space between two tunnels, a nurse was carefully inserting a long, thin syringe into the jelly-like contents of a passing bottle. The students and their guides watched her silently for a few moments.

"Well, Lenina," said Mr. Foster, when she finally pulled out the syringe and stood up straight.

The girl turned, startled. Despite the skin condition and her purple eyes, she was remarkably pretty.

"Henry!" Her smile flashed at him–a row of bright red teeth.

"Charming, charming," murmured the Director and, patting her a few times, received a respectful smile in return.

"What are you giving them?" asked Mr. Foster, his voice very professional.

"Oh, the usual typhoid and sleeping sickness."

"Tropical workers start getting shots at Meter 150," Mr. Foster told the students. "The embryos still have gills. We protect the fish from the future man's diseases." Then, turning back to Lenina, "Ten to five on the roof this afternoon," he said, "as usual."

"Charming," said the Director again, and, with a final pat, walked away after the others.

On Rack 10, rows of future chemical workers were being trained to handle lead, caustic soda, tar, chlorine. The first of a group of two hundred and fifty young rocket-plane engineers was passing the eleven hundred meter mark on Rack 3. A special device kept their containers turning. "To improve their balance," Mr. Foster explained. "Fixing a rocket outside in mid-air is tricky. We slow the blood flow when they're right side up, so they're hungry, and double the fake blood when they're upside down. They learn to connect being upside down with feeling good; they're only really happy when they're standing on their heads.

"And now," Mr. Foster went on, "I want to show you some interesting training for Alpha Plus Intellectuals. We have a big group of them on Rack 5. First Gallery level," he called to two boys who had started to go down to the ground floor.

"They're around Meter 900," he explained. "You can't really do any useful brain training until the fetuses have lost their tails. Follow me."

But the Director looked at his watch. "Ten to three," he said. "No time for the brainy embryos, I'm afraid. We must go up to the Nurseries before the children wake up from their afternoon nap." Mr. Foster was disappointed. "At least a quick look at the Decanting Room," he asked.

"Very well then." The Director smiled kindly. "Just one look."
