Brave New World

Chapter Two

MR. FOSTER was left in the Decanting Room. The D.H.C. and his students entered the closest elevator and went up to the fifth floor.

BABY NURSERIES. NEO-PAVLOVIAN LEARNING ROOMS, the sign read.

The Director opened a door. They were in a large, empty room, very bright and sunny because the entire south wall was a single window. Six nurses, wearing the standard white viscose-linen uniform of pants and jackets, with their hair covered by white caps, were placing bowls of roses in a long line across the floor. Large bowls, filled with blossoms. Thousands of petals, fully open and silky, like the cheeks of countless little angels, but in that bright light, not just pink and Aryan angels, but also glowing Chinese, also Mexican, also red-faced from blowing heavenly trumpets, also pale as death, pale like white marble after death.

The nurses stood up straight when the D.H.C. entered.

"Put out the books," he said briefly.

The nurses silently did as he said. Between the bowls of roses, the books were placed–a line of nursery books open to a brightly colored picture of an animal, fish, or bird.

"Now bring in the children."

They hurried out of the room and returned in a minute or two, each pushing a tall cart with four wire shelves, each shelf holding eight-month-old babies, all identical (obviously a Bokanovsky Group) and all (since they were Delta caste) wearing khaki.

"Put them down on the floor."

The babies were taken out.

"Now turn them so they can see the flowers and books."

Turned, the babies immediately became quiet, then started to crawl towards the bright colors and cheerful shapes on the white pages. As they got closer, the sun came out from behind a cloud for a moment. The roses seemed to burst into flame with a sudden inner passion; the shining pages of the books seemed filled with new and deep meaning. The crawling babies made little squeals of excitement, gurgles and happy sounds.

The Director rubbed his hands. "Excellent!" he said. "It almost looks like it was planned."

The fastest crawlers had already reached their goal. Small hands reached out carefully, touched, grabbed, pulling petals off the bright roses, crumpling the colorful pages of the books. The Director waited until they were all happily busy. Then, "Watch carefully," he said. And, raising his hand, he gave the signal.

The Head Nurse, who was standing by a switchboard at the other end of the room, pressed down a small lever.

There was a loud explosion. A siren shrieked, louder and louder. Alarm bells rang like crazy.

The children jumped, screamed; their faces twisted with terror.

"And now," the Director shouted (because the noise was deafening), "now we'll drive the lesson home with a mild electric shock."

He waved his hand again, and the Head Nurse pressed a second lever. The babies' screaming suddenly changed. There was something desperate, almost insane, about the sharp, jerky yelps they now made. Their little bodies twitched and stiffened; their limbs moved jerkily as if pulled by unseen wires.

"We can electrify that whole strip of floor," the Director shouted to explain. "But that's enough," he signaled to the nurse.

The explosions stopped, the bells stopped ringing, the siren's shriek faded into silence. The stiffly twitching bodies relaxed, and what had been the sob and yelp of infant maniacs broadened out once more into a normal howl of ordinary terror.

"Offer them the flowers and the books again."

The nurses obeyed; but when the roses came near, at the mere sight of those brightly colored pictures of cats, roosters, and black sheep, the infants shrank back in horror, their howling suddenly increased.

"Observe," said the Director triumphantly, "observe."

Books and loud noises, flowers and electric shocks–already in the infant mind these pairs were damagingly linked; and after two hundred repetitions of the same or a similar lesson would be permanently joined. What man has joined, nature cannot separate.

"They'll grow up with what the psychologists used to call an 'instinctive' hatred of books and flowers. Reflexes that can't be changed. They'll be safe from books and botany all their lives." The Director turned to his nurses. "Take them away again."

Still yelling, the khaki babies were loaded onto their dumbwaiters and wheeled out, leaving behind them the smell of sour milk and a welcome silence.

One of the students raised his hand; and though he understood why lower-class people shouldn't waste the Community's time on books, and that there was always the risk of them reading something that might negatively change one of their reflexes, yet … well, he couldn't understand about the flowers. Why bother making it psychologically impossible for Deltas to like flowers?

The D.H.C. explained patiently. Making the children scream at the sight of a rose was based on important economic strategy. Not long ago (about a century), Gammas, Deltas, and even Epsilons were taught to like flowers and nature in general. The goal was to make them want to go to the countryside whenever possible, forcing them to use transportation.

"And didn't they use transportation?" the student asked.

"Quite a bit," the D.H.C. replied. "But nothing else."

He explained that primroses and landscapes have a major flaw: they are free. A love for nature doesn't keep factories busy. So, they decided to get rid of the love of nature, at least among the lower classes, but not the need to use transportation. It was important for them to keep going to the countryside, even if they hated it. The challenge was to find a better economic reason for using transportation than just liking primroses and landscapes. They found it.

"We teach the masses to hate the countryside," the Director concluded. "But we also teach them to love all country sports. At the same time, we make sure that all country sports require a lot of equipment. So, they use manufactured goods as well as transportation. That's why we use electric shocks."

"I see," said the student, quietly admiring.

There was a silence. Then, clearing his throat, "Once upon a time," the Director began, "when Ford was still alive, there was a little boy named Reuben Rabinovitch. Reuben's parents spoke Polish."

The Director stopped himself. "You know what Polish is, right?"

"A dead language."

"Like French and German," another student added, showing off.

"And 'parent'?" the D.H.C. asked.

There was an uneasy silence. Several of the boys blushed. They hadn't yet learned to tell the important but often subtle difference between dirty talk and pure science. Finally, one had the courage to raise a hand.

"Human beings used to be …" he hesitated; his face turned red. "Well, they used to give birth to live babies."

"Quite right." The Director nodded approvingly.

"And when the babies were decanted …"

"'Born,'" came the correction.

"Well, then they were the parents–I mean, not the babies, of course; the other ones." The poor boy was very confused.

"In short," the Director summed up, "the parents were the father and the mother." The dirty talk that was actually science landed heavily in the boys' silent, averted gazes. "Mother," he repeated loudly, emphasizing the science; and, leaning back in his chair, "These," he said seriously, "are unpleasant facts; I know it. But most facts from history are unpleasant."

He returned to Little Reuben–to Little Reuben, in whose room, one evening, by accident, his father and mother (crash, crash!) happened to leave the radio on.

("Remember that in those days of crude live birth, children were always raised by their parents and not in State Conditioning Centers.")

While the child slept, a broadcast from London suddenly came on; and the next morning, to the surprise of his parents (the bolder boys dared to grin at each other), Little Reuben woke up repeating word for word a long lecture by that strange old writer ("one of the few whose works have survived"), George Bernard Shaw, who was, according to a reliable story, talking about his own genius. To Little Reuben's parents, this lecture was, of course, completely meaningless, and thinking their child had suddenly gone crazy, they called a doctor. He, luckily, understood English, recognized the speech as the one Shaw had broadcast the night before, realized what it meant, and wrote a letter to the medical journal about it.

"The idea of sleep-teaching, or hypnopaedia, had been discovered." The D.H.C. paused impressively.

The idea had been discovered, but many years would pass before it was put to good use.

"Little Reuben's case happened only twenty-three years after Ford's first Model T came out." (Here the Director made the sign of the T on his stomach, and all the students respectfully copied him.) "And yet…"

The students scribbled furiously. "Hypnopaedia, first officially used in A.F. 214. Why not sooner? Two reasons. (a)…"

"These early researchers," the D.H.C. said, "were on the wrong path. They believed hypnopaedia could be used for intellectual education…"

(A small boy asleep on his side, his right arm out, his hand hanging over the edge of the bed. Through a round opening in the side of a box, a voice speaks softly.

"The Nile is the longest river in Africa and the second longest in the world. Although it is shorter than the Mississippi-Missouri, the Nile's basin is the largest, stretching across 35 degrees of latitude…"

At breakfast the next morning, "Tommy," someone asks, "do you know which is the longest river in Africa?" He shakes his head. "But don't you remember something that starts: The Nile is the…"

"The - Nile - is - the - longest - river - in - Africa - and - the - second - longest - in - the - world…" The words spill out. "Although - it - is - shorter - than…"

"Well now, which is the longest river in Africa?"

The eyes are blank. "I don't know."

"But the Nile, Tommy."

"The - Nile - is - the - longest - river - in - Africa - and - second …"

"Then which river is the longest, Tommy?"

Tommy burst into tears. "I don't know," he howls.)

That howl, the Director explained, discouraged the first researchers. The experiments stopped. No more attempts were made to teach children the length of the Nile while they slept. And rightly so. You can't learn a science if you don't know what it's about.

"But, if they had only started with moral education," said the Director, heading towards the door. The students followed, writing quickly as they walked and all the way up in the lift. "Moral education, which should never be rational, no matter what."

"Silence, silence," whispered a loudspeaker as they stepped out on the fourteenth floor, and "Silence, silence," the trumpet mouths tirelessly repeated down every corridor. The students and even the Director rose onto their toes without thinking. They were Alphas, of course, but even Alphas had been well trained. "Silence, silence." All the air on the fourteenth floor hissed with the strict command. After tiptoeing for fifty yards, they reached a door that the Director carefully opened. They stepped into the dim light of a darkened dormitory. Eighty cots stood in a row against the wall. There was a sound of light, steady breathing and a constant murmur, like very quiet voices whispering far away.

A nurse stood up as they entered and stood straight before the Director.

"What's the lesson this afternoon?" he asked.

"We had Basic Sex for the first forty minutes," she answered. "But now it's switched over to Basic Class Awareness."

The Director walked slowly down the long line of cots. Rosy and relaxed from sleep, eighty little boys and girls lay softly breathing. There was a whisper under every pillow. The D.H.C. stopped and, bending over one of the little beds, listened closely.

"Basic Class Awareness, did you say? Let's have it repeated a little louder by the trumpet."

At the end of the room a loud speaker stuck out from the wall. The Director walked up to it and pressed a switch.

"… all wear green," said a soft but very clear voice, starting in the middle of a sentence, "and Delta Children wear khaki. Oh no, I don't want to play with Delta children. And Epsilons are even worse. They're too stupid to read or write. Besides they wear black, which is such a horrible colour. I'm so glad I'm a Beta."

There was a pause; then the voice began again.

"Alpha children wear grey. They work much harder than we do, because they're so very clever. I'm really very glad I'm a Beta, because I don't work so hard. And then we are much better than the Gammas and Deltas. Gammas are stupid. They all wear green, and Delta children wear khaki. Oh no, I don't want to play with Delta children. And Epsilons are even worse. They're too stupid to be able …"

The Director pushed back the switch. The voice was silent. Only its faint ghost continued to mutter from beneath the eighty pillows.

"They'll have that repeated forty or fifty times more before they wake; then again on Thursday, and again on Saturday. A hundred and twenty times three times a week for thirty months. After which they go on to a more complex lesson."

Roses and electric shocks, the khaki of Deltas and a hint of asafoetida–forever linked before the child can talk. But training without words is rough and general; it can't teach the small differences, can't instill the more complicated ways of acting. For that, there must be words, but words without explanation. In short, sleep-teaching.

"The greatest force for teaching morals and fitting into society of all time."

The students wrote it down in their notebooks. Straight from the expert.

Again, the Director touched the switch.

"… so terribly smart," the soft, persuasive, tireless voice was saying, "I'm really very glad I'm a Beta, because …"

Not so much like drops of water, though water can wear holes in the hardest rock; rather, drops of liquid wax, drops that stick, coat, become part of what they fall on, until the rock is all one red lump.

"Until the child's mind is these suggestions, and the total of the suggestions is the child's mind. And not just the child's mind. The adult's mind too–all his life. The mind that judges and wants and decides–made up of these suggestions. But all these suggestions are our suggestions!" The Director almost yelled in his excitement. "Suggestions from the State." He hit the nearest table. "Therefore …"

A noise made him turn around. "Oh, Ford!" he said in a different voice, "I've woken the children."
