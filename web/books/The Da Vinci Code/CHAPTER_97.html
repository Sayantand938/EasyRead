The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 97

Over three thousand people are buried or honored inside Westminster Abbey. The huge stone building is filled with the remains of kings, leaders, scientists, poets, and musicians. Their tombs, squeezed into every space, range from grand mausoleums—like that of Queen Elizabeth I, whose covered coffin sits in its own private chapel—to simple floor tiles with worn inscriptions. These worn inscriptions leave you wondering whose remains might lie below in the undercroft.

Westminster Abbey, designed like the great cathedrals of Amiens, Chartres, and Canterbury, is neither a cathedral nor a regular church. It's a royal peculiar, meaning it answers only to the King or Queen. Since William the Conqueror's coronation on Christmas Day in 1066, this stunning place has hosted countless royal events and important ceremonies—from making Edward the Confessor a saint, to the wedding of Prince Andrew and Sarah Ferguson, to the funerals of Henry V, Queen Elizabeth I, and Lady Diana.

However, Robert Langdon wasn't interested in the abbey's history, except for one event—the funeral of the British knight Sir Isaac Newton.

In London lies a knight a Pope interred.

Rushing through the large entrance on the north side, Langdon and Sophie were greeted by guards who politely led them through a metal detector—now common in London's historic buildings. They passed through without setting off the alarm and went to the abbey entrance.

As Langdon stepped into Westminster Abbey, the outside world vanished. No traffic noise. No rain. Just a deep silence that seemed to echo back and forth, as if the building were whispering to itself. Langdon and Sophie, like most visitors, immediately looked up at the abbey's towering height. Gray stone columns rose like tall trees into the shadows, curving gracefully over vast spaces before reaching back down to the stone floor. The wide area of the north side stretched out like a canyon, with high walls of stained glass on either side. On sunny days, the abbey floor was a colorful display of light. Today, the rain and darkness gave this large space a ghostly feel, more like the crypt it truly was.

"It's almost empty," Sophie whispered.

Langdon was disappointed. He had hoped for more people, a more public place. He didn't want to repeat their experience in the deserted Temple Church. He had expected to feel safe in the popular tourist spot, but Langdon remembered the crowds and bright abbey from summer visits. Today was a rainy April morning. Instead of crowds and colorful glass, Langdon saw only empty floor space and shadowy areas.

"We went through metal detectors," Sophie reminded, sensing Langdon's worry. "If anyone's here, they can't have weapons."

Langdon nodded but still felt uneasy. He had wanted to bring the London police, but Sophie's fear of who might be involved stopped him from contacting them. We need to get the cryptex back, Sophie had said. It's the key to everything. She was right, of course.

The key to getting Leigh back alive.

The key to finding the Holy Grail.

The key to learning who's behind this.

Unfortunately, their only chance to get the keystone back seemed to be now... at Isaac Newton's tomb. Whoever had the cryptex would have to visit the tomb to figure out the final clue, and if they hadn't already come and gone, Sophie and Langdon planned to stop them.

Walking toward the left wall to get out of sight, they went into a hidden side aisle behind some pillars. Langdon couldn't forget the image of Leigh Teabing being held prisoner, probably tied up in his own limousine. Whoever had ordered the top Priory members killed wouldn't hesitate to get rid of anyone who got in their way. It seemed unfair that Teabing—a modern British knight—was a hostage in the search for his own countryman, Sir Isaac Newton.

"Which way is it?" Sophie asked, looking around.

The tomb. Langdon had no idea. "We should find a guide and ask." Langdon knew better than to wander around aimlessly. Westminster Abbey was a confusing maze of tombs, side rooms, and burial spots. Like the Louvre's Grand Gallery, it had only one entrance—the door they had just come through—easy to get in, but impossible to find your way out. A real tourist trap, one of Langdon's confused colleagues had called it. Following architectural tradition, the abbey was shaped like a giant cross. But unlike most churches, its entrance was on the side, not at the back of the church through the usual entrance. Also, the abbey had a series of large cloisters attached. One wrong turn through the wrong archway, and a visitor would get lost in a maze of outdoor passages surrounded by high walls.

"Guides wear red robes," Langdon said, walking toward the center of the church. Looking sideways across the tall, gold altar to the far end of the south transept, Langdon saw several people crawling on their hands and knees. This bowing pilgrimage was common in Poets' Corner, though it was less holy than it seemed. Tourists making copies of the grave markings.

"I don't see any guides," Sophie said. "Maybe we can find the tomb ourselves?" Without a word, Langdon led her a few more steps to the center of the abbey and pointed to the right.

Sophie gasped, looking down the long center aisle of the abbey, now able to see the whole building. "Aah," she said. "Let's find someone who knows about this place."

Just then, a hundred yards away, hidden behind the choir screen, the grand tomb of Sir Isaac Newton had a single visitor. The Teacher had been studying the monument for ten minutes.

Newton's tomb was a huge black-marble coffin with a sculpture of Sir Isaac Newton on top. He was dressed in old-style clothes and leaning proudly against a stack of his own books: Divinity, Chronology, Opticks, and Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica. At Newton's feet were two winged boys holding a scroll. Behind Newton was a plain pyramid. The pyramid seemed strange, but the thing that really caught the Teacher's eye was the giant shape halfway up the pyramid.

A sphere.

The Teacher thought about Saunière's puzzling riddle. Find the sphere that should be on his tomb. The large sphere sticking out from the pyramid showed many heavenly bodies—constellations, zodiac signs, comets, stars, and planets. Above it was a picture of the Goddess of Astronomy under a sky full of stars.

So many spheres.

The Teacher had thought that finding the missing sphere would be easy once he found the tomb. Now he wasn't so sure. He was looking at a complicated map of the sky. Was there a missing planet? Had a star been left out of a constellation? He had no idea. Still, the Teacher suspected the answer would be surprisingly simple—"a knight a pope interred." What sphere am I looking for? Surely, you didn't need to be an expert in space science to find the Holy Grail, did you?

It speaks of Rosy flesh and seeded womb.

The Teacher was interrupted by some tourists coming closer. He slipped the cryptex back into his pocket and watched carefully as the visitors went to a nearby table, dropped some money in the cup, and got more of the free supplies the abbey provided for rubbing designs from the graves. With fresh charcoal pencils and large sheets of paper, they headed toward the front of the abbey, probably to the popular Poets' Corner to rub the graves of Chaucer, Tennyson, and Dickens.

Alone again, he moved closer to the tomb, looking at it from top to bottom. He started with the clawed feet under the coffin, moved up past Newton, past his science books, past the two boys with their mathematical scroll, up the pyramid to the giant sphere with its constellations, and finally up to the star-filled ceiling of the alcove.

What object should be here... but is not? He touched the cryptex in his pocket, as if he could somehow guess the answer from Saunière's marble puzzle. Only five letters stand between me and the Grail.

He began to walk near the choir screen, took a deep breath, and looked up the long nave toward the main altar far away. His eyes moved from the gold altar down to the bright red robe of a church guide being called over by two very familiar people. Langdon and Neveu.

Calmly, the Teacher took two steps back behind the choir screen. That was fast. He knew Langdon and Sophie would eventually figure out the poem and come to Newton's tomb, but not this quickly. Taking a deep breath, the Teacher thought about what to do. He was used to dealing with surprises. I have the cryptex.

Reaching into his pocket, he touched the second thing that made him feel confident: the Medusa revolver. As expected, the abbey's metal detectors had gone off when the Teacher walked through with the hidden gun. Also as expected, the guards had immediately stepped back when the Teacher glared angrily and showed his ID card. Official status always got respect.

The Teacher had originally hoped to solve the cryptex alone and avoid problems, but now he felt that Langdon and Neveu's arrival was actually a good thing. Since he wasn't getting anywhere with the "orb" clue, he might be able to use their knowledge. After all, if Langdon had figured out the poem to find the tomb, he probably knew something about the orb. And if Langdon knew the password, then it would just be a matter of using the right force.

Not here, of course.

Somewhere private.

The Teacher remembered a small sign he had seen on his way into the abbey. He immediately knew the perfect place to trick them into going.

The only question now... what to use to lure them.
