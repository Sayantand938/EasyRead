The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 34

The driver who picked up Bishop Aringarosa from Leonardo da Vinci International Airport arrived in a small, plain black Fiat sedan. Aringarosa remembered when all Vatican vehicles were large luxury cars with medallions and flags showing the Holy See's symbol. Those days are gone. Now, Vatican cars were less showy and almost always unmarked. The Vatican said this was to save money to better help their dioceses, but Aringarosa thought it was more for security. The world had become crazy, and in many parts of Europe, showing you loved Jesus Christ was like putting a target on your car. Pulling his black cassock around him, Aringarosa got into the back seat and prepared for the long drive to Castel Gandolfo. It would be the same trip he had taken five months before. Last year's trip to Rome, he sighed. The longest night of my life. Five months ago, the Vatican had called and asked Aringarosa to come to Rome immediately. They gave no reason. Your tickets are at the airport. The Holy See tried hard to keep an air of mystery, even for its highest clergy.

Aringarosa thought the mysterious summons was probably a chance for the Pope and other Vatican officials to take advantage of Opus Dei's recent public success—the completion of their World Headquarters in New York City. Architectural Digest had called Opus Dei's building "a shining beacon of Catholicism beautifully combined with the modern landscape," and lately the Vatican seemed interested in anything that included the word "modern." Aringarosa had to accept the invitation, though he didn't want to. Not a fan of the current papal administration, Aringarosa, like most conservative clergy, had watched with worry as the new Pope began his first year in office. An unusually liberal man, His Holiness had become Pope through one of the most debated and strange conclaves in Vatican history. Now, instead of being humbled by his unexpected rise to power, the Holy Father had quickly used all the power of his high position in Christendom. With strong liberal support within the College of Cardinals, the Pope was now declaring his papal mission to be "rejuvenation of Vatican doctrine and updating Catholicism into the third millennium."

Aringarosa feared that this meant the man was arrogant enough to think he could rewrite God's laws and win back those who felt true Catholicism was too inconvenient in the modern world.

Aringarosa had been using all his political influence—which was significant given the size of Opus Dei's following and their wealth—to convince the Pope and his advisers that weakening the Church's laws was not only unfaithful and cowardly, but also a bad political move. He reminded them that previous changes to Church law—the Vatican II disaster—had had a terrible result: Church attendance was now lower than ever, donations were decreasing, and there were not enough Catholic priests to manage their churches.

People need structure and guidance from the Church, Aringarosa insisted, not pampering and indulgence!

On that night, months ago, as the Fiat left the airport, Aringarosa was surprised to find they were heading not toward Vatican City but east up a winding mountain road. "Where are we going?" he had asked his driver.

"Alban Hills," the man replied. "Your meeting is at Castel Gandolfo." The Pope's summer residence? Aringarosa had never been there, nor had he wanted to see it. Besides being the Pope's summer home, the sixteenth-century fortress housed the Specula Vaticana—the Vatican Observatory—one of the most advanced astronomical observatories in Europe. Aringarosa had never been comfortable with the Vatican's historical interest in science. Why combine science and faith? Unbiased science could not be done by someone who had faith in God. And faith did not need physical proof of its beliefs.

Nonetheless, there it is, he thought as Castel Gandolfo came into view, rising against a star-filled November sky. From the access road, Gandolfo looked like a large stone monster considering a suicidal jump. Perched on the edge of a cliff, the castle leaned out over the valley where the Curiazi and Orazi clans fought long before Rome was founded.

Even in silhouette, Gandolfo was a sight—an impressive, layered, defensive building, reflecting the power of the dramatic cliffside setting. Sadly, Aringarosa now saw, the Vatican had ruined the building by putting two huge aluminum telescope domes on the roof, making this once dignified building look like a proud warrior wearing party hats. When Aringarosa got out of the car, a young Jesuit priest hurried out and greeted him. "Bishop, welcome. I am Father Mangano. I'm an astronomer here."

Good for you. Aringarosa mumbled his hello and followed his host into the castle's foyer—a wide-open space with a clumsy mix of Renaissance art and astronomy images. Following his escort up the wide marble staircase, Aringarosa saw signs for conference centers, science lecture halls, and tourist information. It amazed him that the Vatican couldn't provide clear, strong guidance for spiritual growth, but somehow still found time to give astrophysics lectures to tourists.

"Tell me," Aringarosa said to the young priest, "when did things get so mixed up?" The priest gave him an odd look. "Sir?"

Aringarosa waved it off, deciding not to start that argument again this evening. The Vatican has gone mad. Like a lazy parent who found it easier to give in to a spoiled child's demands than to stand firm and teach values, the Church kept softening, trying to change itself to fit a culture that had gone wrong.

The top floor's corridor was wide, richly decorated, and led in only one direction—toward huge oak doors with a brass sign.

BIBLIOTECA ASTRONOMICA

Aringarosa had heard of this place—the Vatican's Astronomy Library—said to contain more than twenty-five thousand books, including rare works of Copernicus, Galileo, Kepler, Newton, and Secchi. It was also supposedly the place where the Pope's top officials held private meetings... meetings they preferred not to hold inside Vatican City. Approaching the door, Bishop Aringarosa would never have imagined the shocking news he was about to receive, or the deadly chain of events it would set off. It was only an hour later, as he stumbled from the meeting, that the devastating implications sank in. Six months from now! he had thought. God help us!

Now, seated in the Fiat, Bishop Aringarosa realized his fists were clenched just thinking about that first meeting. He released his grip and took a slow breath, relaxing his muscles. Everything will be fine, he told himself as the Fiat climbed higher into the mountains. Still, he wished his cell phone would ring. Why hasn't the Teacher called me? Silas should have the keystone by now.

Trying to calm his nerves, the bishop focused on the purple amethyst in his ring. Feeling the textures of the miter-crozier design and the facets of the diamonds, he reminded himself that this ring was a symbol of power, but far less than what he would soon gain.
