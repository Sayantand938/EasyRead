The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 11

"Une plaisanterie numérique?" Bezu Fache was furious, staring at Sophie Neveu in disbelief. A numeric joke? "Are you saying Saunière's code is just a silly math joke?"

Fache couldn't believe her nerve. Not only had she barged in on him without permission, but now she was trying to tell him that Saunière, in his last moments, decided to leave a mathematical gag?

"This code," Sophie explained quickly in French, "is so simple it's ridiculous. Jacques Saunière must have known we'd figure it out right away." She took a piece of paper from her sweater pocket and gave it to Fache. "Here's the solution." Fache looked at the paper.

1-1-2-3-5-8-13-21

"That's it?" he snapped. "You just put the numbers in order!" Sophie actually had the nerve to smile. "Exactly." Fache's voice dropped to a low growl. "Agent Neveu, I have no idea what you're getting at, but you better get there quickly." He glanced nervously at Langdon, who was standing nearby with the phone to his ear, still listening to his message from the U.S. Embassy. Langdon looked pale, and Fache guessed the news was bad.

"Captain," Sophie said, sounding almost defiant, "the numbers you're holding are part of one of the most famous mathematical sequences in history." Fache didn't know there was such a thing as a famous mathematical sequence, and he didn't like Sophie's casual tone.

"This is the Fibonacci sequence," she said, nodding at the paper in Fache's hand.

"A sequence where each number is the sum of the two before it." Fache looked at the numbers. Each one was indeed the sum of the previous two, but he couldn't see how any of this related to Saunière's death.

"The mathematician Leonardo Fibonacci created this sequence in the thirteenth century. It can't be a coincidence that all the numbers Saunière wrote on the floor are part of Fibonacci's famous sequence."

Fache stared at the young woman for several moments. "Fine, if it's not a coincidence, can you tell me why Jacques Saunière did this? What's he trying to say? What does this mean?"

She shrugged. "It means nothing. That's the point. It's a simple puzzle. Like taking a famous poem and mixing up the words to see if anyone recognizes the connection."

Fache took a threatening step forward, his face close to Sophie's. "I hope you have a better explanation than that."

Sophie's gentle face hardened as she leaned in. "Captain, considering what's at risk for you tonight, I thought you'd want to know that Jacques Saunière might be playing games with you. Apparently not. I'll tell the director of Cryptography we're no longer needed."

She turned and walked away. Fache watched her disappear into the darkness, stunned. Is she crazy? Sophie Neveu had just committed career suicide.

Fache turned to Langdon, who was still on the phone, looking worried, listening to his message. The U.S. Embassy. Bezu Fache hated many things... but few angered him more than the U.S. Embassy.

Fache and the ambassador often fought over shared issues, especially law enforcement for visiting Americans. Almost daily, DCPJ arrested American students with drugs, U.S. businessmen for hiring underage prostitutes, and American tourists for shoplifting or vandalism. Legally, the U.S. Embassy could take them back to the United States, where they would get off easy.

And the embassy always did.

The emasculation of the Police Judiciaire, Fache called it. Paris Match had recently drawn a cartoon of Fache as a police dog, trying to bite an American criminal, but chained to the U.S. Embassy.

Not tonight, Fache thought. Too much is at stake. When Robert Langdon hung up, he looked sick.

"Is everything okay?" Fache asked.

Langdon shook his head weakly.

Bad news from home, Fache guessed, noticing Langdon sweating slightly as Fache took his cell phone back.

"An accident," Langdon stammered, looking at Fache with a strange expression. "A friend..." He paused. "I'll need to fly home first thing in the morning." Fache had no doubt the shock on Langdon's face was real, and yet he sensed another feeling there too, as if a distant fear were suddenly growing in the American's eyes. "I'm sorry to hear that," Fache said, watching Langdon closely. "Would you like to sit down?" He pointed toward one of the benches in the gallery.

Langdon nodded blankly and took a few steps toward the bench. He paused, looking more confused with every moment. "Actually, I think I'd like to use the restroom." Fache frowned inwardly at the delay. "The restroom. Of course. Let's take a break for a few minutes." He pointed back down the long hallway in the direction they had come from. "The restrooms are back toward the curator's office."

Langdon hesitated, pointing in the other direction toward the far end of the Grand Gallery corridor.

"I believe there's a much closer restroom at the end."

Fache realized Langdon was right. They were two thirds of the way down, and the Grand Gallery ended at a pair of restrooms. "Shall I come with you?"

Langdon shook his head, already moving deeper into the gallery. "Not necessary. I think I'd like a few minutes alone."

Fache didn't like the idea of Langdon wandering alone down the remaining length of corridor, but he felt better knowing the Grand Gallery was a dead end whose only exit was at the other end—the gate under which they had entered. Although French fire rules required several emergency stairwells for a space this large, those stairwells had been sealed automatically when Saunière tripped the security system. That system had now been reset, unlocking the stairwells, but it didn't matter—the outside doors, if opened, would set off fire alarms and were guarded outside by DCPJ agents. Langdon could not possibly leave without Fache knowing about it.

"I need to return to Mr. Saunière's office for a moment," Fache said. "Please come find me directly, Mr. Langdon. There is more we need to discuss."

Langdon waved quietly as he vanished into the darkness.

Turning, Fache marched angrily the other way. Reaching the gate, he slid under, left the Grand Gallery, walked down the hall, and stormed into the command center in Saunière's office.

"Who approved letting Sophie Neveu into this building!" Fache yelled. Collet answered first. "She told the guards outside she'd cracked the code." Fache looked around. "Is she gone?"

"She's not with you?"

"She left." Fache glanced at the darkened hallway. Sophie clearly wasn't interested in stopping to chat with the other officers on her way out.

For a moment, Fache considered radioing the guards in the mezzanine and telling them to stop Sophie and drag her back before she could leave. He reconsidered. It was just his pride talking... wanting the last word. He'd had enough distractions tonight. Deal with Agent Neveu later, he told himself, already looking forward to firing her. Pushing Sophie from his mind, Fache stared for a moment at the small knight on Saunière's desk. Then he turned back to Collet. "Do you have him?" Collet nodded briefly and turned the laptop toward Fache. The red dot was clearly visible on the floor plan, blinking steadily in a room marked PUBLIC RESTROOMS.

"Good," Fache said, lighting a cigarette and walking into the hall. "I need to make a call. Make sure the restroom is the only place Langdon goes."
