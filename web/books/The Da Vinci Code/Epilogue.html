The Da Vinci Code

Epilogue

Robert Langdon awoke with a start. He had been dreaming. The bathrobe beside his bed bore the monogram HOTEL RITZ PARIS. He saw a dim light filtering through the blinds. Is it dusk or dawn? he wondered.

Langdon's body felt warm and deeply content. He had slept most of the last two days. Sitting up slowly in bed, he now realized what had awoken him... the strangest thought. For days he had been trying to make sense of so much information, but now Langdon found himself focused on something he'd not thought of before.

Could it be?

He remained still for a long moment.

Getting out of bed, he walked to the marble shower. Stepping inside, he let the powerful jets massage his shoulders. Still, the thought fascinated him.

Impossible.

Twenty minutes later, Langdon left the Hotel Ritz and entered Place Vendôme. Night was coming. The lack of sleep had left him confused... but his mind felt strangely clear. He had planned to stop in the hotel lobby for a coffee to clear his head, but instead, his legs took him straight out the front door and into the growing darkness of Paris. Walking east on Rue des Petits Champs, Langdon felt a rising excitement. He turned south onto Rue Richelieu, where the air smelled sweet with jasmine from the elegant gardens of the Palais Royal.

He kept going south until he saw what he wanted—the well-known royal arcade—a shiny stretch of polished black marble. Stepping onto it, Langdon looked at the ground. Quickly, he found what he knew was there—several bronze medallions set into the ground in a perfectly straight line. Each disk was five inches across and marked with the letters N and S.

North. South.

He turned directly south, letting his eyes follow the long line made by the medallions. He started walking again, following the path, watching the ground as he went. As he crossed the corner of the Comédie-Française, another bronze medallion appeared beneath his feet. Yes!

Langdon had learned years ago that the streets of Paris had 135 of these bronze markers, set into sidewalks, courtyards, and streets, running north-south across the city. He had once followed the line from Sacré-Coeur, north across the Seine, and finally to the old Paris Observatory. There he learned the importance of the sacred path it marked. The earth's first prime meridian.

The world's original zero longitude.

Paris's ancient Rose Line.

Now, as Langdon rushed across Rue de Rivoli, he could feel his destination getting closer. Less than a block away.

The Holy Grail 'neath ancient Roslin waits.

The revelations were coming now, one after another. Saunière's old-fashioned spelling of Roslin... the blade and chalice... the tomb filled with art by masters.

Is that why Saunière needed to talk to me? Had I accidentally figured it out?

He started to jog, feeling the Rose Line under his feet, guiding him, pulling him where he needed to go. As he walked into the long Passage Richelieu tunnel, the hairs on his neck stood up in anticipation. He knew that at the end of this tunnel was the most mysterious monument in Paris—planned and ordered in the 1980s by François Mitterrand, a man people said moved in secret groups, a man whose final gift to Paris was a place Langdon had visited just days before.

A different life.

With a final burst of energy, Langdon ran out of the passageway into the familiar courtyard and stopped. Out of breath, he looked up, slowly, in disbelief, at the shining structure in front of him.

The Louvre Pyramid.

Shining in the dark.

He admired it for only a moment. He was more interested in what was to his right. Turning, he felt his feet again following the invisible path of the ancient Rose Line, taking him across the courtyard to the Carrousel du Louvre—the huge circle of grass surrounded by trimmed bushes—once the place for Paris's ancient nature festivals... happy celebrations of fertility and the Goddess.

Langdon felt like he was stepping into another world as he went over the bushes to the grassy area inside. This sacred place was now marked by one of the city's strangest monuments. There in the center, going into the earth like a crystal hole, was the giant upside-down pyramid of glass that he had seen a few nights ago when he entered the Louvre's underground entrance hall. La Pyramide Inversée.

Shaking, Langdon walked to the edge and looked down into the Louvre's large underground area, lit with yellow light. He was looking not just at the massive upside-down pyramid, but at what was directly under it. There, on the floor of the room below, was the smallest of structures... a structure Langdon had mentioned in his book.

Langdon now felt the exciting rush of an incredible possibility. Looking up at the Louvre again, he felt the museum's massive presence surround him... hallways filled with the world's greatest art.

Da Vinci... Botticelli...

Covered in the beautiful art of masters, She lies.

Filled with wonder, he looked down again through the glass at the small structure below. I must go down there!

Leaving the circle, he quickly walked across the courtyard back to the Louvre's tall pyramid entrance. The last visitors of the day were leaving the museum. Going through the revolving door, Langdon walked down the curved staircase into the pyramid. He felt the air getting cooler. When he reached the bottom, he entered the long tunnel that went under the Louvre's courtyard, back toward La Pyramide Inversée. At the end of the tunnel, he entered a large room. Right in front of him, hanging down from above, shone the inverted pyramid—a stunning V-shaped form of glass. The Chalice.

Langdon's eyes followed its narrowing shape down to its tip, hanging only six feet above the floor. There, right below it, was the small structure.

A tiny pyramid. Only three feet tall. The only structure in this huge complex that was built so small.

Langdon's paper, while talking about the Louvre's large collection of goddess art, had briefly mentioned this small pyramid. "The miniature structure itself sticks up through the floor as if it were the tip of an iceberg — the top of a huge, pyramid-shaped vault, hidden below like a secret chamber."

Lit by the soft lights of the empty mezzanine, the two pyramids pointed at each other, their bodies perfectly lined up, their tips almost touching.

The Chalice above. The Blade below.

The blade and chalice guarding Her gates.

Langdon heard Marie Chauvel's words. One day you will understand.

He was standing beneath the ancient Rose Line, surrounded by the work of masters. What better place for Saunière to watch? Now at last, he felt he understood the true meaning of the Grand Master's verse. Raising his eyes to heaven, he gazed upward through the glass to a glorious, star-filled night.

She rests at last beneath the starry skies.

Like the murmurs of spirits in the darkness, forgotten words echoed. The quest for the Holy Grail is the quest to kneel before the bones of Mary Magdalene. A journey to pray at the feet of the outcast one.

With a sudden feeling of reverence, Robert Langdon fell to his knees. For a moment, he thought he heard a woman's voice... the wisdom of the ages... whispering up from the depths of the earth.
