The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 84

In an alley full of trash near Temple Church, Rémy Legaludec stopped the Jaguar limousine behind some large trash containers. He turned off the engine and looked around. No one was there. He got out of the car, walked to the back, and climbed into the limousine's main area where the monk was.

Sensing Rémy, the monk in the back came out of a prayer-like state, his red eyes looking more curious than scared. All evening, Rémy had been impressed with how calm this tied-up man could stay. After struggling at first in the Range Rover, the monk seemed to accept what was happening and trust his fate to God.

Rémy loosened his bow tie and unbuttoned his stiff collar, feeling like he could finally breathe. He went to the limousine's bar and poured himself a Smirnoff vodka. He drank it all at once and then poured another. Soon I will be able to relax.

Looking in the bar, Rémy found a regular wine opener and opened the sharp blade. The knife was usually used to cut the foil from wine bottles, but it would be used for something much more dramatic this morning. Rémy turned to face Silas, holding up the shiny blade.

Now fear flashed in those red eyes.

Rémy smiled and moved toward the back of the limousine. The monk pulled away, struggling against his ropes.

"Be still," Rémy whispered, raising the blade.

Silas couldn't believe God had left him. He had even turned the pain of being tied up into a spiritual exercise, asking his aching muscles to remind him of the pain Christ felt. I have been praying all night to be freed. Now, as the knife came down, Silas squeezed his eyes shut.

A sharp pain cut through his shoulder blades. He cried out, unable to believe he was going to die here in the back of this limousine, unable to protect himself. I was doing God's work. The Teacher said he would protect me.

Silas felt the sharp warmth spread across his back and shoulders and could picture his own blood, spilling out over his skin. A piercing pain cut through his thighs now, and he felt the familiar wave of confusion coming—the body's way of dealing with pain. As the sharp heat tore through all his muscles, Silas squeezed his eyes tighter, determined that the last thing he saw would not be his killer. Instead he pictured a younger Bishop Aringarosa, standing before the small church in Spain... the church that he and Silas had built themselves. The beginning of my life.

Silas felt as if his body were burning.

"Have a drink," the man in the tuxedo whispered, his accent French. "It will help your blood flow."

Silas's eyes flew open in surprise. A blurry figure was leaning over him, offering a glass of liquid. A pile of shredded duct tape lay on the floor beside the clean knife.

"Drink this," he repeated. "The pain you feel is the blood going back into your muscles." Silas felt the fiery throbbing turning into a prickling sting. The vodka tasted awful, but he drank it, feeling thankful. Fate had given Silas a lot of bad luck tonight, but God had fixed it all with one amazing turn.

God has not abandoned me.

Silas knew what Bishop Aringarosa would call it.

A miracle.

"I wanted to free you sooner," the servant apologized, "but it wasn't possible. With the police arriving at Château Villette, and then at Biggin Hill airport, this was the first chance I had. You understand, don't you, Silas?"

Silas recoiled, surprised. "You know my name?"

The servant smiled.

Silas sat up, rubbing his stiff muscles, his feelings a mix of disbelief, gratitude, and confusion. "Are you... the Teacher?"

Rémy shook his head, amused by the idea. "I wish I had that kind of power. No, I am not the Teacher. Like you, I serve him. But the Teacher speaks well of you. My name is Rémy." Silas was amazed. "I don't understand. If you work for the Teacher, why did Langdon bring the keystone to your home?"

"Not my home. The home of the world's leading Grail historian, Sir Leigh Teabing."

"But you live there. The chances..."

Rémy smiled, not bothered by the seeming coincidence of Langdon's choice. "It was all very predictable. Robert Langdon had the keystone and needed help. Where better to go than the home of Leigh Teabing? The fact that I live there is why the Teacher contacted me to begin with." He paused. "How do you think the Teacher knows so much about the Grail?"

Now it clicked, and Silas was shocked. The Teacher had recruited a servant who had access to all of Sir Leigh Teabing's research. It was clever.

"I have much to tell you," Rémy said, handing Silas the loaded Heckler Koch pistol. Then he reached through the open divider and took a small revolver from the glove box. "But first, you and I have a job to do."

Captain Fache got off his plane at Biggin Hill and listened in disbelief to the Kent chief inspector's story of what happened in Teabing's hangar.

"I searched the plane myself," the inspector insisted, "and there was no one inside." His voice became arrogant. "And I should add that if Sir Leigh Teabing sues me, I will—"

"Did you question the pilot?"

"Of course not. He's French, and our rules require—"

"Take me to the plane."

At the hangar, Fache quickly spotted a strange blood mark on the ground near where the limousine had been parked. Fache went to the plane and knocked hard on the side.

"This is the captain of the French Judicial Police. Open the door!" The scared pilot opened the hatch and lowered the stairs.

Fache went up. Three minutes later, using his gun, he got a full confession, including a description of the tied-up albino monk. He also learned that the pilot saw Langdon and Sophie leave something in Teabing's safe, a wooden box. The pilot didn't know what was in the box, but said Langdon had been very focused on it during the flight to London.

"Open the safe," Fache demanded.

The pilot looked terrified. "I don't know the code!"

"Too bad. I was going to let you keep your pilot's license." The pilot wrung his hands. "I know some maintenance guys here. Maybe they can drill it open?"

"You have half an hour."

The pilot jumped for his radio.

Fache walked to the back of the plane and poured himself a strong drink. It was early, but he had not slept yet, so this barely counted as drinking before noon. Sitting in a soft bucket seat, he closed his eyes, trying to figure out what was happening. The Kent police's mistake could hurt me badly. Now everyone was looking for a black Jaguar limousine.

Fache's phone rang, and he wished for a moment of quiet. "Hello?"

"I'm on my way to London." It was Bishop Aringarosa. "I'll be there in an hour." Fache sat up. "I thought you were going to Paris."

"I'm very worried. I've changed my plans."

"You shouldn't have."

"Do you have Silas?"

"No. His captors got away from the local police before I landed."

Aringarosa's anger sounded sharp. "You promised you would stop that plane!" Fache lowered his voice. "Bishop, considering your situation, I suggest you don't test my patience today. I will find Silas and the others as soon as I can. Where are you landing?"

"One moment." Aringarosa covered the phone and then came back. "The pilot is trying to get permission to land at Heathrow. I'm his only passenger, but our change of course was unplanned."

"Tell him to come to Biggin Hill Executive Airport in Kent. I'll get him permission to land. If I'm not here when you land, I'll have a car waiting."

"Thank you."

"Like I said when we first talked, Bishop, remember you're not the only one about to lose everything."
