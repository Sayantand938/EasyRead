The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 74

"You're quiet," Langdon said, looking at Sophie across the Hawker's cabin.

"Just tired," she replied. "And the poem. I don't know." Langdon felt the same. The engine's hum and the plane's gentle rocking were hypnotic, and his head still hurt where the monk had hit him. Teabing was still at the back of the plane, and Langdon decided to use this moment alone with Sophie to tell her something he'd been thinking about. "I think I know part of why your grandfather planned to get us together. I think he wanted me to explain something to you."

"Isn't the story of the Holy Grail and Mary Magdalene enough?" Langdon wasn't sure how to continue. "The break between you. The reason you haven't spoken in ten years. I think maybe he hoped I could fix that by explaining what drove you apart."

Sophie shifted in her seat. "I haven't told you what drove us apart." Langdon watched her closely. "You saw a sex ritual. Didn't you?" Sophie flinched. "How do you know that?"

"Sophie, you told me you saw something that made you think your grandfather was in a secret society. And whatever you saw upset you so much that you haven't spoken to him since. I know a lot about secret societies. It doesn't take a genius to guess what you saw." Sophie stared.

"Was it in the spring?" Langdon asked. "Around the equinox? Mid-March?" Sophie looked out the window. "I was on spring break from university. I came home a few days early."

"Do you want to tell me about it?"

"I'd rather not." She turned back to Langdon suddenly, her eyes filling with emotion. "I don't know what I saw."

"Were there men and women there?"

After a pause, she nodded.

"Dressed in white and black?"

She wiped her eyes and then nodded, seeming to relax a little. "The women wore white, see-through dresses... with gold shoes. They held gold balls. The men wore black robes and black shoes."

Langdon tried to hide his feelings, but he couldn't believe what he was hearing. Sophie Neveu had unknowingly seen a sacred ceremony that was two thousand years old. "Masks?" he asked, trying to stay calm. "Neutral masks?"

"Yes. Everyone. The same masks. White on the women. Black on the men." Langdon had read about this ceremony and knew its ancient origins. "It's called Hieros Gamos," he said quietly. "It goes back over two thousand years. Egyptian priests and priestesses used to do it regularly to honor the female's ability to create life." He paused, leaning closer to her. "And if you saw Hieros Gamos without knowing what it meant, I can imagine it would be quite disturbing."

Sophie said nothing.

"Hieros Gamos is Greek," he continued. "It means sacred marriage."

"The ritual I saw was not a marriage."

"Marriage as in joining together, Sophie."

"You mean as in sex."

"No."

"No?" she said, her olive eyes testing him.

Langdon backed up a little. "Well... yes, in a way, but not as we think of it today." He explained that even though what she saw probably looked like a sex ritual, Hieros Gamos had nothing to do with being sexy. It was a spiritual act. In the past, sex was how men and women felt God. People long ago believed that a man wasn't spiritually complete until he had sex with a sacred woman. Physical connection with a woman was the only way a man could become spiritually whole and truly understand God. Since the time of Isis, sex rituals had been seen as man's only way to go from earth to heaven. "By being with a woman," Langdon said, "a man could reach a high point when his mind went completely blank and he could see God." Sophie looked doubtful. "Orgasm as prayer?"

Langdon shrugged, not really agreeing or disagreeing, even though Sophie was basically right. When a man climaxes, there's a split second when he's not thinking at all. A short mental blank. A moment of clarity when God might be seen. Meditation teachers reach similar thoughtless states without sex and often describe Nirvana as a never-ending spiritual orgasm.

"Sophie," Langdon said quietly, "it's important to remember that people in the past saw sex completely differently than we do today. Sex created new life—the biggest miracle—and only a god could perform miracles. A woman's ability to create life in her womb made her sacred. A god. Sex was the respected joining of the two halves of the human spirit—male and female—through which the man could find spiritual wholeness and connection with God. What you saw wasn't about sex, it was about spirituality. The Hieros Gamos ritual isn't something dirty. It's a very sacred ceremony."

His words seemed to affect her deeply. Sophie had been very calm all evening, but now, for the first time, Langdon saw her composure starting to break. Tears came to her eyes again, and she wiped them away with her sleeve.

He gave her a moment. He knew that the idea of sex as a way to God was hard to grasp at first. Langdon's Jewish students always looked shocked when he first told them that early Jewish tradition included ritualistic sex. In the Temple, no less. Early Jews believed that the most sacred part of Solomon's Temple held not only God but also His powerful female equal, Shekinah. Men seeking spiritual wholeness came to the Temple to visit priestesses—or hierodules —with whom they had sex and felt the divine through physical union. The Jewish name for God, YHWH—in fact came from Jehovah, a joining of the masculine Jah and the old name for Eve, Havah.

"For the early Church," Langdon explained softly, "mankind's use of sex to connect directly with God was a big threat to the Catholic Church's power. It cut the Church out, hurting their claim to be the only way to God. So, they worked hard to make sex seem bad and sinful. Other major religions did the same."

Sophie was quiet, but Langdon felt she was starting to understand her grandfather better. He had made this same point in a class earlier this semester. "Is it any wonder we feel confused about sex?" he asked his students. "Our history and our bodies tell us sex is natural—a valued path to spiritual fulfillment—but modern religion calls it shameful, teaching us to fear our sexual desire as evil." Langdon decided not to shock his students with the fact that many secret societies around the world—many of them very powerful—still practiced sex rituals and kept the old traditions alive. Tom Cruise's character in the movie Eyes Wide Shut found this out the hard way when he went to a private party of wealthy Manhattanites and saw Hieros Gamos. The filmmakers got some of the details wrong, but the main idea was there—a secret society celebrating the magic of sex.

"Professor Langdon?" A male student in the back raised his hand, sounding hopeful. "Are you saying that instead of going to chapel, we should have more sex?"

Langdon chuckled, not falling for the trick. From what he knew about Harvard parties, these kids were having plenty of sex. "Gentlemen," he said, knowing he was on thin ice,

"may I offer a suggestion? Without encouraging premarital sex, and without being foolish enough to think you're all innocent, I'll give you this advice about your sex lives."

All the men in the audience leaned forward, listening closely.

"The next time you're with a woman, look inside yourself and see if you can approach sex as a spiritual act. Challenge yourself to find that divine spark that humans can only achieve through connection with the sacred feminine."

The women smiled knowingly, nodding.

The men exchanged uncertain giggles and crude jokes.

Langdon sighed. College men were still boys.

Sophie's forehead felt cold as she pressed it against the plane's window and stared blankly into the darkness, trying to understand what Langdon had just told her. She felt a new regret growing inside her. Ten years. She pictured the stacks of unopened letters her grandfather had sent. I will tell Robert everything. Without turning from the window, Sophie began to speak. Quietly. Fearfully. As she began to recount what had happened that night, she felt herself drifting back... arriving in the woods outside her grandfather's Normandy château... searching the empty house in confusion... hearing the voices below her... and then finding the hidden door. She crept down the stone staircase, one step at a time, into that basement grotto. She could taste the earthy air. Cool and light. It was March. In the shadows of her hiding place on the staircase, she watched as the strangers swayed and chanted by flickering orange candles.

I'm dreaming, Sophie told herself. This is a dream. What else could it be?

The women and men were arranged alternately, black, white, black, white. The women's beautiful, flowing gowns billowed as they raised golden orbs in their right hands and called out in unison, "I was with you in the beginning, in the dawn of all that is holy, I bore you from the womb before the start of day."

The women lowered their eyes, and everyone rocked back and forth as if hypnotized. They were honoring something in the center of the circle.

What are they looking at?

The voices sped up now. Louder. Faster.

"The woman you see is love!" The women called, raising their eyes again. The men responded, "She lives in eternity!"

The chanting grew steady again. Accelerating. Thundering now. Faster. The participants stepped inward and knelt.

In that instant, Sophie could finally see what they were all watching.

On a low, fancy altar in the center of the circle lay a man. He was naked, lying on his back, and wearing a black mask. Sophie instantly recognized his body and the birthmark on his shoulder. She almost cried out. Grand-père! This image alone would have shocked Sophie beyond belief, and yet there was more.

Straddling her grandfather was a naked woman wearing a white mask, her long silver hair flowing out behind it. Her body was full, not perfect, and she was moving in rhythm to the chanting—making love to Sophie's grandfather.

Sophie wanted to turn and run, but she couldn't. The stone walls of the grotto trapped her as the chanting rose to a fever pitch. The circle of participants seemed almost to be singing now, the noise rising to a frenzy. With a sudden roar, the entire room seemed to erupt in climax. Sophie could not breathe. She suddenly realized she was quietly sobbing. She turned and stumbled silently up the stairs, out of the house, and drove trembling back to Paris.
