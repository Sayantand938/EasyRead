The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 37

The Bois de Boulogne, a park thick with trees, was known by many names, but those in Paris who knew it well called it "the Garden of Earthly Delights." This nickname, though it sounded nice, was not meant as a compliment. Anyone familiar with Bosch's strange and disturbing painting of the same name understood the comparison; the painting, like the forest, was dark and twisted, a kind of hell for oddballs and those with unusual desires. At night, the forest's winding paths were filled with people offering their bodies for money, earthly pleasures to satisfy hidden yearnings—men, women, and everything in between.

As Langdon prepared to tell Sophie about the Priory of Sion, their taxi drove through the park's wooded entrance and started west on the cobblestone road. Langdon found it hard to focus as some of the park's nighttime visitors were already stepping out of the shadows, showing off what they had to offer in the bright headlights. Up ahead, two teenage girls with bare chests stared intensely at the taxi. Beyond them, a muscular Black man wearing only a G-string turned and flexed his rear. Next to him, a beautiful blond woman lifted her short skirt to reveal that she was not, in fact, a woman.

Heaven help me! Langdon looked back inside the taxi and took a deep breath.

"Tell me about the Priory of Sion," Sophie said.

Langdon nodded, unable to imagine a less suitable setting for the story he was about to tell. He wondered where to start. The brotherhood's history stretched back over a thousand years... an incredible story of secrets, blackmail, betrayal, and even cruel torture at the hands of an angry Pope.

"The Priory of Sion," he began, "was started in Jerusalem in 1099 by a French king named Godefroi de Bouillon, right after he had captured the city." Sophie nodded, her eyes fixed on him.

"King Godefroi supposedly possessed a powerful secret—a secret that had been in his family since the time of Christ. Afraid that his secret might be lost when he died, he founded a secret brotherhood—the Priory of Sion—and tasked them with protecting his secret by quietly passing it down from generation to generation. During their time in Jerusalem, the Priory learned about a collection of hidden documents buried under the ruins of Herod's temple, which had been built on top of the older ruins of Solomon's Temple. They believed these documents confirmed Godefroi's powerful secret and were so explosive that the Church would do anything to get them." Sophie looked unsure.

"The Priory vowed that no matter how long it took, these documents had to be recovered from the rubble beneath the temple and protected forever, so the truth would never die. To retrieve the documents from within the ruins, the Priory created a military division—a group of nine knights called the Order of the Poor Knights of Christ and the Temple of Solomon." Langdon paused.

"More commonly known as the Knights Templar."

Sophie glanced up, surprised. Langdon had lectured on the Knights Templar so often that he knew almost everyone had heard of them. For academics, the Templars' history was a tricky mix of fact, legend, and lies, making it almost impossible to find the truth. Now, Langdon hesitated to even mention the Knights Templar in lectures because it always led to confusing questions about conspiracy theories.

Sophie looked worried. "You're saying the Knights Templar were started by the Priory of Sion to find secret documents? I thought the Templars were created to protect the Holy Land."

"That's a common misunderstanding. Protecting pilgrims was the cover for the Templars' real mission. Their true goal in the Holy Land was to retrieve the documents from under the temple ruins."

"And did they find them?"

Langdon grinned. "Nobody knows for sure, but all academics agree on one thing: The Knights discovered something down there... something that made them incredibly rich and powerful."

Langdon quickly gave Sophie the usual academic explanation of the Knights Templar history, explaining how the Knights were in the Holy Land during the Second Crusade and told King Baldwin II they were there to protect Christian pilgrims. Although unpaid and poor, the Knights asked the king for shelter and permission to live in the stables under the temple ruins. King Baldwin agreed, and the Knights moved into the ruined shrine. Langdon explained that this odd choice of lodging was not random. The Knights believed the documents the Priory sought were buried deep under the ruins—beneath the Holy of Holies, a sacred chamber where God Himself was believed to reside. It was the very center of the Jewish faith. For almost a decade, the nine Knights lived in the ruins, secretly digging through solid rock.

Sophie looked over. "And you said they discovered something?"

"They certainly did," Langdon said, explaining that after nine years, the Knights finally found what they were looking for. They took the treasure from the temple and went to Europe, where their influence quickly grew. No one was sure if the Knights had blackmailed the Vatican or if the Church simply tried to buy their silence, but Pope Innocent II immediately issued a special order giving the Knights Templar unlimited power and declaring them "a law unto themselves"—an independent army free from interference from kings and religious leaders.

With this new freedom from the Vatican, the Knights Templar grew rapidly, both in numbers and political power, gaining large estates in many countries. They began lending money to bankrupt royals and charging interest, creating modern banking and increasing their wealth and influence.

By the 1300s, the Vatican's approval had helped the Knights become so powerful that Pope Clement V decided something had to be done. Working with France's King Philippe IV, the Pope planned a clever operation to crush the Templars, seize their treasure, and take control of the secrets held over the Vatican. In a military move like something from the CIA, Pope Clement issued secret orders to be opened simultaneously by his soldiers across Europe on Friday, October 13, 1307.

At dawn on the thirteenth, the documents were opened, and their terrible contents revealed. Clement's letter said that God had appeared to him and warned him that the Knights Templar were heretics guilty of worshiping the devil, homosexuality, disrespecting the cross, sodomy, and other sinful acts. God had asked Pope Clement to cleanse the earth by rounding up all the Knights and torturing them until they confessed their sins against God. Clement's cunning plan worked perfectly. On that day, many Knights were captured, tortured without mercy, and finally burned as heretics. The tragedy still affects modern culture; even today, Friday the thirteenth is considered unlucky.

Sophie looked confused. "The Knights Templar were destroyed? I thought groups of Templars still exist today?"

"They do, under different names. Despite Clement's false accusations and attempts to wipe them out, the Knights had powerful friends, and some managed to escape the Vatican's attacks. The Templars' valuable collection of documents, which seemed to be their source of power, was Clement's real target, but he didn't get it. The documents had long been entrusted to the Templars' secret protectors, the Priory of Sion, whose secrecy kept them safe from the Vatican's assault. As the Vatican closed in, the Priory secretly moved their documents from a Paris center at night onto Templar ships in La Rochelle."

"Where did the documents go?"

Langdon shrugged. "Only the Priory of Sion knows the answer to that mystery. Because the documents are still being investigated and debated today, they are believed to have been moved and hidden again several times. It is now thought that the documents are somewhere in the United Kingdom."

Sophie looked worried.

"For a thousand years," Langdon continued, "stories of this secret have been passed down. The entire collection of documents, its power, and the secret it reveals have become known by a single name—Sangreal. Hundreds of books have been written about it, and few mysteries have caused as much interest among historians as the Sangreal."

"The Sangreal? Does the word have anything to do with the French word sang or Spanish sangre —meaning 'blood'?"

Langdon nodded. Blood was central to the Sangreal, but not in the way Sophie probably thought. "The story is complicated, but the important thing to remember is that the Priory guards the proof, and is supposedly waiting for the right moment in history to reveal the truth."

"What truth? What secret could be that powerful?"

Langdon took a deep breath and looked out at the hidden parts of Paris lurking in the shadows.

"Sophie, the word Sangreal is an old word. It has changed over time to become something else... a more common name." He paused. "When I tell you its common name, you'll realize you already know a lot about it. Almost everyone on earth has heard the story of the Sangreal." Sophie looked unsure. "I've never heard of it."

"Yes, you have." Langdon smiled. "You just know it by the name 'Holy Grail.' "
