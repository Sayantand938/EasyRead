The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 19

The Church of Saint-Sulpice is known to have the strangest story of any building in Paris. Built on the remains of an old temple to the Egyptian goddess Isis, the church is almost exactly the same size as Notre Dame. The church has been the site of baptisms for the Marquis de Sade and Baudelaire, and the wedding of Victor Hugo. The connected seminary has a history of being different and was once a secret meeting place for many hidden groups.

Tonight, the huge main area of Saint-Sulpice was as quiet as a grave. The only sign of life was the light scent of incense from the evening mass. Silas noticed Sister Sandrine seemed worried as she guided him into the church. He wasn't surprised. Silas was used to people feeling uneasy around him.

"You're an American," she said.

"French by birth," Silas replied. "I found my purpose in Spain, and now I study in the United States."

Sister Sandrine nodded. She was a small woman with calm eyes. "And you have never seen SaintSulpice?"

"I know it's almost a sin to have not seen it."

"It's more beautiful during the day."

"I'm sure. Still, I appreciate you giving me this chance tonight."

"The priest asked for it. You must have important friends." You have no idea, Silas thought.

As he followed Sister Sandrine down the main aisle, Silas was surprised by the plainness of the church. Unlike Notre Dame with its colorful paintings, gold decorations, and warm wood, Saint-Sulpice was stark and cold, feeling almost empty like the simple churches of Spain. The lack of decoration made the inside seem even bigger, and as Silas looked up at the high, arched ceiling, he felt like he was standing under the bottom of a giant, flipped-over ship.

A good image, he thought. The brotherhood's ship was about to be destroyed forever. Wanting to start, Silas wished Sister Sandrine would leave him. She was a small woman he could easily overpower, but he had promised not to use force unless he had to. She is a nun, and it is not her fault the brotherhood chose her church to hide their keystone. She shouldn't be punished for the sins of others.

"I'm sorry, Sister, that you were woken up for me."

"Not at all. You're only in Paris for a short time. You shouldn't miss Saint-Sulpice. Are you interested in the church's architecture or its history?"

"Actually, Sister, I'm interested in its spiritual side."

She laughed kindly. "That goes without saying. I just wondered where to start your tour."

Silas looked at the altar. "A tour isn't needed. You've been very kind. I can look around myself."

"It's no trouble," she said. "After all, I'm awake." Silas stopped walking. They had reached the front pew, and the altar was only fifteen yards away. He turned his large body to face the small woman, and he could feel her pull back as she looked up into his red eyes. "If you don't mind, Sister, I'm not used to just walking into a house of God and taking a tour. Would you mind if I spent some time alone praying before I look around?"

Sister Sandrine hesitated. "Oh, of course. I'll wait at the back of the church for you." Silas put a gentle but heavy hand on her shoulder and looked down. "Sister, I already feel bad for waking you up. Asking you to stay awake is too much. Please, go back to bed. I can enjoy your church and then let myself out."

She looked worried. "Are you sure you won't feel lonely?"

"Not at all. Prayer is a personal joy."

"As you wish."

Silas took his hand from her shoulder. "Sleep well, Sister. May the peace of the Lord be with you."

"And also with you." Sister Sandrine headed for the stairs. "Please make sure the door closes tightly when you leave."

"I will make sure of it." Silas watched her disappear up the stairs. Then he turned and knelt in the front pew, feeling the cilice cut into his leg.

Dear God, I offer you the work I do today....

Hidden in the shadows of the choir balcony high above the altar, Sister Sandrine quietly watched the cloaked monk kneeling alone through the railing. The sudden fear in her soul made it hard to stay still. For a moment, she wondered if this mysterious visitor could be the enemy they had warned her about, and if tonight she would have to carry out the orders she had been holding for years. She decided to stay there in the darkness and watch his every move.
