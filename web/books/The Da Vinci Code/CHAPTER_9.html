The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 9

To make sure no one bothered him while he talked to Mr. Langdon, Bezu Fache had turned off his cell phone. But it was a fancy model with a two-way radio feature, and one of his agents was using it to call him, even though he had said not to.

"Capitaine?" The phone buzzed like a walkie-talkie. Fache was furious. He couldn't think of anything important enough for Collet to interrupt this secret surveillance —especially now. He gave Langdon a calm, apologetic look. "Just a moment." He took the phone from his belt and pushed the radio button. "Oui?"

"Capitaine, an agent from the Cryptography Department has arrived." Fache's anger paused. A cryptographer? Even though the timing was bad, this was probably good news. After finding Saunière's strange message on the floor, Fache had sent photos of the whole crime scene to the Cryptography Department, hoping someone there could figure out what Saunière was trying to say. If a code breaker was here, it probably meant someone had decoded Saunière's message.

"I'm busy right now," Fache radioed back, making it clear he was not happy. "Tell the cryptographer to wait at the command post. I'll talk to him when I'm done."

"Her," the voice corrected. "It's Agent Neveu." Fache was getting more annoyed with this call every second. Sophie Neveu was one of the DCPJ's worst mistakes. A young Parisian code breaker who had studied cryptography in England at the Royal Holloway, Sophie Neveu had been forced on Fache two years ago as part of the ministry's attempt to get more women on the police force. Fache argued that the ministry's focus on being politically correct was making the department weaker. He thought women didn't have the physical strength for police work and that their presence was a distraction for the men. And, as Fache had feared, Sophie Neveu was proving to be more of a distraction than most.

At thirty-two, she was very determined, almost stubborn. She was always pushing Britain's new code-breaking methods, which frustrated the older French cryptographers who worked above her. But the thing that bothered Fache the most was that, in an office full of middle-aged men, an attractive young woman always took attention away from the work.

The man on the radio said, "Agent Neveu insisted on speaking to you right away, Captain. I tried to stop her, but she's coming into the gallery."

Fache was shocked. "That's not okay! I made it very clear—"

Suddenly, Robert Langdon thought Bezu Fache was having a stroke. The captain stopped talking, his eyes wide. He seemed to be staring at something behind Langdon. Before Langdon could turn around, he heard a woman's voice behind him.

"Excuse me, gentlemen."

Langdon turned to see a young woman approaching. She moved down the corridor toward them with long, smooth steps... a haunting confidence in her walk. Dressed casually in a knee-length, cream-colored Irish sweater over black leggings, she was attractive and looked to be about thirty. Her thick burgundy hair fell naturally to her shoulders, framing her warm face. Unlike the thin, identical blondes that filled Harvard dorm rooms, this woman was healthy with a simple beauty and honesty that showed a strong self-assurance. To Langdon's surprise, the woman walked right up to him and offered a polite hand.

"Mr. Langdon, I am Agent Neveu from DCPJ's Cryptology Department." Her words rolled smoothly with her soft mix of English and French accent. "It is a pleasure to meet you." Langdon took her soft hand in his and felt himself briefly caught in her strong gaze. Her eyes were olive-green—sharp and clear.

Fache took a sharp breath, clearly ready to scold her.

"Captain," she said, turning quickly and speaking first, "please excuse the interruption, but—"

"This is not the time!" Fache snapped.

"I tried to call you." Sophie continued in English, as if being polite to Langdon. "But your cell phone was turned off."

"I turned it off for a reason," Fache hissed. "I am speaking to Mr. Langdon."

"I've solved the numeric code," she said simply.

Langdon felt a rush of excitement. She broke the code?

Fache seemed unsure how to respond.

"Before I explain," Sophie said, "I have an urgent message for Mr. Langdon." Fache's expression grew more concerned. "For Mr. Langdon?" She nodded, turning back to Langdon. "You need to contact the U.S. Embassy, Mr. Langdon. They have a message for you from the States."

Langdon reacted with surprise, his excitement about the code fading into sudden worry. A message from the States? He wondered who could be trying to reach him. Only a few of his colleagues knew he was in Paris.

Fache's jaw tightened at the news. "The U.S. Embassy?" he asked, sounding suspicious. "How would they know to find Mr. Langdon here?" Sophie shrugged. "Apparently, they called Mr. Langdon's hotel, and the concierge told them Mr. Langdon had been picked up by a DCPJ agent."

Fache looked troubled. "And the embassy contacted DCPJ Cryptography?"

"No, sir," Sophie said, her voice firm. "When I called the DCPJ switchboard to contact you, they had a message waiting for Mr. Langdon and asked me to pass it along if I got through to you."

Fache's brow furrowed, looking confused. He started to speak, but Sophie had already turned back to Langdon.

"Mr. Langdon," she said, pulling a small slip of paper from her pocket, "this is the number for your embassy's messaging service. They asked that you call them as soon as possible." She handed him the paper, looking intently at him. "While I explain the code to Captain Fache, you need to make this call."

Langdon looked at the slip. It had a Paris phone number and extension on it. "Thank you," he said, feeling worried now. "Where can I find a phone?"

Sophie started to pull a cell phone from her sweater pocket, but Fache waved her away. He now looked like he was about to explode. Without taking his eyes off Sophie, he took out his own cell phone and held it out. "This line is secure, Mr. Langdon. You may use it." Langdon was puzzled by Fache's anger at the young woman. Feeling uneasy, he took the captain's phone. Fache immediately led Sophie away and began scolding her in hushed tones. Disliking the captain more and more, Langdon turned away from the strange scene and switched on the cell phone. Checking the slip of paper Sophie had given him, Langdon dialed the number.

The line started ringing.

One ring... two rings... three rings...

Finally, the call connected.

Langdon expected to hear someone from the embassy, but instead, he heard an answering machine. Strangely, the voice on the recording sounded familiar. It was Sophie Neveu.

"Bonjour, vous êtes bien chez Sophie Neveu," the woman's voice said. "Je suis absenle pour le moment, mais... "

Confused, Langdon turned to Sophie. "I'm sorry, Ms. Neveu? I think you might have given me—"

"No, that's the right number," Sophie said quickly, as if she knew Langdon would be confused.

"The embassy uses an automated message system. You need to dial a code to get your messages."

Langdon stared. "But—"

"It's the three-digit code on the paper I gave you."

Langdon started to explain the odd mistake, but Sophie quickly silenced him with a brief glare. Her green eyes sent a clear message: Don't ask questions. Just do it.

Confused, Langdon entered the extension on the paper: 454. Sophie's outgoing message stopped right away, and Langdon heard a computerized voice say in French: "You have one new message." It seemed 454 was Sophie's code to check her messages from another location.

I'm checking this woman's messages?

Langdon could hear the tape rewinding. Then it stopped, and the machine started playing. Langdon listened as the message began. Again, it was Sophie's voice.

"Mr. Langdon," the message began in a worried whisper. "Do not react to this message. Just listen calmly. You are in danger right now. Follow my directions very carefully."
