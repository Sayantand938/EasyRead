The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 105

Night had fallen over Rosslyn.

Robert Langdon stood alone on the porch of the fieldstone house, enjoying the sounds of laughter and reunion coming through the screen door behind him. The mug of strong Brazilian coffee in his hand had given him a brief escape from his growing exhaustion, but he knew it wouldn't last long. The tiredness in his body ran deep.

"You slipped out quietly," a voice behind him said.

He turned. Sophie's grandmother appeared, her silver hair shining in the night. Her name, for at least the last twenty-eight years, was Marie Chauvel.

Langdon gave a tired smile. "I thought I'd give your family some time together." Through the window, he could see Sophie talking with her brother.

Marie came over and stood beside him. "Mr. Langdon, when I first heard of Jacques's murder, I was terrified for Sophie's safety. Seeing her standing in my doorway tonight was the greatest relief of my life. I can't thank you enough."

Langdon didn't know how to respond. Although he had offered to give Sophie and her grandmother time to talk in private, Marie had asked him to stay and listen. My husband clearly trusted you, Mr. Langdon, so I do too.

So Langdon had stayed, standing beside Sophie and listening in silent shock as Marie told the story of Sophie's late parents. Amazingly, both had been from Merovingian families—direct descendants of Mary Magdalene and Jesus Christ. Sophie's parents and ancestors had changed their family names of Plantard and Saint-Clair to protect themselves. Their children were the most direct surviving royal bloodline and were therefore carefully guarded by the Priory. When Sophie's parents were killed in a car accident with an unknown cause, the Priory feared the royal line's identity had been discovered.

"Your grandfather and I," Marie had explained, her voice filled with pain, "had to make a difficult decision the moment we got the phone call. Your parents' car had just been found in the river." She dabbed at the tears in her eyes. "All six of us—including you two grandchildren—were supposed to be traveling together in that car that night. Luckily, we changed our plans at the last minute, and your parents were alone. Hearing of the accident, Jacques and I couldn't know what had really happened... or if it truly was an accident." Marie looked at Sophie. "We knew we had to protect our grandchildren, and we did what we thought was best. Jacques told the police that your brother and I had been in the car... our two bodies supposedly washed away in the current. Then your brother and I went into hiding with the Priory. Jacques, being a well-known man, couldn't disappear. It made sense that Sophie, being the oldest, would stay in Paris to be taught and raised by Jacques, close to the Priory's heart and protection." Her voice dropped to a whisper. "Separating the family was the hardest thing we ever had to do. Jacques and I saw each other only rarely, and always in the most secret places... under the Priory's protection. There are certain ceremonies that the brotherhood always follows."

Langdon felt the story was much bigger, but he also knew it wasn't his place to hear it. So he had gone outside. Now, looking up at the spires of Rosslyn, Langdon couldn't shake the uneasy feeling of Rosslyn's unsolved mystery. Is the Grail really here at Rosslyn? And if so, where are the blade and cup that Saunière mentioned in his poem?

"I'll take that," Marie said, pointing to Langdon's hand.

"Oh, thank you." Langdon held out his empty coffee cup.

She stared at him. "I meant your other hand, Mr. Langdon." Langdon looked down and realized he was holding Saunière's papyrus. He had taken it from the cryptex again, hoping to see something he had missed before. "Of course, I'm sorry." Marie looked amused as she took the paper. "I know a man at a bank in Paris who is very eager to get this rosewood box back. André Vernet was a close friend of Jacques, and Jacques trusted him completely. André would have done anything to honor Jacques's wishes for the care of this box."

Including shooting me, Langdon thought, deciding not to mention that he had probably broken the poor man's nose. Thinking of Paris, Langdon remembered the three sénéchaux who had been killed the night before. "And the Priory? What happens now?"

"Things are already happening, Mr. Langdon. The brotherhood has lasted for centuries, and it will continue. There are always those ready to step up and rebuild." All evening Langdon had suspected that Sophie's grandmother was closely involved in the Priory's operations. After all, the Priory had always had women members. Four Grand Masters had been women. The sénéchaux were traditionally men—the guardians—and yet women held a more honored position within the Priory and could reach the highest position from almost any rank. Langdon thought of Leigh Teabing and Westminster Abbey. It seemed like a lifetime ago. "Was the Church pressuring your husband not to release the Sangreal documents at the End of Days?"

"Goodness no. The End of Days is a legend for those who are paranoid. There's nothing in the Priory's teachings that says when the Grail should be revealed. In fact, the Priory has always believed that the Grail should never be revealed."

"Never?" Langdon was shocked.

"It's the mystery and wonder that feeds our souls, not the Grail itself. The beauty of the Grail is in its spiritual nature." Marie Chauvel looked up at Rosslyn now. "For some, the Grail is a cup that will bring them eternal life. For others, it's the search for lost documents and secret history. And for most, I think the Holy Grail is simply a grand idea... a glorious, impossible treasure that somehow, even in today's chaotic world, inspires us."

"But if the Sangreal documents stay hidden, the story of Mary Magdalene will be lost forever," Langdon said.

"Will it? Look around. Her story is being told in art, music, and books. More and more each day. Things are changing. We are starting to understand the dangers of our past... and our harmful ways. We are beginning to feel the need to bring back the sacred feminine." She paused. "You said you're writing a book about the symbols of the sacred feminine, right?"

"I am."

She smiled. "Finish it, Mr. Langdon. Share her story. The world needs storytellers today." Langdon was quiet, feeling the importance of her words. Across the open space, a new moon was rising above the trees.

Turning to Rosslyn, Langdon felt like a child wanting to know its secrets. Don't ask, he told himself. Now is not the time. He looked at the papyrus in Marie's hand, then back at Rosslyn.

"Ask the question, Mr. Langdon," Marie said, smiling. "You have earned it." Langdon felt his face get warm.

"You want to know if the Grail is here at Rosslyn."

"Can you tell me?"

She sighed, pretending to be annoyed. "Why can't men just leave the Grail alone?" She laughed, clearly enjoying herself. "Why do you think it's here?" Langdon pointed to the papyrus in her hand. "Your husband's poem mentions Rosslyn, but it also talks about a blade and chalice guarding the Grail. I didn't see any blade and chalice symbols up there."

"The blade and chalice?" Marie asked. "What do they look like?" Langdon felt she was teasing him, but he joined in, quickly describing the symbols. She looked like she vaguely remembered something. "Ah, yes, of course. The blade stands for everything masculine. I think it's drawn like this, right?" She used her finger to draw a shape on her palm.

"Yes," Langdon said. Marie had drawn the less common "closed" blade, even though Langdon had seen the symbol drawn both ways.

"And the opposite," she said, drawing on her palm again, "is the chalice, which stands for the feminine."

"Correct," Langdon said.

"And you're saying that in all the hundreds of symbols we have here in Rosslyn Chapel, these two

shapes don't appear anywhere?"

"I didn't see them."

"And if I show them to you, will you get some sleep?"

Before Langdon could answer, Marie Chauvel had left the porch and was walking toward the chapel. Langdon hurried after her. Inside the old building, Marie turned on the lights and pointed to the middle of the sanctuary floor. "There you are, Mr. Langdon. The blade and chalice." Langdon looked at the worn stone floor. It was empty. "There's nothing here...." Marie sighed and started walking along the well-known path worn into the chapel floor, the same path Langdon had seen the visitors walking earlier. As his eyes got used to seeing the large symbol, he still felt confused. "But that's the Star of Dav—"

Langdon stopped, speechless with surprise as he understood.

The blade and chalice.

Joined as one.

The Star of David... the perfect joining of man and woman... Solomon's Seal... marking the most sacred place, where the male and female gods — Yahweh and Shekinah — were believed to live. Langdon paused to find his words. "The verse does lead here to Rosslyn. Exactly. Perfectly."

Marie smiled. "So it seems."

The idea made him uneasy. "So the Holy Grail is in the vault below us?" She laughed. "Only in spirit. One of the Priory's oldest duties was to one day bring the Grail back to her homeland of France, where she could rest forever. For centuries, she was moved around the country for safety. Very undignified. Jacques's job when he became Grand Master was to restore her honor by returning her to France and building her a resting place worthy of a queen."

"And he did it?"

Now her face turned serious. "Mr. Langdon, after all you've done for me tonight, and as curator of the Rosslyn Trust, I can tell you for sure that the Grail is no longer here." Langdon decided to push further. "But the keystone is supposed to point to where the Holy Grail is hidden now. Why does it point to Rosslyn?"

"Maybe you're misunderstanding its meaning. Remember, the Grail can be misleading. As could my late husband."

"But how much clearer could he be?" he asked. "We're standing over an underground vault marked by the blade and chalice, under a ceiling of stars, surrounded by the art of Master Masons. Everything points to Rosslyn."

"Alright, let me see this strange poem." She unrolled the papyrus and read the poem aloud slowly and carefully.

The Holy Grail 'neath ancient Roslin waits.

The blade and chalice guarding o'er Her gates.

Adorned in masters' loving art, She lies.

She rests at last beneath the starry skies.

When she finished, she paused for a few seconds, then smiled knowingly. "Aah, Jacques."

Langdon watched her, waiting. "You understand this?"

"As you saw on the chapel floor, Mr. Langdon, there are many ways to look at simple things."

Langdon struggled to understand. Everything about Jacques Saunière seemed to have hidden meanings, but Langdon couldn't see them.

Marie yawned, sounding tired. "Mr. Langdon, I'll admit something to you. I've never officially known where the Grail is right now. But, of course, I was married to someone very important... and I have a strong intuition." Langdon started to speak, but Marie kept talking. "I'm sorry that after all your effort, you'll be leaving Rosslyn without any real answers. But, something tells me you will eventually find what you're looking for. One day you'll understand." She smiled. "And when you do, I trust that you, of all people, can keep a secret." There was a sound of someone coming to the doorway. "You both vanished," Sophie said, walking in.

"I was just leaving," her grandmother said, walking to Sophie at the door. "Good night, princess." She kissed Sophie's forehead. "Don't keep Mr. Langdon out too late." Langdon and Sophie watched her grandmother walk back toward the stone house. When Sophie turned to him, her eyes showed deep emotion. "Not the ending I expected." Me neither, he thought. Langdon could see she was overwhelmed. The news tonight had changed her life. "Are you okay? It's a lot to take in." She smiled quietly. "I have a family. That's where I'll start. Learning who we are and where we came from will take time."

Langdon said nothing.

"Will you stay with us longer?" Sophie asked. "At least for a few days?" Langdon sighed, wanting it very much. "You need time with your family, Sophie. I'm going back to Paris in the morning."

She looked sad but seemed to agree it was best. They were silent for a long time. Then Sophie took his hand and led him out of the chapel. They walked to a small hill. From there, the Scottish countryside spread out before them, lit by pale moonlight through the thinning clouds. They stood in silence, holding hands, both fighting their tiredness. The stars were starting to appear, but to the east, one light shone brighter than the others. Langdon smiled when he saw it. It was Venus, the ancient Goddess, shining her steady light.

The night grew cooler, with a breeze from the lowlands. After a while, Langdon looked at Sophie. Her eyes were closed, her lips relaxed in a happy smile. Langdon felt his own eyes getting heavy. Reluctantly, he squeezed her hand. "Sophie?" Slowly, she opened her eyes and turned to him. Her face was beautiful in the moonlight. She gave him a sleepy smile. "Hi."

Langdon felt sad to be returning to Paris without her. "I might be gone before you wake up." He paused, feeling a lump in his throat. "I'm sorry, I'm not good at—"

Sophie reached out and touched his face gently. Then, she leaned forward and kissed his cheek tenderly. "When can I see you again?" Langdon was taken by surprise, lost in her eyes. "When?" He paused, wondering if she knew how much he wanted to know the same thing. "Well, I'm giving a lecture at a conference in Florence next month. I'll be there for a week with not much to do."

"Is that an invitation?"

"We'd be living well. They're giving me a room at the Brunelleschi." Sophie smiled playfully. "You assume a lot, Mr. Langdon."

He felt bad about how it sounded. "What I meant—"

I would love nothing more than to meet you in Florence, Robert. But on one condition." Her tone turned serious. "No museums, no churches, no tombs, no art, no relics."

"In Florence? For a week? There's nothing else to do."

Sophie leaned forward and kissed him again, now on the lips. Their bodies came together, softly at first, and then completely. When she pulled away, her eyes were full of promise.

"Right," Langdon managed. "It's a date."
