The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 100

Bishop Manuel Aringarosa's body had felt many kinds of pain, but the burning heat of the bullet in his chest felt very strange. Deep and serious. Not a wound of the body, but something closer to his soul.

He opened his eyes, trying to see, but the rain blurred his vision. Where am I? He felt strong arms holding him, carrying his limp body like a doll, his black robe flapping. Lifting a tired arm, he wiped his eyes and saw Silas holding him. The large albino was struggling down a wet sidewalk, yelling for a hospital, his voice a sad cry of pain. His red eyes stared straight ahead, tears running down his pale, bloody face.

"My son," Aringarosa whispered, "you're hurt."

Silas looked down, his face twisted in pain. "I am so sorry, Father." He seemed barely able to speak.

"No, Silas," Aringarosa replied. "I am the one who is sorry. This is my fault." The Teacher promised me there would be no killing, and I told you to obey him completely. "I was too eager. Too afraid. We were both tricked." The Teacher was never going to give us the Holy Grail. Held in the arms of the man he had taken in years ago, Bishop Aringarosa felt himself go back in time. To Spain. To his simple beginnings, building a small Catholic church in Oviedo with Silas. And later, to New York City, where he had praised God with the tall Opus Dei Center on Lexington Avenue.

Five months ago, Aringarosa had received terrible news. His life's work was in danger. He remembered clearly the meeting inside Castel Gandolfo that had changed his life, the news that had started all this trouble.

Aringarosa had entered Gandolfo's Astronomy Library proudly, expecting to be praised by many welcoming people, all wanting to congratulate him for his great work representing Catholicism in America.

But only three people were there.

The Vatican secretary. Overweight. Gloomy.

Two high-ranking Italian cardinals. Self-righteous. Smug.

"Secretariat?" Aringarosa said, puzzled.

The round overseer of legal affairs shook Aringarosa's hand and pointed to the chair opposite him. "Please, sit down."

Aringarosa sat, feeling something was wrong.

"I'm not one for small talk, Bishop," the secretariat said, "so let me get straight to the point of your visit."

"Please. Speak freely." Aringarosa glanced at the two cardinals, who seemed to be sizing him up with self-righteous anticipation.

"As you know," the secretariat said, "His Holiness and others in Rome have been worried recently about the political problems caused by Opus Dei's more questionable actions." Aringarosa felt himself tense up immediately. He had already dealt with this many times with the new pope, who, to Aringarosa's disappointment, had become a surprisingly strong voice for liberal change in the Church.

"I want to assure you," the secretariat added quickly, "that His Holiness doesn't want to change how you run your ministry."

I certainly hope not! "Then why am I here?"

The huge man sighed. "Bishop, I'm not sure how to say this gently, so I'll be blunt. Two days ago, the Secretariat Council voted unanimously to end the Vatican's approval of Opus Dei."

Aringarosa was sure he hadn't heard right. "I'm sorry, what?"

"Simply put, in six months, Opus Dei will no longer be part of the Vatican. You'll be your own church. The Vatican will cut ties with you. His Holiness agrees, and we're already preparing the legal documents."

"But... that can't be!"

"Actually, it can. And it must. His Holiness is worried about your forceful recruiting and your self-punishment practices." He paused. "Also, your policies on women. Frankly, Opus Dei has become a problem and a source of shame." Bishop Aringarosa was stunned. "A shame?"

"Surely you're not surprised it's come to this."

"Opus Dei is the only Catholic group that's growing! We have over eleven hundred priests now!"

"True. That worries us."

Aringarosa jumped up. "Ask His Holiness if Opus Dei was a shame in 1982 when we helped the Vatican Bank!"

"The Vatican will always be thankful for that," the secretary said, trying to soothe him, "but some still think your money in 1982 is the only reason you became a prelature."

"That's not true!" The suggestion deeply offended Aringarosa.

"Whatever happens, we plan to be fair. We are preparing severance terms that will include a return of that money. It will be paid in five parts."

"Are you bribing me?" Aringarosa asked. "Paying me to leave quietly? When Opus Dei is the only one making sense!"

One of the cardinals looked up. "I'm sorry, did you say sense?" Aringarosa leaned across the table, making his voice sharp. "Do you wonder why Catholics are leaving the Church? Look around you, Cardinal. People no longer respect it. The strict parts of faith are gone. The doctrine has become a buffet. Giving things up, confession, communion, baptism, mass—pick what you like—choose what pleases you and ignore the rest. What kind of spiritual help is the Church giving?"

"Laws from the third century," the second cardinal said, "cannot be used for today's followers of Christ. The rules don't work in today's world."

"Well, they seem to be working for Opus Dei!"

"Bishop Aringarosa," the secretary said, his voice final. "Because of your organization's connection with the last Pope, His Holiness will give Opus Dei six months to willingly separate from the Vatican. I suggest you mention your disagreements with the Holy See and become your own Christian organization."

"I refuse!" Aringarosa declared. "And I'll tell him that myself!"

"I'm afraid His Holiness doesn't want to meet with you anymore."

Aringarosa stood up. "He wouldn't dare get rid of a personal group created by a past Pope!"

"I'm sorry." The secretary's eyes didn't move. "The Lord gives and the Lord takes away." Aringarosa left that meeting confused and scared. Back in New York, he stared at the skyline, sad and worried about the future of Christianity.

Weeks later, he got the call that changed everything. The caller had a French accent and called himself the Teacher —a common title in the group. He said he knew the Vatican planned to stop supporting Opus Dei.

How could he know that? Aringarosa wondered. He'd hoped only a few powerful people in the Vatican knew Opus Dei was about to be shut down. Apparently, the news had spread. When it came to secrets, the Vatican was like a sieve.

"I hear everything, Bishop," the Teacher whispered, "and I've learned something. If you help me, I can find a sacred object that will give you great power... enough to make the Vatican obey you. Enough to save our Faith." He paused. "Not just for Opus Dei, but for everyone." The Lord takes away... and the Lord gives. Aringarosa felt a surge of hope. "Tell me your plan."

Bishop Aringarosa was unconscious when the doors of St. Mary's Hospital opened. Silas stumbled into the entrance, exhausted and confused. He fell to his knees on the floor and cried for help. Everyone in the waiting area stared at the half-naked albino holding a bleeding priest.

The doctor who helped Silas lift the bishop onto a bed looked worried as he checked Aringarosa's pulse. "He's lost a lot of blood. I don't think he'll make it." Aringarosa's eyes fluttered open, and he saw Silas. "My child..." Silas felt overwhelming guilt and anger. "Father, even if it takes my whole life, I will find the one who tricked us, and I will kill him."

Aringarosa shook his head sadly as they wheeled him away. "Silas... if you've learned nothing from me, please... learn this." He squeezed Silas's hand.

"Forgiveness is God's greatest gift."

"But Father..."

Aringarosa closed his eyes. "Silas, you must pray."
