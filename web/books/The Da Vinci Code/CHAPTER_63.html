The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 63

Lieutenant Collet stood alone at the bottom of Leigh Teabing's driveway, looking up at the huge house. Alone. Dark. Lots of hiding places. Collet watched his six agents quietly spread out along the fence. They could easily climb over it and surround the house in minutes. Langdon couldn't have picked a better place for Collet's men to make a surprise attack.

Collet was about to call Fache when his phone finally rang.

Fache didn't sound as happy about the news as Collet expected. "Why didn't anyone tell me we had a lead on Langdon?"

"You were on a call and—"

"Where are you exactly, Lieutenant Collet?"

Collet gave him the address. "The property belongs to a British man named Teabing. Langdon drove quite a distance to get here, and the car is inside the gate. There's no sign of a break-in, so Langdon probably knows the owner."

"I'm coming there," Fache said. "Don't do anything. I'll take care of this myself." Collet was shocked. "But Captain, you're twenty minutes away! We should move now. I have him surrounded. I'm here with eight men. Four have rifles, and the others have guns."

"Wait for me."

"Captain, what if Langdon has a hostage inside? What if he sees us and tries to escape on foot?"

"We need to move now! My men are ready and waiting."

"Lieutenant Collet, wait for me to get there before you do anything. That's an order." Fache hung up.

Stunned, Lieutenant Collet turned off his phone. Why does Fache want me to wait? Collet knew the answer. Fache was known for his instinct, but even more for his pride. Fache wants the credit for the arrest. He put the American's face on TV, so he wanted his own face to get attention too. Collet's job was just to hold things steady until the boss arrived to save the day.

As he stood there, Collet thought of another reason for the delay. Damage control. In law enforcement, the only time you wait to arrest a fugitive is when you're not sure if they're guilty. Is Fache wondering if Langdon is the right guy? The thought scared him. Captain Fache had gone all out to arrest Robert Langdon—secret surveillance, Interpol, and now TV. Even the great Bezu Fache wouldn't survive the political mess if he wrongly put a famous American's face on French TV, calling him a murderer. If Fache now realized he'd made a mistake, it made sense that he would tell Collet to wait. The last thing Fache needed was for Collet to storm an innocent Brit's property and arrest Langdon at gunpoint. Also, Collet realized, if Langdon was innocent, it explained one of the case's weirdest puzzles: Why had Sophie Neveu, the victim's granddaughter, helped the accused killer escape? Unless Sophie knew Langdon was wrongly accused. Fache had offered many reasons for Sophie's strange behavior, including that Sophie, as Saunière's only heir, had convinced her secret lover Robert Langdon to kill Saunière for the inheritance money. If Saunière suspected this, he might have left the police the message P.S. Find Robert Langdon. Collet was pretty sure something else was happening. Sophie Neveu seemed too decent to be involved in something so awful.

"Lieutenant?" A field agent ran over. "We found a car." Collet followed the agent about fifty yards past the driveway. The agent pointed to a wide shoulder on the other side of the road. There, parked in the bushes, almost hidden, was a black Audi. It had rental plates. Collet felt the hood. Still warm. Even hot.

"That must be how Langdon got here," Collet said. "Call the rental company. See if it's stolen."

"Yes, sir."

Another agent waved Collet back toward the fence. "Lieutenant, look at this." He handed Collet night vision binoculars. "The trees near the top of the driveway."

Collet aimed the binoculars up the hill and adjusted the focus. Slowly, the green shapes became clear. He found the curve of the driveway and followed it up to the trees. He could only stare. There, hidden in the trees, was an armored truck. A truck just like the one Collet had allowed to leave the Depository Bank of Zurich earlier that night. He hoped it was a strange coincidence, but he knew it wasn't.

"It seems clear," the agent said, "that Langdon and Neveu used this truck to escape from the bank."

Collet was speechless. He thought of the armored truck driver he had stopped at the roadblock. The Rolex. His eagerness to leave. I never checked the cargo hold.

In disbelief, Collet realized that someone in the bank had actually lied to DCPJ about where Langdon and Sophie were and then helped them escape. But who? And why? Collet wondered if maybe this was why Fache had told him not to act yet. Maybe Fache knew there were more people involved tonight than just Langdon and Sophie. And if Langdon and Neveu arrived in the armored truck, then who drove the Audi?

Hundreds of miles to the south, a private Beechcraft Baron 58 sped north over the Tyrrhenian Sea. Despite calm skies, Bishop Aringarosa held an airsickness bag, sure he would be sick soon. His conversation with Paris had not gone as he had hoped. Alone in the small cabin, Aringarosa twisted the gold ring on his finger and tried to calm his great fear and despair. Everything in Paris has gone terribly wrong. Closing his eyes, Aringarosa prayed that Bezu Fache would be able to fix it.
