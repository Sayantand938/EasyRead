The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 103

It was late afternoon when the London sun appeared and the city began to dry. Bezu Fache felt tired as he left the interrogation room and called for a taxi. Sir Leigh Teabing had loudly declared his innocence, but from his confused ramblings about the Holy Grail, secret documents, and mysterious groups, Fache thought the clever historian was preparing his lawyers to claim he was insane.

Sure, Fache thought. Insane. Teabing had shown great skill in creating a plan that protected his innocence at every step. He had used both the Vatican and Opus Dei, two groups that turned out to be completely innocent. His dirty work had been done without their knowledge by a fanatical monk and a desperate bishop. Even smarter, Teabing had placed his electronic listening device in the one place a man with polio could not possibly reach. The actual spying had been done by his servant, Rémy—the only person who knew Teabing's true identity—now conveniently dead from an allergic reaction.

Not the work of someone mentally impaired, Fache thought. The information Collet was sending from Château Villette suggested that Teabing's craftiness was so advanced that Fache himself might even learn from it. To successfully hide bugs in some of Paris's most important offices, the British historian had used the Greeks. Trojan horses. Some of Teabing's targets received expensive gifts of art, others unknowingly bid at auctions where Teabing had placed specific items. In Saunière's case, the curator had been invited to dinner at Château Villette to discuss Teabing's possibly funding a new Da Vinci Wing at the Louvre. Saunière's invitation had a casual note expressing interest in a robotic knight that Saunière was rumored to have built. Bring it to dinner, Teabing had suggested. Saunière apparently had done so and left the knight unattended long enough for Rémy Legaludec to make a small addition.

Now, sitting in the back of the taxi, Fache closed his eyes. One more thing to do before I return to Paris.

The St. Mary's Hospital recovery room was sunny.

"You've impressed us all," the nurse said, smiling at him. "It's quite a miracle." Bishop Aringarosa gave a weak smile. "I have always been fortunate." The nurse finished her work, leaving the bishop alone. The sunlight felt nice and warm on his face. Last night had been the worst night of his life.

Sadly, he thought of Silas, whose body had been found in the park. Please forgive me, my son.

Aringarosa had wanted Silas to be part of his grand plan. But last night, Aringarosa had received a call from Bezu Fache, asking the bishop about his connection to a nun who had been murdered in Saint-Sulpice. Aringarosa realized the evening had taken a terrible turn. News of the four additional murders changed his horror to pain. Silas, what have you done! Unable to reach the Teacher, the bishop knew he had been abandoned. Used. The only way to stop the terrible series of events he had helped start was to confess everything to Fache, and from that moment on, Aringarosa and Fache had been racing to catch up with Silas before the Teacher persuaded him to kill again.

Feeling exhausted, Aringarosa closed his eyes and listened to the television coverage of the arrest of a well-known British knight, Sir Leigh Teabing. The Teacher exposed for everyone to see. Teabing had learned of the Vatican's plans to distance itself from Opus Dei. He had chosen Aringarosa as the perfect tool in his plan. After all, who would be more likely to blindly chase after the Holy Grail than a man like myself with everything to lose? The Grail would have given great power to anyone who possessed it.

Leigh Teabing had hidden his real self well—faking a French accent and a religious heart, and asking for the one thing he didn't need—money. Aringarosa had been too quick to suspect nothing. The twenty million euro price was small compared to getting the Grail, and with the Vatican's payment to Opus Dei, the money worked out fine. People see what they want to see. Teabing's biggest insult was asking for payment in Vatican bonds, so if anything went wrong, the investigation would point to Rome.

"I am glad you're well, My Lord."

Aringarosa knew the rough voice in the doorway, but the face was a surprise—serious, strong features, slicked-back hair, and a thick neck pushing against his dark suit. "Captain Fache?" Aringarosa asked. The kindness and care the captain had shown for Aringarosa's trouble last night had made him think of a much gentler man.

The captain came to the bed and put a familiar, heavy black briefcase on a chair. "I believe this is yours."

Aringarosa looked at the briefcase full of bonds and quickly looked away, feeling only shame. "Yes... thank you." He stopped, rubbing his fingers on the bedsheet, then went on. "Captain, I have been thinking hard about this, and I need to ask you a favor."

"Of course."

"The families of those in Paris who Silas..." He paused, fighting back his emotions. "I know no amount of money can truly make up for it, but if you would be kind enough to divide what's in this briefcase among them... the families of those who died." Fache's dark eyes studied him for a moment. "A good thing to do, My Lord. I will make sure it is done."

A heavy silence filled the room.

On the television, a thin French police officer was giving a press conference in front of a large house. Fache saw who it was and watched the screen.

"Lieutenant Collet," a BBC reporter said, her voice sounding accusing. "Last night, your captain publicly accused two innocent people of murder. Will Robert Langdon and Sophie Neveu demand justice from your department? Will this cost Captain Fache his job?" Lieutenant Collet's smile was tired but calm. "I know that Captain Bezu Fache rarely makes mistakes. I haven't spoken to him about this yet, but knowing how he works, I think his public search for Agent Neveu and Mr. Langdon was a trick to draw out the real killer."

The reporters looked at each other, surprised.

Collet went on. "I don't know if Mr. Langdon and Agent Neveu willingly took part in the trap. Captain Fache usually keeps his inventive methods secret. All I can say now is that the captain has caught the person responsible, and that Mr. Langdon and Agent Neveu are both innocent and safe."

Fache smiled slightly as he turned back to Aringarosa. "Collet is a good man." He paused for a moment. Then, Fache ran his hand over his forehead, smoothing back his hair as he looked down at Aringarosa. "My Lord, before I go back to Paris, there's one more thing I want to talk about—your sudden flight to London. You paid a pilot to change the flight path. By doing that, you broke several international laws."

Aringarosa sagged. "I was desperate."

"Yes. So was the pilot when my men questioned him." Fache reached into his pocket and took out a purple amethyst ring with a familiar handmade design of a mitre and crozier. Aringarosa felt tears filling his eyes as he took the ring and put it back on his finger. "You've been so kind." He reached out and shook Fache's hand. "Thank you." Fache dismissed the thanks, walking to the window and looking out at the city, clearly lost in thought. When he turned back, he seemed unsure. "My Lord, what will you do now?"

Aringarosa had been asked the same question as he left Castel Gandolfo the night before. "I think my future is as unclear as yours."

"Yes." Fache paused. "I think I'll be retiring soon." Aringarosa smiled. "A little faith can work wonders, Captain. A little faith."
