The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 33

Sophie's SmartCar sped through the diplomatic area, dodging embassies and consulates, before shooting down a side street and turning right onto the Champs-Elysées.

Langdon, tense and gripping the seat, twisted around to watch for the police. He suddenly regretted running. You didn't, he reminded himself. Sophie decided when she threw the GPS tracker out the window. Now, speeding away from the embassy through the light traffic on the Champs-Elysées, Langdon felt his options shrinking. Sophie seemed to have lost the police, for now, but Langdon doubted their luck would last.

Sophie, driving, reached into her sweater pocket. She pulled out a small metal object and held it out. "Robert, look at this. My grandfather left it behind Madonna of the Rocks."

Langdon, excited, took the object and examined it. It was heavy and cross-shaped. He thought it was a small funeral marker, like the ones stuck in the ground at graves. But then he saw that the shaft was angled and triangular. It also had hundreds of tiny hexagons, precisely made and scattered randomly.

"It's a laser-cut key," Sophie said. "An electric eye reads the hexagons." A key? Langdon had never seen anything like it.

"Look at the other side," she said, changing lanes and speeding through an intersection. Langdon turned the key and was amazed. There, on the center of the cross, was a fleur-de-lis with the initials P.S.! "Sophie," he said, "it's the seal I told you about! The Priory of Sion's official symbol."

She nodded. "I saw the key a long time ago. He told me never to mention it." Langdon stared at the key. Its advanced design and ancient symbol felt like a strange mix of old and new.

"He said the key opened a box with many secrets." Langdon felt uneasy thinking about what secrets Jacques Saunière might have kept. He couldn't imagine what an ancient group would do with a futuristic key. The Priory existed only to protect a secret. A very powerful secret. Could this key be related? The thought was overwhelming. "Do you know what it opens?" Sophie seemed disappointed. "I hoped you did."

Langdon turned the cross in his hand, examining it, and remained silent.

"It looks Christian," Sophie insisted.

Langdon wasn't so sure. The head of this key wasn't the usual long Christian cross, but a square cross—with four equal arms—that was around fifteen hundred years older than Christianity. This kind of cross didn't have the Christian ideas of crucifixion that went with the longer Latin Cross, which Romans created as a way to torture people. Langdon was always surprised that many Christians who looked at "the crucifix" didn't realize its violent history was right there in its name: "cross" and "crucifix" came from the Latin word cruciare —to torture.

"Sophie," he said, "all I know is that equal-armed crosses like this are seen as peaceful crosses. Their square shape makes them hard to use for crucifixion, and the balanced up and down and side to side parts show a natural joining of male and female, which fits with the Priory's beliefs."

She looked tired. "You don't get it, do you?"

Langdon frowned. "Not at all."

"Okay, we have to get off the road." Sophie looked in her rearview mirror. "We need a safe place to figure out what this key unlocks."

Langdon wished he was back in his cozy room at the Ritz. But that was out of the question.

"What about my friends at the American University of Paris?"

"Too easy. Fache will check there."

"You must know people. You live here."

Fache will check my phone and e-mail records, talk to my coworkers. My contacts are at risk, and a hotel won't work because they all need ID." Langdon wondered again if he should have just let Fache arrest him at the Louvre. "Let's call the embassy. I can explain and have them send someone to meet us."

"Meet us?" Sophie turned and stared like he was crazy. "Robert, you're dreaming. Your embassy only has power on their own property. Sending someone to get us would be helping a fugitive of the French government. It won't happen. If you go to your embassy and ask for temporary safety, that's one thing, but asking them to act against French police in the field?" She shook her head. "Call your embassy now, and they'll tell you to avoid making things worse and turn yourself in to Fache. Then they'll promise to use diplomatic channels to get you a fair trial." She looked up at the fancy storefronts on the Champs-Elysées. "How much cash do you have?"

Langdon checked his wallet. "A hundred dollars. Some euros. Why?"

"Credit cards?"

"Of course."

As Sophie sped up, Langdon felt she was making a plan. Straight ahead, at the end of the Champs-Elysées, was the Arc de Triomphe—Napoleon's 164-foot-tall tribute to his military strength—surrounded by France's biggest roundabout, a nine-lane monster.

Sophie's eyes were on the rearview mirror again as they neared the roundabout. "We lost them for now," she said, "but we won't last another five minutes if we stay in this car." So steal another one, Langdon thought, now that we're criminals. "What are you going to do?" Sophie gunned the SmartCar into the roundabout. "Trust me."

Langdon didn't answer. Trust hadn't helped him much tonight. Pulling back his jacket sleeve, he checked his watch—a vintage, collector's-edition Mickey Mouse wristwatch his parents gave him on his tenth birthday. Although its childish face often got strange looks, Langdon had never owned another watch; Disney cartoons were his first look into the magic of form and color, and Mickey was now Langdon's daily reminder to stay young at heart. But right now, Mickey's hands were at an awkward angle, showing an equally awkward hour.

2:51 A.M.

"Interesting watch," Sophie said, glancing at his wrist and steering the SmartCar around the wide, counterclockwise roundabout.

"It's a long story," he said, pulling his sleeve back down.

"I'm sure it is." She smiled quickly and left the roundabout, heading straight north, away from the city center. Barely catching two green lights, she reached the third intersection and turned sharply right onto Boulevard Malesherbes. They'd left the wealthy, tree-lined streets of the embassy area and entered a darker, industrial neighborhood. Sophie turned quickly left, and Langdon immediately knew where they were.

Gare Saint-Lazare.

The glass-roofed train terminal ahead looked like a strange mix of an airplane hangar and a greenhouse. European train stations never rested. Even at this hour, several taxis waited near the main entrance. Vendors were selling sandwiches and water, while tired-looking kids with backpacks came out of the station, rubbing their eyes and looking around as if trying to remember where they were. Up ahead, two city policemen stood on the sidewalk, giving directions to some lost tourists.

Sophie pulled her SmartCar in behind the taxis and parked illegally, even though there was plenty of legal parking across the street. Before Langdon could ask what was happening, she was out of the car. She rushed to the window of the taxi in front of them and started talking to the driver. As Langdon got out of the SmartCar, he saw Sophie give the taxi driver a large stack of money. The taxi driver nodded and then, surprisingly, drove off without them.

"What was that about?" Langdon asked, joining Sophie on the sidewalk as the taxi disappeared. Sophie was already heading for the train station entrance. "Come on. We're buying two tickets on the next train leaving Paris."

Langdon hurried after her. What started as a short trip to the U.S. Embassy had become a complete escape from Paris. Langdon liked this idea less and less.
