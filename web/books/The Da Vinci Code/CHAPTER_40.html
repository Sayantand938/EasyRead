The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 40

Fumbling with the gears, Langdon managed to steer the stolen taxi to the edge of the Bois de Boulogne, only stalling twice. But the funny side of it was lost as the taxi dispatcher kept calling their cab on the radio.

"Car five-six-three. Where are you? Answer!"

When Langdon reached the park exit, he gave in and slammed on the brakes. "You drive."

Sophie looked glad as she got behind the wheel. Soon she had the car running smoothly west on Allée de Longchamp, leaving the Garden of Earthly Delights behind.

"Which way to Rue Haxo?" Langdon asked, watching Sophie push the speedometer past a hundred kilometers an hour.

Sophie kept her eyes on the road. "The cab driver said it's next to the Roland Garros tennis stadium. I know that area."

Langdon took the heavy key from his pocket again, feeling its weight. He knew it was very important, maybe the key to his freedom. Earlier, telling Sophie about the Knights Templar, Langdon saw that the key, besides having the Priory seal, had another link to the Priory of Sion. The equal-armed cross stood for balance and harmony, but also for the Knights Templar. Everyone had seen paintings of Knights Templar in white robes with red equal-armed crosses. The Templar cross arms were slightly flared, but still equal.

A square cross. Like the one on this key.

Langdon's mind raced as he thought about what they might find. The Holy Grail. He almost laughed at how crazy that sounded. The Grail was thought to be in England, buried under a Templar church, hidden since at least 1500.

The era of Grand Master Da Vinci.

To keep their important documents safe, the Priory had to move them many times in the early centuries. Historians now think the Grail was moved perhaps six times after arriving in Europe from Jerusalem. The last time the Grail was "seen" was in 1447 when many people said they saw a fire that almost destroyed the documents before they were moved to safety in four large chests that each needed six men to carry. After that, no one claimed to see the Grail again. All that was left were rumors that it was hidden in Great Britain, the land of King Arthur and the Knights of the Round Table. Wherever it was, two things were still true:

Leonardo knew where the Grail was during his life.

That hiding place probably hasn't changed.

Because of this, Grail fans still studied Da Vinci's art and diaries, hoping to find a hidden clue to where the Grail is now. Some said the mountains in the background of Madonna of the Rocks looked like the hills with caves in Scotland. Others insisted that the way the disciples were placed in The Last Supper was a kind of code. Still others claimed that X-rays of the Mona Lisa showed she was first painted wearing a lapis lazuli pendant of Isis—a detail Da Vinci supposedly decided to paint over later. Langdon had never seen any proof of the pendant, and he couldn't imagine how it could reveal the Holy Grail, but Grail lovers still talked about it endlessly on Internet forums and chat rooms.

Everyone loves a conspiracy.

And the conspiracies kept coming. Most recently, there was the shocking discovery that Da Vinci's famous Adoration of the Magi was hiding a dark secret under its layers of paint. Italian art expert Maurizio Seracini revealed the disturbing truth, which the New York Times Magazine featured in a story called "The Leonardo Cover-Up." Seracini proved that while the gray-green sketched underdrawing of the Adoration was indeed Da Vinci's work, the painting itself was not. The truth was that an unknown painter had filled in Da Vinci's sketch like a paint-by-numbers picture years after Da Vinci died. Even more troubling was what was under the fake paint. Photos taken with infrared reflectography and X-rays suggested that this unknown painter, while filling in Da Vinci's sketch, had made strange changes to the underdrawing... as if to change Da Vinci's real intention. Whatever the true nature of the underdrawing, it hadn't been made public yet. Even so, embarrassed officials at Florence's Uffizi Gallery quickly moved the painting to a warehouse across the street. Visitors to the gallery's Leonardo Room now saw a misleading and unapologetic sign where the Adoration used to hang.

THIS WORK IS UNDERGOING

DIAGNOSTIC TESTS IN PREPARATION

FOR RESTORATION.

In the strange world of modern Grail seekers, Leonardo da Vinci was still the quest's big puzzle. His art seemed ready to tell a secret, but it stayed hidden, maybe under some paint, maybe coded in plain sight, or maybe nowhere at all. Perhaps Da Vinci's many tempting clues were just a trick to frustrate those who were curious and bring a smile to the face of his knowing Mona Lisa.

"Is it possible," Sophie asked, pulling Langdon back, "that the key you have unlocks the hiding place of the Holy Grail?"

Langdon's laugh sounded fake, even to him. "I can't really believe that. Besides, the Grail is thought to be hidden in the United Kingdom, not France." He gave her the short version of the history.

"But the Grail seems like the only logical answer," she insisted. "We have a very secure key, marked with the Priory of Sion seal, given to us by a member of the Priory of Sion—a group which, you just told me, guards the Holy Grail." Langdon knew she made sense, but he still couldn't accept it. There were rumors that the Priory had promised to bring the Grail back to France someday to its final resting place, but there was no real proof that this had happened. Even if the Priory had brought the Grail back to France, the address 24 Rue Haxo near a tennis stadium didn't sound like a proper final resting place. "Sophie, I really don't see how this key could have anything to do with the Grail."

"Because the Grail is supposed to be in England?"

"Not only that. The location of the Holy Grail is one of the best-kept secrets in history. Priory members wait years to prove themselves trustworthy before being allowed to know where the Grail is. That secret is protected by a careful system of limited knowledge, and even though the Priory is very large, only four members know where the Grail is hidden—the Grand Master and his three sénéchaux. The chance of your grandfather being one of those four top people is very small." My grandfather was one of them, Sophie thought, pressing down on the gas. She had a clear memory that proved her grandfather's high position in the brotherhood beyond any doubt.

"And even if your grandfather were in that top group, he would never be allowed to tell anyone outside the brotherhood anything. It's impossible that he would bring you into their inner circle."

I've already been there, Sophie thought, picturing the ritual in the basement. She wondered if she should tell Langdon what she had seen that night in the Normandy château. For ten years, simple shame had kept her from telling anyone. Just thinking about it made her shiver. Sirens wailed somewhere in the distance, and she felt very tired.

"There!" Langdon said, excited to see the huge Roland Garros tennis stadium ahead.

Sophie carefully drove toward the stadium. After driving around a few times, they found the intersection of Rue Haxo and turned onto it, heading toward the lower numbers. The road became more industrial, with businesses lining the street.

We need number twenty-four, Langdon thought, noticing he was subconsciously searching for church spires. Don't be absurd. A lost Templar church around here?

"There it is," Sophie said, pointing.

Langdon looked to where she was pointing.

What on earth?

The building was modern. A short, strong building with a huge, neon cross with equal arms shining on the front. Under the cross were the words:

DEPOSITORY BANK OF ZURICH

Langdon was glad he hadn't told Sophie about the Templar church. A common problem for symbologists was seeing hidden meanings where there were none. He had simply forgotten that the simple cross with equal arms was the symbol for neutral Switzerland.

At least the puzzle was solved.

Sophie and Langdon had the key to a Swiss bank safe deposit box.
