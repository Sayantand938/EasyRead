The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 94

St. James's Park is a green space in the middle of London, a public park next to the palaces of Westminster, Buckingham, and St. James's. King Henry VIII once fenced it in and filled it with deer for hunting. Now, St. James's Park is open to everyone. On sunny afternoons, Londoners picnic under the willow trees and feed the pelicans in the pond, whose ancestors were a gift to Charles II from the Russian ambassador.

The Teacher didn't see any pelicans today. The stormy weather had brought seagulls from the ocean instead. The lawns were covered with them—hundreds of white birds all facing the same way, patiently waiting out the damp wind. Despite the morning fog, the park had great views of the Houses of Parliament and Big Ben. Looking across the sloping lawns, past the duck pond and the delicate shapes of the weeping willows, the Teacher could see the spires of the building that held the knight's tomb—the real reason he had told Rémy to come here. As the Teacher walked up to the front passenger door of the parked limousine, Rémy leaned over and opened the door. The Teacher paused outside, taking a drink from the flask of cognac he was carrying. Then, wiping his mouth, he slid in beside Rémy and closed the door. Rémy held up the keystone like a trophy. "It was almost lost."

"You have done well," the Teacher said.

"We have done well," Rémy replied, placing the keystone in the Teacher's eager hands. The Teacher admired it for a moment, smiling. "And the gun? You wiped it down?"

"Back in the glove box where I found it."

"Excellent." The Teacher took another drink of cognac and handed the flask to Rémy. "Let's toast our success. The end is near."

Rémy took the bottle gratefully. The cognac tasted salty, but Rémy didn't care. He and the Teacher were truly partners now. He could feel himself rising to a higher position in life. I will never be a servant again. As Rémy looked down the embankment at the duck pond below, Château Villette seemed miles away.

Taking another drink from the flask, Rémy could feel the cognac warming his blood. The warmth in Rémy's throat, however, quickly changed to an uncomfortable heat. Loosening his bow tie, Rémy tasted an unpleasant grittiness and handed the flask back to the Teacher. "I've probably had enough," he managed, weakly.

Taking the flask, the Teacher said, "Rémy, as you know, you are the only one who knows my face. I placed enormous trust in you."

"Yes," he said, feeling hot as he loosened his tie more. "And your secret dies with me."

The Teacher was quiet for a moment. "I believe you." He put the flask and the keystone in his pocket, then reached for the glove box and took out the small Medusa revolver. Rémy felt a quick flash of fear, but the Teacher just slipped it into his pants pocket. What's he doing? Rémy suddenly felt himself sweating.

"I know I promised you freedom," the Teacher said, his voice sounding sad now. "But given the situation, this is the best I can do."

Rémy's throat tightened suddenly, and he leaned against the steering wheel, grabbing his throat and tasting vomit in his throat. He made a quiet, choked scream, not even loud enough to be heard outside the car. He now tasted the salt in the cognac.

I'm being murdered!

In disbelief, Rémy turned to see the Teacher sitting calmly beside him, staring straight ahead. Rémy's vision blurred, and he gasped for air. I made everything possible for him! How could he do this! Rémy would never know if the Teacher had always planned to kill him, or if it was Rémy's actions in the Temple Church that had made the Teacher change his mind. Terror and anger rushed through him. Rémy tried to lunge at the Teacher, but his body was stiffening and he could barely move. I trusted you with everything!

Rémy tried to lift his clenched fists to honk the horn, but instead he slipped sideways, rolling onto the seat, lying on his side next to the Teacher, clutching at his throat. The rain fell harder now. Rémy could no longer see, but he could feel his brain struggling to hold onto his last clear thoughts as it ran out of oxygen. As his world slowly went dark, Rémy Legaludec thought he heard the soft sounds of the Riviera surf.

The Teacher stepped out of the limousine, relieved that no one was watching him. I had no choice, he told himself, surprised at how little guilt he felt for what he had just done. Rémy brought this on himself. The Teacher had always worried that Rémy might have to be killed when the mission was over, but Rémy's bold appearance in the Temple Church had made it necessary much sooner. Robert Langdon's unexpected visit to Château Villette had given the Teacher both a lucky break and a difficult problem. Langdon had delivered the keystone right to their doorstep, which was a nice surprise, but he had also brought the police with him. Rémy's fingerprints were all over Château Villette, and in the barn's listening post, where Rémy had been doing surveillance. The Teacher was glad he had been careful to hide any connection between Rémy's actions and himself. No one could blame the Teacher unless Rémy talked, and that was no longer a concern. One more loose end to take care of, the Teacher thought, moving toward the back door of the limousine. The police will have no idea what happened... and no one will be left alive to tell them. Looking around to make sure no one was watching, he opened the door and climbed into the spacious back seat.

Minutes later, the Teacher was walking across St. James's Park. Only two people left now. Langdon and Neveu. They were more difficult. But manageable. Right now, though, the Teacher had to focus on the cryptex.

Looking proudly across the park, he could see where he was going. In London lies a knight a Pope interred. As soon as the Teacher had heard the poem, he had known the answer. Still, he wasn't surprised that the others hadn't figured it out. I have an unfair advantage. Having listened to Saunière's conversations for months, the Teacher had heard the Grand Master mention this famous knight sometimes, showing almost as much respect as he had for Da Vinci. The poem's reference to the knight was very simple once you saw it—a sign of Saunière's cleverness—but how this tomb would reveal the final password was still a mystery.

You seek the orb that should be on his tomb.

The Teacher vaguely remembered photos of the famous tomb and, especially, its most noticeable feature. A magnificent orb. The huge sphere on top of the tomb was almost as big as the tomb itself. The orb's presence felt both hopeful and worrying to the Teacher. On one hand, it seemed like a sign, yet, according to the poem, the missing piece was an orb that should be on his tomb... not one that was already there. He was counting on examining the tomb closely to reveal the answer.

The rain was getting heavier now, and he tucked the cryptex deep in his right pocket to protect it from the dampness. He kept the tiny Medusa revolver in his left, hidden from view. Within minutes, he was stepping into the quiet peace of London's grandest nine-hundred-year-old building.

Just as the Teacher was stepping out of the rain, Bishop Aringarosa was stepping into it. On the rainy tarmac at Biggin Hill Executive Airport, Aringarosa emerged from his cramped plane, pulling his cassock tight against the cold damp. He had hoped to be greeted by Captain Fache. Instead, a young British police officer approached with an umbrella.

"Bishop Aringarosa? Captain Fache had to leave. He asked me to look after you. He suggested I take you to Scotland Yard. He thought it would be safest."

Safest? Aringarosa looked down at the heavy briefcase of Vatican bonds clutched in his hand. He had almost forgotten. "Yes, thank you."

Aringarosa climbed into the police car, wondering where Silas could be. Minutes later, the police scanner crackled with the answer.

5 Orme Court.

Aringarosa recognized the address instantly.

The Opus Dei Centre in London.

He turned quickly to the driver. "Take me there now!"
