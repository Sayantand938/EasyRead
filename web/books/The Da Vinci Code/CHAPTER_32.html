The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 32

The security alarm on the west side of the Denon Wing startled the pigeons in the nearby Tuileries Gardens as Langdon and Sophie hurried from the doorway into the Paris night. As they ran across the plaza to Sophie's car, Langdon heard police sirens in the distance.

"It's there," Sophie said, pointing to a red, short, two-seater parked on the plaza. Is she serious? It was the smallest car Langdon had ever seen.

"SmartCar," she said. "A hundred kilometers per liter." Langdon had barely gotten into the passenger seat before Sophie drove the SmartCar over a curb onto a gravel divider. He held onto the dash as the car sped across a sidewalk and back down into the small roundabout at Carrousel du Louvre.

For a moment, Sophie seemed to think about cutting across the roundabout by driving straight ahead, through the hedge, and across the large circle of grass in the center.

"No!" Langdon shouted, knowing the hedges hid the dangerous pit in the center— La Pyramide Inversée —the upside-down pyramid skylight he had seen earlier. It was big enough to swallow their SmartCar whole. Luckily, Sophie took the normal route, turning the wheel hard to the right, circling until she exited, cut left, and swung into the northbound lane, heading toward Rue de Rivoli.

The police sirens grew louder behind them, and Langdon could now see the lights in his side mirror. The SmartCar engine struggled as Sophie sped away from the Louvre. Fifty yards ahead, the traffic light at Rivoli turned red. Sophie cursed quietly and kept going. Langdon tensed up.

"Sophie?"

Slowing a little as they reached the intersection, Sophie flashed her headlights and quickly looked both ways before speeding up again and making a sharp left turn through the empty intersection onto Rivoli. Speeding west for a quarter of a mile, Sophie turned right around a wide roundabout. Soon they were heading out the other side onto the wide avenue of Champs-Elysées.

As they straightened out, Langdon turned to look out the rear window toward the Louvre. The police didn't seem to be following them. The blue lights were gathering at the museum.

His heartbeat finally slowing, Langdon turned back around. "That was interesting." Sophie didn't seem to hear. Her eyes remained fixed ahead down the long street of Champs-Elysées, the two-mile stretch of fancy stores that was often called the Fifth Avenue of Paris. The embassy was only about a mile away, and Langdon settled into his seat. So dark the con of man. Sophie's quick thinking had been impressive. Madonna of the Rocks. Sophie had said her grandfather left her something behind the painting. A final message? Langdon could not help but marvel over Saunière's clever hiding place; Madonna of the Rocks was yet another fitting link in the evening's chain of interconnected symbolism. Saunière, it seemed, at every turn, was reinforcing his fondness for the dark and mischievous side of Leonardo da Vinci. Da Vinci's original commission for Madonna of the Rocks had come from an organization known as the Confraternity of the Immaculate Conception, which needed a painting for the centerpiece of an altar triptych in their church of San Francesco in Milan. The nuns gave Leonardo specific dimensions, and the desired theme for the painting—the Virgin Mary, baby John the Baptist, Uriel, and Baby Jesus sheltering in a cave. Although Da Vinci did as they requested, when he delivered the work, the group reacted with horror. He had filled the painting with shocking and disturbing details.

The painting showed a blue-robed Virgin Mary sitting with her arm around an infant child, presumably Baby Jesus. Opposite Mary sat Uriel, also with an infant, presumably baby John the Baptist. Oddly, though, rather than the usual Jesus-blessing-John scene, it was baby John who was blessing Jesus... and Jesus was submitting to his authority! More troubling still, Mary was holding one hand high above the head of infant John and making a decidedly threatening gesture—her fingers looking like eagle's talons, gripping an invisible head. Finally, the most obvious and frightening image: Just below Mary's curled fingers, Uriel was making a cutting gesture with his hand—as if slicing the neck of the invisible head gripped by Mary's claw-like hand.

Langdon's students were always amused to learn that Da Vinci eventually satisfied the confraternity by painting them a second, "toned-down" version of Madonna of the Rocks in which everyone was arranged in a more traditional manner. The second version now hung in London's National Gallery under the name Virgin of the Rocks, although Langdon still preferred the Louvre's more intriguing original.

As Sophie sped the car up Champs-Elysées, Langdon said, "The painting. What was behind it?" Her eyes remained on the road. "I'll show you once we're safely inside the embassy."

"You'll show it to me?" Langdon was surprised. "He left you a physical object?" Sophie gave a quick nod. "Embossed with a fleur-de-lis and the initials P.S." Langdon couldn't believe his ears.

We're going to make it, Sophie thought as she swung the SmartCar's wheel to the right, cutting sharply past the luxurious Hôtel de Crillon into Paris's tree-lined diplomatic neighborhood. The embassy was less than a mile away now. She was finally feeling like she could breathe normally again.

Even as she drove, Sophie's mind remained locked on the key in her pocket, her memories of seeing it many years ago, the gold head shaped as an equal-armed cross, the triangular shaft, the indentations, the embossed flowery seal, and the letters P.S.

Although the key barely had entered Sophie's thoughts through the years, her work in the intelligence community had taught her plenty about security, and now the key's peculiar tooling no longer looked so mysterious. A laser-tooled varying matrix. Impossible to duplicate. Rather than teeth that moved tumblers, this key's complex series of laser-burned marks was examined by an electric eye. If the eye determined that the hexagonal marks were correctly spaced, arranged, and rotated, then the lock would open.

Sophie could not begin to imagine what a key like this opened, but she sensed Robert would be able to tell her. After all, he had described the key's embossed seal without ever seeing it. The cross shape on top implied the key belonged to some kind of Christian organization, and yet Sophie knew of no churches that used laser-tooled varying matrix keys.

Besides, my grandfather was no Christian....

Sophie had seen proof of that ten years ago. Ironically, another key—a more ordinary one—had shown her what he was really like.

It was a warm afternoon when she arrived at Charles de Gaulle Airport and took a taxi home. Grand-père will be so surprised to see me, she thought. Sophie was back from graduate school in Britain for spring break a few days early, and she couldn't wait to see him and tell him about the encryption methods she was studying.

But when she got to their Paris home, her grandfather wasn't there. Disappointed, she knew he hadn't been expecting her and was probably at the Louvre. But it's Saturday afternoon, she thought. He rarely worked on weekends. Weekends were usually for—

Smiling, Sophie ran to the garage. His car was gone, just as she suspected. It was the weekend, and Jacques Saunière hated driving in the city. He only had a car to go to one place—his vacation home in Normandy, north of Paris. Sophie was eager to be out in nature after being in busy London for months, and she wanted to start her vacation right away. It was still early evening, so she decided to leave immediately and surprise him. After borrowing a friend's car, Sophie drove north, up into the quiet, moonlit hills near Creully. She arrived just after ten o'clock and turned onto the long driveway leading to her grandfather's home. The driveway was over a mile long, and she could only see the house through the trees halfway there—a huge, old stone château nestled in the woods on the side of a hill.

Sophie had almost expected to find her grandfather asleep at this hour, so she was surprised to see the house lit up. But her delight turned to surprise when she saw the driveway full of parked cars—Mercedeses, BMWs, Audis, and a Rolls-Royce. Sophie stared for a moment and then laughed. My grand-père, the famous recluse! It seemed Jacques Saunière was much less of a recluse than he pretended to be. He was clearly having a party while Sophie was away, and judging by the cars, some of Paris's most important people were there.

Excited to surprise him, she hurried to the front door. But it was locked. She knocked, but no one answered. Confused, she walked around and tried the back door. It was also locked, and no one answered.

Puzzled, she stood for a moment and listened. The only sound was the cool Normandy air moaning softly as it moved through the valley.

No music.

No voices.

Nothing.

In the quiet woods, Sophie rushed to the side of the house and climbed onto a woodpile, pressing her face against the living room window. What she saw inside didn't make sense.

"Nobody's here!"

The whole first floor looked empty.

Where is everyone?

Heart racing, Sophie ran to the woodshed and grabbed the spare key her grandfather hid under the kindling box. She ran to the front door and let herself in. As she stepped into the empty entrance hall, the security system's control panel began flashing red—warning that whoever entered had ten seconds to type in the right code before the alarms went off.

He has the alarm on during a party?

Sophie quickly typed the code and turned off the system.

Going inside, she found the whole house empty. Upstairs too. As she came back down to the deserted living room, she paused in the silence, wondering what could be happening.

That's when Sophie heard it.

Quiet voices. And they seemed to be coming from below her. Sophie couldn't believe it. Crouching, she put her ear to the floor and listened. Yes, the sound was definitely coming from underneath. The voices seemed to be singing, or... chanting? She was scared. Almost more unsettling than the sound was the thought that this house didn't even have a basement. At least none I've ever seen.

Turning and looking around the living room, Sophie noticed the only thing that seemed odd—her grandfather's favorite antique, a large Aubusson tapestry. It usually hung on the east wall by the fireplace, but tonight it had been moved aside on its brass rod, showing the wall behind it.

Walking to the bare wooden wall, Sophie felt the chanting getting louder. Unsure, she put her ear against the wood. The voices were clearer now. People were definitely chanting... saying words Sophie couldn't understand.

The space behind this wall is empty!

Feeling around the edge of the panels, Sophie found a hidden fingerhold. It was carefully made. A sliding door. Her heart pounding, she put her finger in the slot and pulled. The heavy wall slid sideways quietly. From the darkness beyond, the voices echoed up.

Sophie slipped through the door and found herself on a rough stone staircase that went down in a spiral. She'd been coming to this house since she was a child but had no idea this staircase was even here!

As she went down, the air got cooler. The voices clearer. She heard men and women now. She couldn't see far because of the spiral of the staircase, but the last step was now coming into view. Beyond it, she could see a small bit of the basement floor—stone, lit by the flickering orange light of a fire.

Holding her breath, Sophie moved down a few more steps and crouched to look. It took her a few seconds to understand what she was seeing.

The room was a grotto—a rough chamber that seemed to have been carved from the granite of the hillside. The only light came from torches on the walls. In the light of the flames, about thirty people stood in a circle in the center of the room.

I'm dreaming, Sophie thought. A dream. What else could this be?

Everyone in the room was wearing a mask. The women were dressed in white, see-through gowns and golden shoes. Their masks were white, and they held golden balls in their hands. The men wore long black robes, and their masks were black. They looked like pieces in a giant chess set. Everyone in the circle rocked back and forth and chanted in respect to something on the floor before them... something Sophie couldn't see.

The chanting grew steady again. Faster. Louder now. Even faster. The participants stepped inward and knelt. At that moment, Sophie could finally see what they all were seeing. Though she stumbled back in horror, she felt the image burning into her memory forever. Overcome with nausea, Sophie spun, grabbing the stone walls as she hurried back up the stairs. Shutting the door, she fled the empty house, and drove back to Paris in a tearful daze. That night, her life ruined by disappointment and betrayal, she packed her things and left her home. On the dining room table, she left a note.

I WAS THERE. DON'T TRY TO FIND ME.

Beside the note, she put the old spare key from the château's woodshed.

"Sophie!" Langdon's voice broke in. "Stop! Stop!"

Coming out of the memory, Sophie slammed on the brakes, screeching to a halt. "What? What is it?!"

Langdon pointed down the long street ahead.

When she saw it, Sophie's blood ran cold. A hundred yards ahead, the intersection was blocked by a couple of DCPJ police cars, parked at angles, their purpose clear. They've blocked off Avenue Gabriel!

Langdon sighed heavily. "I guess we can't get to the embassy tonight?" Down the street, the two DCPJ officers standing by their cars were now staring at them, curious about the headlights that had stopped so suddenly up the street from them.

Okay, Sophie, turn around very slowly.

Putting the SmartCar in reverse, she did a smooth three-point turn and went the other way. As she drove off, she heard tires screeching behind them. Sirens began to wail.

Sophie cursed and floored the accelerator.
