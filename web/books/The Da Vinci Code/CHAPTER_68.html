The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 68

New York editor Jonas Faukman had just gotten into bed when the phone rang. A bit late for calls, he muttered, picking up the phone. An operator asked, "Will you accept a collect call from Robert Langdon?" Confused, Jonas turned on the light. "Uh... sure, okay."

The line clicked. "Jonas?"

"Robert? You wake me up and make me pay for it?"

"Jonas, I'm sorry," Langdon said. "I'll be quick. I need to know. The manuscript I gave you. Have you—"

"Robert, I know I said I'd send you the edits this week, but I'm too busy. Next Monday, I promise."

"I'm not worried about the edits. I need to know if you sent out any copies for reviews without telling me?"

Faukman hesitated. Langdon's latest manuscript—about the history of goddess worship—had some parts about Mary Magdalene that would cause a stir. Although it was well researched and others had written about it, Faukman didn't want to print early copies of Langdon's book without endorsements from respected historians and art experts. Jonas had picked ten famous people in the art world and sent them parts of the manuscript with a letter asking if they'd write a short endorsement for the book jacket. Usually, people were eager to have their name in print.

"Jonas?" Langdon asked. "You sent out my manuscript, didn't you?" Faukman frowned, sensing Langdon wasn't pleased. "The manuscript was good, Robert, and I wanted to surprise you with great reviews."

A pause. "Did you send one to the curator of the Paris Louvre?"

"What do you think? You mentioned his Louvre collection in your manuscript a bunch of times, his books are in your bibliography, and the guy's got serious pull for foreign sales. Saunière was an easy choice." The silence on the other end stretched out. "When did you send it?"

"About a month ago. I also said you'd be in Paris soon and suggested you two talk. Did he ever call you to meet?" Faukman paused, rubbing his eyes. "Wait, aren't you supposed to be in Paris this week?"

"I am in Paris."

Faukman sat up straight. "You called me collect from Paris?"

"Take it out of my earnings, Jonas. Did you ever hear back from Saunière? Did he like the manuscript?"

"I don't know. I haven't heard from him yet."

"Well, don't expect much. I've got to go, but this makes sense now. Thanks."

"Robert—"

But Langdon had already hung up.

Faukman put down the phone, shaking his head in disbelief. Authors, he thought. Even the normal ones are crazy.

Inside the Range Rover, Leigh Teabing laughed loudly. "Robert, you're saying you wrote a book about a secret society, and your editor sent a copy to that secret society?" Langdon slumped. "Apparently."

"A bad coincidence, my friend."

Coincidence had nothing to do with it, Langdon knew. Asking Jacques Saunière to endorse a book on goddess worship was as obvious as asking Tiger Woods to endorse a book on golf. Plus, any book on goddess worship would almost certainly have to mention the Priory of Sion.

"Here's the big question," Teabing said, still chuckling. "Did you say good or bad things about the Priory?"

Langdon understood Teabing's real question clearly. Many historians wondered why the Priory was still hiding the Sangreal documents. Some thought the information should have been shared with the world a long time ago. "I didn't say whether the Priory was right or wrong."

"You mean, didn't do anything."

Langdon shrugged. Teabing seemed to think the documents should be made public. "I just gave the history of the group and said they were a modern goddess worship society, keepers of the Grail, and protectors of old documents."

Sophie looked at him. "Did you mention the keystone?"

Langdon cringed. He had, many times. "I talked about the keystone as an example of how far the Priory would go to protect the Sangreal documents." Sophie looked surprised. "I guess that explains P.S. Find Robert Langdon." Langdon felt that something else in the book had really caught Saunière's attention, but he would talk about that with Sophie later when they were alone.

"So," Sophie said, "you lied to Captain Fache."

"What?" Langdon asked.

"You told him you had never written to my grandfather."

"I didn't! My editor sent him a book."

"Think about it, Robert. If Captain Fache didn't find the envelope your editor used to send the book, he would think you sent it." She paused. "Or worse, that you delivered it yourself and lied about it."

When the Range Rover arrived at Le Bourget Airfield, Rémy drove to a small hangar at the far end of the airstrip. As they got closer, a messy-haired man in wrinkled khakis rushed from the hangar, waved, and slid open the huge metal door to show a shiny white jet inside. Langdon stared at the gleaming body. "That's Elizabeth?" Teabing grinned. "Better than the Chunnel."

The man in khakis hurried toward them, squinting into the headlights. "Almost ready, sir," he called in a British accent. "Sorry for the delay, but you surprised me and—" He stopped as the group got out. He looked at Sophie and Langdon, and then Teabing. Teabing said, "My friends and I have important business in London. We have no time to waste. Please get ready to leave now." As he spoke, Teabing took the pistol out of the car and handed it to Langdon.

The pilot's eyes widened at the sight of the gun. He walked over to Teabing and whispered, "Sir, I'm very sorry, but my flight agreement only covers you and your servant. I can't take your guests."

"Richard," Teabing said, smiling kindly, "two thousand pounds and that loaded gun say you can take my guests." He pointed to the Range Rover. "And the unlucky man in the back."
