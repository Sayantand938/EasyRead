The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 71

As the Hawker evened out, heading toward England, Langdon gently picked up the rosewood box from his lap, where he'd been keeping it safe during takeoff. Setting the box on the table, he felt Sophie and Teabing eagerly leaning closer. He unfastened the lid and opened the box, looking not at the lettered dials of the cryptex, but at the small hole on the underside of the lid. With the tip of a pen, he carefully took off the inlaid Rose on top, revealing the text underneath. Sub Rosa, he thought, hoping a new look at the text would make things clearer. Giving it his full attention, Langdon examined the

strange text.

After a few seconds, he started to feel frustrated again. "Leigh, I just can't seem to figure it out."

Sophie, sitting across the table, couldn't see the text yet, but Langdon's inability to name the language right away surprised her. Did my grandfather speak a language so rare that even a symbologist doesn't know it? She quickly remembered this shouldn't surprise her. It wasn't the first secret Jacques Saunière had kept from her.

Across from Sophie, Leigh Teabing was practically jumping out of his seat. Anxious to see the text for himself, he shook with excitement, leaning in, trying to peek around Langdon, who was still bent over the box.

"I'm not sure," Langdon said quietly. "I first thought it was Semitic, but now I doubt it. Most basic Semitic languages have nekkudot. This doesn't have any."

"Probably ancient," Teabing suggested.

"Nekkudot?" Sophie asked.

Teabing never took his eyes from the box. "Most modern Semitic alphabets don't have vowels and use nekkudot —small dots and dashes written below or inside the consonants—to show the vowel sound. Nekkudot are a newer addition to language."

Langdon was still looking at the script. "A Sephardic translation, maybe...?" Teabing couldn't take it anymore. "Maybe if I just..." Reaching over, he moved the box away from Langdon and pulled it closer. Langdon knew a lot about the usual ancient languages—Greek, Latin, the Romance languages—but from the quick look Teabing had of this language, he thought it seemed more specific, maybe a Rashi script or a STA'M with crowns. Taking a deep breath, Teabing stared at the engraving. He didn't say anything for a long time. With each second, Teabing felt his confidence drop. "I'm amazed," he said.

"This language is like nothing I've ever seen!"

Langdon sank down.

"Can I see it?" Sophie asked.

Teabing acted like he didn't hear her. "Robert, you said you thought you'd seen something like this before?"

Langdon looked annoyed. "I thought so. I'm not sure. The script looks familiar somehow."

"Leigh?" Sophie repeated, clearly not happy about being left out. "Can I look at the box my grandfather made?"

"Of course, dear," Teabing said, pushing it to her. He didn't mean to sound condescending, but Sophie Neveu was out of her depth. If a British Royal Historian and a Harvard symbologist couldn't even name the language—

"Aah," Sophie said, after looking at the box for a few seconds. "I should have known." Teabing and Langdon turned at the same time, staring at her.

"Guess what?" Teabing asked.

Sophie shrugged. "Guess that this would be the language my grandfather would have used."

"You're saying you can read this text?" Teabing exclaimed.

"Very easily," Sophie replied, clearly enjoying herself now. "My grandfather taught me this language when I was only six years old. I'm fluent." She leaned across the table and looked sternly at Teabing. "And frankly, sir, considering your loyalty to the Crown, I'm a little surprised you didn't recognize it."

Instantly, Langdon knew.

No wonder the script looks so familiar!

Years ago, Langdon had been to an event at Harvard's Fogg Museum. Harvard dropout Bill Gates had returned to his old school to loan the museum one of his valuable purchases—eighteen sheets of paper he had recently bought at an auction from the Armand Hammar Estate.

His winning offer—a staggering $30.8 million.

The author of the pages—Leonardo da Vinci.

The eighteen pages—now known as Leonardo's Codex Leicester after their famous owner, the Earl of Leicester—were all that was left of one of Leonardo's most interesting notebooks: essays and drawings describing Da Vinci's advanced ideas on astronomy, geology, archaeology, and hydrology.

Langdon would never forget his reaction after waiting in line to see the priceless parchment. He was completely let down. The pages made no sense. Although they were well-preserved and written in very neat handwriting—red ink on cream paper—the codex looked like nonsense. At first, Langdon thought he couldn't read it because Da Vinci wrote his notebooks in old Italian. But after looking closer, he realized he couldn't find a single Italian word, or even one letter.

"Try this, sir," whispered the female guide at the display case. She pointed to a hand mirror attached to the display with a chain. Langdon picked it up and looked at the text in the mirror.

Suddenly, it was clear.

Langdon had been so excited to read some of the great thinker's ideas that he had forgotten one of the man's many artistic skills was writing in a mirrored script that was almost impossible for anyone else to read. Historians still debated whether Da Vinci wrote this way just to amuse himself or to prevent people from looking over his shoulder and stealing his ideas, but it didn't matter. Da Vinci did what he wanted.

Sophie smiled to see that Robert understood her. "I can read the first few words," she said. "It's English."

Teabing was still complaining. "What's going on?"

"Reversed text," Langdon said. "We need a mirror."

"No, we don't," Sophie said. "I bet this wood is thin enough." She lifted the rosewood box up to a light on the wall and began looking at the underside of the lid. Her grandfather couldn't actually write in reverse, so he always cheated by writing normally, then flipping the paper over and tracing the reversed image. Sophie guessed that he had burned normal text into a block of wood and then sanded the back of the block until the wood was paper thin and the burning could be seen through the wood. Then he simply flipped the piece over and placed it in.

As Sophie moved the lid closer to the light, she saw she was right. The bright light shone through the thin layer of wood, and the writing appeared in reverse on the underside of the lid. It was instantly readable.

"English," Teabing groaned, hanging his head in shame. "My own language."

At the back of the plane, Rémy Legaludec struggled to hear over the loud engines, but he couldn't make out the conversation in front. Rémy didn't like how the night was going. Not one bit. He looked down at the tied-up monk at his feet. The man was perfectly still now, as if he had accepted his fate, or maybe, was silently praying to be saved.
