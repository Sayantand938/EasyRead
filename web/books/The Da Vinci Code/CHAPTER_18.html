The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 18

Fache raced down the Grand Gallery as Collet's radio shouted over the loud alarm.

"He jumped!" Collet yelled. "The signal's showing on Place du Carrousel! Outside the bathroom window! And it's not moving! Jesus, I think Langdon killed himself!" Fache heard the words, but they didn't register. He kept running. The hallway seemed endless. As he ran past Saunière's body, he focused on the partitions at the far end of the Denon Wing. The alarm grew louder.

"Wait!" Collet's voice blared again. "He's moving! My God, he's alive. Langdon's moving!"

Fache kept running, cursing the long hallway with every step.

"Langdon's moving faster!" Collet yelled into the radio. "He's running down Carrousel. Wait... he's speeding up. He's moving too fast!"

Reaching the partitions, Fache weaved through them, saw the restroom door, and ran for it.

The walkie-talkie was almost drowned out by the alarm. "He must be in a car! I think he's in a car! I can't—"

Collet's words were lost in the alarm as Fache finally burst into the men's room, gun in hand. Wincing at the piercing noise, he looked around.

The stalls were empty. The bathroom was deserted. Fache's eyes went straight to the shattered window at the far end of the room. He ran to the opening and looked over the edge. Langdon was gone. Fache couldn't believe anyone would risk such a jump. If he had fallen that far, he would be badly hurt.

The alarm stopped, and Collet's voice came through the walkie-talkie again.

"...moving south... faster... crossing the Seine on Pont du Carrousel!" Fache turned. The only vehicle on Pont du Carrousel was a huge Trailor delivery truck heading south away from the Louvre. Its open bed was covered with a vinyl tarp, like a giant hammock. Fache felt a chill. That truck had probably been stopped at a red light right under the restroom window moments ago.

A crazy risk, Fache thought. Langdon couldn't know what was under the tarp. What if the truck was carrying steel? Or cement? Or garbage? A forty-foot jump? It was insane.

"The dot is turning!" Collet said. "He's turning right on Pont des Saints-Peres!" The Trailor truck that had crossed the bridge was slowing down and turning right onto Pont des Saints-Peres. So be it, Fache thought. He watched, amazed, as the truck disappeared. Collet was already radioing the agents outside, pulling them from the Louvre and sending them to their cars to follow, announcing the truck's location like a strange sports commentator.

It's over, Fache knew. His men would surround the truck in minutes. Langdon wasn't getting away.

Putting away his gun, Fache left the restroom and radioed Collet. "Bring my car around. I want to be there when we arrest him."

As Fache walked quickly back down the Grand Gallery, he wondered if Langdon had survived the fall.

Not that it mattered.

Langdon ran. Guilty.

Just fifteen yards from the restroom, Langdon and Sophie stood in the dark Grand Gallery, their backs against one of the large walls that hid the bathrooms from the gallery. They had barely hidden before Fache had run past them, gun out, and disappeared into the bathroom.

The last sixty seconds had been a blur.

Langdon stood in the men's room, refusing to run from a crime he didn't commit. Sophie was looking at the large glass window, checking out the alarm wires running through it. Then she looked down at the street, as if judging the distance.

"With some luck, you can get out of here," she said.

Luck? Feeling worried, he looked out the restroom window.

Up the street, a huge truck was approaching the stoplight beneath the window. A blue tarp was spread over the truck's large cargo area, loosely covering what it was carrying. Langdon hoped Sophie wasn't thinking what he thought she was.

"Sophie, I'm not jump—"

"Get rid of the tracker."

Confused, Langdon reached into his pocket and found the small metal disk. Sophie took it and went straight to the sink. She grabbed a big bar of soap, put the tracker on top, and pressed it down hard with her thumb. As the disk sank into the soap, she closed the hole, trapping the device inside. She gave the soap to Langdon and grabbed a heavy, round trash can from under the sinks. Before Langdon could say anything, Sophie ran at the window, holding the can like a weapon. She smashed the bottom of the trash can into the center of the window, breaking the glass.

Loud alarms went off above them.

"Give me the soap!" Sophie shouted, barely loud enough to hear over the alarm. Langdon pushed the bar into her hand.

Holding the soap, she looked out the broken window at the big rig idling below. The target was plenty big—a large, still tarp—and it was less than ten feet from the building. As the traffic lights were about to change, Sophie took a deep breath and threw the soap out into the night.

The soap fell toward the truck, landing on the edge of the tarp, and sliding down into the cargo bay as the traffic light turned green.

"Congratulations," Sophie said, pulling him toward the door. "You just escaped from the Louvre."

Leaving the men's room, they moved into the shadows as Fache rushed past.

Now that the fire alarm was off, Langdon could hear the sounds of police sirens leaving the Louvre. The police were leaving. Fache had hurried off, too, leaving the Grand Gallery empty.

"There's an emergency stairwell about fifty meters back in the Grand Gallery," Sophie said.

"Now that the guards are leaving, we can get out of here." Langdon decided to be quiet for the rest of the night. Sophie Neveu was clearly much smarter than he was.
