The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 55

Sitting on the couch next to Langdon, Sophie drank her tea and ate a scone, feeling the caffeine and food help her feel better. Sir Leigh Teabing was smiling widely as he walked back and forth awkwardly in front of the fireplace, his leg braces clicking on the stone floor.

"The Holy Grail," Teabing said, sounding like he was giving a speech. "Most people only ask me where it is. I'm afraid that's a question I may never answer." He turned and looked right at Sophie. "However... the more important question is this: What is the Holy Grail?"

Sophie felt that both of her male companions were now very interested and eager to learn.

"To really understand the Grail," Teabing continued, "we need to first understand the Bible. How familiar are you with the New Testament?"

Sophie shrugged. "Not at all, really. I was raised by a man who admired Leonardo da Vinci." Teabing looked both surprised and happy. "A wise man. Excellent! Then you must know that Leonardo was one of the people who protected the secret of the Holy Grail. And he hid clues in his art."

"Robert told me that, yes."

"And what did Da Vinci think about the New Testament?"

"I have no idea."

Teabing's eyes twinkled with amusement as he pointed to the bookshelf across the room. "Robert, would you mind? On the bottom shelf. La Storia di Leonardo."

Langdon walked over, picked up a big art book, and brought it back to the table. He turned the book toward Sophie, and Teabing opened it to a page inside the back cover with several quotes. "From Da Vinci's notebook, about arguments and ideas," Teabing said, pointing to one quote. "I think this is important to what we're talking about."

Sophie read the words.

Many have made a business of lies

and fake miracles, fooling the foolish.

—LEONARDO DA VINCI

"Here's another," Teabing said, pointing to a different quote. Complete ignorance tricks us.

Oh! Sad humans, open your eyes!

—LEONARDO DA VINCI

Sophie felt a slight shiver. "Da Vinci is talking about the Bible?" Teabing nodded. "Leonardo's thoughts on the Bible are directly related to the Holy Grail. Da Vinci actually painted the real Grail, which I'll show you in a moment, but first, we need to talk about the Bible." Teabing smiled. "And everything you need to know about the Bible can be said by the great expert Martyn Percy." Teabing cleared his throat and announced, "The Bible wasn't sent from heaven by fax."

"I'm sorry, what?"

The Bible is a product of man, my dear. Not of God. The Bible didn't magically fall from the sky. Man created it as a historical record of difficult times, and it has changed through many translations, additions, and revisions. History has never had one final version of the book."

"Okay."

"Jesus Christ was a historical figure with great influence, maybe the most mysterious and inspiring leader the world has ever seen. As the promised Messiah, Jesus overthrew kings, inspired millions, and started new philosophies. As a descendant of King Solomon and King David, Jesus had a rightful claim to the throne of the King of the Jews. Naturally, His life was written down by thousands of followers across the land." Teabing paused to sip his tea and then put the cup back on the mantel. "More than eighty gospels were considered for the New Testament, but only a few were chosen—Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John among them.

"Who chose which gospels to include?" Sophie asked.

"Aha!" Teabing said with enthusiasm. "The basic irony of Christianity! The Bible, as we know it today, was put together by the pagan Roman emperor Constantine the Great."

"I thought Constantine was a Christian," Sophie said.

"Hardly," Teabing scoffed. "He was a pagan his whole life and was only baptized on his deathbed, when he was too weak to argue. In Constantine's time, Rome's official religion was sun worship—the cult of Sol Invictus, or the Invincible Sun—and Constantine was its main priest. But a growing religious problem was taking over Rome. Three centuries after Jesus Christ's death, His followers had grown greatly in number. Christians and pagans started fighting, and the conflict became so big that it threatened to tear Rome apart. Constantine decided something had to be done. In 325 A.D., he decided to unite Rome under one religion: Christianity." Sophie was surprised. "Why would a pagan emperor choose Christianity as the official religion?" Teabing chuckled. "Constantine was a very good businessman. He could see that Christianity was becoming more popular, and he simply supported the winner. Historians still admire how cleverly Constantine converted the sun-worshipping pagans to Christianity. By mixing pagan symbols, dates, and rituals into the growing Christian tradition, he created a kind of combined religion that was acceptable to both groups."

"Transmogrification," Langdon said. "The signs of pagan religion in Christian symbols are clear. Egyptian sun disks became the halos of Catholic saints. Pictures of Isis nursing her miraculously conceived son Horus became the basis for our modern images of the Virgin Mary nursing Baby Jesus. And almost all the parts of the Catholic ritual—the miter, the altar, the doxology, and communion, the act of "God-eating"—were taken directly from earlier pagan mystery religions."

Teabing groaned. "Don't get a symbologist started on Christian icons. Nothing in Christianity is original. The pre-Christian God Mithras—called the Son of God and the Light of the World —was born on December 25, died, was buried in a rock tomb, and then came back to life in three days. By the way, December 25 is also the birthday of Osiris, Adonis, and Dionysus. The newborn Krishna was given gold, frankincense, and myrrh. Even Christianity's weekly holy day was taken from the pagans."

"What do you mean?"

"Originally," Langdon said, "Christianity followed the Jewish Sabbath on Saturday, but Constantine changed it to match the pagans' day of worship for the sun." He paused, grinning. "Even today, most churchgoers go to services on Sunday morning without knowing they are there because of the pagan sun god's weekly tribute— Sun day."

Sophie's head was spinning. "And how does this relate to the Grail?"

"Indeed," Teabing said. "Follow me. During this blending of religions, Constantine needed to reinforce the new Christian tradition, and he held a famous meeting known as the Council of Nicaea."

Sophie had only heard of it as the origin of the Nicene Creed.

"At this meeting," Teabing said, "many parts of Christianity were discussed and voted on—the date of Easter, the role of bishops, the performance of sacraments, and, of course, the divinity of Jesus."

"I don't understand. His divinity?"

"My dear," Teabing said, "until that point in history, Jesus was seen by His followers as a human prophet... a great and powerful man, but still a man. A mortal."

"Not the Son of God?"

"Correct," Teabing said. "Jesus being declared 'the Son of God' was officially suggested and voted on by the Council of Nicaea."

"Wait. You're saying Jesus' divinity was decided by a vote?"

"It was a close vote," Teabing added. "However, declaring Christ divine was key to uniting the Roman empire and building the Vatican's power. By officially calling Jesus the Son of God, Constantine made Jesus a deity beyond human reach, with power that couldn't be questioned. This stopped pagan challenges to Christianity, and now Christ's followers could only be saved through the Church." Sophie looked at Langdon, who nodded slightly in agreement.

"It was all about power," Teabing continued. "Christ as Messiah was vital to the Church and state. Many experts say the early Church stole Jesus from His followers, twisting His human message, hiding it with a thick layer of divinity, and using it to gain power. I've written books about it."

"So, Christians send you hate mail every day?"

"Why would they?" Teabing asked. "Most educated Christians know their faith's history. Jesus was a great man. Constantine's sneaky politics don't lessen Christ's importance. No one says Christ was a fake or denies He lived and inspired people. We're just saying Constantine used Christ's influence. In doing so, he shaped Christianity as we know it."

Sophie glanced at the art book, eager to see the Da Vinci painting of the Holy Grail.

"Here's the twist," Teabing said, talking faster. "Because Constantine upgraded Jesus almost four centuries after His death, many documents already showed His life as a human. To rewrite history, Constantine needed a bold move. This led to the most important moment in Christian history." Teabing paused, looking at Sophie.

"Constantine ordered a new Bible, which left out gospels that showed Christ's human side and emphasized those that made Him seem godlike. The older gospels were banned, collected, and burned."

"An interesting point," Langdon added. "Anyone who chose the banned gospels was called a heretic. The word heretic comes from that time. The Latin word haereticus means 'choice.' Those who 'chose' the original history of Christ were the first heretics."

"Luckily for historians," Teabing said, "some gospels Constantine tried to destroy survived. The Dead Sea Scrolls were found in the 1950s in a cave near Qumran. And the Coptic Scrolls in 1945 at Nag Hammadi. Besides telling the true Grail story, these documents show Christ's ministry in human terms. Of course, the Vatican, as usual, tried to hide these scrolls. Why wouldn't they? The scrolls show historical errors and lies, proving the modern Bible was written and edited by men with a political goal—to promote Jesus' divinity and use His influence to strengthen their power."

"Yet," Langdon said, "it's important to remember that the Church's desire to hide these documents comes from their strong belief in their view of Christ. The Vatican is made of devoted men who think these opposing documents must be false."

Teabing chuckled as he sat down in a chair across from Sophie. "As you can see, our professor loves Rome more than I do. Still, he's right that today's clergy thinks these conflicting papers are lies. That makes sense. Constantine's Bible has been their truth for so long. No one is more brainwashed than the brainwasher."

"He means," Langdon said, "that we believe what our parents believed."

"What I mean," Teabing replied, "is that almost everything our parents told us about Christ is wrong. And so are the stories about the Holy Grail."

Sophie looked again at the Da Vinci quote in front of her. Blinding ignorance does mislead us. O!

Wretched mortals, open your eyes!

Teabing grabbed the book and turned to the middle. "And lastly, before I show you Da Vinci's paintings of the Holy Grail, take a quick look at this." He opened the book to a bright picture that took up two pages. "I guess you know this painting?" He has to be joking, right? Sophie was looking at the most famous painting ever— The Last Supper —Da Vinci's famous painting from the wall of Santa Maria delle Grazie near Milan. The old painting showed Jesus and His followers when Jesus said one of them would betray Him. "I know the painting, yes."

"Then will you play this little game with me? Close your eyes." Not sure, Sophie closed her eyes.

"Where is Jesus sitting?" Teabing asked.

"In the middle."

"Good. And what food are He and His followers sharing?"

"Bread." Of course.

"Excellent. And what to drink?"

"Wine. They drank wine."

"Great. One last question. How many wineglasses are on the table?" Sophie hesitated, knowing it was a trick. And after dinner, Jesus took the cup of wine, sharing it with His disciples. "One cup," she said. "The chalice." The Cup of Christ. The Holy Grail. "Jesus passed a single chalice of wine, like modern Christians do at communion." Teabing sighed. "Open your eyes."

She did. Teabing was grinning. Sophie looked at the painting, surprised to see that everyone at the table had a glass of wine, including Christ. Thirteen cups. Also, the cups were small, without stems, and made of glass. There was no chalice in the painting. No Holy Grail.

Teabing's eyes twinkled. "A little strange, don't you think, since the Bible and the Grail story say this is when the Holy Grail appeared? Oddly, Da Vinci seems to have forgotten to paint the Cup of Christ."

"Surely art experts have noticed that."

"You'll be surprised by the strange things Da Vinci put here that most experts don't see or ignore. This painting is the key to the Holy Grail mystery. Da Vinci shows it all in The Last Supper."

Sophie looked at the work eagerly. "Does this fresco tell us what the Grail really is?"

"Not what it is," Teabing whispered. "But rather who it is. The Holy Grail is not an object. It is, in fact... a person."
