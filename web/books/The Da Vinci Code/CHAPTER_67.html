The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 67

The Range Rover was Java Black Pearl, four-wheel drive, standard transmission, with strong plastic lamps, rear light fittings, and the steering wheel on the right. Langdon was glad he wasn't driving.

Teabing's servant Rémy, following his master's orders, was skillfully driving the vehicle across the moonlit fields behind Château Villette. Without headlights, he had crossed an open hill and was now going down a long slope, moving farther from the estate. He seemed to be going toward a rough outline of wooded land in the distance. Langdon, holding the keystone, turned in the passenger seat and looked at Teabing and Sophie in the back seat.

"How's your head, Robert?" Sophie asked, sounding worried.

Langdon forced a weak smile. "Better, thanks." It was really hurting. Beside her, Teabing glanced at the tied and gagged monk lying in the small luggage area behind the back seat. Teabing had the monk's gun on his lap and looked like an old photo of a British safari hunter posing with his kill.

"So glad you came by this evening, Robert," Teabing said, grinning as if he were enjoying himself for the first time in years.

"Sorry to get you involved in this, Leigh."

"Oh, please, I've waited my whole life to be involved." Teabing looked past Langdon out the windshield at the shadow of a long hedgerow. He tapped Rémy on the shoulder from behind.

"Remember, no brake lights. Use the emergency brake if you need it. I want to get into the woods a bit. No need to risk them seeing us from the house."

Rémy slowed to a crawl and guided the Range Rover through an opening in the hedge. As the vehicle bumped onto an overgrown path, the trees overhead quickly blocked out the moonlight.

I can't see a thing, Langdon thought, trying to make out any shapes at all in front of them. It was completely dark. Branches scraped against the left side of the car, and Rémy steered in the other direction. Keeping the wheel mostly straight, he moved forward about thirty yards.

"You're doing great, Rémy," Teabing said. "That should be far enough. Robert, if you could push that little blue button just below the vent there. See it?"

Langdon found the button and pushed it.

A soft yellow light spread across the path in front of them, showing thick bushes on either side of the trail. Fog lights, Langdon realized. They gave off just enough light to stay on the path, but they were far enough into the woods that the lights wouldn't give them away.

"Well, Rémy," Teabing said cheerfully. "The lights are on. Our lives are in your hands."

"Where are we going?" Sophie asked.

"This trail goes on for about three kilometers into the forest," Teabing said. "Cutting across the estate and then curving north. If we don't hit any puddles or fallen trees, we'll come out safely on the side of highway five."

Safely. Langdon's head disagreed. He looked down at his lap, where the keystone was safely stored in its wooden box. The inlaid Rose on the lid was back in place, and even though his head felt confused, Langdon was eager to remove the inlay again and look at the engraving underneath more closely. He unlatched the lid and started to lift it when Teabing put a hand on his shoulder from behind.

"Patience, Robert," Teabing said. "It's bumpy and dark. God help us if we break anything. If you didn't recognize the language in the light, you won't do any better in the dark. Let's focus on getting away safely, okay? There will be time for that very soon." Langdon knew Teabing was right. He nodded and relatched the box.

The monk in back was moaning now, struggling against his ropes. Suddenly, he began kicking wildly.

Teabing spun around and aimed the pistol over the seat. "I don't understand your complaint, sir. You broke into my home and left a nasty bump on a dear friend's head. I have every right to shoot you right now and leave you in the woods." The monk went silent.

"Are you sure we should have brought him?" Langdon asked.

"Absolutely!" Teabing exclaimed. "You're wanted for murder, Robert. This man is your way to freedom. The police clearly want you badly enough to have followed you to my home."

"My fault," Sophie said. "The armored car probably had a tracker."

"That's not the point," Teabing said. "I'm not surprised the police found you, but I am surprised that this Opus Dei man found you. From what you've told me, I don't see how this man could have followed you to my home unless he had someone on the inside, either in the Judicial Police or in the Zurich Depository."

Langdon thought about it. Bezu Fache seemed determined to find someone to blame for tonight's murders. And Vernet had turned on them quickly, although since Langdon was being accused of four murders, the banker's change of heart seemed understandable.

"This monk isn't working alone, Robert," Teabing said, "and until you know who is behind this, you're both in danger. The good news, my friend, is that you now have the upper hand. This monster behind me has that information, and whoever is controlling him must be quite nervous right now."

Rémy was speeding up, getting used to the trail. They splashed through some water, climbed a small hill, and started going down again.

"Robert, could you hand me that phone?" Teabing pointed to the car phone on the dash. Langdon gave it to him, and Teabing dialed a number. He waited a long time before someone answered. "Richard? Did I wake you? Of course, I did. Stupid question. I'm sorry. I have a small problem. I'm not feeling well. Rémy and I need to go to the Isles for my treatments. Right away, actually. Sorry for the short notice. Can you have Elizabeth ready in about twenty minutes? I know, just do your best. See you soon." He hung up.

"Elizabeth?" Langdon said.

"My plane. She cost me a fortune."

Langdon turned around and looked at him.

"What?" Teabing demanded. "You two can't expect to stay in France with the entire police force after you. London will be much safer."

Sophie had turned to Teabing as well. "You think we should leave the country?"

"My friends, I have more influence in the world than here in France. Besides, the Grail is thought to be in Great Britain. If we unlock the keystone, I'm sure we'll find a map that shows we're on the right track."

"You're taking a big risk," Sophie said, "by helping us. You won't make friends with the French police."

Teabing waved his hand in disgust. "I'm done with France. I moved here to find the keystone. That's finished now. I don't care if I ever see Château Villette again." Sophie sounded unsure. "How will we get through airport security?" Teabing chuckled. "I fly from Le Bourget—a private airfield near here. French doctors make me nervous, so every two weeks, I fly to England for my treatments. I pay for special treatment at both airports. Once we're in the air, you can decide if you want someone from the U.S. Embassy to meet us."

Langdon suddenly didn't want anything to do with the embassy. All he could think about was the keystone, the inscription, and whether it would all lead to the Grail. He wondered if Teabing was right about Britain. Most modern stories placed the Grail in the United Kingdom. Even King Arthur's mythical Isle of Avalon, full of Grail legends, was now thought to be Glastonbury, England. Wherever the Grail was, Langdon never thought he would be looking for it. The Sangreal documents. The real story of Jesus Christ. The tomb of Mary Magdalene. He felt like he was in some kind of dream tonight... a bubble where the real world couldn't reach him.

"Sir?" Rémy said. "Are you really thinking of moving back to England for good?"

"Rémy, don't worry," Teabing said. "Just because I'm returning to England doesn't mean I'm going to eat sausages and mashed potatoes every day. I expect you'll join me there permanently. I'm planning to buy a beautiful house in Devonshire, and we'll have all your things sent there right away. An adventure, Rémy. I say, an adventure!" Langdon smiled. As Teabing talked excitedly about his plans to return to Britain, Langdon felt caught up in the man's contagious enthusiasm.

Looking blankly out the window, Langdon watched the woods go by, pale and ghostly in the yellow glow of the fog lights. The side mirror was turned in, bumped out of place by branches, and Langdon saw Sophie's reflection in the back seat, sitting quietly. He watched her for a while and felt a surprising sense of peace. Despite his problems tonight, Langdon was grateful to have ended up with such good company.

After a few minutes, as if she felt him watching her, Sophie leaned forward and put her hands on his shoulders, quickly rubbing them. "You okay?"

"Yeah," Langdon said. "Somehow."

Sophie sat back in her seat, and Langdon saw a small smile appear on her face. He realized he was smiling too.

Squeezed into the back of the Range Rover, Silas could hardly breathe. His arms were pulled back and tightly tied to his ankles with kitchen twine and duct tape. Every bump in the road sent pain through his twisted shoulders. At least his captors had taken off the cilice. Unable to breathe in through the tape over his mouth, he could only breathe through his nose, which was slowly getting blocked by the dusty cargo area he was crammed into. He started coughing.

"I think he's choking," the French driver said, sounding worried. The British man who had hit Silas with his crutch turned and looked over the seat, frowning coldly at Silas. "Luckily for you, we British measure a man's decency not by how he treats his friends, but how he treats his enemies." The Brit reached down and grabbed the duct tape on Silas's mouth. In one quick motion, he ripped it off. Silas felt like his lips were on fire, but the air rushing into his lungs was a blessing.

"Who do you work for?" the British man asked.

"I do God's work," Silas replied, despite the pain in his jaw where the woman had kicked him.

"You belong to Opus Dei," the man said. It wasn't a question.

"You know nothing about me."

"Why does Opus Dei want the keystone?"

Silas didn't plan to answer. The keystone led to the Holy Grail, and the Holy Grail was vital for protecting the faith.

I do God's work. The Way is in danger.

Now, in the Range Rover, struggling against his restraints, Silas worried he had failed the Teacher and the bishop for good. He couldn't even reach them to tell them what had happened. My captors have the keystone! They'll find the Grail before us! In the stuffy darkness, Silas prayed. He used his physical pain to strengthen his prayers.

A miracle, Lord. I need a miracle. Silas didn't know that in a few hours, he would get one.

"Robert?" Sophie was still watching him. "You just had a strange look on your face." Langdon looked at her, realizing his jaw was tight and his heart was pounding. He had just had an amazing idea. Could the explanation really be that simple? "I need to use your cell phone, Sophie."

"Now?"

"I think I just figured something out."

"What?"

"I'll tell you in a minute. I need your phone."

Sophie seemed cautious. "I doubt Fache is tracking, but keep it under a minute just in case." She handed him her phone.

"How do I call the States?"

"You need to call collect. My plan doesn't cover overseas." Langdon dialed zero, knowing that the next minute might solve a mystery that had confused him all night.
