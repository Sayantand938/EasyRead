The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 12

Robert Langdon felt dizzy as he walked toward the end of the Grand Gallery. Sophie's phone message kept playing in his mind. At the end of the hall, signs with the international stick-figure symbols for restrooms guided him through a maze of dividers showing Italian drawings that hid the restrooms. He found the men's room door, went in, and turned on the lights.

The room was empty.

He walked to the sink, splashed cold water on his face, and tried to wake up. Bright fluorescent lights shone on the plain tile, and the room smelled like ammonia. As he dried his face, the restroom door creaked open behind him. He turned around quickly.

Sophie Neveu came in, her green eyes showing fear. "Thank God you came. We don't have much time."

Langdon stood by the sinks, staring in surprise at DCPJ cryptographer Sophie Neveu. Just minutes ago, he had heard her phone message and thought she must be crazy. But the more he listened, the more he felt she was being serious. Do not react to this message. Just listen calmly. You are in danger right now. Follow my directions very closely. Feeling uncertain, Langdon had decided to do as she said. He told Fache that the phone message was about a friend back home who was hurt. Then he had asked to use the restroom at the end of the Grand Gallery. Sophie stood before him now, still catching her breath from rushing back to the restroom. In the fluorescent lights, Langdon was surprised to see that her strong presence came from surprisingly soft features. Only her eyes were sharp, and the contrast made him think of a layered Renoir painting... hidden but clear, with a boldness that still felt mysterious.

"I wanted to warn you, Mr. Langdon..." Sophie began, still catching her breath, "that you are sous surveillance cachée. Under secret observation." As she spoke, her accented English echoed off the tile walls, making her voice sound hollow.

"But... why?" Langdon asked. Sophie had already explained it on the phone, but he wanted to hear it from her directly.

"Because," she said, stepping toward him, "Fache thinks you are the main suspect in this murder." Langdon was ready for the words, but they still sounded absurd. According to Sophie, Langdon had been called to the Louvre not as a symbologist but as a suspect and was now the unknowing target of one of DCPJ's favorite interrogation methods— surveillance cachée —a clever trick where the police calmly invite a suspect to a crime scene and interview him, hoping he will get nervous and accidentally admit his guilt.

"Look in the left pocket of your jacket," Sophie said. "You'll find proof they're watching you." Langdon felt his worry growing. Look in my pocket? It sounded like a cheap magic trick.

"Just look."

Confused, Langdon reached into the left pocket of his tweed jacket—a pocket he never used. He felt around inside but found nothing. What did you expect? He wondered if Sophie might be crazy after all. Then his fingers touched something unexpected. Small and hard. He pinched the tiny object and pulled it out, staring in surprise. It was a metallic disk, shaped like a button, about the size of a watch battery. He had never seen it before. "What the...?"

"GPS tracking dot," Sophie said. "It constantly sends its location to a GPS satellite that DCPJ can watch. We use them to track people. It's accurate within two feet anywhere in the world. They're watching you electronically. The agent who picked you up at the hotel slipped it into your pocket before you left your room." Langdon remembered the hotel room... his quick shower, getting dressed, the DCPJ agent politely holding his tweed coat as they left. It's cool outside, Mr. Langdon, the agent had said. Paris in spring isn't always what you expect. Langdon had thanked him and put on the jacket.

Sophie's sharp gaze was intense. "I didn't tell you about the tracking dot earlier because I didn't want you checking your pocket in front of Fache. He can't know you've found it." Langdon didn't know what to say.

"They put the GPS on you because they thought you might run." She paused. "Actually, they hoped you would run; it would make their case stronger."

"Why would I run!" Langdon demanded. "I'm innocent!"

"Fache doesn't think so."

Angrily, Langdon walked toward the trash can to throw away the tracking dot.

"No!" Sophie grabbed his arm and stopped him. "Leave it in your pocket. If you throw it away, the signal will stop, and they'll know you found it. Fache is only leaving you alone because he can see where you are. If he thinks you've found out what he's doing..." Sophie didn't finish. Instead, she took the metallic disk from Langdon's hand and put it back in his coat pocket. "The dot stays with you. For now, anyway." Langdon felt lost. "How could Fache really think I killed Jacques Saunière!"

"He has some pretty good reasons to suspect you." Sophie looked serious. "There's a piece of evidence here that you haven't seen yet. Fache has kept it hidden from you." Langdon could only stare.

"Do you remember the three lines Saunière wrote on the floor?" Langdon nodded. The numbers and words were stuck in his mind. Sophie whispered, "What you saw wasn't the whole message. There was a fourth line Fache photographed and then erased before you got there."

Langdon knew the special ink could be easily wiped away, but he couldn't understand why Fache would erase evidence.

"The last line," Sophie said, "was something Fache didn't want you to know." She paused. "At least, not until he was done with you." Sophie pulled a computer printout from her sweater pocket and unfolded it.

"Fache sent images of the crime scene to the Cryptology Department earlier so we could try to figure out Saunière's message. This is a photo of the complete message." She handed the page to Langdon.

Confused, Langdon looked at the image. The close-up photo showed the glowing message on the floor. The final line hit Langdon hard.

13-3-2-21-1-1-8-5

O, Draconian devil!

Oh, lame saint!
