The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 52

The large, 185-acre estate of Château Villette was located twenty-five minutes northwest of Paris, near Versailles. Designed by François Mansart in 1668 for the Count of Aufflay, it was one of Paris's most important historical châteaux. With two rectangular lakes and gardens designed by Le Nôtre, Château Villette was more like a small castle than a large house. The estate had become known as la Petite Versailles.

Langdon stopped the armored truck at the start of the mile-long driveway. Beyond the large security gate, Sir Leigh Teabing's home stood in a field in the distance. The sign on the gate was in English: PRIVATE PROPERTY. NO TRESPASSING. As if to declare his home a small piece of Britain, Teabing had not only put up signs in English, but he had also installed the gate's intercom system on the right-hand side of the truck—the passenger's side everywhere in Europe except England.

Sophie looked strangely at the misplaced intercom. "What if someone comes alone?"

"Don't ask." Langdon had already talked about that with Teabing. "He likes things the way they are at home."

Sophie rolled down her window. "Robert, you should do the talking." Langdon moved, leaning over Sophie to press the intercom button. As he did, he smelled Sophie's perfume, and he realized how close they were. He waited there, awkwardly leaning, while a telephone began ringing over the small speaker. Finally, the intercom crackled and an annoyed French voice spoke. "Château Villette. Who is calling?"

"This is Robert Langdon," Langdon called out, leaning across Sophie's lap. "I'm a friend of Sir Leigh Teabing. I need his help."

"My master is sleeping. As was I. What do you want with him?"

"It is a private matter. One he would be very interested in."

"Then I'm sure he will be happy to see you in the morning." Langdon shifted his weight. "It's quite important."

"Like Sir Leigh's sleep. If you're a friend, you know he's not well." Sir Leigh Teabing had polio as a child and now wore leg braces and walked with crutches, but Langdon had found him so lively and interesting on his last visit that it barely seemed like a weakness. "If you could, please tell him I've found new information about the Grail. Information that can't wait until morning."

There was a long pause.

Langdon and Sophie waited, the truck idling loudly.

A full minute passed.

Finally, someone spoke. "My good man, I think you're still on Harvard time." The voice was clear and light.

Langdon grinned, recognizing the strong British accent. "Leigh, sorry for waking you so early."

"My servant tells me you're in Paris and talking about the Grail."

"I thought that might get you out of bed."

"And it has."

"Any chance you'd open the gate for an old friend?"

"Those who seek the truth are more than friends. They are brothers." Langdon rolled his eyes at Sophie, used to Teabing's love for dramatic actions.

"I will open the gate," Teabing announced, "but first I must know your heart is true. A test of your honor. You will answer three questions."

Langdon groaned, whispering to Sophie. "Just go along with it. Like I said, he's quite a character."

"Your first question," Teabing said, his voice strong. "Should I serve you coffee, or tea?" Langdon knew Teabing's feelings about Americans drinking coffee. "Tea," he replied.

"Earl Grey."

"Excellent. Your second question. Milk or sugar?"

Langdon paused.

"Milk," Sophie whispered in his ear. "I think the British like milk."

"Milk," Langdon said.

Silence.

"Sugar?"

Teabing didn't answer.

Wait! Langdon remembered the awful tea from before and knew this was a test. "Lemon!" he said. "Earl Grey with lemon."

"Indeed." Teabing seemed very amused. "And now, I must ask the most serious question." Teabing paused and then said solemnly, "When did a Harvard rower last beat an Oxford rower at Henley?"

Langdon had no idea, but he knew why he was being asked. "That has surely never happened."

The gate clicked open. "You are loyal, my friend. You may enter."
