The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 41

Outside Castel Gandolfo, a rush of mountain air blew over the cliff and across the high edge, making Bishop Aringarosa feel cold as he got out of the Fiat. I should have worn something warmer than this, he thought, trying not to shiver. He didn't want to look weak or scared tonight.

The castle was dark except for the windows at the very top, which glowed in a threatening way. The library, Aringarosa thought. They're awake and waiting. He lowered his head against the wind and walked on without looking at the observatory domes. The priest who met him at the door seemed sleepy. It was the same priest who had met Aringarosa five months ago, but tonight he was much less welcoming. "We were worried about you, Bishop," the priest said, checking his watch and looking more annoyed than worried.

"I'm sorry. The airlines are so unpredictable these days."

The priest mumbled something and then said, "They're waiting upstairs. I'll take you up."

The library was a large square room with dark wood from floor to ceiling. Tall bookcases full of books lined all the walls. The floor was amber marble with black basalt trim, a beautiful reminder that this building had once been a palace.

"Welcome, Bishop," a man's voice said from across the room.

Aringarosa tried to see who was speaking, but the lights were very dim—much dimmer than during his first visit, when everything was brightly lit. The night I learned the truth. Tonight, these men sat in the shadows, as if they were ashamed of what was about to happen.

Aringarosa walked in slowly, even with dignity. He could see the shapes of three men at a long table on the other side of the room. He immediately recognized the figure in the middle—the large Secretary of the Vatican, in charge of all legal matters in Vatican City. The other two were high-ranking Italian cardinals.

Aringarosa walked across the library toward them. "I'm very sorry for the late hour. We're in different time zones. You must be tired."

"Not at all," the secretary said, his hands folded on his large belly. "We appreciate you coming so far. The least we can do is be awake to meet you. Can we offer you some coffee or something to drink?"

"I'd prefer we don't act like this is a friendly visit. I have another plane to catch. Shall we get started?"

"Of course," the secretary said. "You have acted faster than we expected."

"Have I?"

"You still have a month."

"You told us your concerns five months ago," Aringarosa said. "Why should I wait?"

"Indeed. We are very pleased with your speed."

Aringarosa's eyes went down the long table to a large black briefcase. "Is that what I asked for?"

"It is." The secretary sounded worried. "Although, I must admit, we are concerned about the request. It seems quite..."

"Dangerous," one of the cardinals finished. "Are you sure we can't send it to you somewhere?"

"The amount is outrageous."

Freedom is expensive. "I'm not worried about my safety. God is with me." The men looked unsure.

"Is the money exactly as I asked?"

The secretary nodded. "Large bonds from the Vatican Bank, payable to whoever has them. They can be used like cash anywhere."

Aringarosa walked to the end of the table and opened the briefcase. Inside were two thick stacks of bonds, each marked with the Vatican seal and the word PORTATORE, meaning they could be cashed in by whoever held them.

The secretary looked nervous. "I must say, Bishop, we'd all feel better if this money was in cash."

I couldn't carry that much cash, Aringarosa thought, closing the case. "Bonds can be used like cash. You said so yourself."

The cardinals exchanged uneasy glances, and finally one said, "Yes, but these bonds can be traced back to the Vatican Bank."

Aringarosa smiled to himself. That was exactly why the Teacher told Aringarosa to get the money in Vatican Bank bonds. It was a safety measure. We're all in this together now. "This is a completely legal transaction," Aringarosa defended. "Opus Dei is a part of Vatican City, and the Pope can spend money however he wants. No law has been broken."

"True, and yet..." The secretary leaned forward and his chair creaked under his weight. "We don't know what you plan to do with this money, and if it's in any way illegal..."

"Considering what you're asking," Aringarosa replied, "what I do with this money isn't your business."

There was a long silence.

They know I'm right, Aringarosa thought. "Now, I guess you have something for me to sign?" They all jumped, quickly pushing the paper toward him, as if they wanted him to just leave. Aringarosa looked at the sheet in front of him. It had the papal seal. "This is the same as the copy you sent me?"

"Exactly."

Aringarosa was surprised he felt so little as he signed the document. But the three men there seemed to sigh in relief.

"Thank you, Bishop," the secretary said. "Your service to the Church will never be forgotten." Aringarosa picked up the briefcase, feeling the promise and power in its weight. The four men looked at each other for a moment as if there was something more to say, but apparently there wasn't. Aringarosa turned and went to the door.

"Bishop?" one of the cardinals called as Aringarosa reached the doorway. Aringarosa stopped and turned. "Yes?"

"Where will you go from here?"

Aringarosa felt the question was more about his soul than his location, but he didn't want to talk about right and wrong at this time. "Paris," he said, and walked out the door.
