The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 54

As Langdon and Sophie drove the armored truck up the curving, poplar-lined driveway toward the house, Sophie could feel her muscles start to relax. It felt good to be off the road, and she couldn't think of many safer places to catch their breath than this private, gated estate owned by a friendly foreigner.

They turned into the wide circular driveway, and Château Villette came into view on their right. The building was three stories tall and at least sixty meters long, with gray stone walls lit up by spotlights. The rough exterior was a sharp contrast to the perfectly kept gardens and smooth pond.

The lights inside were just turning on.

Instead of driving to the front door, Langdon pulled into a parking area hidden among the evergreens.

"No need to risk being seen from the road," he said. "Or having Leigh wonder why we arrived in a beat-up armored truck."

Sophie nodded. "What do we do with the cryptex? We probably shouldn't leave it out here, but if Leigh sees it, he'll definitely want to know what it is."

"Don't worry," Langdon said, taking off his jacket as he got out of the car. He wrapped the tweed coat around the box and held it like a baby.

Sophie looked unsure. "Smooth."

"Teabing never answers his own door; he likes to make a dramatic entrance. I'll find somewhere inside to hide this before he comes out. Langdon paused. "Actually, I should probably warn you before you meet him. Sir Leigh has a sense of humor that people often find a bit... odd." Sophie doubted anything tonight would surprise her anymore.

The path to the front door was made of cobblestones, laid by hand. It curved to a door of carved oak and cherry with a brass knocker as big as a grapefruit. Before Sophie could grab the knocker, the door opened from inside.

A neat and elegant butler stood before them, adjusting the white tie and tuxedo he seemed to have just put on. He looked to be about fifty, with fine features and a stern look that made it clear he wasn't happy about them being there.

"Sir Leigh will be down shortly," he said, with a strong French accent. "He is getting dressed. He prefers not to greet guests in just a nightshirt. May I take your coat?" He frowned at the bundled tweed in Langdon's arms.

"Thank you, I'm fine."

"Of course. This way, please."

The butler led them through a grand marble entrance hall into a beautifully decorated drawing room, softly lit by Victorian lamps with tassels. The air inside smelled old, regal somehow, with hints of pipe tobacco, tea leaves, cooking sherry, and the earthy smell of stone. Against the far wall, between two shining suits of armor, was a rough fireplace big enough to roast an ox. Walking to the fireplace, the butler knelt and lit a pile of oak logs and kindling. A fire quickly crackled to life. The man stood, straightening his jacket. "His master asks that you make yourselves at home." With that, he left, leaving Langdon and Sophie alone.

Sophie wondered which of the antiques by the fire she should sit on—the Renaissance velvet couch, the rustic rocker with eagle claws, or the pair of stone benches that looked like they came from some ancient temple.

Langdon took the cryptex out of his coat, walked to the velvet couch, and slid the wooden box far underneath it, hidden from sight. Then, shaking out his jacket, he put it back on, smoothed the lapels, and smiled at Sophie as he sat down right over the hidden treasure. The couch it is, Sophie thought, sitting down beside him.

As she stared into the growing fire, enjoying the warmth, Sophie felt that her grandfather would have loved this room. The dark wood walls were covered with Old Master paintings, one of which Sophie recognized as a Poussin, her grandfather's second-favorite painter. On the mantel above the fireplace, an alabaster bust of Isis watched over the room. Below the Egyptian goddess, in the fireplace, two stone gargoyles served as andirons, their mouths open to show their scary hollow throats. Gargoyles had always scared Sophie as a child, until her grandfather cured her fear by taking her to the top of Notre Dame Cathedral in a rainstorm. "Princess, look at these silly creatures," he had told her, pointing to the gargoyle rainspouts with water gushing from their mouths. "Do you hear that funny sound in their throats?" Sophie nodded, smiling at the burping sound of the water going through their throats.

"They're gargling," her grandfather told her. "Gargariser! And that's where they get the silly name.

'gargoyles.' " Sophie had never been afraid since then.

The good memory made Sophie sad, as the awful truth of the murder hit her again. Grand-père is gone. She imagined the cryptex under the couch and wondered if Leigh Teabing would know how to open it. Or if we should even ask him. Sophie's grandfather's last words told her to find Robert Langdon. He didn't say anything about involving anyone else. We needed a place to hide, Sophie said, deciding to trust Robert's decision.

"Sir Robert!" a voice shouted from behind them. "I see you have a lady with you." Langdon stood up. Sophie jumped up too. The voice came from the top of a winding staircase that went up to the dark second floor. At the top of the stairs, a figure moved in the shadows, only his outline visible.

"Good evening," Langdon called up. "Sir Leigh, this is Sophie Neveu."

"A pleasure." Teabing stepped into the light.

"Thank you for having us," Sophie said, now seeing that the man wore metal leg supports and used crutches. He was slowly coming down the stairs. "I know it's very late."

"It's so late, my dear, it's early." He laughed. "Vous n'êtes pas Américaine?" Sophie shook her head. "Parisienne."

"Your English is excellent."

"Thank you. I studied at the Royal Holloway."

"That explains it." Teabing slowly moved down through the shadows. "Robert might have told you I went to school just down the road at Oxford." Teabing gave Langdon a mischievous smile. "Of course, I also applied to Harvard as my backup school."

Their host arrived at the bottom of the stairs, looking to Sophie more like Sir Elton John than a knight. Plump and red-faced, Sir Leigh Teabing had thick red hair and cheerful hazel eyes that seemed to sparkle as he spoke. He wore pleated pants and a loose silk shirt under a patterned vest. Despite the metal supports on his legs, he stood with a strong, upright dignity that seemed more natural to him because of his noble family than something he tried to do. Teabing came over and held out a hand to Langdon. "Robert, you've lost weight." Langdon smiled. "And you've found some."

Teabing laughed loudly, patting his round stomach. "Touché. Eating seems to be my only pleasure these days." Turning to Sophie, he gently took her hand, bowing his head slightly, breathing lightly on her fingers, and looking away. "M'lady." Sophie looked at Langdon, unsure if she'd gone back in time or into a madhouse. The butler who had opened the door came in carrying tea, which he set out on a table in front of the fireplace.

"This is Rémy Legaludec," Teabing said, "my servant." The thin butler gave a quick nod and disappeared again.

"Rémy is from Lyon," Teabing whispered, as if it were a bad disease. "But he makes sauces very well."

Langdon looked amused. "I thought you would have hired English staff?"

"Good heavens, no! I wouldn't want a British chef on anyone except the French tax collectors." He looked over at Sophie. "Excuse me, Mademoiselle Neveu. Please know that I only dislike the French when it comes to politics and soccer. Your government takes my money, and your soccer team recently embarrassed us."

Sophie smiled easily.

Teabing looked at her for a moment and then at Langdon. "Something has happened. You both look upset."

Langdon nodded. "We've had an interesting night, Leigh."

"No doubt. You show up at my door in the middle of the night talking about the Grail. Tell me, is this really about the Grail, or did you just say that because you know it's the only thing that would get me out of bed in the middle of the night?" A little of both, Sophie thought, picturing the cryptex hidden under the couch.

"Leigh," Langdon said, "we want to talk to you about the Priory of Sion." Teabing's thick eyebrows rose with interest. "The keepers. So this really is about the Grail. You say you have information? Something new, Robert?"

"Maybe. We're not sure yet. We might know more if we could get some information from you first."

Teabing wagged his finger. "Always the clever American. A game of give and take. Very well. I'm here to help. What can I tell you?"

Langdon sighed. "I was hoping you'd be kind enough to explain to Ms. Neveu the real meaning of the Holy Grail."

Teabing looked shocked. "She doesn't know?"

Langdon shook his head.

The smile that spread across Teabing's face was almost inappropriate. "Robert, you've brought me someone who's never heard the story before?" Langdon cringed, looking at Sophie. "Someone who's never heard the story before is what Grail fans call a 'virgin.'"

Teabing turned to Sophie, excited. "How much do you know, my dear?" Sophie quickly explained what Langdon had said earlier—the Priory of Sion, the Knights Templar, the Sangreal documents, and the Holy Grail, which many said wasn't a cup... but something much more powerful.

"That's all?" Teabing gave Langdon a disapproving look. "Robert, I thought you were a gentleman. You've spoiled the surprise!"

"I know, I thought maybe you and I could..." Langdon seemed to decide the inappropriate comparison had gone on long enough.

Teabing was already looking intently at Sophie. "You are new to the Grail, my dear. And trust me, you will never forget this."
