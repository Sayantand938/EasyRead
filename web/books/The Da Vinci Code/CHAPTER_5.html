The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 5

Murray Hill Place—the new Opus Dei World Headquarters and conference center—is at 243 Lexington Avenue in New York City. Costing just over $47 million, the 133,000 square-foot building is covered in red brick and Indiana limestone. Designed by May & Pinska, it has over one hundred bedrooms, six dining rooms, libraries, living rooms, meeting rooms, and offices. The second, eighth, and sixteenth floors have chapels, decorated with woodwork and marble. The seventeenth floor is entirely for living. Men enter the building through the main doors on Lexington Avenue. Women enter through a side street and are kept "acoustically and visually separated" from the men at all times inside the building. Earlier this evening, in the privacy of his penthouse apartment, Bishop Manuel Aringarosa had packed a small travel bag and put on a traditional black robe. Usually, he would have worn a purple belt around his waist, but tonight he would be traveling in public, and he didn't want to draw attention to his position. Only those who looked closely would notice his 14-karat gold bishop's ring with purple amethyst, large diamonds, and a handmade mitrecrozier design. Throwing the travel bag over his shoulder, he said a silent prayer and left his apartment, going down to the lobby where his driver was waiting to take him to the airport. Now, on a commercial plane headed for Rome, Aringarosa looked out the window at the dark Atlantic. The sun had already set, but Aringarosa knew his own success was coming. Tonight the battle will be won, he thought, amazed that only months ago he had felt helpless against those who threatened to destroy his organization.

As president-general of Opus Dei, Bishop Aringarosa had spent the last ten years spreading the message of "God's Work"—literally, Opus Dei. The group, started in 1928 by the Spanish priest Josemaría Escrivá, encouraged a return to traditional Catholic values and told its members to make big sacrifices in their own lives to do God's Work.

Opus Dei's traditional ideas first took hold in Spain before Franco's rule, but with the 1934 publication of Josemaría Escrivá's spiritual book The Way —999 points to think about for doing God's Work in your own life—Escrivá's message spread rapidly around the world. Now, with over four million copies of The Way in circulation in forty-two languages, Opus Dei was a global force. Its dorms, teaching centers, and even universities could be found in almost every major city on earth. Opus Dei was the fastest-growing and most financially stable Catholic organization in the world. Unfortunately, Aringarosa had learned that in a time of religious doubt, cults, and TV preachers, Opus Dei's growing wealth and power made people suspicious.

"Many call Opus Dei a brainwashing cult," reporters often challenged. "Others call you a very conservative Christian secret society. Which are you?"

"Opus Dei is neither," the bishop would patiently answer. "We are a Catholic Church. We are a group of Catholics who have chosen to follow Catholic teachings as closely as we can in our daily lives."

"Does God's Work always include vows of chastity, tithing, and making up for sins through self-whipping and the cilice?"

"You are describing only a small part of the Opus Dei population," Aringarosa said. "There are many levels of involvement. Thousands of Opus Dei members are married, have families, and do God's Work in their own communities. Others choose lives of strict self-discipline within our private residence halls. These choices are personal, but everyone in Opus Dei shares the goal of making the world better by doing the Work of God. Surely this is a good goal." But reason seldom worked. The media always looked for scandal, and Opus Dei, like most large organizations, had a few members who made mistakes that cast a bad light on the entire group.

Two months ago, an Opus Dei group at a midwestern university had been caught giving new recruits mescaline to create a feeling of intense happiness that the new members would think was a religious experience. Another university student had used his barbed cilice belt more often than the recommended two hours a day and had gotten a near-fatal infection. In Boston not long ago, a disappointed young investment banker had signed over his entire life savings to Opus Dei before trying to kill himself.

Misguided sheep, Aringarosa thought, feeling sorry for them. Of course the biggest embarrassment had been the widely publicized trial of FBI spy Robert Hanssen, who, besides being a prominent member of Opus Dei, had turned out to be a sexual pervert. His trial revealed that he had secretly installed hidden video cameras in his own bedroom so his friends could watch him having sex with his wife. "Hardly the activity of a devout Catholic," the judge had said.

Sadly, all these events helped create a new watch group called the Opus Dei Awareness Network (ODAN). The group's popular website—www.odan.org—shared alarming stories from former Opus Dei members who warned about the dangers of joining. The media was now calling Opus Dei "God's Mafia" and "the Cult of Christ." We fear what we do not understand, Aringarosa thought, wondering if these critics knew how many lives Opus Dei had improved. The group had the full support and blessing of the Vatican. Opus Dei is a personal prelature of the Pope himself.

Recently, however, Opus Dei had been threatened by a force far stronger than the media... an unexpected enemy Aringarosa couldn't possibly hide from. Five months ago, the balance of power had shifted dramatically, and Aringarosa was still struggling with the impact.

"They don't know the war they've started," Aringarosa whispered, staring out the plane's window at the dark ocean below. For a moment, his eyes focused on the reflection of his own face—dark and long, with a flat, crooked nose broken by a fist in Spain when he was a young missionary. The physical flaw barely mattered now. Aringarosa lived in a world of the soul, not of the body.

As the jet flew over the coast of Portugal, the cell phone in Aringarosa's cassock vibrated silently. Despite airline rules against using cell phones during flights, Aringarosa knew he had to answer this call. Only one man had this number, the man who had sent Aringarosa the phone.

Excited, the bishop answered quietly. "Yes?"

"Silas has found the keystone," the caller said. "It's in Paris, inside the Church of Saint-Sulpice." Bishop Aringarosa smiled. "Then we're close."

"We can get it immediately, but we need your help."

"Of course. Tell me what to do."

When Aringarosa turned off the phone, his heart was racing. He looked again into the darkness, feeling small compared to the events he had set in motion.

Five hundred miles away, the albino named Silas stood over a small basin of water, dabbing blood from his back and watching the red patterns swirl. Purge me with hyssop, and I will be clean, he prayed, quoting Psalms. Wash me, and I will be whiter than snow. Silas felt a thrilling anticipation he hadn't felt since his old life. It surprised and energized him. For the last ten years, he had been following The Way, cleansing himself of sins, rebuilding his life, and erasing the violence of his past. But tonight, it had all come rushing back. The hatred he had fought so hard to bury had been awakened. He was surprised how quickly his past had returned. And with it, of course, had come his skills—rusty but still useful.

Jesus' message is one of peace... of nonviolence... of love. This was the message Silas had been taught from the start, and the message he held dear. And yet this was the message the enemies of Christ now threatened to destroy. Those who threaten God with force will be met with force. Immovable and steadfast.

For two millennia, Christian soldiers had defended their faith against those who tried to replace it. Tonight, Silas had been called to battle.

Drying his wounds, he put on his ankle-length, hooded robe. It was simple, made of dark wool, making his white skin and hair stand out. Tightening the rope around his waist, he pulled the hood over his head and let his red eyes admire his reflection in the mirror. Things are set in motion.
