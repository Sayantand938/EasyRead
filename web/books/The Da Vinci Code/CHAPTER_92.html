The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 92

King's College, founded by King George IV in 1829, has its Theology and Religious Studies Department next to Parliament on land given by the Crown. The Religion Department at King's College not only has 150 years of teaching and research experience but also established the Research Institute in Systematic Theology in 1982. This institute has one of the most complete and technologically advanced religious research libraries in the world. Langdon still felt unsteady as he and Sophie came in from the rain and entered the library. The main research room was as Teabing had described—a striking octagonal room with a huge round table as its centerpiece. King Arthur and his knights might have felt at home there, except for the twelve flat-screen computer workstations. On the other side of the room, a reference librarian was just pouring tea and getting ready for her day.

"Lovely morning," she said with a cheerful British accent, leaving the tea and walking over. "May I help you?"

"Thank you, yes," Langdon replied. "My name is—"

"Robert Langdon." She smiled kindly. "I know who you are." For a moment, he worried Fache had put him on English television too, but the librarian's smile suggested otherwise. Langdon still wasn't used to these moments of sudden fame. Then again, if anyone would recognize him, it would be a librarian in a Religious Studies library.

"Pamela Gettum," the librarian said, offering her hand. She had a friendly, intelligent face and a smooth voice. The thick glasses hanging around her neck looked scholarly.

"A pleasure," Langdon said. "This is my friend Sophie Neveu." The two women greeted each other, and Gettum turned back to Langdon right away. "I didn't know you were coming."

"Neither did we. If it's not too much trouble, we really need your help finding some information."

Gettum paused, looking unsure. "Normally, we only offer our services by request and appointment, unless you're a guest of someone at the college?"

Langdon shook his head. "I'm sorry, we didn't call ahead. A friend of mine thinks very highly of you. Sir Leigh Teabing?" Saying the name made Langdon feel a little sad. "The British Royal Historian."

Gettum's face lit up, and she laughed. "Oh, yes. What a guy. Obsessed! Every time he comes in, he always searches for the same things. Grail. Grail. Grail. I bet he'll keep looking until his dying day." She winked. "Having time and money lets you do fun things like that, right? He's like Don Quixote, chasing a dream."

"Can you help us?" Sophie asked. "It's really important." Gettum looked around the empty library and then winked at them. "Well, I can't say I'm busy, can I? As long as you sign in, I don't think anyone will mind. What are you looking for?"

"We're trying to find a tomb in London."

Gettum looked unsure. "We have about twenty thousand of them. Can you be more specific?"

"It's the tomb of a knight. We don't know his name."

"A knight. That narrows it down a lot. Much less common."

"We don't have much information about the knight," Sophie said, "but here's what we know." She showed a piece of paper with just the first two lines of the poem on it.

Langdon and Sophie didn't want to show the whole poem to a stranger, so they only shared the first two lines, which named the knight. Sophie called it "divided secrecy." When a spy agency found a code with important information, each code breaker worked on a separate part of it. That way, no one person knew the whole message.

In this case, it was probably too careful. Even if the librarian saw the whole poem, found the knight's tomb, and knew about the missing orb, the information wouldn't help without the cryptex.

Gettum saw a pressing need in the famous American scholar's eyes, as if finding this tomb quickly was vital. The green-eyed woman with him also seemed worried.

Confused, Gettum put on her glasses and looked at the paper they had given her. "In London lies a knight a Pope interred.

His labor's fruit a Holy wrath incurred."

She looked at her guests. "What's this? A Harvard game?" Langdon's laugh seemed fake. "Yeah, something like that."

Gettum paused, feeling she wasn't getting the full story. Still, she was curious and thought about the verse. "This rhyme says a knight angered God, but a Pope buried him in London." Langdon nodded. "Does it sound familiar?"

Gettum walked to a workstation. "Not right away, but let's check the database."

For twenty years, King's College Research Institute in Systematic Theology had used software to scan and translate a huge collection of texts—religious encyclopedias, biographies, scriptures in many languages, histories, Vatican letters, diaries, anything about human spirituality. Now that the collection was digital, the data was much easier to access. Settling at a workstation, Gettum looked at the paper and began typing. "First, we'll search using simple keywords and see what we find."

"Thank you."

Gettum typed:

LONDON, KNIGHT, POPE

As she clicked the SEARCH button, she felt the hum of the big computer downstairs scanning data at 500 MB/sec. "I'm asking the system to find any documents with all three keywords in the text. We'll get more results than we need, but it's a good start."

The screen was already showing the first results.

Painting the Pope. The Collected Portraits of Sir Joshua Reynolds. London University Press.

Gettum shook her head. "Not what you're looking for." She scrolled to the next result. The London Writings of Alexander Pope by G. Wilson Knight.

Again, she shook her head.

As the system worked, the results came up faster than usual. Dozens of texts appeared, many about the eighteenth-century British writer Alexander Pope, whose anti-religious, mock-heroic poetry seemed to have many references to knights and London.

Gettum glanced at the number at the bottom of the screen. The computer guessed how much information would be found by calculating the current number of results and how much of the database was left to search. This search looked like it would return a huge amount of data.

Estimated number of total hits: 2,692

"We need to narrow the search," Gettum said, stopping it. "Is this all the information you have about the tomb? Nothing else to add?" Langdon looked at Sophie Neveu, uncertain.

This is no game, Gettum thought. She had heard about Robert Langdon's experience in Rome last year. This American had been allowed into the most secure library on earth—the Vatican Secret Archives. She wondered what secrets Langdon might have learned there and if his desperate search for a mysterious London tomb was related to that information. Gettum had been a librarian long enough to know why people usually looked for knights in London. The Grail. Gettum smiled and adjusted her glasses. "You're friends with Leigh Teabing, you're in England, and you're looking for a knight." She folded her hands. "I can only guess you're on a Grail quest."

Langdon and Sophie exchanged startled looks.

Gettum laughed. "My friends, this library is a hub for Grail seekers. Leigh Teabing among them. I wish I had a dollar for every time I'd searched for the Rose, Mary Magdalene, Sangreal, Merovingian, Priory of Sion, and so on. Everyone loves a conspiracy." She took off her glasses and looked at them. "I need more information."

In the silence, Gettum sensed her guests' wish for privacy was quickly being overcome by their need for a quick answer.

"Here," Sophie Neveu blurted. "This is everything we know." Taking a pen from Langdon, she wrote two more lines on the slip of paper and handed it to Gettum.

You seek the orb that should be on his tomb.

It speaks of Rosy flesh and seeded womb.

Gettum smiled to herself. The Grail indeed, she thought, noticing the references to the Rose and her seeded womb. "I can help you," she said, looking up from the slip of paper. "May I ask where this verse came from? And why you are seeking an orb?"

"You may ask," Langdon said, with a friendly smile, "but it's a long story and we have very little time."

"Sounds like a polite way of saying 'it's none of your concern.' "

"We would be forever grateful, Pamela," Langdon said, "if you could find out who this knight is and where he is buried."

"Very well," Gettum said, typing again. "I'll play along. If this has to do with the Grail, we should check it against Grail keywords. I'll add a closeness setting and remove the title importance. That will limit our results to only those times the keywords appear near a Grail-related word."

Search for: KNIGHT, LONDON, POPE, TOMB

Within 100 words of: GRAIL, ROSE, SANGREAL, CHALICE

"How long will this take?" Sophie asked.

"A few hundred terabytes with many cross-references?" Gettum's eyes sparkled as she clicked the SEARCH key. "Just fifteen minutes."

Langdon and Sophie said nothing, but Gettum knew this sounded like forever to them.

"Tea?" Gettum asked, standing and walking toward the pot she had made earlier. "Leigh always loves my tea."
