The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 26

Despite her huge fame, the Mona Lisa was only thirty-one inches by twenty-one inches—smaller than the posters of her sold in the Louvre gift shop. She hung on the northwest wall of the Salle des Etats behind a two-inch-thick piece of protective Plexiglas. Painted on a poplar wood panel, her dreamy, misty look was due to Da Vinci's skill with the sfumato style, in which shapes seem to fade into each other. Since coming to the Louvre, the Mona Lisa —or La Jaconde as she is called in France—had been stolen twice, most recently in 1911, when she disappeared from the Louvre's

"satte impénétrable" —Le Salon Carre. Parisians cried in the streets and wrote newspaper articles begging the thieves to return the painting. Two years later, the Mona Lisa was found hidden in the false bottom of a trunk in a Florence hotel room.

Langdon, now making it clear to Sophie that he wasn't going to leave, moved with her across the Salle des Etats. The Mona Lisa was still twenty yards ahead when Sophie turned on the black light, and the bluish light spread out on the floor in front of them. She swung the light back and forth across the floor like a minesweeper, looking for any sign of glowing ink.

Walking beside her, Langdon was already feeling excited about seeing the great artwork up close. He tried to see beyond the purple light from the black light in Sophie's hand. To the left, the room's octagonal viewing couch appeared, looking like a dark island on the empty wooden floor.

Langdon could now see the dark glass on the wall. Behind it, he knew, inside her own private space, hung the most famous painting in the world. The Mona Lisa's fame, Langdon knew, had nothing to do with her mysterious smile. It also wasn't because of the strange interpretations given to her by many art historians and conspiracy theorists. Simply put, the Mona Lisa was famous because Leonardo da Vinci said she was his best work. He carried the painting with him when he traveled and, when asked why, would say that he found it hard to be apart from his most beautiful expression of female beauty.

Even so, many art historians thought Da Vinci's love for the Mona Lisa had nothing to do with its artistic quality. Actually, the painting was a surprisingly normal sfumato portrait. Da Vinci's admiration for this work, many claimed, came from something much deeper: a hidden message in the layers of paint. The Mona Lisa was, in fact, one of the world's most well-known inside jokes. The painting's many double meanings and playful references had been revealed in most art history books, and yet, surprisingly, the public still thought her smile was a great mystery.

No mystery at all, Langdon thought, moving forward and watching as the faint outline of the painting began to appear. No mystery at all.

Most recently Langdon had shared the Mona Lisa's secret with an unlikely group—a dozen inmates at the Essex County Penitentiary. Langdon's jail seminar was part of a Harvard program trying to bring education into the prison system— Culture for Convicts, as Langdon's colleagues liked to call it.

Standing at an overhead projector in a darkened prison library, Langdon had shared the Mona Lisa's secret with the prisoners in the class, men whom he found surprisingly interested—rough, but smart. "You may notice," Langdon told them, walking up to the projected image of the Mona Lisa on the library wall, "that the background behind her face is uneven." Langdon pointed to the obvious difference. "Da Vinci painted the horizon line on the left much lower than the right."

"He messed it up?" one of the inmates asked.

Langdon chuckled. "No. Da Vinci didn't do that very often. Actually, this is a little trick Da Vinci used. By making the land lower on the left, Da Vinci made Mona Lisa look much bigger from the left side than from the right side. A little Da Vinci inside joke. In the past, male and female ideas have been linked to sides—left is female, and right is male. Because Da Vinci really liked feminine ideas, he made Mona Lisa look more impressive from the left than the right."

"I heard he was gay," said a small man with a goatee.

Langdon winced. "Historians don't usually say it like that, but yes, Da Vinci was gay."

"Is that why he liked that whole feminine thing?"

"Actually, Da Vinci understood the balance between male and female. He believed that a person could not be truly enlightened unless they had both male and female parts."

"You mean like girls with dicks?" someone called.

This got a lot of laughs. Langdon thought about explaining where the word hermaphrodite comes from and how it's related to Hermes and Aphrodite, but he figured this group wouldn't understand.

"Hey, Mr. Langford," a muscle-bound man said. "Is it true that the Mona Lisa is a picture of Da Vinci dressed as a woman? I heard that was true."

"It's very possible," Langdon said. "Da Vinci liked to play jokes, and computer analysis of the Mona Lisa and Da Vinci's self-portraits shows some surprising similarities in their faces. Whatever Da Vinci was doing," Langdon said, "his Mona Lisa is neither male nor female. It has a subtle message of being both. It is a combination of both."

"You sure that's not just a fancy way of saying the Mona Lisa is ugly?" Langdon laughed. "You might be right. But Da Vinci actually left a big hint that the painting was meant to be both male and female. Has anyone here heard of an Egyptian god named Amon?"

"Of course!" the big guy said. "God of male fertility!" Langdon was surprised.

"It's on every box of Amon condoms." The muscular man grinned. "It has a guy with a ram's head on the front and says he's the Egyptian god of fertility." Langdon didn't know the brand, but he was glad the condom makers had gotten their symbols right. "Good. Amon is shown as a man with a ram's head, and his love life and curved horns are related to the word 'horny.'"

"Really!"

"Really," Langdon said. "And do you know who Amon's female partner was? The Egyptian goddess of fertility?"

The question was met with silence.

"It was Isis," Langdon told them, grabbing a marker. "So we have the male god, Amon." He wrote it down. "And the female goddess, Isis, whose old symbol was once called L'ISA." Langdon finished writing and stepped back from the projector.

AMON L'ISA

"Sound familiar?" he asked.

"Mona Lisa... wow," someone gasped.

Langdon nodded. "Gentlemen, the Mona Lisa's face not only looks androgynous, but her name is an anagram for the divine union of male and female. And that, my friends, is Da Vinci's little secret, and the reason for Mona Lisa's knowing smile."

"My grandfather was here," Sophie said, suddenly kneeling, only ten feet from the Mona Lisa. She pointed the black light at a spot on the floor. At first, Langdon saw nothing. Then, kneeling beside her, he saw a tiny drop of dried liquid glowing. Ink? He suddenly remembered what black lights were really used for. Blood. His senses sharpened. Sophie was right. Jacques Saunière had visited the Mona Lisa before he died.

"He had a reason to come here," Sophie whispered, standing up. "I know he left a message for me here." She quickly walked to the Mona Lisa, shining the light on the floor in front of the painting. She waved the light across the bare floor.

"There's nothing here!"

Langdon then saw a faint purple glimmer on the protective glass in front of the Mona Lisa. Reaching down, he took Sophie's wrist and slowly moved the light up to the painting. They both froze.

Six purple words glowed on the glass, scrawled across the Mona Lisa's face.
