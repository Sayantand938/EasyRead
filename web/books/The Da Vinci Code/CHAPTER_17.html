The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 17

"What do you mean she's not answering?" Fache looked like he couldn't believe it. "You're calling her cell phone, right? I know she has it with her."

Collet had been trying to reach Sophie for several minutes. "Maybe her battery died. Or she turned off the ringer."

Fache had looked worried since talking to the head of Cryptology on the phone. After hanging up, he had walked quickly to Collet and told him to get Agent Neveu on the phone. Now Collet had failed, and Fache was pacing like a caged lion.

"Why did Crypto call?" Collet asked.

Fache turned. "To tell us they found nothing about Draconian devils and lame saints."

"That's it?"

"No, also to say they figured out the numbers were Fibonacci numbers, but they thought the series didn't mean anything."

Collet was confused. "But Agent Neveu already told us that." Fache shook his head. "They didn't send Neveu."

"What?"

"According to the director, I told him to page his whole team to look at the images I'd sent. When Agent Neveu arrived, she glanced at the photos of Saunière and the code and left without a word. The director said he didn't ask why because she was understandably upset by the photos."

"Upset? Has she never seen a dead body before?"

Fache paused. "I didn't know this, and apparently neither did the director until someone told him, but Sophie Neveu is Jacques Saunière's granddaughter." Collet was stunned.

"The director said she never mentioned Saunière, and he thought she didn't want special treatment for having a famous grandfather." No wonder she was upset by the pictures. Collet could hardly believe the coincidence of calling in a young woman to decode a message written by her dead relative. Still, her actions didn't make sense. "But she knew they were Fibonacci numbers because she came here and told us. I don't get why she'd leave without saying she'd figured it out."

Collet could only think of one explanation: Saunière had written a number code hoping Fache would get cryptographers involved, and thus his granddaughter. As for the rest, was Saunière talking to his granddaughter? If so, what did the message say? And how did Langdon fit in?

Before Collet could think more, the silence of the empty museum was broken by an alarm. It seemed to be coming from the Grand Gallery.

"Alarme!" one of the agents yelled, looking at his feed from the Louvre security center. "Grande Galerie! Toilettes Messieurs!"

Fache turned to Collet. "Where's Langdon?"

"Still in the men's room!" Collet pointed to the blinking red dot on his laptop. "He must have broken the window!" Collet knew Langdon wouldn't get far. Paris fire codes required windows above fifteen meters to be breakable in case of fire, but jumping from a second-story window without a ladder would be suicide. Also, there were no trees or grass on the west side of the Denon Wing to soften a fall. The Place du Carrousel ran close to the outer wall under the restroom window. "My God," Collet said, looking at the screen. "Langdon's moving to the window ledge!" But Fache was already moving. He pulled his Manurhin MR-93 revolver from its holster and ran out.

Collet watched the screen, confused as the dot reached the ledge and did something unexpected. It moved outside the building. What's going on? he wondered. Is Langdon on a ledge or —

"Jesus!" Collet jumped up as the dot moved farther outside the wall. The signal seemed to shake for a moment, and then the blinking dot stopped suddenly about ten yards outside the building.

Fumbling with the controls, Collet pulled up a Paris street map and reset the GPS. Zooming in, he could now see exactly where the signal was.

It was no longer moving.

It was stopped in the middle of Place du Carrousel.

Langdon had jumped.
