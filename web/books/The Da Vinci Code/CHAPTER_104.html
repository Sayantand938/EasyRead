The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 104

Rosslyn Chapel—often called the Cathedral of Codes—is located seven miles south of Edinburgh, Scotland, where an ancient temple once stood. Built in 1446 by the Knights Templar, the chapel is covered in a bewildering number of symbols from Jewish, Christian, Egyptian, Masonic, and pagan traditions.

The chapel's location lines up exactly with the north-south line that goes through Glastonbury. This Rose Line marks the traditional location of King Arthur's Isle of Avalon and is considered the main point of Britain's sacred layout. Rosslyn—originally spelled Roslin—gets its name from this important Rose Line.

The chapel's rough towers were casting long shadows as Robert Langdon and Sophie Neveu drove their rental car into the grassy parking area at the bottom of the hill where the chapel stood. Their short flight from London to Edinburgh had been relaxing, but they were both too excited about what was ahead to sleep. Looking up at the bare building against the cloudy sky, Langdon felt like Alice falling into the rabbit hole. This must be a dream. But he knew that Saunière's final message couldn't have been clearer. The Holy Grail 'neath ancient Roslin waits.

Langdon had imagined that Saunière's "Grail map" would be a simple drawing with an X marking the spot. But the Priory's final secret was revealed in the same way Saunière had spoken to them from the start. Simple verse. Four clear lines that pointed directly to this place. Besides naming Rosslyn, the verse also mentioned some of the chapel's famous architectural features.

Even though Saunière's final message was clear, Langdon felt more confused than enlightened. To him, Rosslyn Chapel seemed like too obvious a place. For centuries, people had whispered about the Holy Grail being hidden in this stone chapel. The whispers had become shouts in recent years when radar showed a surprising structure underneath the chapel—a huge underground chamber. This vault was not only bigger than the chapel above it, but it also seemed to have no way in or out. Archaeologists wanted to start digging through the rock to reach the mysterious chamber, but the Rosslyn Trust strictly forbade any digging at the sacred site. Of course, this only made people wonder more. What was the Rosslyn Trust trying to hide?

Rosslyn had now become a popular place for people seeking mysteries. Some said they were drawn there by the strong magnetic field that seemed to come from the area, others said they came to search the hillside for a hidden entrance to the vault, but most admitted they had simply come to walk around and learn about the Holy Grail. Although Langdon had never been to Rosslyn before, he always chuckled when he heard the chapel called the current home of the Holy Grail. He thought Rosslyn might have been home to the Grail once, long ago... but certainly not anymore. Too much attention had been paid to Rosslyn in recent years, and someone would eventually find a way to break into the vault. True Grail experts agreed that Rosslyn was a fake—one of the clever false leads that the Priory created so well. But tonight, with the Priory's key message offering a verse that pointed straight to this place, Langdon no longer felt so sure. A puzzling question had been on his mind all day:

Why would Saunière go to so much trouble to lead us to such an obvious place?

There seemed to be only one logical answer.

There is something about Rosslyn we have yet to understand.

"Robert?" Sophie was outside the car, looking back. "Coming?" She held the rosewood box Captain Fache had given back. Inside, both cryptexes were put back together as they were found. The papyrus verse was locked safely inside—without the broken vinegar vial.

Walking up the long gravel path, Langdon and Sophie passed the chapel's famous west wall. Visitors thought this strange wall was part of the chapel that wasn't finished. Langdon knew the truth was more interesting.

The west wall of Solomon's Temple.

The Knights Templar designed Rosslyn Chapel to be like Solomon's Temple in Jerusalem—with a west wall, a narrow sanctuary, and an underground vault like the Holy of Holies. Here, the first nine knights found their treasure. Langdon thought it was interesting that the Templars built a modern place for the Grail that was like the Grail's first hiding place. Rosslyn Chapel's entrance was smaller than Langdon expected. The small wooden door had

two iron hinges and a simple, oak sign.

ROSLIN

Langdon told Sophie this old spelling came from the Rose Line where the chapel was. Or, as Grail experts liked to think, from the "Line of Rose"—Mary Magdalene's family line.

The chapel would close soon. Langdon opened the door, and warm air came out, like the old building was sighing after a long day. The arches at the entrance were covered in carved roses.

Roses. The goddess's womb.

Entering with Sophie, Langdon felt his eyes scan the famous chapel, taking it all in. He had read about Rosslyn's strikingly complex stonework, but seeing it in person was overwhelming.

A symbol lover's dream, one of Langdon's colleagues had called it.

Every surface in the chapel was carved with symbols—Christian crosses, Jewish stars, Masonic symbols, Templar crosses, horns of plenty, pyramids, astrological signs, plants, vegetables, pentacles, and roses. The Knights Templar were master stonemasons who built Templar churches all over Europe, but Rosslyn was considered their greatest work of love and respect. The master masons had left no stone uncarved. Rosslyn Chapel was a tribute to all faiths... to all traditions... and, most of all, to nature and the goddess. The chapel was empty except for a few visitors listening to a young man giving the last tour of the day. He was leading them in a line along a well-known path on the floor—an invisible route connecting six key architectural points inside. Generations of visitors had walked these straight lines, connecting the points, and their many footsteps had etched a huge symbol on the floor.

The Star of David, Langdon thought. That's no accident. Also known as Solomon's Seal, this hexagram was once the secret symbol of priests who studied the stars and was later adopted by the Israelite kings—David and Solomon.

The guide had seen Langdon and Sophie enter, and even though it was closing time, he smiled kindly and told them to look around.

Langdon nodded his thanks and started to walk further into the chapel. Sophie, however, stood still in the doorway, looking puzzled.

"What is it?" Langdon asked.

Sophie stared at the chapel. "I think... I've been here." Langdon was surprised. "But you said you hadn't even heard of Rosslyn."

"I hadn't..." She looked around the chapel, seeming unsure. "My grandfather must have brought me here when I was very young. I don't know. It feels familiar." As she looked around the room, she started nodding more confidently. "Yes." She pointed to the front of the chapel. "Those two pillars... I've seen them."

Langdon looked at the two intricately carved columns at the far end of the chapel. Their white, lacy carvings seemed to glow with a reddish light as the last of the day's sunlight streamed in through the west window. The pillars—placed where the altar would normally be—were an oddly mismatched pair. The pillar on the left was carved with simple, vertical lines, while the pillar on the right was decorated with a fancy, flowery spiral. Sophie was already walking toward them. Langdon hurried after her, and as they reached the pillars, Sophie was nodding in disbelief. "Yes, I'm sure I've seen these!"

"I'm sure you've seen them," Langdon said, "but maybe not here." She turned. "What do you mean?"

"These two pillars are the most copied architectural structures ever. There are copies all over the world."

"Copies of Rosslyn?" She seemed doubtful.

"No. Of the pillars. Remember I said Rosslyn itself is a copy of Solomon's Temple? Those two pillars are exact copies of the two pillars that stood at the entrance of Solomon's Temple." Langdon pointed to the pillar on the left. "That's called Boaz —or the Mason's Pillar. The other is called Jachin —or the Apprentice Pillar." He paused. "Almost every Masonic temple in the world has two pillars like these."

Langdon had already told her about the Templars' strong historical links to the modern Masonic secret societies. Their main levels—Apprentice Freemason, Fellowcraft Freemason, and Master Mason—went back to early Templar times. Sophie's grandfather's last verse mentioned the Master Masons who decorated Rosslyn with their carvings. It also mentioned Rosslyn's central ceiling, which was covered with carvings of stars and planets.

"I've never been in a Masonic temple," Sophie said, still looking at the pillars. "But I'm almost sure I saw these here." She turned back into the chapel, as if looking for something to remind her.

The other visitors were now leaving, and the young guide walked over to them with a friendly smile. He was a good-looking young man in his late twenties, with a Scottish accent and strawberry blond hair. "I'm about to close for the day. Can I help you find anything?"

How about the Holy Grail? Langdon thought.

"The code," Sophie blurted out, suddenly realizing. "There's a code here!" The guide looked happy she was so interested. "Yes, there is, ma'am."

"It's on the ceiling," she said, turning to the right-hand wall. "Somewhere over... there." He smiled. "I see this isn't your first time at Rosslyn."

The code, Langdon thought. He had forgotten that little bit of knowledge. Among Rosslyn's many mysteries was an arched doorway from which hundreds of stone blocks stuck out, forming a strange, many-sided surface. Each block had a symbol carved on it, seemingly at random, creating a code of immense size. Some people thought the code revealed the entrance to the vault under the chapel.

Others believed it told the real story of the Grail. But it didn't matter—code breakers had been trying for centuries to figure it out. Even now, the Rosslyn Trust offered a large reward to anyone who could reveal the secret meaning, but the code remained a mystery. "I'd be happy to show..."

The guide's voice faded.

My first code, Sophie thought, walking alone, as if in a dream, toward the coded archway. After giving the rosewood box to Langdon, she felt herself briefly forgetting about the Holy Grail, the Priory of Sion, and all the mysteries of the past day. When she reached the coded ceiling and saw the symbols above her, the memories rushed back. She was remembering her first visit here, and strangely, the memories brought an unexpected sadness. She was a little girl... about a year after her family's death. Her grandfather had brought her to Scotland for a short trip. They had come to see Rosslyn Chapel before going back to Paris. It was late evening, and the chapel was closed. But they were still inside.

"Can we go home, Grand-père?" Sophie asked, feeling tired.

"Soon, dear, very soon." His voice was sad. "I have one last thing I need to do here. How about you wait in the car?"

"Are you doing another grown-up thing?"

He nodded. "I'll be quick. I promise."

"Can I do the archway code again? That was fun."

"I don't know. I have to go outside. You won't be scared in here alone?"

"Of course not!" she said, annoyed. "It's not even dark yet!" He smiled. "Very well then." He led her to the fancy archway he'd shown her before. Sophie immediately sat down on the stone floor, lying on her back and looking up at the puzzle pieces above. "I'm going to solve this before you get back!"

"It's a race, then." He bent down, kissed her forehead, and walked to the side door. "I'll be right outside. I'll leave the door open. If you need me, just call." He went out into the soft evening light.

Sophie lay there, looking up at the code. Her eyes felt heavy. After a few minutes, the symbols blurred. And then they vanished.

When Sophie woke up, the floor felt cold.

"Grand-père?"

There was no answer. She stood up and brushed herself off. The side door was still open. The evening was getting darker. She walked outside and saw her grandfather standing on the porch of a stone house behind the church. He was talking quietly to someone barely visible inside the screen door.

"Grand-père?" she called.

Her grandfather turned and waved, telling her to wait. Then, slowly, he said some final words to the person inside and blew a kiss toward the screen door. He came to her with tearful eyes.

"Why are you crying, Grand-père?"

He picked her up and held her close. "Oh, Sophie, we've said good-bye to a lot of people this year. It's hard."

Sophie thought of the accident, of saying good-bye to her mother and father, her grandmother and baby brother. "Were you saying goodbye to another person?"

"To a dear friend whom I love very much," he replied, his voice full of emotion. "And I fear I will not see her again for a very long time."

Standing with the docent, Langdon had been looking at the chapel walls and feeling more and more worried that they were at a dead end. Sophie had gone off to look at the code and left Langdon holding the rosewood box, which contained a Grail map that now seemed useless. Although Saunière's poem clearly pointed to Rosslyn, Langdon wasn't sure what to do now that they were there. The poem mentioned a "blade and chalice," which Langdon didn't see anywhere.

The Holy Grail 'neath ancient Roslin waits.

The blade and chalice guarding o'er Her gates.

Again Langdon felt that there was still some part of the mystery he hadn't figured out.

"I hate to pry," the docent said, looking at the rosewood box in Langdon's hands. "But this box... may I ask where you got it?"

Langdon gave a tired laugh. "That's a very long story." The young man hesitated, his eyes on the box again. "It's strange—my grandmother has a box exactly like that—a jewelry box. Same polished rosewood, same inlaid rose, even the hinges look the same."

Langdon knew the young man had to be wrong. If any box was unique, it was this one—the box custom-made for the Priory keystone. "The two boxes might be similar but—" The side door closed loudly, making them both look over. Sophie had left without a word and was now walking down the hill toward a fieldstone house nearby. Langdon stared after her. Where is she going? She had been acting strangely ever since they came inside. He turned to the docent. "Do you know what that house is?"

He nodded, also looking confused about why Sophie was going down there. "That's the chapel rectory. The chapel curator lives there. She's also the head of the Rosslyn Trust." He paused. "And my grandmother."

"Your grandmother runs the Rosslyn Trust?"

The young man nodded. "I live with her in the rectory and help take care of the chapel and give tours." He shrugged. "I've lived here my whole life. My grandmother raised me in that house." Worried about Sophie, Langdon moved across the chapel toward the door to call out to her. He was only halfway there when he stopped suddenly. Something the young man said just clicked. My grandmother raised me.

Langdon looked out at Sophie on the bluff, then down at the rosewood box in his hand. Impossible. Slowly, Langdon turned back to the young man. "You said your grandmother has a box like this one?"

"Almost exactly the same."

"Where did she get it?"

"My grandfather made it for her. He died when I was a baby, but my grandmother still talks about him. She says he was incredibly talented. He made all sorts of things." Langdon saw a huge network of connections forming. "You said your grandmother raised you. Do you mind me asking what happened to your parents?"

The young man looked surprised. "They died when I was young." He paused. "The same day as my grandfather."

Langdon's heart beat fast. "In a car accident?"

The docent flinched, looking confused. "Yes. In a car accident. My entire family died that day. I lost my grandfather, my parents, and..." He hesitated, looking down at the floor. "And your sister," Langdon said.

Out on the bluff, the fieldstone house was just as Sophie remembered it. Night was coming, and the house seemed warm and welcoming. The smell of bread floated through the open screen door, and a golden light shone in the windows. As Sophie got closer, she could hear the soft sounds of crying from inside.

Through the screened door, Sophie saw an elderly woman in the hallway. Her back was to the door, but Sophie could see she was crying. The woman had long, beautiful, silver hair that brought back a sudden memory. Feeling drawn closer, Sophie stepped onto the porch stairs. The woman was holding a framed photograph of a man and touching his face with loving sadness.

It was a face Sophie knew well.

Grand-père.

The woman had clearly heard the sad news of his death last night.

A board squeaked beneath Sophie's feet, and the woman turned slowly, her sad eyes finding Sophie's. Sophie wanted to run, but she stood still. The woman's intense gaze never wavered as she set down the photo and approached the screened door. It felt like forever as the two women stared at each other through the thin mesh. Then, like a slowly growing ocean wave, the woman's face changed from uncertainty... to disbelief... to hope... and finally, to overwhelming joy.

Throwing open the door, she came out, reaching with soft hands, cradling Sophie's stunned face. "Oh, dear child... look at you!"

Although Sophie did not recognize her, she knew who this woman was. She tried to speak but could not even breathe.

"Sophie," the woman sobbed, kissing her forehead.

Sophie's words were a choked whisper. "But... Grand-père said you were..."

"I know." The woman placed her gentle hands on Sophie's shoulders and looked at her with familiar eyes. "Your grandfather and I had to say so many things. We did what we thought was right. I'm so sorry. It was for your own safety, princess."

Sophie heard her last name and immediately thought of her grandfather, who had called her princess for so long. The sound of his voice seemed to echo in the old stones of Rosslyn, settling through the ground and bouncing in the hidden spaces below. The woman hugged Sophie tightly, crying harder. "Your grandfather really wanted to tell you everything. But things were hard between you two. He tried so much. There's so much to explain. So very much to explain." She kissed Sophie's forehead again, then whispered in her ear. "No more secrets, princess. It's time you learned the truth about our family."

Sophie and her grandmother were sitting on the porch steps, hugging and crying, when the young guide ran across the lawn, his eyes bright with hope and shock.

"Sophie?"

Through her tears, Sophie nodded, standing up. She didn't know the young man, but as they hugged, she could feel the strength of their shared blood.

When Langdon walked over to them, Sophie couldn't believe that just yesterday she had felt so alone. And now, somehow, in this strange place, with three people she hardly knew, she finally felt at home.
