The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 16

Sophie wondered how long it would take Fache to realize she was still inside. Langdon seemed lost, and Sophie wondered if she had done the right thing by bringing him to the men's room.

What else could I do?

She thought of her grandfather's body, naked and spread out on the floor. He had once been everything to her, but tonight, Sophie was surprised that she felt almost no sadness for him. Jacques Saunière was now a stranger. Their relationship had vanished one night in March when she was twenty-two. Ten years ago. Sophie had returned early from graduate school in England and accidentally saw her grandfather doing something she shouldn't have. Even now, she could hardly believe what she had seen.

If I hadn't seen it myself...

Too ashamed and shocked to listen to her grandfather's awkward attempts to explain, Sophie immediately moved out, using her savings to rent a small apartment with roommates. She promised herself never to tell anyone what she had seen. Her grandfather tried hard to reach her, sending cards and letters, begging her to meet him so he could explain. Explain what!? Sophie only responded once—to tell him never to call or try to meet her in public. She feared his explanation would be worse than what she had seen.

Surprisingly, Saunière had never given up, and Sophie now had ten years of unopened letters in a drawer. To his credit, her grandfather had always respected her wish and never called her.

Until this afternoon.

"Sophie?" His voice sounded surprisingly old on her answering machine. "I have respected your wishes for so long... and it hurts me to call, but I must speak to you. Something awful has happened."

Standing in her Paris apartment, Sophie felt a chill hearing him again after all this time. His kind voice brought back many happy childhood memories.

"Sophie, please listen." He was speaking English to her, as he always did when she was a little girl. Practice French at school. Practice English at home. "You can't be angry forever. Haven't you read the letters I've sent all these years? Don't you understand yet?" He paused. "We must talk right away. Please grant your grandfather this one wish. Call me at the Louvre. Immediately. I think you and I are in great danger." Sophie stared at the answering machine. Danger? What was he talking about?

"Princess..." Her grandfather's voice broke with an emotion Sophie couldn't understand. "I know I've kept things from you, and I know it has cost me your love. But it was for your own safety. Now you must know the truth. Please, I must tell you the truth about your family." Sophie suddenly could hear her own heart. My family? Sophie's parents had died when she was only four. Their car went off a bridge into fast-moving water. Her grandmother and younger brother had also been in the car, and Sophie's entire family had disappeared in an instant. She had a box of newspaper clippings to prove it.

His words sent a sudden rush of longing through her. My family! In that brief moment, Sophie saw images from the dream that had woken her countless times when she was a little girl: My family is alive! They are coming home! But, as in her dream, the pictures vanished.

Your family is dead, Sophie. They are not coming home.

"Sophie..." her grandfather said on the machine. "I have been waiting for years to tell you. Waiting for the right moment, but now time is running out. Call me at the Louvre. As soon as you get this. I'll wait here all night. I'm afraid we both may be in danger. There's so much you need to know." The message ended.

In the silence, Sophie stood trembling for what felt like minutes. As she thought about her grandfather's message, only one idea made sense, and his real plan became clear. It was a trick.

Clearly, her grandfather really wanted to see her. He was trying anything. Her dislike for the man grew stronger. Sophie wondered if maybe he had become terminally ill and had decided to try any scheme he could think of to get Sophie to visit him one last time. If so, he had chosen well.

My family.

Now, standing in the darkness of the Louvre men's room, Sophie could hear the echoes of this afternoon's phone message. Sophie, we both may be in danger. Call me. She had not called him. Nor had she planned to. Now, however, her doubt had been greatly shaken. Her grandfather lay murdered inside his own museum. And he had written a code on the floor.

A code for her. Of this, she was sure.

Even though she didn't understand his message, Sophie felt sure its mysterious nature meant the words were for her. Sophie's love and skill for cryptography came from growing up with Jacques Saunière—a man obsessed with codes, word games, and puzzles. How many Sundays did we spend doing the cryptograms and crosswords in the newspaper?

By twelve, Sophie could finish the Le Monde crossword by herself, and her grandfather moved her on to English crosswords, math puzzles, and substitution ciphers. Sophie loved them all. Eventually, she made her passion a career, becoming a codebreaker for the Judicial Police.

Tonight, the codebreaker in Sophie had to admire how efficiently her grandfather had used a simple code to bring together two complete strangers—Sophie Neveu and Robert Langdon.

The question was why?

Sadly, from Langdon's confused look, Sophie guessed the American had no more idea than she did why her grandfather had brought them together.

She asked again, "You and my grandfather planned to meet tonight. What about?" Langdon looked truly puzzled. "His secretary set up the meeting and didn't give a specific reason, and I didn't ask. I thought he'd heard I was lecturing on the pagan symbols in French cathedrals, was interested, and thought it would be nice to meet for drinks after the talk."

Sophie didn't believe it. The connection was weak. Her grandfather knew more about pagan symbols than anyone. Plus, he was very private, not someone who would chat with random American professors without a good reason. Sophie took a deep breath and asked more. "My grandfather called me this afternoon and said he and I were in great danger. Does that mean anything to you?" Langdon's blue eyes now showed worry. "No, but given what just happened..." Sophie nodded. After tonight's events, she would be foolish not to be scared. Feeling tired, she walked to the small glass window at the end of the bathroom and looked out silently through the alarm tape in the glass. They were high up—at least forty feet.

Sighing, she looked up at the beautiful view of Paris. To her left, across the Seine, was the lit-up Eiffel Tower. Straight ahead, the Arc de Triomphe. And to the right, on top of Montmartre, was the elegant dome of Sacré-Coeur, its white stone glowing like a bright sanctuary.

Here at the far west end of the Denon Wing, the north-south road of Place du Carrousel was right next to the building, with only a small sidewalk separating it from the Louvre's outer wall. Far below, the usual delivery trucks were waiting, their lights seeming to wink at Sophie.

"I don't know what to say," Langdon said, coming up behind her. "Your grandfather is clearly trying to tell us something. I'm sorry I'm not more helpful."

Sophie turned from the window, sensing Langdon's genuine regret. Even with his own problems, he clearly wanted to help her. The teacher in him, she thought, remembering DCPJ's report on him. He was an academic who hated not understanding. We're alike in that way, she thought.

As a codebreaker, Sophie deciphered meaning from what seemed like nonsense. Tonight, she believed Langdon had information she desperately needed, whether he knew it or not. Princesse Sophie, Find Robert Langdon. Her grandfather's message couldn't be clearer. Sophie needed more time with Langdon. Time to think. Time to solve this mystery together. But time was running out.

Looking at Langdon, Sophie made her only possible move. "Bezu Fache will arrest you any minute. I can get you out of this museum. But we have to go now." Langdon's eyes widened. "You want me to run?"

"It's the best thing to do. If Fache arrests you, you'll be in a French jail for weeks while DCPJ and the U.S. Embassy argue over who gets to try you. If we escape and get to your embassy, your government will protect you while we prove you're innocent." Langdon looked unconvinced. "No way! Fache has guards at every exit! Even if we get out without being shot, running makes me look guilty. Tell Fache the message was for you, and my name isn't an accusation."

"I will," Sophie said quickly, "but after you're safe at the U.S. Embassy. It's only a mile away, and my car is just outside the museum. Arguing with Fache here is too risky. Don't you see? Fache wants to prove you're guilty. He only delayed your arrest to see if you'd do something to help his case."

"Exactly. Like running!"

Sophie's cell phone rang in her pocket. Probably Fache. She reached in and turned it off.

"Mr. Langdon," she said urgently, "I need to ask you one last question." Your future depends on it. "The writing on the floor doesn't prove you're guilty, yet Fache told our team he's certain you are. Can you think of any reason he'd think that?"

Langdon was silent for a moment. "None at all."

Sophie sighed. Which means Fache is lying. She couldn't guess why, but that didn't matter now. Fache was determined to imprison Robert Langdon tonight, no matter what. Sophie needed Langdon for herself, and this left her with only one choice.

I need to get Langdon to the U.S. Embassy.

Turning to the window, Sophie looked through the alarm mesh in the glass, down the steep forty feet to the ground. A jump from this height would likely break Langdon's legs. At best.

Still, Sophie made up her mind.

Robert Langdon was going to escape the Louvre, whether he liked it or not.
