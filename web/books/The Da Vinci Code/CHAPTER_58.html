The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 58

Teabing's "study" was unlike any Sophie had seen. Much larger than even the fanciest offices, the knight's workspace looked like a strange mix of science lab, library, and indoor market. Lit by chandeliers, the huge tiled floor was covered with groups of worktables piled with books, art, artifacts, and lots of electronics—computers, projectors, microscopes, copiers, and scanners.

"I turned the ballroom into this," Teabing said, looking embarrassed as he walked in. "I don't dance much."

Sophie felt like the whole night was a strange dream where nothing made sense. "Is this all for your work?"

"Discovering the truth is what I love most," Teabing said. "And the Sangreal is my greatest passion."

The Holy Grail is a woman, Sophie thought, her mind filled with confusing ideas. "You said you have a picture of this woman who you say is the Holy Grail."

"Yes, but I'm not the one saying she's the Grail. Christ Himself said it."

"Which one is the painting?" Sophie asked, looking around.

"Hmmm..." Teabing pretended to have forgotten. "The Holy Grail. The Sangreal. The Chalice." He turned quickly and pointed to the far wall. There hung a large print of The Last Supper, exactly like the one Sophie had just seen. "There she is!" Sophie was sure she was missing something. "That's the same painting you just showed me." He winked. "I know, but it's much more exciting when it's bigger. Don't you think?" Sophie turned to Langdon for help. "I'm confused."

Langdon smiled. "It turns out, the Holy Grail does appear in The Last Supper. Leonardo made her very visible."

"Hold on," Sophie said. "You told me the Holy Grail is a woman. The Last Supper is a painting of thirteen men."

"Is it?" Teabing raised his eyebrows. "Look closer." Sophie, unsure, walked closer to the painting, looking at the thirteen figures—Jesus Christ in the center, six disciples on His left, and six on His right. "They're all men," she said.

"Really?" Teabing said. "What about the one sitting in the most important spot, on the Lord's right?"

Sophie looked closely at the figure to Jesus' right. As she studied the person's face and body, she was very surprised. The person had long red hair, delicate hands, and a hint of breasts. It was, without a doubt... a woman.

"That's a woman!" Sophie shouted.

Teabing laughed. "Surprise! It's no mistake. Leonardo was good at painting the difference between men and women."

Sophie couldn't stop looking at the woman next to Christ. The Last Supper is supposed to be thirteen men. Who is this woman? Sophie had seen this famous picture many times, but she had never noticed this obvious difference.

"Everyone misses it," Teabing said. "We expect this scene to look a certain way, so our minds ignore the difference and trick our eyes."

"It's called skitoma," Langdon added. "The brain does it sometimes with strong symbols."

"Another reason you might have missed the woman," Teabing said, "is that many photos in art books were taken before 1954, when the details were still hidden under dirt and bad repainting from the 1700s. Now, the painting has been cleaned to show Da Vinci's original paint." He pointed to the photograph. "And there you have it!"

Sophie moved closer to the image. The woman to Jesus' right was young and looked very religious, with a modest face, beautiful red hair, and hands folded quietly. Could this woman really bring down the Church all by herself?

"Who is she?" Sophie asked.

"That, my dear," Teabing replied, "is Mary Magdalene." Sophie turned. "The prostitute?"

Teabing took a quick breath, as if the word had hurt him. "Magdalene was nothing of the sort. That wrong idea came from a campaign to ruin her reputation, started by the early Church. The Church needed to defame Mary Magdalene to hide her dangerous secret—her role as the Holy Grail."

"Her role?"

"As I said," Teabing explained, "the early Church needed to make the world believe that Jesus, a human prophet, was a divine being. So, any gospels that showed human parts of Jesus' life had to be left out of the Bible. But the early editors had a problem. One particular human theme kept showing up in the gospels. Mary Magdalene." He paused. "More specifically, her marriage to Jesus Christ."

"What?" Sophie looked at Langdon and then back at Teabing.

"It's a matter of historical record," Teabing said, "and Da Vinci knew it. The Last Supper almost screams that Jesus and Magdalene were a couple." Sophie looked back at the painting.

"Notice that Jesus and Magdalene are dressed like reflections of each other." Teabing pointed to the two figures in the middle of the painting.

Sophie was amazed. It was true, their clothes were opposite colors. Jesus wore a red robe and blue cloak; Mary Magdalene wore a blue robe and red cloak. Yin and yang.

"Getting stranger," Teabing said, "notice that Jesus and His bride seem very close but are leaning away from each other, creating a clear empty space between them."

Even before Teabing pointed it out, Sophie saw it—the obvious V shape right in the center of the painting. It was the same symbol Langdon had drawn earlier for the Grail, the chalice, and the female womb.

"Finally," Teabing said, "if you think of Jesus and Magdalene as parts of the picture instead of people, you'll see another clear shape appear." He paused. "A letter of the alphabet."

Sophie saw it right away. It wasn't just that the letter appeared; it was all she could see. Staring out from the center of the painting was the clear outline of a huge, perfect letter M.

"A bit too perfect to be a coincidence, don't you think?" Teabing asked. Sophie was amazed. "Why is it there?"

Teabing shrugged. "Some conspiracy theorists say it stands for Matrimonio or Mary Magdalene. Honestly, no one knows for sure. All we know is that the hidden M is no accident. Many Grail-related works have this hidden letter M—as watermarks, underpaintings, or hints in the picture. The most obvious M is on the altar at Our Lady of Paris in London, designed by Jean Cocteau, a former Grand Master of the Priory of Sion."

Sophie thought about it. "I admit, the hidden M's are interesting, but I guess no one claims they prove Jesus was married to Magdalene."

"No, no," Teabing said, going to a nearby table of books. "As I said, the marriage of Jesus and Mary Magdalene is a matter of historical record." He started searching through his books. "Besides, Jesus as a married man makes much more sense than the usual biblical idea of Jesus as a bachelor."

"Why?" Sophie asked.

"Because Jesus was a Jew," Langdon said, taking over while Teabing looked for his book, "and Jewish custom at the time almost required a man to be married. Jewish custom frowned on celibacy, and a Jewish father had to find a wife for his son. If Jesus wasn't married, at least one of the Bible's gospels would have mentioned it and explained why He was unmarried." Teabing found a huge book and pulled it toward him across the table. The leather-bound book was huge, like an atlas. The cover read: The Gnostic Gospels. Teabing opened it, and Langdon and Sophie joined him. Sophie saw photographs of what looked like close-ups of ancient documents—torn papyrus with handwritten text. She didn't recognize the old language, but the facing pages had typed translations.

"These are copies of the Nag Hammadi and Dead Sea scrolls, which I mentioned before," Teabing said. "The oldest Christian records. The problem is, they don't match the gospels in the Bible." Turning to the middle of the book, Teabing pointed to a section. "The Gospel of Philip is always a good place to start." Sophie read the passage:

And the companion of the Saviour is Mary Magdalene. Christ loved her more than all the disciples and used to kiss her often on her mouth. The rest of the disciples were bothered by it and disapproved. They said to him, "Why do you love her more than all of us?"

The words surprised Sophie, but they didn't really prove anything. "It doesn't say anything about marriage."

"Quite the opposite." Teabing smiled, pointing to the first line. "Any Aramaic expert will tell you that the word companion back then actually meant spouse." Langdon agreed with a nod.

Sophie read the first line again. And the companion of the Saviour is Mary Magdalene. Teabing flipped through the book and pointed out other sections that, to Sophie's surprise, clearly suggested Magdalene and Jesus were romantically involved. As she read, Sophie remembered an angry priest who had banged on her grandfather's door when she was a schoolgirl.

"Is this Jacques Saunière's house?" the priest had demanded, glaring down at young Sophie when she opened the door. "I want to talk to him about this article he wrote." The priest held up a newspaper.

Sophie called her grandfather, and the two men went into his study and closed the door. My grandfather wrote something in the paper? Sophie immediately ran to the kitchen and looked through that morning's paper. She found her grandfather's name on an article on the second page. She read it. Sophie didn't understand everything it said, but it sounded like the French government, pressured by priests, had agreed to ban an American movie called The Last Temptation of Christ, which was about Jesus having sex with a woman named Mary Magdalene. Her grandfather's article said the Church was being arrogant and wrong to ban it. No wonder the priest is angry, Sophie thought.

"It's pornography! It's sacrilegious!" the priest yelled, coming out of the study and storming to the front door. "How can you possibly support that! This American, Martin Scorsese, is a blasphemer, and the Church won't allow him any place to speak in France!" The priest slammed the door as he left. When her grandfather came into the kitchen, he saw Sophie with the paper and frowned. "You're quick."

Sophie said, "Do you think Jesus Christ had a girlfriend?"

"No, dear, I said the Church shouldn't be allowed to tell us what ideas we can and can't have."

"Did Jesus have a girlfriend?"

Her grandfather was silent for a while. "Would it be so terrible if He did?" Sophie thought about it and then shrugged. "I wouldn't care."

Sir Leigh Teabing was still talking. "I won't bore you with the many mentions of Jesus and Magdalene's relationship. Modern historians have explored that topic to death. But I do want to point out this." He pointed to another part. "This is from the Gospel of Mary Magdalene."

Sophie didn't know there was a gospel written by Magdalene. She read the text: And Peter said, "Did the Savior really talk with a woman without us knowing?

Should we turn around and listen to her? Did he like her more than us?" And Levi replied, "Peter, you've always been quick to anger. Now I see you arguing against the woman like an enemy. If the Savior made her worthy, who are you to reject her? Surely the Savior knows her very well. That's why he loved her more than us."

"The woman they're talking about," Teabing explained, "is Mary Magdalene. Peter is jealous of her."

"Because Jesus liked Mary more?"

"Not just that. There was more at stake than just affection. At this point in the gospels, Jesus thinks He will soon be captured and crucified. So He gives Mary Magdalene instructions on how to continue His Church after He's gone. Because of this, Peter is unhappy about being second to a woman. I'd say Peter was a bit sexist." Sophie was trying to follow. "This is Saint Peter. The rock on which Jesus built His Church."

"The same one, but with one difference. According to these unchanged gospels, Christ didn't give Peter instructions to start the Christian Church. He gave them to Mary Magdalene." Sophie looked at him. "You're saying the Christian Church was supposed to be continued by a woman?"

"That was the idea. Jesus was ahead of His time on women's rights. He wanted Mary Magdalene to be in charge of His Church's future."

"And Peter didn't like that," Langdon said, pointing to The Last Supper. "That's Peter there. You can see that Da Vinci knew how Peter felt about Mary Magdalene." Again, Sophie was speechless. In the painting, Peter was leaning in a threatening way toward Mary Magdalene, slicing his hand across her neck like a blade. The same threatening gesture as in Madonna of the Rocks!

"And here too," Langdon said, now pointing to the group of disciples near Peter. "A bit scary, don't you think?"

Sophie squinted and saw a hand coming out of the crowd of disciples. "Is that hand holding a dagger?"

"Yes. Even stranger, if you count the arms, you'll see that this hand doesn't belong to anyone. It's not attached to a body. It's anonymous."

Sophie was starting to feel lost. "I'm sorry, I still don't get how all this makes Mary Magdalene the Holy Grail."

"Aha!" Teabing exclaimed again. "That's the heart of the matter!" He turned back to the table and pulled out a large chart, spreading it out for her. It was a detailed family tree. "Not many people know that Mary Magdalene, besides being Christ's closest companion, was already a powerful woman." Sophie could now see the title of the family tree.

THE TRIBE OF BENJAMIN

"Mary Magdalene is here," Teabing said, pointing near the top of the genealogy. Sophie was surprised. "She was from the House of Benjamin?"

"Indeed," Teabing said. "Mary Magdalene was of royal blood."

"But I thought Magdalene was poor."

Teabing shook his head. "Magdalene was made out to be a prostitute to hide the fact that she had powerful family connections."

Sophie again glanced at Langdon, who nodded again. She turned back to Teabing.

"But why would the early Church care if Magdalene had royal blood?" The Briton smiled. "My dear child, the Church wasn't so worried about Mary Magdalene's royal blood as they were about her being with Christ, who also had royal blood. As you know, the Book of Matthew says that Jesus was from the House of David. A descendant of King Solomon—King of the Jews. By marrying into the powerful House of Benjamin, Jesus brought together two royal lines, creating a strong political union that could have claimed the throne and brought back the line of kings like in Solomon's time." Sophie felt he was finally getting to his point.

Teabing looked excited now. "The story of the Holy Grail is a story about royal blood. When the Grail story talks about 'the chalice that held the blood of Christ'... it's really talking about Mary Magdalene—the female womb that carried Jesus' royal bloodline."

The words seemed to bounce around the ballroom before Sophie fully understood them. Mary Magdalene carried the royal bloodline of Jesus Christ? "But how could Christ have a bloodline unless...?" She stopped and looked at Langdon.

Langdon smiled gently. "Unless they had a child."

Sophie stood still, stunned.

"Here it is," Teabing announced, "the biggest cover-up in human history. Jesus Christ was not only married, but He was also a father. My dear, Mary Magdalene was the Holy Vessel. She was the chalice that carried the royal bloodline of Jesus Christ. She was the womb that carried the lineage, and the source from which the sacred fruit grew!"

Sophie felt her arms tingle. "But how could a secret that big be kept hidden for so long?"

"Goodness!" Teabing said. "It hasn't been hidden! The royal bloodline of Jesus Christ is the reason for the most famous story of all time—the Holy Grail. Magdalene's story has been told loudly for centuries in many different ways and languages. Her story is everywhere if you just look."

"And the Sangreal documents?" Sophie said. "They supposedly have proof that Jesus had royal descendants?"

"They do."

"So the whole Holy Grail story is about royal blood?"

"Exactly," Teabing said. "The word Sangreal comes from San Greal — or Holy Grail. But in its oldest form, the word Sangreal was split differently." Teabing wrote on a scrap of paper and gave it to her.

She read what he had written.

Sang Real

Right away, Sophie knew the translation. Sang Real literally meant Royal Blood.
