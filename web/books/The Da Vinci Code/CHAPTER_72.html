The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 72

Fifteen thousand feet in the air, Robert Langdon felt the real world disappear as all his thoughts focused on Saunière's mirror-image poem, lit up by the box's lid.

Sophie quickly found paper and copied it down by hand. When she finished, the three of them read the text in turn. It was like an archaeological crossword puzzle... a riddle that promised to reveal how to open the cryptex. Langdon read the verse slowly. An ancient word of wisdom frees this scroll... and helps us keep her scatter'd family whole... a headstone praised by templars is the key... and atbash will reveal the truth to thee. Before Langdon could even think about what ancient password the verse was trying to reveal, he felt something more basic resonate within him—the rhythm of the poem. Iambic pentameter. Langdon had encountered this rhythm often over the years while researching secret societies across Europe, including just last year in the Vatican Secret Archives. For centuries, iambic pentameter had been a favorite poetic rhythm of outspoken writers around the world, from the ancient Greek writer Archilochus to Shakespeare, Milton, Chaucer, and Voltaire—brave people who chose to write their social commentaries in a rhythm that many at the time believed had mystical powers. The roots of iambic pentameter were deeply pagan.

Iambs. Two syllables with opposite emphasis. Stressed and unstressed. Yin yang. A balanced pair. Arranged in strings of five. Pentameter. Five for the pentacle of Venus and the sacred feminine.

"It's pentameter!" Teabing blurted, turning to Langdon. "And the verse is in English! La lingua pura!"

Langdon nodded. The Priory, like many European secret societies at odds with the Church, had considered English the only European pure language for centuries. Unlike French, Spanish, and Italian, which were rooted in Latin— the tongue of the Vatican —English was linguistically separate from Rome's propaganda machine, and therefore became a sacred, secret tongue for those brotherhoods educated enough to learn it.

"This poem," Teabing gushed, "references not only the Grail, but the Knights Templar and the scattered family of Mary Magdalene! What more could we ask for?"

"The password," Sophie said, looking again at the poem. "It sounds like we need some kind of ancient word of wisdom?"

"Abracadabra?" Teabing ventured, his eyes twinkling.

A word of five letters, Langdon thought, considering the huge number of old words that might be considered words of wisdom—quotes from mystic chants, astrological predictions, secret society ceremonies, Wicca spells, Egyptian magic spells, pagan prayers—the list went on and on.

"The password," Sophie said, "seems to be about the Templars." She read the words aloud. " 'A headstone praised by Templars is the key.' "

"Leigh," Langdon said, "you know the most about the Templars. Any ideas?" Teabing was quiet for a few seconds and then sighed. "Well, a headstone is obviously a grave marker. Maybe the poem means a gravestone the Templars praised at Magdalene's tomb, but that doesn't help much because we don't know where her tomb is."

"The last line," Sophie said, "says that Atbash will reveal the truth. I've heard that word. Atbash."

"That's not surprising," Langdon replied. "You probably heard it in Cryptology 101. The Atbash Cipher is one of the oldest codes ever."

Of course! Sophie thought. The famous Hebrew code. The Atbash Cipher had been part of Sophie's early training in codes. The code was created around 500 B.C. and is now used as an example of a simple substitution method. A common type of Jewish code, the Atbash Cipher is a simple substitution code based on the twenty-two-letter Hebrew alphabet. In Atbash, the first letter is replaced by the last letter, the second letter by the second to last letter, and so on.

"Atbash is perfect," Teabing said. "Text coded with Atbash is found in the Kabbala, the Dead Sea Scrolls, and even the Old Testament. Jewish scholars and mystics are still finding hidden meanings using Atbash. The Priory would definitely include the Atbash Cipher in their teachings."

"The only problem," Langdon said, "is that we don't have anything to use the cipher on." Teabing sighed. "There must be a code word on the headstone. We have to find this headstone praised by Templars."

Sophie could tell from Langdon's worried look that finding the Templar headstone would be very difficult.

Atbash is the key, Sophie thought. But we don't have a door. Three minutes later, Teabing sighed in frustration and shook his head. "My friends, I'm stuck. Let me think about this while I get us some snacks and check on Rémy and our guest." He stood up and walked to the back of the plane.

Sophie felt tired as she watched him leave.

Outside the window, the darkness before dawn was complete. Sophie felt as if she were being thrown through space, not knowing where she would end up. Having spent her childhood solving her grandfather's puzzles, she now had the unsettling feeling that this poem held information they still hadn't found.

There is more there, she thought. Cleverly hidden... but still there. She also worried that what they eventually found inside this cryptex would not be as simple as "a map to the Holy Grail." Despite Teabing's and Langdon's belief that the answer was inside the marble cylinder, Sophie had solved enough of her grandfather's treasure hunts to know that Jacques Saunière did not give up his secrets easily.
