The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 35

Gare Saint-Lazare's interior resembled any European train station, a vast indoor space filled with familiar sights—homeless people with cardboard signs, groups of tired students asleep on backpacks and lost in their music, and clusters of baggage handlers in blue uniforms smoking.

Sophie looked up at the large departure board. The black and white panels flipped, updating the information. When it stopped, Langdon studied the options. The first listing read: LYON—RAPIDE—3:06

"I wish it left earlier," Sophie said, "but Lyon will work." Earlier? Langdon checked his watch 2:59 A.M. The train left in seven minutes, and they didn't have tickets yet. Sophie led Langdon to the ticket window and said, "Buy two tickets with your credit card."

"I thought using a credit card could be traced by—"

"Exactly."

Langdon stopped trying to anticipate Sophie Neveu's actions. He used his Visa card to buy two second-class tickets to Lyon and gave them to Sophie.

Sophie led him toward the tracks. A familiar chime sounded, and an announcer made the final boarding call for Lyon. Sixteen tracks stretched before them. In the distance to the right, at platform three, the Lyon train was hissing, ready to leave. But Sophie pulled Langdon in the opposite direction. They hurried through a side lobby, past an all-night cafe, and out a side door onto a quiet street on the station's west side.

A single taxi waited by the door, engine running.

The driver saw Sophie and flashed his lights.

Sophie jumped in the back seat. Langdon got in after her.

As the taxi pulled away from the station, Sophie took out their new train tickets and tore them up.

Langdon sighed. Seventy dollars well spent.

It wasn't until their taxi settled into a steady northbound hum on Rue de Clichy that Langdon felt they had truly escaped. Out the window to his right, he could see Montmartre and the beautiful dome of Sacré-Coeur. The image was interrupted by the flash of police lights speeding past them in the opposite direction.

Langdon and Sophie ducked down as the sirens faded.

Sophie had simply told the cab driver to head out of the city, and from her determined jaw, Langdon sensed she was trying to figure out their next move.

Langdon examined the cross-shaped key again, holding it to the window and close to his eyes, trying to find any markings on it that might show where it had been made. In the brief light of passing streetlights, he saw no markings except the Priory seal.

"It doesn't make sense," he finally said.

"Which part?"

"That your grandfather would go to so much trouble to give you a key that you wouldn't know what to do with."

"I agree."

"Are you sure he didn't write anything else on the back of the painting?"

"I looked everywhere. This is all there was. This key, stuck behind the painting. I saw the Priory seal, put the key in my pocket, then we left."

Langdon frowned, looking closely at the flat end of the triangular shaft. Nothing. Squinting, he brought the key close to his eyes and checked the edge of the head. Nothing there either. "I think this key was cleaned recently."

"Why?"

"It smells like rubbing alcohol."

She turned. "I'm sorry?"

"It smells like someone polished it with a cleaner." Langdon held the key to his nose and sniffed.

"It's stronger on the other side." He flipped it over. "Yes, it's alcohol-based, like it's been shined with a cleaner or—" Langdon stopped.

"What?"

He tilted the key toward the light and examined the smooth surface of the cross's wide arm. It seemed to shimmer in spots, as if damp. "Did you check the back of this key carefully before putting it in your pocket?"

"What? Not really. I was rushing."

Langdon faced her. "Do you still have the black light?"

Sophie reached into her pocket and pulled out the UV penlight. Langdon took it, turned it on, and shone the light on the key's back.

The back glowed immediately. There was writing there, in handwriting that was quick but clear.

"Well," Langdon said, smiling. "I guess we know what the alcohol smell was."

Sophie stared in wonder at the purple writing on the key's back. 24 Rue Haxo

An address! My grandfather wrote down an address!

"Where is this?" Langdon asked.

Sophie had no idea. Turning forward, she leaned in and eagerly asked the driver,

"Do you know Rue Haxo?"

The driver paused, then nodded. He said it was near the tennis stadium on the west side of Paris. She asked him to take them there right away.

"The fastest way is through the Bois de Boulogne," he said in French. "Is that all right?" Sophie frowned. She could think of less risky routes, but tonight she wouldn't be choosy. "Yes." We can surprise the American.

Sophie looked at the key again, wondering what they'd find at 24 Rue Haxo. A church? Some kind of Priory center?

Her mind filled with images of the secret ritual she'd seen in the basement grotto ten years ago, and she sighed. "Robert, I have a lot to tell you." She stopped, looking him in the eye as the taxi sped west. "But first, tell me everything you know about this Priory of Sion."
