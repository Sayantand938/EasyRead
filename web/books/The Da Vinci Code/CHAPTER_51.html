The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 51

Even at a slow sixty kilometers an hour, the armored truck's loose front bumper scraped against the empty suburban road with a loud, grinding noise, sending sparks flying onto the hood. We have to get off the road, Langdon thought.

He could hardly see where they were going. The truck's only working headlight was knocked to the side, shining a slanted beam into the woods next to the country highway. It seemed the armor in this "armored truck" only protected the cargo area, not the front.

Sophie sat in the passenger seat, staring blankly at the rosewood box on her lap.

"Are you okay?" Langdon asked.

Sophie looked upset. "Do you believe him?"

"About the three more murders? Definitely. It explains a lot—why your grandfather was so desperate to give away the keystone, and why Fache is so intensely hunting me."

"No, I mean about Vernet trying to protect his bank."

Langdon glanced over. "Instead of what?"

"Taking the keystone for himself."

Langdon hadn't even thought of it. "How could he know what's in this box?"

"His bank kept it. He knew my grandfather. Maybe he knew things. He might have wanted the Grail for himself."

Langdon shook his head. Vernet didn't seem like that kind of person. "I think people look for the Grail for only two reasons. Either they're innocent and think they're finding the Cup of Christ..."

"Or?"

"Or they know the truth and are scared of it. Many groups have tried to destroy the Grail."

The silence made the scraping sound louder. They had driven a few kilometers, and Langdon wondered if the sparks from the truck were dangerous. If another car passed, it would definitely notice. Langdon decided what to do.

"I'm going to try to bend the bumper back."

He pulled the truck onto the side of the road and stopped.

Finally, silence.

As Langdon walked to the front of the truck, he felt awake. Having a gun pointed at him again had given him energy. He took a deep breath of the night air and tried to think clearly. Besides being hunted, Langdon felt a heavy responsibility. He and Sophie might have a coded guide to one of history's biggest mysteries. And now, Langdon realized they couldn't return the keystone to the Priory. The news of the three murders was bad. The Priory has been infiltrated. They are not safe. Someone was watching the brotherhood, or there was a traitor. That explained why Saunière gave the keystone to Sophie and Langdon—people outside the brotherhood, people he knew were safe. We can't give the keystone back to the brotherhood. Even if Langdon knew how to find a Priory member, the person who came to take the keystone could be the enemy. For now, Sophie and Langdon were stuck with the keystone, whether they liked it or not. The front of the truck was worse than Langdon thought. The left headlight was gone, and the right one was hanging loose. Langdon straightened it, but it fell out again. The good news was that the bumper was almost completely torn off. Langdon kicked it hard and thought he could break it off. As he kicked the twisted metal, Langdon remembered his conversation with Sophie. My grandfather left me a phone message, Sophie had said. He said he needed to tell me the truth about my family. It hadn't meant anything then, but now that the Priory of Sion was involved, Langdon saw a new possibility.

The bumper broke off suddenly with a crash. Langdon paused to catch his breath. At least the truck would no longer look like a Fourth of July sparkler. He grabbed the bumper and began dragging it out of sight into the woods, wondering where they should go next. They had no idea how to open the cryptex, or why Saunière had given it to them. Sadly, their survival tonight seemed to depend on getting answers to those very questions.

We need help, Langdon decided. Expert help.

In the world of the Holy Grail and the Priory of Sion, that meant only one man. The problem, of course, would be convincing Sophie.

Inside the armored car, while Sophie waited for Langdon to return, she felt the weight of the rosewood box on her lap and disliked it. Why did my grandfather give this to me? She had no idea what to do with it.

Think, Sophie! Use your head. Grand-père is trying to tell you something!

Opening the box, she looked at the cryptex's dials. A proof of worth. She could feel her grandfather's influence. The keystone is a map that can be followed only by those who deserve it. It sounded just like her grandfather.

Lifting the cryptex out of the box, Sophie ran her fingers over the dials. Five letters. She rotated the dials one by one. The mechanism moved smoothly. She lined up the disks so her chosen letters were between the cryptex's two brass arrows on each end of the cylinder. The dials now spelled a five-letter word that Sophie knew was ridiculously obvious. G-R-A-I-L.

Gently, she held the two ends of the cylinder and pulled, applying pressure slowly. The cryptex didn't move. She heard the vinegar inside gurgle and stopped pulling. Then she tried again. V-I-N-C-I

Again, no movement.

V-O-U-T-E

Nothing. The cryptex stayed firmly locked.

Frowning, she put it back in the rosewood box and closed the lid. Looking at Langdon outside, Sophie was glad he was with her tonight. P.S. Find Robert Langdon. Her grandfather's reason for including him was now obvious. Sophie couldn't understand her grandfather's intentions, so he had chosen Robert Langdon to guide her. A teacher to help her learn. But tonight, Langdon had become much more than a teacher. He was now the target of Bezu Fache... and some hidden power wanting the Holy Grail. Whatever the Grail is.

Sophie wondered if finding out was worth her life.

As the armored truck sped up again, Langdon was happy with how smoothly it drove.

"Do you know how to get to Versailles?"

Sophie looked at him. "Sightseeing?"

"No, I have a plan. I know a religious historian who lives near Versailles. I can't remember exactly where, but we can find it. I've been to his place a few times. His name is Leigh Teabing. He used to be a British Royal Historian."

"And he lives in Paris?"

"Teabing is obsessed with the Grail. When rumors about the Priory keystone started about fifteen years ago, he moved to France to search churches, hoping to find it. He's written books about the keystone and the Grail. He might be able to help us figure out how to open it and what to do with it."

Sophie's eyes showed concern. "Can you trust him?"

"Trust him to do what? Not steal the information?"

"And not to turn us in."

"I don't plan to tell him the police want us. I hope he'll let us stay until we can figure things out."

"Robert, do you realize every TV in France is probably about to show our pictures? Bezu Fache always uses the media. He'll make it impossible for us to go anywhere without being recognized."

Great, Langdon thought. My first time on French TV will be on "Paris's Most Wanted." At least Jonas Faukman would be happy; Langdon's book sales always went up when he was on the news.

"Is this man a good enough friend?" Sophie asked.

Langdon doubted Teabing watched TV, especially this late, but the question was worth thinking about. Langdon's gut told him Teabing was completely trustworthy. A perfect safe place. Given the situation, Teabing would probably bend over backward to help. He not only owed Langdon a favor, but he was a Grail expert, and Sophie said her grandfather was the Grand Master of the Priory of Sion. If Teabing heard that, he would be thrilled to help them solve this.

"Teabing could be a strong ally," Langdon said. Depending on how much you want to tell him.

"Fache will probably offer a reward."

Langdon laughed. "Trust me, money is the last thing he needs." Leigh Teabing was rich like small countries are rich. A descendant of Britain's First Duke of Lancaster, Teabing had gotten his money the old-fashioned way—he'd inherited it. His estate outside Paris was a seventeenth-century palace with two private lakes. Langdon had met Teabing years ago through the British Broadcasting Corporation. Teabing had come to the BBC with an idea for a historical documentary where he would reveal the shocking history of the Holy Grail to a wide TV audience. The BBC

Producers loved Teabing's exciting idea, his research, and his qualifications, but they worried that the concept was so shocking and unbelievable that the network might damage its reputation for good journalism. At Teabing's suggestion, the BBC eased its worries about being credible by getting three respected historians from around the world to make brief appearances, all of whom confirmed the shocking nature of the Holy Grail secret with their own research.

Langdon had been one of those chosen.

The BBC had flown Langdon to Teabing's estate in Paris for the filming. He sat before cameras in Teabing's fancy living room and shared his story, admitting he was initially doubtful when he heard the alternate Holy Grail story, then explaining how years of research had convinced him it was true. Finally, Langdon offered some of his own research—a series of symbolic connections that strongly supported the seemingly controversial claims. When the program was broadcast in Britain, despite its large cast and well-documented evidence, the idea clashed so strongly with popular Christian beliefs that it immediately faced a storm of anger. It never aired in the States, but the effects were felt across the Atlantic. Shortly afterward, Langdon received a postcard from an old friend—the Catholic Bishop of Philadelphia. The card simply read: Et tu, Robert?

"Robert," Sophie asked, "are you sure we can trust this man?"

"Absolutely. We're colleagues, he doesn't need money, and I happen to know he hates the French authorities. The French government taxes him at ridiculous rates because he bought a historic building. He won't be quick to cooperate with Fache."

Sophie stared out at the dark road. "If we go to him, how much do you want to tell him?" Langdon looked calm. "Believe me, Leigh Teabing knows more about the Priory of Sion and the Holy Grail than anyone on earth."

Sophie watched him. "More than my grandfather?"

"I meant more than anyone outside the brotherhood."

"How do you know Teabing isn't a member of the brotherhood?"

"Teabing has spent his life trying to reveal the truth about the Holy Grail. The Priory's oath is to keep its true nature a secret."

"Sounds to me like a conflict of interest."

Langdon understood her worries. Saunière had given the cryptex right to Sophie, and even though she didn't know what was inside or what to do with it, she didn't want to involve a complete stranger. Considering what might be inside, that was probably smart. "We don't have to tell Teabing about the keystone right away. Or ever, even. His house will give us a place to hide and think, and maybe when we talk to him about the Grail, you'll start to understand why your grandfather gave this to you."

"Us," Sophie pointed out.

Langdon felt a quiet pride and wondered again why Saunière had included him.

"Do you know about where Mr. Teabing lives?" Sophie asked.

"His place is called Château Villette."

Sophie turned, looking surprised. "The Château Villette?"

"That's it."

"Nice friends."

"You know the place?"

"I've passed it. It's in the castle area. Twenty minutes from here." Langdon frowned. "That far?"

"Yes, which will give you enough time to tell me what the Holy Grail really is." Langdon paused. "I'll tell you at Teabing's. He and I know different parts of the legend, so together, we'll give you the whole story." Langdon smiled. "Besides, the Grail has been Teabing's life. Hearing about the Holy Grail from Leigh Teabing will be like hearing the theory of relativity from Einstein himself."

"Let's hope Leigh doesn't mind visitors so late."

"Just so you know, it's Sir Leigh." Langdon had made that mistake only once. "Teabing is quite a guy. The Queen made him a knight a few years ago after he wrote a long history about the House of York."

Sophie looked over. "You're kidding, right? We're going to see a knight?" Langdon gave an awkward smile. "We're on a Grail quest, Sophie. Who better to help us than a knight?"
