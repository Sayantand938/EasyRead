The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 20

Leaving the shadows, Langdon and Sophie quietly made their way up the empty Grand Gallery corridor toward the emergency exit stairs.

As he moved, Langdon felt like he was trying to piece together a puzzle in the dark. The latest part of this mystery was very worrying: The head of the police is trying to make it look like I committed murder.

"Do you think," he whispered, "that maybe Fache wrote that message on the floor?" Sophie didn't even turn. "Impossible."

Langdon wasn't so sure. "He seems very determined to make me look guilty. Maybe he thought writing my name on the floor would help his case?"

"The Fibonacci sequence? The P.S.? All the Da Vinci and goddess symbols? That had to be my grandfather."

Langdon knew she was right. The symbols in the clues fit together too perfectly—the pentacle, The Vitruvian Man, Da Vinci, the goddess, and even the Fibonacci sequence. A clear set of symbols, as experts would call it. All closely connected.

"And his phone call to me this afternoon," Sophie added. "He said he had to tell me something. I'm sure his message at the Louvre was his last attempt to tell me something important, something he thought you could help me understand."

Langdon frowned. O, Draconian devil! Oh, lame saint! He wished he could understand the message, both for Sophie's sake and for his own. Things had definitely become more complicated since he first saw the mysterious words. His fake escape out the bathroom window was not going to make Fache like Langdon any more. He doubted the head of the French police would find it funny that they had chased down and arrested a bar of soap.

"The doorway isn't much farther," Sophie said.

"Do you think the numbers in your grandfather's message might help us understand the other lines?" Langdon had once worked on old papers that contained hidden codes, where some lines were clues to unlock the others.

"I've been thinking about the numbers all night. Adding, dividing, multiplying. I don't see anything. They seem random, like nonsense."

"But they're all part of the Fibonacci sequence. That can't be a coincidence."

"It's not. Using Fibonacci numbers was my grandfather's way of getting my attention—like writing the message in English, or posing like my favorite artwork, or drawing a pentacle on himself. He was trying to make me notice."

"The pentacle means something to you?"

"Yes. I didn't tell you, but the pentacle was a special symbol for my grandfather and me when I was a kid. We played Tarot cards for fun, and my card always came from the suit of pentacles. He probably cheated, but pentacles became our little joke."

Langdon felt a chill. They played Tarot? The old Italian card game was full of hidden, forbidden symbols, so Langdon had written a whole chapter about Tarot in his new book. The game's cards had names like The Female Pope, The Empress, and The Star. Tarot was first created as a secret way to share ideas that the Church didn't allow. Now, fortune-tellers use Tarot's mystical meanings. The Tarot suit for feminine power is pentacles, Langdon thought, realizing that if Saunière had been fixing his granddaughter's deck for fun, pentacles was a fitting inside joke. They got to the emergency stairs, and Sophie carefully opened the door. No alarm went off. Only the outside doors had alarms. Sophie led Langdon down a narrow set of stairs toward the ground floor, walking faster as they went.

"Your grandfather," Langdon said, hurrying after her, "when he told you about the pentacle, did he talk about goddess worship or disliking the Catholic Church?" Sophie shook her head. "I was more interested in the math—the Divine Proportion, PHI, Fibonacci sequences, things like that."

Langdon was surprised. "Your grandfather taught you about the number PHI?"

"Of course. The Divine Proportion." She looked embarrassed. "He even joked that I was half divine... because of the letters in my name."

Langdon thought about it for a moment and then groaned.

s-o-PHI-e.

Still going down, Langdon looked again at PHI. He was beginning to see that Saunière's clues fit together even better than he first thought.

Da Vinci... Fibonacci numbers... the pentacle.

Amazingly, all these things were linked by one idea so basic to art history that Langdon often spent several classes on it.

PHI.

He suddenly felt like he was back at Harvard, standing before his "Symbolism in Art" class, writing his favorite number on the board.

1.618

Langdon turned to his many eager students. "Who knows what this number is?" A tall math student in the back raised his hand. "That's the number PHI." He said it fee.

"Good job, Stettner," Langdon said. "Everyone, this is PHI."

"Not to be confused with PI," Stettner added, grinning. "As we mathematicians like to say: PHI is way cooler than PI!"

Langdon laughed, but nobody else seemed to get the joke.

Stettner slumped.

"This number PHI," Langdon continued, "one-point-six-one-eight, is very important in art. Who can tell me why?"

Stettner tried to recover. "Because it's so pretty?"

Everyone laughed.

"Actually," Langdon said, "Stettner's right again. PHI is generally considered the most beautiful number in the universe."

The laughter stopped, and Stettner gloated.

As Langdon loaded his slide projector, he explained that the number PHI came from the Fibonacci sequence—a series famous because adding two numbers next to each other gives you the next number, but also because dividing numbers next to each other gets closer and closer to 1.618—PHI!

Despite PHI's seemingly mysterious math beginnings, Langdon explained, the amazing thing about PHI was that it was a basic part of nature. Plants, animals, and even people all have sizes and shapes that closely follow the ratio of PHI to 1.

"PHI's commonness in nature," Langdon said, turning off the lights, "is clearly beyond chance, so the ancients thought the number PHI must have been planned by the universe's Creator. Early scientists called one-point-six-one-eight the Divine Proportion."

"Wait," said a young woman in the front row. "I study biology and I've never seen this Divine Proportion in nature."

"Really?" Langdon grinned. "Ever look at the ratio of females to males in a honeybee group?"

"Sure. There are always more female bees than male bees."

"Right. And did you know that if you divide the number of female bees by the number of male bees in any beehive, you always get the same number?"

"You do?"

"Yup. PHI."

The girl stared. "NO WAY!"

"Way!" Langdon replied, smiling as he showed a slide of a spiral seashell. "Know what this is?"

"It's a nautilus," the bio major said. "A mollusk that pumps gas into its shell to control its buoyancy."

"Correct. And can you guess the ratio of each spiral's width to the next?" The girl seemed unsure as she looked at the nautilus shell's circles. Langdon nodded. "PHI. The Divine Proportion. One-point-six-one-eight to one." The girl looked surprised.

Langdon moved to the next slide—a close-up of a sunflower's center. "Sunflower seeds grow in opposite spirals. Can you guess the ratio of each turn's width to the next?"

"PHI?" everyone said.

"Exactly." Langdon started quickly showing slides—spiraled pinecone petals, leaf arrangement on plant stems, insect sections—all showing amazing agreement with the Divine Proportion.

"This is amazing!" someone shouted.

"Yeah," someone else said, "but what does it have to do with art?"

"Aha!" Langdon said. "I'm glad you asked." He brought up another slide—a light yellow paper showing Leonardo da Vinci's famous naked man— The Vitruvian Man —named after Marcus Vitruvius, the smart Roman architect who praised the Divine Proportion in his book De Architectura.

"Da Vinci understood the human body's perfect structure better than anyone. Da Vinci even dug up dead bodies to measure the exact sizes of human bones. He was the first to show that the human body is made of parts whose size ratios always equal PHI."

Everyone in class looked doubtful.

"Don't believe me?" Langdon asked. "Next time you're in the shower, grab a tape measure." A couple of football players chuckled.

"Not just you insecure jocks," Langdon said. "All of you. Guys and girls. Try it. Measure from the top of your head to the floor. Then divide that by the distance from your belly button to the floor. Guess what number you get."

"Not PHI!" one of the jocks said, surprised.

"Yes, PHI," Langdon replied. "One-point-six-one-eight. Want another example? Measure from your shoulder to your fingertips, then divide it by the distance from your elbow to your fingertips. PHI again. Another? Hip to floor divided by knee to floor. PHI again. Finger joints. Toes. Spinal divisions. PHI. PHI. PHI. My friends, each of you is a walking example of the Divine Proportion."

Even in the darkness, Langdon could see they were all amazed. He felt a familiar warmth inside. This is why he taught. "My friends, as you can see, the world's chaos has an order underneath. When people long ago found PHI, they thought they had found God's building block for the world, and they worshipped Nature because of it. And it's easy to see why. God's hand is clear in Nature, and even today there are religions that worship nature and Mother Earth. Many of us celebrate nature like those people did, without even knowing it. May Day is a perfect example, celebrating spring... the earth coming back to life to produce its bounty. The mysterious magic in the Divine Proportion was there from the beginning. People are just following Nature's rules, and because art is how people try to copy the beauty of God's work, you can imagine we'll see the Divine Proportion a lot in art this semester."

Over the next half hour, Langdon showed them slides of artwork by Michelangelo, Albrecht Dürer, Da Vinci, and many others, showing how each artist carefully used the Divine Proportion in their compositions. Langdon pointed out PHI in the sizes of the Greek Parthenon, the pyramids of Egypt, and even the United Nations Building in New York. PHI appeared in the structures of Mozart's sonatas, Beethoven's Fifth Symphony, as well as the works of Bartók, Debussy, and Schubert. Langdon told them that the number PHI was even used by Stradivarius to figure out where to put the f-holes on his famous violins.

"In closing," Langdon said, walking to the chalkboard, "we go back to symbols." He drew five lines crossing to make a five-pointed star. "This symbol is one of the most important images you'll see this term. It's called a pentagram—or pentacle, as people long ago called it—and many cultures see it as divine and magical. Can anyone tell me why?"

Stettner, the math major, raised his hand. "Because if you draw a pentagram, the lines automatically split into sections that follow the Divine Proportion." Langdon nodded at the kid, proud. "Nice job. Yes, the ratios of line sections in a pentacle all equal PHI, making this symbol the best example of the Divine Proportion. That's why the five-pointed star has always been the symbol for beauty and perfection linked to the goddess and the sacred feminine."

The girls in class smiled.

"One thing to note, folks. We've only talked about Da Vinci a little today, but we'll see a lot more of him this semester. Leonardo really followed the old ways of the goddess. Tomorrow, I'll show you his painting The Last Supper, which is one of the most amazing examples of the sacred feminine you'll ever see."

"You're kidding, right?" someone said. "I thought The Last Supper was about Jesus!" Langdon winked. "There are symbols hidden in places you'd never expect."

"Come on," Sophie whispered. "What's wrong? We're almost there. Hurry!" Langdon glanced up, feeling himself return from his thoughts. He realized he was standing still on the stairs, frozen by a sudden realization.

O, Draconian devil! Oh, lame saint!

Sophie was looking back at him.

It can't be that simple, Langdon thought.

But he knew it was.

There in the depths of the Louvre... with images of PHI and Da Vinci swirling in his mind, Robert Langdon suddenly and unexpectedly solved Saunière's code.

"O, Draconian devil!" he said. "Oh, lame saint! It's the simplest kind of code!"

Sophie was stopped on the stairs below him, staring up in confusion. A code? She had been thinking about the words all night and hadn't seen a code, especially a simple one.

"You said it yourself." Langdon's voice echoed with excitement. "Fibonacci numbers only make sense in the right order. Otherwise, they're just mathematical nonsense." Sophie had no idea what he was talking about. The Fibonacci numbers? She was sure they were just meant to get the Cryptography Department involved tonight. Do they have another purpose? She reached into her pocket and pulled out the printout, studying her grandfather's message again.

13-3-2-21-1-1-8-5

O, cruel devil!

Oh, weak saint!

What about the numbers?

"The mixed-up Fibonacci sequence is a clue," Langdon said, taking the paper. "The numbers hint at how to figure out the rest of the message. He wrote the sequence out of order to tell us to do the same with the text. O, cruel devil? Oh, weak saint? Those lines mean nothing. They are just letters written out of order."

Sophie quickly understood what Langdon meant, and it seemed very simple.

"You think this message is... an anagram?" She stared at him. "Like a word puzzle from a newspaper?"

Langdon saw the doubt on Sophie's face and understood it. Few people knew that anagrams, though a simple modern game, had a long history of sacred meaning. The Kabbala's mystical teachings used anagrams a lot—rearranging Hebrew letters to find new meanings. French kings in the Renaissance thought anagrams had magic power, so they hired royal anagram experts to help them make better choices by studying important words. The Romans called the study of anagrams ars magna —"the great art." Langdon looked at Sophie, meeting her eyes. "Your grandfather's meaning was right in front of us, and he gave us plenty of clues to see it." Without another word, Langdon took a pen from his pocket and rearranged the letters in each line.

O, cruel devil! Oh, weak saint!

was a perfect anagram of...

Leonardo da Vinci! The Mona Lisa!
