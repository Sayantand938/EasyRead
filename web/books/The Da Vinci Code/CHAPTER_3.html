The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 3

The sharp April air rushed through the open window of the Citroën ZX as it sped south past the Opera House and crossed Place Vendôme. In the passenger seat, Robert Langdon felt the city blur by as he tried to clear his thoughts. His quick shower and shave had made him look reasonably neat but had done little to calm his worry. The horrifying image of the curator's body stayed stuck in his mind.

Jacques Saunière is dead.

Langdon couldn't help but feel a deep sense of loss at the curator's death. Despite Saunière's reputation for being private, his recognition for dedication to the arts made him easy to respect. His books on the secret codes hidden in the paintings of Poussin and Teniers were some of Langdon's favorite texts to use in class. He had been very much looking forward to tonight's meeting, and he was disappointed when the curator hadn't shown up. Again the image of the curator's body flashed in his mind. Jacques Saunière did that to himself?

Langdon turned and looked out the window, forcing the picture out of his mind. Outside, the city was just now settling down for the night—street vendors pushing carts of candied almonds, waiters carrying bags of garbage to the curb, a pair of late night lovers cuddling to stay warm in a breeze that smelled like jasmine blossoms. The Citroën moved through the chaos easily, its loud, two-tone siren cutting through the traffic like a knife.

"Le capitaine was pleased to find out you were still in Paris tonight," the agent said, speaking for the first time since they'd left the hotel. "A lucky coincidence." Langdon felt anything but lucky, and he didn't completely trust the idea of coincidence. As someone who had spent his life studying the hidden connections between different symbols and ideas, Langdon saw the world as a web of deeply connected histories and events. The connections may be invisible, he often told his symbology classes at Harvard, but they are always there, hidden just beneath the surface.

"I assume," Langdon said, "that the American University of Paris told you where I was staying?" The driver shook his head. "Interpol."

Interpol, Langdon thought. Of course. He had forgotten that the seemingly harmless request of all European hotels to see a passport at check-in was more than just a formality—it was the law. On any given night, all across Europe, Interpol officials could know exactly who was sleeping where. Finding Langdon at the Ritz had probably taken only five seconds. As the Citroën sped south across the city, the bright outline of the Eiffel Tower appeared, reaching upward in the distance to the right. Seeing it, Langdon thought of Vittoria, remembering their playful promise a year ago that every six months they would meet again at a different romantic spot on the globe. The Eiffel Tower, Langdon thought, would have been on their list. Sadly, he had last kissed Vittoria in a noisy airport in Rome more than a year ago.

"Did you mount her?" the agent asked, looking over.

Langdon glanced up, sure he had misheard. "I'm sorry?"

"She is lovely, yes?" The agent pointed through the windshield at the Eiffel Tower. "Have you been up it?"

Langdon rolled his eyes. "No, I haven't climbed the tower."

"It is the symbol of France. I think it is perfect."

Langdon nodded absently. Experts often joked that France—a country known for its macho image, womanizing, and short, insecure leaders like Napoleon and Pepin the Short—couldn't have picked a more fitting national symbol than a giant phallus. When they reached the intersection at Rue de Rivoli, the light was red, but the Citroën didn't slow down. The agent sped the car through the intersection and onto a wooded part of Rue Castiglione, which led to the north entrance of the Tuileries Gardens—Paris's version of Central Park. Most tourists thought Jardins des Tuileries meant the thousands of tulips that bloomed there, but Tuileries actually referred to something less romantic. The park had once been a large, dirty pit where Parisian builders dug clay to make the city's red roofing tiles—or tuiles. As they entered the empty park, the agent reached under the dashboard and turned off the loud siren. Langdon sighed, enjoying the sudden quiet. Outside the car, the headlights shone on the gravel path, and the tires made a steady, hypnotic sound. Langdon had always thought of the Tuileries as a special place. It was in these gardens that Claude Monet had experimented with form and color, inspiring the Impressionist movement. But tonight, it felt strangely ominous. The Citroën turned left, heading west down the park's main path. It went around a circular pond, then across a deserted road into a large open area. Langdon could now see the end of the Tuileries Gardens, marked by a large stone arch. Arc du Carrousel.

Despite the wild rituals once held at the Arc du Carrousel, art lovers admired this place for another reason. From the open area at the end of the Tuileries, you could see four of the world's best art museums... one in each direction. Out the right window, south across the Seine, Langdon saw the brightly lit front of the old train station—now the Musée d'Orsay. Looking left, he could see the top of the modern Pompidou Center, which housed the Museum of Modern Art. Behind him to the west, Langdon knew the ancient obelisk of Ramses stood above the trees, marking the Musée du Jeu de Paume.

But it was straight ahead, to the east, through the archway, that Langdon now saw the massive Renaissance palace that had become the most famous art museum in the world. Musée du Louvre.

Langdon felt a familiar sense of awe as he tried to take in the entire building. Across a huge plaza, the Louvre's impressive front rose like a fortress against the Paris sky. Shaped like a giant horseshoe, the Louvre was the longest building in Europe, longer than three Eiffel Towers placed end to end. Even the large open plaza between the museum wings couldn't compete with the grandeur of the building's width. Langdon had once walked the entire perimeter of the Louvre, an amazing three-mile walk.

Although it would take about five days to truly appreciate the 65,300 works of art in the museum, most tourists chose a quick visit Langdon called "Louvre Lite"—a fast run through the museum to see the three most famous pieces: the Mona Lisa, Venus de Milo, and Winged Victory. Art Buchwald had once joked that he'd seen all three masterpieces in five minutes and fifty-six seconds.

The driver took out a walkie-talkie and spoke quickly in French. "Monsieur Langdon est arrivé. Deux minutes."

An unclear reply crackled back.

The agent put away the device and turned to Langdon. "You will meet the capitaine at the main entrance."

The driver ignored the signs that said cars weren't allowed on the plaza, revved the engine, and drove the Citroën over the curb. The Louvre's main entrance was now visible, standing out in the distance, surrounded by seven triangular pools with illuminated fountains. La Pyramide.

The new entrance to the Paris Louvre had become almost as famous as the museum itself. The controversial, modern glass pyramid designed by the Chinese-American architect I. M. Pei still made traditionalists angry, as they felt it ruined the elegance of the Renaissance courtyard. Goethe had described architecture as frozen music, and Pei's critics said this pyramid was like nails on a chalkboard. But fans of progress praised Pei's seventy-one-foot-tall transparent pyramid as a stunning mix of old structure and new methods—a symbolic bridge between the past and present—helping the Louvre move into the next thousand years.

"Do you like our pyramid?" the agent asked.

Langdon frowned. It seemed the French loved to ask Americans this. It was a tricky question, of course. If you said you liked the pyramid, you seemed like an American with bad taste, and if you said you didn't like it, you were insulting the French.

"Mitterrand was a bold man," Langdon replied, choosing a middle ground. The late French president who had ordered the pyramid was said to have a "Pharaoh complex." He was responsible for filling Paris with Egyptian obelisks, art, and artifacts. François Mitterrand was so interested in Egyptian culture that the French still called him the Sphinx.

"What is the captain's name?" Langdon asked, changing the subject.

"Bezu Fache," the driver said, as they approached the pyramid's main entrance. "We call him le Taureau." Langdon glanced at him, wondering if every Frenchman had a mysterious animal nickname.

"You call your captain the Bull?"

The man raised his eyebrows. "Your French is better than you let on, Monsieur Langdon." My French is terrible, Langdon thought, but I know a lot about zodiac symbols. Taurus was always the bull. Astrology was a symbol that stayed the same all over the world.

The agent stopped the car and pointed between two fountains to a large door in the side of the pyramid. "There's the entrance. Good luck, monsieur."

"You're not coming?"

"My orders are to leave you here. I have other things to do." Langdon sighed and got out. It's your circus.

The agent started the engine and drove off quickly.

As Langdon stood alone and watched the taillights disappear, he knew he could easily change his mind, leave the courtyard, get a taxi, and go home to bed. But something told him it was a bad idea.

As he walked toward the mist from the fountains, Langdon felt like he was crossing an invisible line into another world. The dreamlike feeling of the evening was surrounding him again. Twenty minutes ago he had been asleep in his hotel room. Now he was standing in front of a clear pyramid built by the Sphinx, waiting for a policeman called the Bull. I'm trapped in a Salvador Dali painting, he thought.

Langdon walked to the main entrance—a huge revolving door. The lobby inside was dimly lit and empty.

Do I knock?

Langdon wondered if any of Harvard's respected Egyptologists had ever knocked on the front door of a pyramid and expected an answer. He raised his hand to hit the glass, but from the darkness below, a figure appeared, walking up the curved staircase. The man was short and dark, almost like a Neanderthal, wearing a dark suit that was tight across his wide shoulders. He came forward with clear authority on short, strong legs. He was talking on his cell phone but ended the call when he arrived. He gestured for Langdon to come in.

"I am Bezu Fache," he said as Langdon pushed through the revolving door. "Captain of the Central Directorate Judicial Police." His voice matched his appearance—a deep rumble... like a storm brewing.

Langdon held out his hand to shake. "Robert Langdon."

Fache's large hand gripped Langdon's tightly.

"I saw the photo," Langdon said. "Your agent said Jacques Saunière himself—"

"Mr. Langdon," Fache's dark eyes stared intently. "What you see in the photo is just the start of what Saunière did."
