The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 99

Sir Leigh Teabing looked regretful as he stared at Robert Langdon and Sophie Neveu over the barrel of his Medusa revolver. "My friends," he said, "since you entered my home last night, I have tried my best to keep you safe. But your stubbornness has now put me in a tough spot."

He saw the shock and betrayal on Sophie's and Langdon's faces, but he was sure they would soon understand how they had all ended up at this strange point.

There is so much I need to tell you both... so much you don't know yet.

"Please believe me," Teabing said, "I never wanted you involved. You came to my home. You came looking for me."

"Leigh?" Langdon finally said. "What are you doing? We thought you were in danger. We came to help you!"

"As I knew you would," he said. "We have a lot to talk about." Langdon and Sophie couldn't seem to look away from the revolver pointed at them.

"It's just to make sure I have your full attention," Teabing said. "If I wanted to hurt you, you would be dead already. When you came into my home last night, I risked everything to save you. I am an honorable man, and I promised myself only to hurt those who had betrayed the Sangreal."

"What are you talking about?" Langdon said. "Betrayed the Sangreal?"

"I found out something terrible," Teabing said, sighing. "I learned why the Sangreal documents were never shared with the world. I learned that the Priory had decided not to reveal the truth after all. That's why the millennium passed without any reveal, why nothing happened as we entered the End of Days."

Langdon drew a breath, about to protest.

"The Priory," Teabing continued, "was trusted with a sacred duty: to share the truth. To reveal the Sangreal documents when the End of Days arrived. For centuries, men like Da Vinci, Botticelli, and Newton risked everything to protect these documents and do their duty. And now, at the most important moment, Jacques Saunière changed his mind. The man with the greatest responsibility in Christian history refused to do what he was supposed to do. He decided it wasn't the right time." Teabing turned to Sophie. "He failed the Grail. He failed the Priory. And he failed the memory of all the generations who worked to make that moment possible."

"You?" Sophie declared, glancing up, her green eyes staring at him with anger and understanding. "You are the one who killed my grandfather?" Teabing scoffed. "Your grandfather and his helpers betrayed the Grail." Sophie felt a fury rising from deep within. He's lying!

Teabing's voice was steady. "Your grandfather sold out to the Church. They obviously pressured him to keep the truth hidden."

Sophie shook her head. "The Church had no influence on my grandfather!" Teabing laughed coldly. "My dear, the Church has two thousand years of experience pressuring those who threaten to reveal its lies. Since the time of Constantine, the Church has hidden the truth about Mary Magdalene and Jesus. It's not surprising that now, they've found a way to keep the world in the dark. The Church may not use crusaders to kill non-believers anymore, but their influence is still strong and sneaky." He paused, as if to emphasize his next point. "Miss Neveu, your grandfather has wanted to tell you the truth about your family for some time now."

Sophie was stunned. "How could you know that?"

"How I know is not important. The important thing is for you to understand this." He took a deep breath. "The deaths of your mother, father, grandmother, and brother were not accidents." The words overwhelmed Sophie. She opened her mouth to speak but couldn't. Langdon shook his head. "What are you saying?"

"Robert, this explains everything. It all makes sense. History repeats itself. The Church has a history of murder when it comes to silencing the Sangreal. With the End of Days coming soon, killing the Grand Master's loved ones sent a clear message. Be quiet, or you and Sophie are next."

"It was a car accident," Sophie stammered, feeling the childhood pain rising within her. "An accident!"

"Stories to protect you," Teabing said. "Consider that only two family members were untouched—the Priory's Grand Master and his only granddaughter—the perfect pair for the Church to control the brotherhood. I can only imagine how the Church threatened your grandfather these past years, threatening to kill you if he revealed the Sangreal secret, threatening to finish what they started unless Saunière convinced the Priory to break its ancient vow."

"Leigh," Langdon argued, now clearly annoyed, "you don't have any proof that the Church had anything to do with those deaths, or that it made the Priory stay quiet."

"Proof?" Teabing snapped back. "You want proof the Priory was influenced? It's the new millennium, and the world still doesn't know! Isn't that proof enough?" In the echoes of Teabing's words, Sophie heard another voice speaking. Sophie, I must tell you the truth about your family. She realized she was trembling. Could this be the truth her grandfather had wanted to tell her? That her family had been killed? What did she really know about the crash that killed her family? Just a few details. Even the newspaper stories were unclear. An accident? Stories to tell children? Sophie suddenly remembered how protective her grandfather was, how he never liked to leave her alone when she was young. Even when Sophie was grown up and away at university, she felt like her grandfather was watching over her. She wondered if there had been Priory members watching her from a distance her whole life, taking care of her.

"You thought he was being controlled," Langdon said, glaring at Teabing in disbelief. "So you killed him?"

"I didn't pull the trigger," Teabing said. "Saunière died years ago, when the Church took his family from him. He was ruined. Now he is free of that pain, released from the shame of not being able to do his sacred duty. Think about it. Something had to be done. Should the world stay ignorant forever? Should the Church be allowed to put its lies into our history books forever? Should the Church be allowed to keep influencing people with murder and threats? No, something had to be done! And now we are ready to carry out Saunière's legacy and fix a terrible wrong." He paused. "The three of us. Together." Sophie felt only disbelief. "How could you possibly think that we would help you?"

"Because, my dear, you are why the Priory didn't release the documents. Your grandfather's love for you stopped him from challenging the Church. He was afraid of what they would do to his only remaining family, so he couldn't act. He never had a chance to explain the truth because you rejected him, tying his hands, making him wait. Now you owe the world the truth. You owe it to your grandfather's memory."

Robert Langdon had stopped trying to understand. Even though he had so many questions, he knew only one thing mattered now—getting Sophie out of here alive. All the guilt Langdon had mistakenly felt earlier for involving Teabing had now been transferred to Sophie.

I took her to Château Villette. I am responsible.

Langdon couldn't believe that Leigh Teabing would be able to kill them in cold blood here in the Chapter House, but Teabing had certainly been involved in killing others during his misguided quest. Langdon had the uneasy feeling that gunshots in this secluded, thick-walled room would go unheard, especially in this rain. And Leigh just admitted he was guilty to us. Langdon glanced at Sophie, who looked shaken. The Church killed Sophie's family to silence the Priory? Langdon felt sure the modern Church didn't kill people. There had to be some other explanation.

"Let Sophie leave," Langdon said, staring at Leigh. "You and I should talk about this alone." Teabing gave a strange laugh. "I can't trust you that much, I'm afraid. But I can offer you this." He stood up straighter on his crutches, awkwardly keeping the gun aimed at Sophie, and took the keystone from his pocket. He swayed a little as he held it out for Langdon. "A sign of trust, Robert."

Robert felt cautious and didn't move. Leigh is giving the keystone back to us?

"Take it," Teabing said, pushing it awkwardly toward Langdon. Langdon could think of only one reason Teabing would give it back. "You already opened it. You took out the map."

Teabing shook his head. "Robert, if I had solved the keystone, I would have disappeared to find the Grail myself and left you out of it. No, I don't know the answer. And I can say that easily. A true knight learns to be humble when facing the Grail. He learns to follow the signs in front of him. When I saw you enter the abbey, I understood. You were here for a reason. To help. I'm not looking for personal glory here. I serve something much greater than my own pride. The Truth. People deserve to know that truth. The Grail found us all, and now it wants to be revealed. We must work together."

Despite Teabing's requests for cooperation and trust, his gun stayed pointed at Sophie as Langdon stepped forward and took the cold marble cylinder. The vinegar inside made a bubbling sound as Langdon held it and stepped backward. The dials were still in random order, and the cryptex remained locked.

Langdon looked at Teabing. "How do you know I won't smash it right now?" Teabing's laugh was a strange chuckle. "I should have known your threat to break it in the Temple Church was just talk. Robert Langdon would never break the keystone. You're a historian, Robert. You're holding the key to two thousand years of history—the lost key to the Sangreal. You can feel the spirits of all the knights who were burned at the stake to protect her secret. Would you let them die for nothing? No, you will prove they were right. You'll join the ranks of the great men you admire—Da Vinci, Botticelli, Newton—each of whom would have been honored to be in your place right now. The contents of the keystone are calling out to us. Wanting to be set free. The time has come. Destiny has brought us to this moment."

"I can't help you, Leigh. I have no idea how to open this. I only saw Newton's tomb for a moment. And even if I knew the password..." Langdon stopped, realizing he had said too much.

"You wouldn't tell me?" Teabing sighed. "I'm disappointed and surprised, Robert, that you don't understand how much you owe me. My job would have been much easier if Rémy and I had gotten rid of you both when you walked into Château Villette. Instead, I risked everything to take the more honorable path."

"This is honorable?" Langdon asked, looking at the gun.

"Saunière's fault," Teabing said. "He and his sénéchaux lied to Silas. Otherwise, I would have gotten the keystone without any problems. How could I have guessed the Grand Master would go to such lengths to trick me and leave the keystone to a granddaughter he didn't even know?" Teabing looked at Sophie with disgust. "Someone so unqualified to hold this knowledge that she needed a symbologist babysitter." Teabing glanced back at Langdon. "Fortunately, Robert, your involvement turned out to be my saving grace. Instead of the keystone staying locked in the depository bank forever, you took it out and walked into my home." Where else would I go? Langdon thought. The community of Grail historians is small, and Teabing and I have a history together.

Teabing now looked pleased with himself. "When I learned Saunière left you a dying message, I had a pretty good idea you were holding valuable Priory information. Whether it was the keystone itself, or information on where to find it, I wasn't sure. But with the police chasing you, I had a feeling you might end up on my doorstep."

Langdon glared. "And if we hadn't?"

"I was planning to help you. One way or another, the keystone was coming to Château Villette. The fact that you brought it to me just proves that I'm right."

"What!" Langdon was shocked.

"Silas was supposed to break in and steal the keystone from you at Château Villette—taking you out of the picture without hurting you, and clearing me of any suspicion. But when I saw how complex Saunière's codes were, I decided to keep you both involved a little longer. I could have Silas steal the keystone later, once I knew enough to continue on my own."

"The Temple Church," Sophie said, her voice full of betrayal.

Light begins to dawn, Teabing thought. The Temple Church was the perfect place to steal the keystone from Robert and Sophie, and its connection to the poem made it a believable distraction. Rémy's orders were clear—stay hidden while Silas gets the keystone. Unfortunately, Langdon's threat to smash the keystone on the chapel floor made Rémy panic. If only Rémy hadn't shown himself, Teabing thought sadly, remembering his own fake kidnapping. Rémy was the only link to me, and he revealed his face!

Luckily, Silas didn't know Teabing's real identity and was easily tricked into taking him from the church and then watching as Rémy pretended to tie up their hostage in the back of the limousine. With the soundproof divider up, Teabing was able to call Silas in the front seat, use the fake French accent of the Teacher, and tell Silas to go straight to Opus Dei. A simple anonymous tip to the police would be enough to get rid of Silas. One loose end tied up.

The other loose end was harder. Rémy.

Teabing struggled with the decision, but in the end, Rémy had proven to be a liability. Every Grail quest requires sacrifice. The easiest solution had been right in front of Teabing in the limousine's bar—a flask, some cognac, and a can of peanuts. The powder at the bottom of the can would be more than enough to trigger Rémy's deadly allergy. When Rémy parked the limo on Horse Guards Parade, Teabing climbed out of the back, walked to the side passenger door, and sat in the front next to Rémy. Minutes later, Teabing got out of the car, climbed into the back again, cleaned up the evidence, and finally got out to finish his mission.

Westminster Abbey was a short walk, and although Teabing's leg braces, crutches, and gun had set off the metal detector, the security guards didn't know what to do. Do we ask him to remove his braces and crawl through? Do we pat down his deformed body? Teabing gave the confused guards an easier solution—a card showing he was a Knight of the Realm. The poor guys practically tripped over each other to let him in.

Now, looking at the confused Langdon and Neveu, Teabing resisted the urge to reveal how he had cleverly made Opus Dei look like they were behind the plot that would soon destroy the entire Church. That would have to wait. Right now, there was work to do.

"My friends," Teabing said in perfect French, "you don't find the Holy Grail, the Holy Grail finds you." He smiled. "Our path together is very clear. The Grail has found us."

Silence.

He spoke to them in a whisper now. "Listen. Can you hear it? The Grail is speaking to us across time. It's begging to be saved from the Priory's mistake. I urge you both to see this chance. There couldn't be three better people here right now to break the last code and open the cryptex." Teabing paused, his eyes bright. "We need to promise each other something. A pledge of trust. A knight's promise to find the truth and share it."

Sophie looked into Teabing's eyes and said firmly, "I will never make a promise with my grandfather's killer. Except a promise that I will see you in prison." Teabing's heart sank, then became firm. "I'm sorry you feel that way, mademoiselle." He turned and pointed the gun at Langdon. "And you, Robert? Are you with me, or against me?"
