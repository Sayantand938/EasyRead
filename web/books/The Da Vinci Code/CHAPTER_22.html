The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 22

Kneeling in the first pew, Silas pretended to pray as he looked around the sanctuary. Saint-Sulpice, like most churches, was built like a giant Roman cross. Its long middle part—the nave—led right to the main altar, where it was crossed by a shorter section, called the transept. The place where the nave and transept met was right under the main dome and was seen as the heart of the church... its most holy and mysterious point. Not tonight, Silas thought. Saint-Sulpice hides its secrets elsewhere. Turning his head to the right, he looked into the south transept, toward the open floor beyond the pews, at what his victims had described.

There it is.

Set into the gray granite floor was a thin, shiny strip of brass... a golden line running across the church floor. The line had markings like a ruler. It was a gnomon, Silas had been told, a pagan tool like a sundial used for astronomy. Tourists, scientists, historians, and pagans from all over came to Saint-Sulpice to see this famous line. The Rose Line.

Slowly, Silas followed the brass strip as it went across the floor from his right to left, slanting in front of him at an odd angle, not matching the church's symmetry at all. Cutting across the main altar, the line looked to Silas like a cut on a beautiful face. The strip split the communion rail in two and then crossed the whole width of the church, ending at the corner of the north transept, where it met a very unexpected thing.

A huge Egyptian obelisk.

Here, the shiny Rose Line turned ninety degrees upward and went straight up the obelisk itself, climbing thirty-three feet to the very top of the pyramid-shaped point, where it stopped.

The Rose Line, Silas thought. The brotherhood hid the keystone at the Rose Line. Earlier, when Silas told the Teacher that the Priory keystone was hidden in Saint-Sulpice, the Teacher seemed unsure. But when Silas added that the brothers had given him a precise location, related to a brass line in Saint-Sulpice, the Teacher gasped in realization. "You speak of the Rose Line!"

The Teacher quickly told Silas about Saint-Sulpice's famous oddity—a strip of brass that divided the sanctuary on a perfect north-south line. It was an old sundial, a leftover from the pagan temple that had once stood there. The sun's rays, shining through the opening on the south wall, moved down the line each day, marking the passage of time from one solstice to the next.

The north-south stripe was known as the Rose Line. For centuries, the Rose symbol had been linked to maps and guiding people in the right direction. The Compass Rose—on almost every map—showed North, East, South, and West. Originally called the Wind Rose, it showed the directions of the thirty-two winds, blowing from the directions of eight major winds, eight half-winds, and sixteen quarter-winds. When drawn inside a circle, these thirty-two points looked just like a traditional thirty-two petal rose. Even now, the main tool for navigation is still called a Compass Rose, with its northern direction marked by an arrowhead... or, more often, the fleur-de-lis symbol. On a globe, a Rose Line—also called a meridian or longitude—was any imaginary line from the North Pole to the South Pole. Of course, there were countless Rose Lines because any point on the globe could have a longitude line connecting the north and south poles. The question for early navigators was which of these lines would be the Rose Line—the zero longitude—the line from which all other longitudes on earth would be measured. Today that line is in Greenwich, England.

But it had not always been.

Before Greenwich became the world's prime meridian, the zero longitude went right through Paris, and through the Church of Saint-Sulpice. The brass marker in Saint-Sulpice was a reminder of the world's first prime meridian. Even though Greenwich took the honor from Paris in 1888, the original Rose Line was still there.

"So the legend is true," the Teacher had told Silas. "The Priory keystone is said to lie 'beneath the Sign of the Rose.'"

Still on his knees, Silas looked around the church to be sure he was alone. For a moment, he thought he heard a noise in the choir balcony. He looked up for a few seconds. Nothing.

I am alone.

He stood, faced the altar, and bowed three times. Then he turned left and followed the brass line north toward the obelisk.

At that moment, at Leonardo da Vinci International Airport in Rome, the bump of the tires on the runway woke Bishop Aringarosa.

I fell asleep, he thought, surprised he was relaxed enough to do so.

"Benvenuto a Roma," the intercom announced.

Sitting up, Aringarosa straightened his black cassock and allowed himself a rare smile. He had been happy to make this trip. I have been on the defensive for too long. Tonight, however, things had changed. Only five months ago, Aringarosa had feared for the future of the Church. Now, as if by God's will, the solution had appeared.

Divine intervention.

If all went as planned tonight in Paris, Aringarosa would soon have something that would make him the most powerful man in the Christian world.
