The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 48

Langdon could hardly believe his idea, but considering who gave them this stone cylinder, how he gave it to them, and now, the inlaid Rose on the container, Langdon could only reach one conclusion.

I am holding the Priory keystone.

The legend was clear.

The keystone is a coded stone that lies under the sign of the Rose.

"Robert?" Sophie was watching him. "What's going on?" Langdon needed a moment to collect his thoughts. "Did your grandfather ever mention something called la clef de voûte?"

"The key to the vault?" Sophie translated.

"No, that's the direct translation. Clef de voûte is a common term in architecture. Voûte doesn't refer to a bank vault, but to a vault in an archway. Like a vaulted ceiling."

"But vaulted ceilings don't have keys."

"Actually, they do. Every stone archway needs a central, wedge-shaped stone at the top that locks the pieces together and supports all the weight. This stone is, in architectural terms, the key to the vault. In English, we call it a keystone." Langdon watched her eyes for any sign of understanding.

Sophie shrugged, glancing down at the cryptex. "But this obviously is not a keystone." Langdon didn't know where to begin. Keystones, used in stone archways, were a closely guarded secret of the early Masons. The Royal Arch Degree. Architecture. Keystones. It was all connected. Knowing how to use a keystone to build an archway was part of what made the Masons wealthy, and they kept it secret. Keystones had always been linked to secrecy. Yet, the stone cylinder in the rosewood box was clearly something different. The Priory keystone—if that's what they had—was not what Langdon had expected.

"The Priory keystone is not my specialty," Langdon admitted. "I study the Holy Grail through symbols, so I don't focus on how to actually find it." Sophie's eyebrows went up. "Find the Holy Grail?"

Langdon nodded slightly, choosing his words carefully. "Sophie, according to Priory stories, the keystone is a coded map... a map to the Holy Grail's hiding place." Sophie's face went blank. "And you think this is it?"

Langdon didn't know what to say. It sounded unbelievable even to him, but the keystone was the only logical explanation he could think of. A coded stone, hidden under the sign of the Rose.

The idea that Leonardo da Vinci—a former leader of the Priory of Sion—had designed the cryptex made it even more likely that this was the Priory keystone. A former leader's design... brought to life centuries later by another Priory member. The connection was too strong to ignore.

For the past ten years, historians had looked for the keystone in French churches. Grail searchers, knowing the Priory used confusing language, thought la clef de voûte was a real keystone—a stone wedge—an engraved, coded stone in a church archway. Beneath the sign of the Rose. Churches had many roses in their architecture. Rose windows. Rosette reliefs. And many cinquefoils —the five-petaled flowers often found above archways, over the keystone. The hiding place seemed deceptively simple. The map to the Holy Grail was part of a church archway, mocking the unaware churchgoers below.

"This cryptex can't be the keystone," Sophie argued. "It's not old enough. My grandfather made it. It can't be part of an ancient Grail legend."

"Actually," Langdon replied, feeling excited, "the keystone is thought to have been made by the Priory in recent decades." Sophie looked disbelieving. "But if this cryptex shows where the Holy Grail is hidden, why would my grandfather give it to me? I don't know how to open it or what to do with it. I don't even know what the Holy Grail is!"

Langdon realized she was right. He hadn't explained the Holy Grail to Sophie yet. That would have to wait. Right now, they were focused on the keystone.

If that is what this is....

Against the hum of the bulletproof wheels beneath them, Langdon quickly explained to Sophie everything he had heard about the keystone. Supposedly, for centuries, the Priory's biggest secret—the location of the Holy Grail—was never written down. For safety, it was verbally passed to each new rising sénéchal at a secret ceremony. However, at some point during the last century, rumors began to spread that the Priory policy had changed. Maybe it was because of new electronic listening devices, but the Priory promised never again to even speak the location of the sacred hiding place.

"But then how could they pass on the secret?" Sophie asked.

"That's where the keystone comes in," Langdon explained. "When one of the top four members died, the remaining three would choose the next candidate to become sénéchal from the lower ranks. Instead of telling the new sénéchal where the Grail was hidden, they gave him a test to prove he was worthy."

Sophie seemed uneasy, and Langdon suddenly remembered her mentioning how her grandfather used to make treasure hunts for her— preuves de mérite. The keystone was like that in some ways. Then again, tests like this were very common in secret societies. The most famous was the Masons', where members moved up to higher levels by proving they could keep a secret and by performing rituals and various tests of merit over many years. The tasks became harder and harder until a successful candidate was made a thirty-second-degree Mason.

"So the keystone is a preuve de mérite," Sophie said. "If a rising Priory sénéchal can open it, he proves he deserves the information it holds."

Langdon nodded. "I forgot you'd had experience with this sort of thing."

"Not only with my grandfather. In cryptology, that's called a 'self-authorizing language.' If you're smart enough to read it, you're allowed to know what it says." Langdon hesitated a moment. "Sophie, you realize that if this is the keystone, your grandfather's access to it means he was very powerful within the Priory of Sion. He would have had to have been one of the top four members."

Sophie sighed. "He was powerful in a secret society. I'm sure of it. I can only guess it was the Priory."

Langdon was surprised. "You knew he was in a secret society?"

"I saw some things I wasn't supposed to see ten years ago. We haven't spoken since." She paused.

"My grandfather was not only a high-ranking member of the group... I believe he was the highest."

Langdon could not believe what she had just said. "Grand Master? But... there's no way you could know that!"

"I'd rather not talk about it." Sophie looked away, her expression determined and pained. Langdon sat in stunned silence. Jacques Saunière? Grand Master? Despite the shocking implications if it were true, Langdon felt it almost made perfect sense. After all, previous Priory Grand Masters had also been well-known public figures with artistic spirits. Proof of that had been found years ago in Paris's Bibliothèque Nationale in papers known as Les Dossiers Secrets.

Every Priory historian and Grail enthusiast had read the Dossiers. Listed under Number 4° lm1 249, the Dossiers Secrets had been verified by many experts and undeniably confirmed what historians had long suspected: Priory Grand Masters included Leonardo da Vinci, Botticelli, Sir Isaac Newton, Victor Hugo, and, more recently, Jean Cocteau, the famous Parisian artist.

Why not Jacques Saunière?

Langdon's disbelief grew as he realized he was supposed to meet Saunière tonight. The Priory Grand Master called a meeting with me. Why? To chat about art? It suddenly seemed unlikely. After all, if Langdon's instincts were right, the Grand Master of the Priory of Sion had just given the brotherhood's legendary keystone to his granddaughter and ordered her to find Robert Langdon.

Unbelievable!

Langdon couldn't imagine any situation that would explain Saunière's actions. Even if Saunière feared for his life, there were three sénéchaux who also knew the secret and ensured the Priory's safety. Why would Saunière risk so much by giving his granddaughter the keystone, especially since they didn't get along? And why involve Langdon... a complete stranger?

Something is missing, Langdon thought.

The answers would have to wait. The sound of the slowing engine made them both look up. Gravel crunched under the tires. Why is he stopping already? Langdon wondered. Vernet had said he would take them far outside the city to safety. The truck slowed to a crawl and moved over unexpectedly rough ground. Sophie gave Langdon a worried look, quickly closing and latching the cryptex box. Langdon put his jacket back on. When the truck stopped, the engine kept running as the locks on the rear doors began to turn. When the doors opened, Langdon was surprised to see they were parked in a wooded area, far from the road. Vernet stepped into view, looking strained. He held a pistol in his hand.

"I'm sorry about this," he said. "I really can't do anything else."
