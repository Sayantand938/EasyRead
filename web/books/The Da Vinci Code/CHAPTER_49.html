The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 49

André Vernet looked clumsy with a pistol, but his eyes showed a determination that Langdon knew better than to challenge.

"I'm afraid I have to insist," Vernet said, pointing the gun at them in the back of the idling truck. "Put the box down."

Sophie held the box tightly. "You said you and my grandfather were friends."

"I have a duty to protect your grandfather's things," Vernet replied. "And that's what I'm doing. Now put the box on the floor."

"My grandfather trusted me with this!" Sophie said.

"Do it," Vernet ordered, raising the gun.

Sophie set the box down.

Langdon watched the gun point toward him.

"Mr. Langdon," Vernet said, "you will bring the box to me. And know that I'm asking you because I would not hesitate to shoot you."

Langdon stared at the banker in disbelief. "Why are you doing this?"

"Why do you think?" Vernet snapped, his accented English now sharp. "To protect my client's assets."

"We are your clients now," Sophie said.

Vernet's face turned ice-cold, an eerie change. "Mademoiselle Neveu, I don't know how you got that key and account number tonight, but it seems clear that something bad happened. If I had known how bad your crimes were, I would never have helped you leave the bank."

"I told you," Sophie said, "we had nothing to do with my grandfather's death!" Vernet looked at Langdon. "And yet the radio says you are wanted not only for the murder of Jacques Saunière but for those of three other men as well?"

"What!" Langdon was stunned. Three more murders? The matching number hit him harder than being the main suspect. It seemed too unlikely to be a coincidence. The three sénéchaux? Langdon's eyes dropped to the rosewood box. If the sénéchaux were murdered, Saunière had no choice. He had to give the keystone to someone.

"The police can figure that out when I turn you in," Vernet said. "I have already involved my bank too much."

Sophie glared at Vernet. "You clearly don't plan to turn us in. You would have driven us back to the bank. Instead, you bring us out here and hold us at gunpoint?"

"Your grandfather hired me for one reason—to keep his things safe and private. Whatever this box contains, I will not let it become evidence in a police investigation. Mr. Langdon, bring me the box."

Sophie shook her head. "Don't do it."

A gunshot blasted, and a bullet ripped into the wall above him. The echo shook the back of the truck as an empty shell pinged onto the floor.

Shit! Langdon froze.

Vernet sounded surer now. "Mr. Langdon, pick up the box." Langdon lifted the box.

"Now bring it to me." Vernet was aiming right at him, standing on the ground behind the bumper, his gun reaching into the truck.

Box in hand, Langdon moved toward the open door.

I've got to do something! Langdon thought. I'm about to hand over the Priory keystone! As Langdon moved toward the doorway, his higher position became clearer, and he wondered if he could use it to help himself. Vernet's gun, though raised, was at Langdon's knee. Could I kick him? But as Langdon got closer, Vernet seemed to notice the danger and stepped back, putting himself six feet away. Out of reach.

Vernet ordered, "Place the box beside the door."

Seeing no choice, Langdon knelt and set the box at the edge of the truck, right in front of the open doors.

"Now stand up."

Langdon started to stand but stopped, spotting the small, empty shell on the floor beside the truck's doorsill.

"Stand up, and step away from the box."

Langdon paused, looking at the metal edge. Then he stood. As he did, he quietly brushed the shell over the edge onto the narrow ledge at the bottom of the door. Now standing straight, Langdon stepped back.

"Return to the back wall and turn around."

Langdon did as he was told.

Vernet could feel his heart beating fast. Holding the gun with his right hand, he reached for the wooden box with his left. It was too heavy. I need two hands. He looked back at his prisoners, judging the risk. They were far away, at the other end of the cargo hold, facing away from him. Vernet decided quickly. He put the gun down on the bumper, lifted the box with both hands, and set it on the ground. Then he immediately grabbed the gun again and pointed it back into the hold. Neither prisoner had moved. Perfect. Now he just had to close and lock the door. Leaving the box on the ground, he grabbed the metal door and started to pull it shut. As the door swung past him, Vernet reached up to grab the bolt that needed to be slid into place. The door closed with a thud, and Vernet quickly grabbed the bolt, pulling it to the left. The bolt slid a little and then stopped suddenly, not lined up correctly. What's going on? Vernet pulled again, but the bolt wouldn't lock. The mechanism was not aligned. The door isn't fully closed! Panicked, Vernet pushed hard against the outside of the door, but it wouldn't move. Something is blocking it! Vernet turned to slam his shoulder into the door, but instead, the door burst open, hitting Vernet in the face and sending him staggering backward onto the ground, his nose breaking. The gun flew as Vernet reached for his face and felt the warm blood running from his nose.

Robert Langdon landed somewhere nearby, and Vernet tried to get up, but he couldn't see clearly. His vision blurred, and he fell backward again. Sophie Neveu was yelling. Moments later, Vernet felt dirt and exhaust blowing over him. He heard tires crunching on gravel and sat up just in time to see the truck's wide tires struggle to make a turn. There was a crash as the front bumper hit a tree. The engine roared, and the tree bent. Finally, the bumper broke, tearing half off. The armored car jerked away, its front bumper dragging. When the truck reached the paved road, sparks flew up into the night, following the truck as it sped away. Vernet looked back at the ground where the truck had been parked. Even in the dim moonlight, he could see that it was empty.

The wooden box was gone.
