The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 62

"I'm being set up, Leigh," Langdon said, trying to stay calm. You know me. I wouldn't kill anyone. Teabing's tone did not soften. "Robert, you're on television, for God's sake. Did you know the police were looking for you?"

"Yes."

"Then you betrayed my trust. I'm shocked you would endanger me by coming here and asking me to talk about the Grail so you could hide in my house."

"I didn't kill anyone."

"Jacques Saunière is dead, and the police say you did it." Teabing looked sad. "Such a supporter of the arts..."

"Sir?" The butler had now appeared, standing behind Teabing in the study doorway, his arms crossed. "Shall I show them out?"

"Allow me." Teabing hobbled across the study, unlocked a set of wide glass doors, and swung them open onto a side lawn. "Please find your car, and leave." Sophie did not move. "We have information about the keystone. The Priory keystone." Teabing stared at her for several seconds and scoffed. "A desperate trick. Robert knows how much I've searched for it."

"She's telling the truth," Langdon said. "That's why we came to you tonight. To talk to you about the keystone."

The butler stepped in now. "Leave, or I will call the police."

"Leigh," Langdon whispered, "we know where it is."

Teabing seemed to lose his balance slightly.

Rémy walked stiffly across the room. "Leave now! Or I will make you—"

"Rémy!" Teabing turned, scolding his servant. "Excuse us for a moment." The servant looked shocked. "Sir? I must object. These people are—"

"I'll take care of this." Teabing pointed to the hallway.

After a moment of stunned silence, Rémy slunk out like a scolded dog. In the cool night air coming through the open doors, Teabing turned back to Sophie and Langdon, still looking cautious. "This better be good. What do you know about the keystone?"

In the thick bushes outside Teabing's study, Silas gripped his pistol and looked through the glass doors. Just moments before, he had walked around the house and seen Langdon and the woman talking in the large study. Before he could go in, a man on crutches came in, yelled at Langdon, threw open the doors, and demanded that his guests leave. Then the woman mentioned the keystone, and everything changed. Shouting turned to whispers. Tempers calmed. And the glass doors were quickly shut.

Now, as he hid in the shadows, Silas peered through the glass. The keystone is somewhere inside the house. Silas could feel it.

Staying hidden, he moved closer to the glass, wanting to hear what was being said. He would give them five minutes. If they didn't say where the keystone was, Silas would have to go in and force them to tell him.

Inside the study, Langdon could feel their host's confusion.

"Grand Master?" Teabing gasped, looking at Sophie. "Jacques Saunière?" Sophie nodded, seeing his shock.

"But you can't possibly know that!"

"Jacques Saunière was my grandfather."

Teabing stumbled back on his crutches, glancing at Langdon, who nodded. Teabing turned back to Sophie. "Miss Neveu, I'm speechless. If this is true, I'm very sorry for your loss. I must admit, for my research, I've kept lists of men in Paris who I thought might be good candidates for the Priory. Jacques Saunière was on that list with many others. But Grand Master, you say? It's hard to believe." Teabing was silent for a moment, then shook his head. "But it still doesn't make sense. Even if your grandfather was the Priory Grand Master and made the keystone himself, he would never tell you how to find it. The keystone reveals the way to the brotherhood's greatest treasure. Granddaughter or not, you're not allowed to have that knowledge."

"Mr. Saunière was dying when he gave her the information," Langdon said. "He didn't have many choices."

"He didn't need choices," Teabing argued. "There are three sénéchaux who also know the secret. That's the beauty of their system. One will become Grand Master, and they'll choose a new sénéchal and share the keystone's secret."

"I guess you didn't see the whole news report," Sophie said. "Besides my grandfather, three other important Parisians were murdered today. All in similar ways. All looked like they had been questioned."

Teabing's jaw dropped. "And you think they were..."

"The sénéchaux," Langdon said.

"But how? A murderer couldn't possibly learn the identities of all four top members of the Priory of Sion! Look at me, I've been researching them for decades, and I can't even name one Priory member. It's impossible that all three sénéchaux and the Grand Master could be found and killed in one day."

"I doubt the information was gathered in a single day," Sophie said. "It sounds like a well-planned takedown. It's a technique we use to fight organized crime groups. If DCPJ wants to move on a certain group, they will silently listen and watch for months, identify all the main players, and then move in and take them all at the same moment. Decapitation. With no leadership, the group falls into chaos and gives away other information. It's possible someone patiently watched the Priory and then attacked, hoping the top people would reveal the location of the keystone." Teabing looked unconvinced. "But the brothers would never talk. They are sworn to secrecy. Even if they are about to die."

"Exactly," Langdon said. "Meaning, if they never gave away the secret, and they were killed..." Teabing gasped. "Then the location of the keystone would be lost forever!"

"And with it," Langdon said, "the location of the Holy Grail." Teabing's body seemed to sway with the weight of Langdon's words. Then, as if too tired to stand another moment, he flopped in a chair and stared out the window.

Sophie walked over, her voice soft. "Considering my grandfather's situation, it seems possible that in total desperation he tried to pass the secret on to someone outside the brotherhood. Someone he thought he could trust. Someone in his family."

Teabing was pale. "But someone capable of such an attack... of discovering so much about the brotherhood..." He paused, showing a new fear. "It could only be one force. This kind of infiltration could only have come from the Priory's oldest enemy." Langdon glanced up. "The Church."

"Who else? Rome has been seeking the Grail for centuries."

Sophie was skeptical. "You think the Church killed my grandfather?" Teabing replied, "It would not be the first time in history the Church has killed to protect itself. The documents that go with the Holy Grail are explosive, and the Church has wanted to destroy them for years."

Langdon was having trouble believing Teabing's idea that the Church would blatantly murder people to obtain these documents. Having met the new Pope and many of the cardinals, Langdon knew they were deeply spiritual men who would never allow assassination. No matter what was at stake.

Sophie seemed to be having similar thoughts. "Isn't it possible that these Priory members were murdered by someone outside the Church? Someone who didn't understand what the Grail really is? The Cup of Christ, after all, would be quite an enticing treasure. Certainly treasure hunters have killed for less."

"In my experience," Teabing said, "men go to far greater lengths to avoid what they fear than to obtain what they desire. I sense a desperation in this assault on the Priory."

"Leigh," Langdon said, "the argument doesn't make sense. Why would Catholic priests murder Priory members to find and destroy documents they think are false?"

Teabing chuckled. "Your time at Harvard has made you naive, Robert. Yes, the clergy in Rome have strong faith, so they can handle anything, even documents that disagree with their beliefs. But what about everyone else? What about those who aren't so sure? What about those who see the world's cruelty and ask, where is God? Those who see Church scandals and ask, who are these men who claim to speak for Christ but lie to cover up priests sexually abusing children?" Teabing paused. "What happens to those people, Robert, if convincing scientific evidence shows that the Church's story about Christ is wrong, and that the greatest story ever told is really just a clever lie?" Langdon didn't answer.

"I'll tell you what happens if the documents are revealed," Teabing said. "The Vatican will face a crisis of faith like never before in its two-thousand-year history."

After a long silence, Sophie said, "But if the Church is behind this attack, why now? After all this time? The Priory keeps the Sangreal documents hidden. They aren't an immediate threat to the Church."

Teabing sighed heavily and looked at Langdon. "Robert, I assume you know about the Priory's final mission?"

Langdon felt a jolt of fear. "I do."

"Miss Neveu," Teabing said, "the Church and the Priory have had a silent agreement for years. The Church doesn't attack the Priory, and the Priory keeps the Sangreal documents hidden." He paused. "However, the Priory has always planned to reveal the secret. When a specific date arrives, the brotherhood plans to break the silence and announce its victory by revealing the Sangreal documents to the world and proclaiming the true story of Jesus Christ."

Sophie stared at Teabing in silence. Finally, she sat down too. "And you think that date is near? And the Church knows it?"

"It's a guess," Teabing said, "but it would give the Church a reason to attack and find the documents before it's too late."

Langdon had the sinking feeling that Teabing was right. "Do you think the Church could really find solid proof of the Priory's date?"

"Why not—if we think the Church figured out who the Priory members were, they probably also learned their plans. And even if they don't know the exact date, their fears might be taking over."

"Fears?" Sophie asked.

"When it comes to prophecy," Teabing said, "we're in a time of huge change. The year 2000 just passed, ending the 2,000-year-long astrological Age of Pisces—the fish, which is also a symbol of Jesus. Anyone who studies astrological symbols will tell you that the Piscean idea is that people need to be told what to do by higher powers because they can't think for themselves. That's why it's been a time of strong religion. But now we're entering the Age of Aquarius—the water carrier—where people are supposed to learn the truth and be able to think for themselves. It's a massive shift in thinking, and it's happening right now."

Langdon felt a chill. He didn't care much about astrological prophecy, but he knew some people in the Church took it seriously. "The Church calls this time of change the End of Days."

Sophie looked unsure. "Like the end of the world? The Apocalypse?"

"No," Langdon said. "That's a common misunderstanding. Many religions talk about the End of Days. It doesn't mean the world will end, but rather that our current age—Pisces, which started around the time Jesus was born, lasted 2,000 years, and ended with the new millennium—is ending. Now that we're in the Age of Aquarius, the End of Days is here."

"Many experts on the Holy Grail," Teabing added, "believe that if the Priory is planning to reveal this truth, this time in history would be symbolically perfect. Most Priory scholars, including me, thought the brotherhood would reveal it right around the year 2000. Obviously, they didn't. Of course, the Roman calendar doesn't line up perfectly with astrological signs, so there's some room for error. Whether the Church knows the exact date is coming or is just getting worried because of astrological prophecy, I don't know. But either way, it explains why the Church might want to attack the Priory first." Teabing frowned.

"And trust me, if the Church finds the Holy Grail, they'll destroy it. The documents and the relics of Mary Magdalene, too." His eyes grew sad. "Then, with the Sangreal documents gone, all the proof will be lost. The Church will have won their long battle to change history. The past will be erased forever."

Slowly, Sophie took the cross-shaped key from her sweater pocket and showed it to Teabing. He took the key and examined it. "Good heavens. The Priory seal. Where did you get this?"

"My grandfather gave it to me tonight before he died."

Teabing ran his fingers across the cross. "A key to a church?" She took a deep breath. "This key opens the way to the keystone." Teabing's head shot up, his face full of disbelief. "Impossible! What church did I miss? I've looked in every church in France!"

"It's not in a church," Sophie said. "It's in a Swiss bank." Teabing's excitement faded. "The keystone is in a bank?"

"A vault," Langdon added.

"A bank vault?" Teabing shook his head hard. "That's impossible. The keystone is supposed to be hidden under the sign of the Rose."

"It is," Langdon said. "It was kept in a rosewood box with a five-petal Rose on it." Teabing looked stunned. "You've seen the keystone?" Sophie nodded. "We went to the bank."

Teabing came over to them, his eyes wide with fear. "My friends, we have to do something. The keystone is in danger! We have a duty to protect it. What if there are other keys? Maybe stolen from the murdered sénéchaux? If the Church can get into the bank like you did—"

"Then they'll be too late," Sophie said. "We took the keystone."

"What! You took the keystone from its hiding place?"

"Don't worry," Langdon said. "The keystone is well hidden."

"Very well hidden, I hope!"

"Actually," Langdon said, unable to hide his grin, "that depends on how often you clean under your couch."

The wind outside Château Villette had picked up, and Silas's robe fluttered in the breeze as he crouched near the window. Although he had been unable to hear much of the conversation, the word keystone had come through the glass many times. It is inside.

The Teacher's words were fresh in his mind. Enter Château Villette. Take the keystone. Hurt no one.

Now, Langdon and the others had suddenly moved to another room, turning off the study lights as they went. Feeling like a panther stalking prey, Silas crept to the glass doors. Finding them unlocked, he slipped inside and closed the doors silently behind him. He could hear voices from another room. Silas pulled the pistol from his pocket, turned off the safety, and moved slowly down the hallway.
