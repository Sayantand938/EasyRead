The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 8

Langdon stared at the glowing purple text on the floor. Jacques Saunière's last words seemed like the strangest message he could imagine.

The message read:

13-3-2-21-1-1-8-5

O, Draconian devil!

Oh, lame saint!

Langdon had no idea what it meant, but he understood why Fache thought the pentacle was related to devil worship.

O, Draconian devil!

Saunière had directly mentioned the devil. The numbers were just as odd. "Part of it looks like a number code."

"Yes," Fache said. "Our code experts are working on it. We think these numbers might lead us to his killer. Maybe it's a phone number or some kind of ID. Do the numbers mean anything to you?"

Langdon looked again at the numbers, feeling it would take him hours to find any symbolic meaning. If Saunière had even meant any. To Langdon, the numbers seemed completely random. He was used to symbolic patterns that made some kind of sense, but everything here—the pentacle, the text, the numbers—seemed disconnected at the most basic level.

"You said earlier," Fache said, "that Saunière did all this to send some kind of message... goddess worship or something like that? How does this message fit in?" Langdon knew the question was just for show. This weird message clearly didn't fit Langdon's idea of goddess worship at all.

O, Draconian devil? Oh, lame saint?

Fache said, "This text looks like some kind of accusation. Wouldn't you agree?" Langdon tried to picture the curator's last minutes trapped alone in the Grand Gallery, knowing he was about to die. It seemed to make sense. "An accusation against his killer makes sense, I guess."

"My job, of course, is to find out who that is. Let me ask you this, Mr. Langdon. Besides the numbers, what about this message seems the strangest to you?"

Most strange? A dying man had locked himself in the gallery, drawn a pentacle on himself, and written a mysterious accusation on the floor. What about this situation wasn't strange?

"The word 'Draconian'?" he offered, saying the first thing that came to mind. Langdon was pretty sure that a reference to Draco—the harsh politician from the seventh century B.C.—wasn't something someone would think of as they were dying. " 'Draconian devil' seems like a weird choice of words."

"Draconian?" Fache sounded a little impatient now. "Saunière's choice of words doesn't seem like the main problem here."

Langdon wasn't sure what problem Fache was thinking of, but he was starting to think that Draco and Fache would have gotten along well.

"Saunière was French," Fache said plainly. "He lived in Paris. And yet he chose to write this message..."

"In English," Langdon said, now understanding what the captain meant. Fache nodded. "Exactly. Any idea why?"

Langdon knew Saunière spoke perfect English, but he didn't know why he would choose English for his last words. He shrugged. Fache pointed back to the pentacle on Saunière's stomach. "Nothing to do with devil worship? Are you still sure?"

Langdon wasn't sure of anything anymore. "The symbols and text don't seem to match. I'm sorry I can't help more."

"Maybe this will make it clearer." Fache stepped away from the body and raised the black light higher, making the beam wider. "And now?"

Langdon was shocked to see a simple circle glowing around the curator's body. Saunière had apparently laid down and swung the pen around himself in long, sweeping lines, drawing a circle around himself.

Suddenly, the meaning was clear.

"The Vitruvian Man," Langdon exclaimed. Saunière had made a life-sized copy of Leonardo da Vinci's most famous drawing.

Da Vinci's The Vitruvian Man, considered the most accurate anatomical drawing of its time, had become a well-known cultural symbol, appearing on posters, mouse pads, and T-shirts everywhere. The famous drawing showed a perfect circle with a naked man inside, his arms and legs stretched out wide.

Da Vinci. Langdon felt a shiver of awe. Saunière's message was now undeniable. In his last moments, the curator had taken off his clothes and arranged his body to clearly resemble Leonardo da Vinci's Vitruvian Man.

The circle had been the key piece missing. A feminine symbol of protection, the circle around the naked man's body made Da Vinci's message complete—male and female balance. The question now, though, was why Saunière would copy a famous drawing.

"Mr. Langdon," Fache said, "surely a man like you knows that Leonardo da Vinci was interested in the dark arts."

Langdon was surprised Fache knew about Da Vinci, and it helped explain the captain's suspicions about devil worship. Da Vinci had always been a difficult topic for historians, especially in the Christian world. Despite his genius, he was openly homosexual and worshipped Nature's divine order, both of which made him a sinner in the eyes of God. Also, the artist's strange habits gave him a demonic image: Da Vinci dug up bodies to study human anatomy; he kept secret journals in unreadable backward writing; he believed he could turn lead into gold and even trick God by creating a potion to prevent death; and he invented terrible, new weapons of war and torture.

Misunderstanding creates distrust, Langdon thought.

Even Da Vinci's many beautiful Christian artworks only made his reputation for spiritual hypocrisy worse. Da Vinci accepted many well-paying jobs from the Vatican, painting Christian themes not because he believed in them, but as a business deal—a way to pay for his fancy lifestyle. Sadly, Da Vinci was a prankster who often secretly bit the hand that fed him. He included hidden symbols in many of his Christian paintings that were not Christian at all—honoring his own beliefs and subtly mocking the Church. Langdon had even given a lecture at the National Gallery in London called: "The Secret Life of Leonardo: Pagan Symbolism in Christian Art."

"I understand your concerns," Langdon now said, "but Da Vinci never really practiced any dark arts. He was a very spiritual man, but he was always in conflict with the Church." As Langdon said this, a strange thought came to him. He looked down at the message on the floor again. O, Draconian devil! Oh, lame saint!

"Yes?" Fache said.

Langdon chose his words carefully. "I was just thinking that Saunière shared many spiritual ideas with Da Vinci, including a concern about the Church removing the sacred feminine from modern religion. Maybe, by copying a famous Da Vinci drawing, Saunière was simply repeating some of their shared frustrations with the modern Church's demonization of the goddess." Fache's eyes narrowed. "You think Saunière is calling the Church a lame saint and a Draconian devil?"

Langdon had to admit it seemed unlikely, but the pentacle seemed to support the idea somewhat. "All I'm saying is that Mr. Saunière dedicated his life to studying the history of the goddess, and nothing has done more to erase that history than the Catholic Church. It seems possible that Saunière might have chosen to express his disappointment in his final message."

"Disappointment?" Fache asked, sounding angry now. "This message sounds more angry than disappointed, wouldn't you say?"

Langdon was losing patience. "Captain, you asked for my gut feeling about what Saunière is trying to say, and that's what I'm giving you."

"That this is a criticism of the Church?" Fache's jaw tightened as he spoke through clenched teeth. "Mr. Langdon, I've seen a lot of death, and let me tell you something. When someone is murdered, I don't think their final thoughts are to write a confusing spiritual message that no one will understand. I think they're thinking of one thing only." Fache's whispery voice cut through the air. "La vengeance. I believe Saunière wrote this note to tell us who killed him." Langdon stared. "But that doesn't make any sense."

"No?"

"No," he snapped, tired and frustrated. "You told me Saunière was attacked in his office by someone he had apparently invited in."

"Yes."

"So it seems fair to say that the curator knew his attacker." Fache nodded. "Go on."

"So if Saunière knew the person who killed him, what kind of accusation is this?" He pointed at the floor. "Number codes? Weak saints? Harsh devils? Pentacles on his stomach? It's all too mysterious."

Fache frowned as if he'd never thought of that. "You're right."

"Given the situation," Langdon said, "I'd guess that if Saunière wanted to tell you who killed him, he would have written down a name." As Langdon said this, a smug smile appeared on Fache's face for the first time all night.

"Précisément," Fache said. "Précisément."

I am witnessing the work of a master, Lieutenant Collet thought as he adjusted his audio equipment and listened to Fache's voice through the headphones. The senior agent knew it was moments like these that had elevated the captain to the top of French law enforcement. Fache will do what no one else dares.

The delicate art of persuasion was a lost skill in modern law enforcement, one that demanded exceptional composure under pressure. Few men had the necessary calm for this kind of operation, but Fache seemed born for it. His control and patience were almost robotic. Fache's only emotion this evening seemed to be intense determination, as if this arrest were somehow personal. Fache's briefing of his agents an hour ago had been unusually short and confident. I know who murdered Jacques Saunière, Fache had said. You know what to do. No mistakes tonight.

And so far, no mistakes had been made.

Collet wasn't yet aware of the evidence that had solidified Fache's certainty of their suspect's guilt, but he knew better than to question the Bull's instincts. Fache's intuition seemed almost supernatural at times. God whispers in his ear, one agent had insisted after Fache displayed an especially impressive hunch. Collet had to admit, if there was a God, Bezu Fache would be on His A-list. The captain attended mass and confession with zealous regularity—far more than the required holiday attendance fulfilled by other officials for good public relations. When the Pope visited Paris a few years back, Fache had used all his influence to get an audience. A photo of Fache with the Pope now hung in his office. The Papal Bull, the agents secretly called him.

Collet found it ironic that one of Fache's rare popular public stances in recent years had been his outspoken reaction to the Catholic pedophilia scandal. These priests should be hanged twice! Fache had declared. Once for their crimes against children. And once for shaming the good name of the Catholic Church. Collet had the odd sense it was the latter that angered Fache more. Turning now to his laptop, Collet attended to the other half of his responsibilities tonight—the GPS tracking system. The image on screen revealed a detailed floor plan of the Denon Wing, a structural schematic uploaded from the Louvre Security Office. Letting his eyes trace the maze of galleries and hallways, Collet found what he was looking for.

Deep in the heart of the Grand Gallery blinked a tiny red dot.

The mark.

Fache was keeping his prey on a very tight leash tonight. Wisely so. Robert Langdon had proven himself one cool customer.
