The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 28

Inside the Salle des Etats, Langdon stared in amazement at the six words glowing on the Plexiglas. The text seemed to float in space, casting a sharp shadow across Mona Lisa's mysterious smile.

"The Priory," Langdon whispered. "This proves your grandfather was a member!" Sophie looked at him, confused. "You understand this?"

"It's perfect," Langdon said, nodding as he thought hard. "It's a statement of one of the Priory's most basic beliefs!"

Sophie looked puzzled in the light of the message written across the Mona Lisa's face.

SO DARK THE CON OF MAN

"Sophie," Langdon said, "the Priory's tradition of continuing goddess worship is based on a belief that powerful men in the early Christian church 'conned' the world by spreading lies that made women seem less important and favored men instead." Sophie stayed quiet, staring at the words.

"The Priory believes that Constantine and the men who came after him successfully changed the world from paganism that honored women to Christianity that honored men by using propaganda that made the sacred feminine seem evil, erasing the goddess from modern religion forever." Sophie still looked unsure. "My grandfather sent me to this spot to find this. He must be trying to tell me more than that."

Langdon understood what she meant. She thinks this is another code. Whether there was a hidden meaning here or not, Langdon couldn't tell right away. He was still trying to understand the clear message Saunière had left.

So dark the con of man, he thought. So dark indeed.

No one could deny the great good the modern Church did in today's troubled world, yet the Church had a deceitful and violent history. Their brutal crusade to "reeducate" the pagan and feminine-worshipping religions lasted three centuries, using methods as clever as they were horrific.

The Catholic Inquisition published what could be called the most blood-soaked book in human history. Malleus Maleficarum —or The Witches' Hammer —taught the world about "the dangers of freethinking women" and told the clergy how to find, torture, and kill them. Those labeled "witches" by the Church included all female scholars, priestesses, gypsies, mystics, nature lovers, herb gatherers, and any women "suspiciously connected to the natural world." Midwives were also killed for using medical knowledge to ease childbirth pain—a suffering, the Church claimed, was God's rightful punishment for Eve eating the Apple of Knowledge, creating the idea of Original Sin. During three hundred years of witch hunts, the Church burned an astounding five million women at the stake.

The propaganda and bloodshed had worked.

Today's world was living proof.

Women, once honored as a vital part of spiritual enlightenment, had been removed from the world's temples. There were no female Orthodox rabbis, Catholic priests, or Islamic clerics. The once sacred act of Hieros Gamos—the natural sexual union between man and woman through which each became spiritually whole—had been changed into a shameful act. Holy men who once needed sexual union with their female partners to connect with God now feared their natural sexual urges as the work of the devil, working with his favorite helper... woman.

Even the feminine link with the left-hand side was defamed by the Church. In France and Italy, the words for "left"— gauche and sinistra —became very negative, while their right-hand counterparts sounded right eous, skillful, and correct. To this day, radical thought is considered left wing, irrational thought is left brain, and anything evil, sinister.

The days of the goddess were over. The pendulum had swung. Mother Earth had become a man's world, and the gods of destruction and war were taking their toll. The male ego had spent two millennia unchecked by its female counterpart. The Priory of Sion believed that this destruction of the sacred feminine in modern life had caused what the Hopi Native Americans called koyanisquatsi —"life out of balance"—an unstable situation marked by testosterone-fueled wars, many misogynistic societies, and a growing disrespect for Mother Earth.

"Robert!" Sophie whispered, pulling him back. "Someone's coming!" He heard footsteps approaching in the hallway.

"Over here!" Sophie turned off the black light and seemed to vanish before Langdon's eyes. For a moment he felt completely blind. Over where! As his vision cleared he saw Sophie running toward the center of the room and hiding behind the octagonal viewing bench. He was about to run after her when a loud voice stopped him.

"Arrêtez!" a man commanded from the doorway.

The Louvre security agent moved quickly into the Salle des Etats, his pistol aimed at Langdon's chest.

Langdon's arms went up without him thinking.

"Couchez-vous!" the guard yelled. "Lie down!" Langdon was on the floor in seconds. The guard rushed over and kicked his legs apart, making him stretch out.

"Mauvaise idée, Monsieur Langdon," he said, pushing the gun into Langdon's back.

"Mauvaise idée."

Lying face down on the floor with his arms and legs spread wide, Langdon didn't find the situation funny. The Vitruvian Man, he thought. Face down.
