The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 43

André Vernet—president of the Paris branch of the Depository Bank of Zurich—lived in a fancy apartment above the bank. Despite his luxurious home, he had always dreamed of owning an apartment by the river on L'lle Saint-Louis, where he could mingle with the truly cultured, instead of just meeting the very rich.

When I retire, Vernet thought, I will fill my cellar with fine Bordeaux wines, decorate my living room with a Fragonard and maybe a Boucher painting, and spend my days looking for antique furniture and rare books in the Quartier Latin.

Tonight, Vernet had been awake for only six and a half minutes. Still, as he hurried through the bank's underground corridor, he looked as if his personal tailor and hairdresser had made him perfectly polished. Dressed in a silk suit, Vernet sprayed breath freshener in his mouth and tightened his tie as he walked. Used to being woken up to help his international clients arriving from different time zones, Vernet based his sleep habits on the Maasai warriors—the African tribe known for their ability to wake from deep sleep to full readiness in seconds.

Ready for battle, Vernet thought, worried the comparison might be fitting tonight. The arrival of a gold key client always needed extra attention, but the arrival of a gold key client who was wanted by the police would be very tricky. The bank had enough problems with law enforcement over client privacy without proof that some clients were criminals.

Five minutes, Vernet thought. I need these people out of my bank before the police get here. If he acted fast, he could avoid this disaster. Vernet could tell the police that the people they were looking for had come into his bank as reported, but because they weren't clients and didn't have an account number, they were turned away. He wished the watchman hadn't called Interpol. Being discreet was clearly not something a 15-euro-per-hour watchman knew about. Stopping at the doorway, he took a deep breath and relaxed his muscles. Then, forcing a friendly smile, he unlocked the door and entered the room like a warm breeze.

"Good evening," he said, his eyes finding his clients. "I am André Vernet. How can I help y—" The rest of the sentence caught in his throat. The woman before him was as unexpected a visitor as Vernet had ever had.

"I'm sorry, do we know each other?" Sophie asked. She didn't recognize the banker, but he looked for a moment as if he'd seen a ghost.

"No...," the bank president stammered. "I don't... think so. Our services are private." He breathed out and forced a calm smile. "My assistant tells me you have a gold key but no account number? May I ask how you got this key?"

"My grandfather gave it to me," Sophie replied, watching the man closely. His nervousness seemed even more obvious now.

"Really? Your grandfather gave you the key but didn't give you the account number?"

"I don't think he had time," Sophie said. "He was murdered tonight." Her words made the man step back, shocked. "Jacques Saunière is dead?" he asked, his eyes wide with horror. "But... how?!"

Now Sophie was the one who felt stunned, shocked and numb. "You knew my grandfather?" Banker André Vernet looked just as surprised, holding himself up by leaning on a small table.

"Jacques and I were close friends. When did this happen?"

"Earlier tonight. Inside the Louvre."

Vernet walked to a big leather chair and sat down heavily. "I need to ask you both something very important." He looked at Langdon and then back at Sophie. "Did either of you have anything to do with his death?"

"No!" Sophie said. "Absolutely not."

Vernet's face was serious, and he stopped, thinking. "Your pictures are being sent around by Interpol. That's how I knew who you were. You're wanted for murder."

Sophie felt weak. Fache sent an Interpol message already? It seemed the captain was more determined than Sophie thought. She quickly told Vernet who Langdon was and what had happened inside the Louvre tonight.

Vernet looked astonished. "And as your grandfather was dying, he left you a message telling you to find Mr. Langdon?"

Yes. And this key." Sophie put the gold key on the coffee table in front of Vernet, with the Priory seal face down.

Vernet looked at the key but didn't touch it. "He only left you this key? Nothing else? No note?"

Sophie knew she had been in a hurry inside the Louvre, but she was sure she had seen nothing else behind Madonna of the Rocks. "No. Just the key." Vernet sighed, sounding helpless. "I'm afraid every key needs a ten-digit account number to work, like a password. Without that number, your key is useless." Ten digits. Sophie did a quick calculation. Over ten billion possible choices. Even with DCPJ's best computers, it would still take weeks to break the code. "Surely, monsieur, considering what's happening, you can help us."

"I'm sorry. I really can't do anything. Clients pick their own account numbers on a secure terminal, so only the client and the computer know them. This is how we keep things private. And keep our employees safe."

Sophie understood. Stores did the same thing. EMPLOYEES DO NOT HAVE KEYS TO THE SAFE. This bank clearly didn't want someone stealing a key and forcing an employee to give up the account number.

Sophie sat next to Langdon, looked at the key, and then at Vernet. "Do you have any idea what my grandfather is keeping in your bank?"

"None at all. That's what a Geldschrank bank is."

"Monsieur Vernet," she said urgently, "we don't have much time tonight. I'm going to be very direct, if I may." She reached for the gold key and turned it over, watching his eyes as she showed the Priory of Sion seal. "Does this symbol mean anything to you?" Vernet looked at the fleur-de-lis seal and didn't react. "No, but many of our clients put company logos or initials on their keys."

Sophie sighed, still watching him closely. "This seal is the symbol of a secret group called the Priory of Sion."

Vernet still didn't react. "I don't know anything about that. Your grandfather was a friend, but we talked mostly about business." He adjusted his tie, now looking nervous.

"Monsieur Vernet," Sophie said firmly. "My grandfather called me tonight and said he and I were in serious danger. He said he had to give me something. He gave me a key to your bank. Now he's dead. Anything you can tell us would help."

Vernet started sweating. "We need to leave the building. I'm afraid the police will be here soon. My guard felt he had to call Interpol."

Sophie had expected this. She tried one last thing. "My grandfather said he needed to tell me the truth about my family. Does that mean anything to you?"

"Mademoiselle, your family died in a car accident when you were young. I'm sorry. I know your grandfather loved you very much. He often told me how sad he was that you two had lost touch."

Sophie didn't know what to say.

Langdon asked, "Does what's in this account have anything to do with the Sangreal?" Vernet looked at him strangely. "I have no idea what that is." Just then, Vernet's cell phone rang, and he grabbed it from his belt. "Oui?" He listened, looking surprised and worried. "La police? Si rapidement?" He swore, gave some quick directions in French, and said he'd be in the lobby in a minute.

Hanging up, he turned back to Sophie. "The police are here much faster than normal. They're arriving right now."

Sophie didn't want to leave with nothing. "Tell them we already came and left. If they want to search the bank, make them get a warrant. That will take time."

"Listen," Vernet said, "Jacques was a friend, and my bank doesn't need this kind of attention, so for those two reasons, I won't let this arrest happen here. Give me a minute, and I'll see how to help you leave the bank without being seen. After that, I can't get involved." He stood and rushed to the door. "Stay here. I'll set things up and be right back."

"But the safe-deposit box," Sophie said. "We can't just leave it."

"There's nothing I can do," Vernet said, hurrying out. "I'm sorry." Sophie watched him go, wondering if the account number was hidden in one of the many letters and packages her grandfather had sent her that she hadn't opened.

Langdon stood suddenly, and Sophie saw a hint of happiness in his eyes.

"Robert? You're smiling."

"Your grandfather was a genius."

"What?"

"Ten digits?"

Sophie didn't know what he meant.

"The account number," he said, a grin spreading across his face. "I think he did leave it for us after all."

"Where?"

Langdon took out the crime scene photo and laid it on the coffee table. Sophie only had to read the first line to know Langdon was right.

13-3-2-21-1-1-8-5

O, harsh devil!

Oh, weak saint!
