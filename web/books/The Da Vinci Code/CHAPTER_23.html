The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 23

Sophie arrived breathless outside the large wooden doors of the Salle des Etats—the room that held the Mona Lisa. Before going in, she sadly looked farther down the hall, about twenty yards, to where her grandfather's body still lay under the spotlight. She suddenly felt a strong sense of regret, a deep sadness mixed with guilt. The man had tried to connect with her many times over the past ten years, but Sophie had been unyielding—leaving his letters and packages unopened in a drawer and refusing to see him. He lied to me! Kept terrible secrets! What else could I do? So she had shut him out. Completely.

Now her grandfather was dead, and he was speaking to her from beyond the grave. The Mona Lisa.

She reached for the huge wooden doors and pushed. The entrance opened wide. Sophie paused, looking at the large rectangular room beyond. It was also bathed in a soft red light. The Salle des Etats was one of the museum's rare dead ends—the only room off the middle of the Grand Gallery. This door, the room's only entrance, faced a large fifteen-foot Botticelli painting on the far wall. Under it, in the center of the floor, a large octagonal bench offered a welcome rest for the thousands of visitors admiring the Louvre's most valuable piece.

Even before Sophie entered, she knew something was missing. A black light. She looked down the hall at her grandfather under the lights, surrounded by equipment. If he had written anything in here, he almost certainly would have used the watermark pen.

Taking a deep breath, Sophie hurried to the brightly lit crime scene. Unable to look at her grandfather, she focused on the tools. Finding a small ultraviolet penlight, she slipped it into her sweater pocket and hurried back toward the open doors of the Salle des Etats.

Sophie turned the corner and stepped inside. But she was met by the unexpected sound of muffled footsteps racing toward her from inside the room. Someone's in here! A ghostly figure suddenly appeared from the reddish haze. Sophie jumped back.

"There you are!" Langdon's hoarse whisper cut the air as his silhouette stopped in front of her. Her relief was brief. "Robert, I told you to get out of here! If Fache—"

"Where were you?"

"I had to get the black light," she whispered, holding it up. "If my grandfather left me a message—"

"Sophie, listen." Langdon paused, his blue eyes fixed on hers. "The letters P.S.... do they mean anything else to you? Anything at all?"

Afraid someone might hear them, Sophie pulled him into the Salle des Etats and quietly closed the huge doors, trapping them inside. "I told you, the initials mean Princess Sophie."

"I know, but have you ever seen them anywhere else? Did your grandfather ever use P.S. in any other way? Like on a monogram, or maybe on his stationery or something personal?"

The question surprised her. How would Robert know that? Sophie had seen the initials P.S. once before, as a monogram. It was the day before her ninth birthday. She was secretly searching the house for hidden birthday presents. Even then, she couldn't stand secrets. What did Grand-père get me this year? She looked through cupboards and drawers. Did he get me the doll I wanted? Where would he hide it?

Finding nothing, Sophie decided to sneak into her grandfather's bedroom. She wasn't allowed in there, but her grandfather was downstairs asleep on the couch. I'll just take a quick look!

Walking quietly across the creaky wood floor to his closet, Sophie peeked on the shelves behind his clothes. Nothing. Next, she looked under the bed. Still nothing. Moving to his bureau, she opened the drawers and carefully looked through them. There must be something for me here! As she reached the bottom drawer, she still hadn't found any sign of a doll. Disappointed, she opened the last drawer and moved aside some black clothes she had never seen him wear. She was about to close the drawer when she saw something gold in the back. It looked like a pocket watch chain, but she knew he didn't wear one. Her heart beat faster as she realized what it must be.

A necklace!

Sophie carefully pulled the chain from the drawer. To her surprise, there was a shiny gold key on the end. Heavy and bright. Fascinated, she held it up. It looked like no key she had ever seen. Most keys were flat with jagged teeth, but this one had a triangular shape with little marks all over it. Its large golden head was shaped like a cross, but not a normal cross. This was an even one, like a plus sign. In the middle of the cross was a strange symbol—two letters connected with a flowery design.

"P.S.," she whispered, frowning as she read the letters. What could this be?

"Sophie?" her grandfather said from the doorway.

Startled, she spun, dropping the key on the floor with a loud clang. She stared down at the key, afraid to look up at her grandfather's face. "I... was looking for my birthday present," she said, hanging her head, knowing she had betrayed his trust.

For what seemed like forever, her grandfather stood silently in the doorway. Finally, he let out a long, troubled breath. "Pick up the key, Sophie."

Sophie picked up the key.

Her grandfather walked in. "Sophie, you need to respect other people's privacy." Gently, he knelt down and took the key from her. "This key is very special. If you had lost it..." Her grandfather's quiet voice made Sophie feel even worse. "I'm sorry, Grand-père. I really am." She paused. "I thought it was a necklace for my birthday."

He gazed at her for several seconds. "I'll say this again, Sophie, because it's important. You need to learn to respect other people's privacy."

"Yes, Grand-père."

"We'll talk about this later. Right now, the garden needs to be weeded." Sophie hurried outside to do her chores.

The next morning, Sophie received no birthday present from her grandfather. She hadn't expected one, not after what she had done. But he didn't even wish her happy birthday all day. Sadly, she trudged up to bed that night. As she climbed in, though, she found a note card lying on her pillow. On the card was written a simple riddle. Even before she solved the riddle, she was smiling. I know what this is! Her grandfather had done this for her last Christmas morning. A treasure hunt!

Eagerly, she studied the riddle until she solved it. The solution pointed her to another part of the house, where she found another card and another riddle. She solved this one too, racing on to the next card. Running wildly, she darted back and forth across the house, from clue to clue, until at last she found a clue that directed her back to her own bedroom. Sophie dashed up the stairs, rushed into her room, and stopped in her tracks. There in the middle of the room sat a shining red bicycle with a ribbon tied to the handlebars. Sophie shrieked with delight.

"I know you asked for a doll," her grandfather said, smiling in the corner. "I thought you might like this even better."

The next day, her grandfather taught her to ride, running beside her down the walkway. When Sophie steered out over the thick lawn and lost her balance, they both went tumbling onto the grass, rolling and laughing.

"Grand-père," Sophie said, hugging him. "I'm really sorry about the key."

"I know, sweetie. You're forgiven. I can't stay mad at you. Grandfathers and granddaughters always forgive each other."

Sophie knew she shouldn't ask, but she couldn't help it. "What does it open? I never saw a key like that. It was very pretty."

Her grandfather was silent a long moment, and Sophie could see he wasn't sure how to answer. Grand-père never lies. "It opens a box," he finally said. "Where I keep many secrets." Sophie pouted. "I hate secrets!"

"I know, but these are important secrets. And someday, you'll learn to like them as much as I do."

"I saw letters on the key, and a flower."

"Yes, that's my favorite flower. It's called a fleur-de-lis. We have them in the garden. The white ones. In English we call that kind of flower a lily."

"I know those! They're my favorite too!"

"Then I'll make a deal with you." Her grandfather's eyebrows raised the way they always did when he was about to give her a challenge. "If you can keep my key a secret, and never talk about it ever again, to me or anybody, then someday I will give it to you."

Sophie couldn't believe her ears. "You will?"

"I promise. When the time comes, the key will be yours. It has your name on it." Sophie frowned. "No, it doesn't. It says P.S. My name isn't P.S.!" Her grandfather lowered his voice and looked around as if to make sure no one was listening.

"Okay, Sophie, if you really want to know, P.S. is a code. It's your secret initials." Her eyes went wide. "I have secret initials?"

"Of course. Granddaughters always have secret initials that only their grandfathers know."

"P.S.?"

He tickled her. "Princess Sophie."

She giggled. "I'm not a princess!"

He winked. "You are to me."

From that day on, they never spoke of the key again. And she became his Princess Sophie.

Inside the Salle des Etats, Sophie stood in silence and felt the sharp sting of loss.

"The initials," Langdon whispered, looking at her strangely. "Have you seen them?" Sophie felt her grandfather's voice whisper in the museum halls. Never speak of this key, Sophie. Not to me or anyone. She knew she hadn't forgiven him, and she wondered if she could break his trust again. P.S. Find Robert Langdon. Her grandfather wanted Langdon to help. Sophie nodded. "Yes, I saw the initials P.S. once. When I was very young."

"Where?"

Sophie hesitated. "On something very important to him."

Langdon looked straight at her. "Sophie, this is important. Can you tell me if the initials appeared with a symbol? A fleur-de-lis?"

Sophie felt herself step back in surprise. "But... how could you know that!" Langdon sighed and lowered his voice. "I'm pretty sure your grandfather was a member of a secret society. A very old hidden group."

Sophie felt her stomach tighten. She was sure of it too. For ten years she had tried to forget the event that had confirmed that terrible fact for her. She had seen something impossible to accept. Unforgivable.

"The fleur-de-lis," Langdon said, "with the initials P.S., that is the group's official symbol. Their coat of arms. Their logo."

"How do you know this?" Sophie hoped Langdon wasn't going to tell her that he himself was a member.

"I've written about this group," he said, his voice shaking with excitement. "Studying the symbols of secret societies is something I specialize in. They call themselves the Prieuré de Sion — the Priory of Sion. They're based here in France and have powerful members from all over Europe. They are one of the oldest secret societies on earth." Sophie had never heard of them.

Langdon was talking quickly now. "The Priory's members have included some of history's most educated people: men like Botticelli, Sir Isaac Newton, Victor Hugo." He paused, his voice full of academic interest. "And, Leonardo da Vinci." Sophie stared. "Da Vinci was in a secret society?"

Da Vinci led the Priory from 1510 to 1519 as its Grand Master, which might explain your grandfather's love for Leonardo's work. The two men share a historical connection. And it all fits with their interest in goddess symbols, paganism, female deities, and dislike for the Church. The Priory is known to have always honored the sacred feminine.

"You're saying this group is a pagan goddess worship cult?"

"More like the pagan goddess worship cult. But more important, they are known as the guardians of an ancient secret. One that made them incredibly powerful."

Despite Langdon's confidence, Sophie's first thought was disbelief. A secret pagan cult? Once led by Leonardo da Vinci? It all seemed ridiculous. And yet, even as she rejected it, she remembered a night ten years ago when she had surprised her grandfather and seen something she still couldn't accept. Could that explain —?

"The identities of living Priory members are closely guarded," Langdon said, "but the P.S. and fleur-de-lis that you saw as a child are proof. It could only have been related to the Priory." Sophie realized Langdon knew more about her grandfather than she thought. This American clearly had a lot to tell her, but not here. "I can't let them catch you, Robert. We need to talk. You need to leave!"

Langdon barely heard her. He wasn't going anywhere. He was lost in another world now. A world where old secrets came to light. A world where forgotten histories emerged.

Slowly, as if underwater, Langdon turned and looked through the reddish light toward the Mona Lisa.

The fleur-de-lis... the flower of Lisa... the Mona Lisa.

It was all connected, a silent song echoing the deepest secrets of the Priory of Sion and Leonardo da Vinci.

A few miles away, on the riverbank beyond Les Invalides, the confused driver of a twin-bed truck stood at gunpoint and watched as the captain of the Judicial Police roared in anger and threw a bar of soap into the swollen waters of the Seine.
