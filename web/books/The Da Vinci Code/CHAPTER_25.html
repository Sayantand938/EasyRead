The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 25

The U.S. Embassy in Paris is a small area on Avenue Gabriel, just north of the Champs-Elysées. The three-acre space is considered U.S. land, meaning anyone on it has the same laws and protections as in the United States. The embassy's night operator was reading Time magazine's International Edition when her phone rang.

"U.S. Embassy," she answered.

"Good evening." The caller spoke English with a French accent. "I need some help." Although the man was polite, he sounded stern and formal. "I was told you had a phone message for me on your automated system. The name is Langdon. I've forgotten my three-digit code. If you could help me, I'd appreciate it." The operator paused, confused. "I'm sorry, sir. Your message must be old. That system was removed two years ago for security reasons. Also, all the codes were five-digit. Who told you we had a message for you?"

"You have no automated phone system?"

"No, sir. Any message for you would be written by hand in our services department. What was your name again?"

But the man had hung up.

Bezu Fache was stunned as he walked along the Seine. He was sure he had seen Langdon dial a local number, enter a three-digit code, and then listen to a recording. But if Langdon didn't call the embassy, then who did he call?

Just then, looking at his cell phone, Fache realized the answer was in his hand. Langdon used my phone to make that call.

Using the cell phone's menu, Fache found the list of recent calls and saw the one Langdon had made.

An exchange in Paris, then the code 454.

Fache dialed again, waiting as the phone rang.

Finally, a woman answered. "Bonjour, vous êtes bien chez Sophie Neveu," the recording said. "Je suis absente pour le moment, mais..." Fache's blood boiled as he typed 4... 5... 4.
