The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 81

The Hawker is on final approach.

Simon Edwards—Executive Services Officer at Biggin Hill Airport—walked back and forth in the control tower, anxiously watching the rain-soaked runway. He never liked being woken up early on a Saturday, but it was especially annoying to be called in to watch the arrest of one of his most valuable clients. Sir Leigh Teabing paid Biggin Hill not only for a private hangar but also a "per landing fee" for his frequent arrivals and departures. Usually, the airfield knew his schedule in advance and followed a strict procedure for his arrival. Teabing liked things done perfectly. The custom-built Jaguar limousine he kept in his hangar had to be fully fueled, polished, and the day's London Times placed on the back seat. A customs official was to be at the hangar waiting for the plane to quickly process the required paperwork and luggage check. Sometimes, customs agents accepted large tips from Teabing in exchange for ignoring the transport of harmless items—mostly luxury foods—French snails, a very ripe Roquefort cheese, certain fruits. Many customs laws were ridiculous, anyway, and if Biggin Hill didn't take care of its clients, other airfields would. Teabing got what he wanted at Biggin Hill, and the employees benefited. Edwards felt nervous as he watched the jet coming in. He wondered if Teabing's habit of sharing his wealth had gotten him into trouble; the French authorities seemed very determined to catch him. Edwards hadn't been told what the charges were yet, but they were clearly serious. At the French authorities' request, Kent police had told the Biggin Hill air traffic controller to radio the Hawker's pilot and order him to go directly to the terminal instead of the client's hangar. The pilot had agreed, apparently believing the unlikely story of a gas leak.

Although the British police didn't usually carry guns, the seriousness of the situation had brought out an armed response team. Now, eight police officers with handguns stood just inside the terminal building, waiting for the moment when the plane's engines shut down. As soon as this happened, a runway worker would place safety blocks under the tires so the plane couldn't move. Then the police would appear and hold the occupants until the French police arrived to handle things.

The Hawker was now low in the sky, passing close to the treetops on their right. Simon Edwards went downstairs to watch the landing from the ground. The Kent police were ready, out of sight, and the maintenance man waited with his blocks. On the runway, the Hawker's nose went up, and the tires touched down in a puff of smoke. The plane slowed down, moving from right to left in front of the terminal, its white body shining in the rain. But instead of braking and turning into the terminal, the jet calmly rolled past the access lane and continued toward Teabing's hangar in the distance.

All the police turned and stared at Edwards. "I thought you said the pilot agreed to come to the terminal!"

Edwards was confused. "He did!"

Moments later, Edwards found himself in a police car racing across the tarmac toward the distant hangar. The line of police cars was still about five hundred yards away as Teabing's Hawker taxied calmly into the private hangar and disappeared. When the cars finally arrived and stopped suddenly outside the open hangar door, the police jumped out, guns drawn. Edwards jumped out too.

The noise was deafening.

The Hawker's engines were still loud as the jet completed its usual turn inside the hangar, positioning itself facing outward for a later departure. As the plane finished its 180-degree turn and moved toward the front of the hangar, Edwards could see the pilot's face, which looked understandably surprised and scared to see the line of police cars. The pilot brought the plane to a final stop and shut off the engines. The police rushed in, taking positions around the jet. Edwards joined the Kent chief inspector, who moved cautiously toward the hatch. After a few seconds, the fuselage door popped open. Leigh Teabing appeared in the doorway as the plane's electronic stairs smoothly lowered. As he looked out at the crowd of weapons pointed at him, he leaned on his crutches and scratched his head. "Simon, did I win some kind of police lottery while I was away?" He sounded more confused than worried.

Simon Edwards stepped forward, clearing his throat. "Good morning, sir. I apologize for the confusion. We've had a gas leak, and your pilot said he was coming to the terminal."

"Yes, yes, well, I told him to come here instead. I'm late for an appointment. I pay for this hangar, and this talk about avoiding a gas leak seemed like overkill."

"I'm afraid your arrival has caught us by surprise, sir."

"I know. I'm behind schedule, I am. Between you and me, the new medication gives me the runs. Thought I'd come over for a checkup."

The policemen all looked at each other. Edwards cringed. "Very good, sir."

"Sir," the Kent chief inspector said, stepping forward. "I need to ask you to stay onboard for another half hour or so."

Teabing looked annoyed as he limped down the stairs. "I'm afraid that is impossible. I have a doctor's appointment." He reached the tarmac. "I cannot afford to miss it." The chief inspector moved to block Teabing from leaving the plane. "I am here on orders from the French Judicial Police. They claim you are transporting wanted criminals on this plane."

Teabing stared at the chief inspector for a moment, and then burst out laughing. "Is this one of those prank shows? Very funny!"

The chief inspector didn't react. "This is serious, sir. The French police claim you may also have a hostage onboard."

Teabing's manservant Rémy appeared in the doorway at the top of the stairs. "I feel like a hostage working for Sir Leigh, but he assures me I am free to leave." Rémy checked his watch. "Master, we really are running late." He nodded toward the Jaguar stretch limousine in the far corner of the hangar. The huge car was black with tinted windows and whitewall tires. "I'll get the car." Rémy started down the stairs.

"I'm afraid we can't let you leave," the chief inspector said. "Please go back to your plane. Both of you. Officers from the French police will be landing soon." Teabing then looked at Simon Edwards. "Simon, this is ridiculous! We don't have anyone else on board. Just the usual—Rémy, our pilot, and me. Maybe you could help? Go look inside and see that the plane is empty." Edwards knew he was stuck. "Yes, sir. I can look."

"The hell you will!" the Kent chief inspector said, knowing enough about private airfields to think Simon Edwards might lie about who was on the plane to keep Teabing's business at Biggin Hill. "I'll look myself." Teabing shook his head. "No, you won't, Inspector. This is private property, and until you have a search warrant, you will stay off my plane. I'm giving you a reasonable option here. Mr. Edwards can do the inspection."

"No deal."

Teabing's face turned cold. "Inspector, I don't have time for your games. I'm late, and I'm leaving. If stopping me is that important to you, you'll have to shoot me." With that, Teabing and Rémy walked around the chief inspector and headed across the hangar toward the parked limousine.

The Kent chief inspector just felt disgusted by Leigh Teabing as the man limped around him in defiance. Rich people always thought they were above the law.

They are not. The chief inspector turned and aimed at Teabing's back. "Stop! I'll shoot!"

"Go ahead," Teabing said without stopping or looking back. "My lawyers will destroy you. And if you dare go on my plane without a warrant, you'll regret it."

The chief inspector was used to power plays and wasn't impressed. Teabing was right that the police needed a warrant to go on his jet. But because the flight started in France, and because the powerful Bezu Fache had given his approval, the Kent chief inspector thought his career would be better if he found out what Teabing was so eager to hide on the plane.

"Stop them," the inspector ordered. "I'm searching the plane." His men ran over, guns raised, and blocked Teabing and his servant from getting to the limousine.

Now Teabing turned. "Inspector, this is your last warning. Don't even think about going on that plane. You'll regret it."

Ignoring the threat, the chief inspector gripped his gun and walked up the plane's steps. At the entrance, he looked inside. After a moment, he stepped into the cabin. What the devil?

Except for the scared pilot in the cockpit, the plane was empty. Completely without anyone. He quickly checked the bathroom, the seats, and the luggage areas, but the inspector found no one hiding... let alone several people. What the hell was Bezu Fache thinking? It seemed Leigh Teabing was telling the truth. The Kent chief inspector stood alone in the empty cabin and swallowed hard. Shit. His face red, he stepped back onto the steps, looking across the hangar at Leigh Teabing and his servant, who were now held at gunpoint near the limousine. "Let them go," the inspector ordered.

"We got a bad tip."

Teabing's eyes were threatening even from across the hangar. "You'll be hearing from my lawyers. And remember, the French police can't be trusted."

With that, Teabing's servant opened the back door of the long limousine and helped his disabled master into the back seat. Then the servant walked to the front of the car, got behind the wheel, and started the engine. Policemen scattered as the Jaguar sped out of the hangar.

"Well done, my friend," Teabing said from the back seat as the limousine sped away from the airport. He looked at the dimly lit front part of the spacious interior.

"Everyone comfortable?"

Langdon nodded weakly. He and Sophie were still crouched on the floor beside the tied and gagged albino.

A few moments earlier, as the Hawker taxied into the empty hangar, Rémy had opened the door as the plane jerked to a stop halfway through its turn. With the police closing in, Langdon and Sophie dragged the monk down the steps to the ground and out of sight behind the limousine. Then the jet engines had roared again, turning the plane around as the police cars skidded into the hangar.

Now, as the limousine raced toward Kent, Langdon and Sophie climbed toward the back of the long limo, leaving the monk tied up on the floor. They sat on the long seat facing Teabing. The Brit gave them both a playful smile and opened the cabinet on the limo's bar. "Can I offer you a drink? Some snacks? Chips? Nuts? Seltzer?"

Sophie and Langdon both shook their heads.

Teabing grinned and closed the bar. "So, about this knight's tomb..."
