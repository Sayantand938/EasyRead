The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 61

Princess Sophie.

Sophie felt empty as she heard Teabing's crutches clicking away down the hall. Numb, she turned to face Langdon in the empty ballroom. He was already shaking his head, as if he knew what she was thinking.

"No, Sophie," he whispered, his eyes gentle. "I thought the same thing when I learned your grandfather was in the Priory, and you said he wanted to tell you a family secret. But it can't be." Langdon paused. "Saunière isn't a Merovingian name." Sophie didn't know if she was relieved or disappointed. Earlier, Langdon had asked a strange question about Sophie's mother's maiden name: Chauvel. Now the question made sense.

"What about Chauvel?" she asked, worried.

He shook his head again. "I'm sorry. I know that would have answered some questions. Only two direct Merovingian lines are left. Their last names are Plantard and Saint-Clair. Both families are in hiding, likely protected by the Priory."

Sophie repeated the names to herself and then shook her head. No one in her family was named Plantard or Saint-Clair. A tired feeling washed over her. She realized she was no closer to understanding what her grandfather wanted to tell her than she had been at the Louvre. Sophie wished her grandfather hadn't mentioned her family. He had reopened old wounds that hurt as much as ever. They are dead, Sophie. They aren't coming back. She thought of her mother singing her to sleep, her father carrying her on his shoulders, and her grandmother and younger brother smiling at her with their bright green eyes. All of that was gone. And all she had left was her grandfather. And now he's gone too. I am alone.

Sophie turned quietly back to The Last Supper and looked at Mary Magdalene's long red hair and calm eyes. There was something in her face that showed the loss of someone loved. Sophie felt it too.

"Robert?" she said softly.

He came closer.

"I know Leigh said the Grail story is all around us, but tonight is the first time I've ever heard any of this."

Langdon looked like he wanted to comfort her with a hand on her shoulder, but he stopped himself.

"You've heard her story before, Sophie. Everyone has. We just don't realize it when we hear it."

"I don't understand."

"The Grail story is everywhere, but it's hidden. When the Church made it forbidden to talk about Mary Magdalene, who they didn't like, her story and importance had to be shared in secret ways... ways that used symbols and metaphors."

"Of course. The arts."

Langdon pointed to The Last Supper. "A perfect example. Some of today's most famous art, books, and music secretly tell the story of Mary Magdalene and Jesus." Langdon quickly told her about works by Da Vinci, Botticelli, Poussin, Bernini, Mozart, and Victor Hugo that all hinted at the quest to bring back respect for the sacred feminine. Popular stories like Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, King Arthur, and Sleeping Beauty were Grail stories in disguise. Victor Hugo's Hunchback of Notre Dame and Mozart's Magic Flute were full of Masonic symbols and Grail secrets.

"Once you understand the Holy Grail," Langdon said, "you see it everywhere. Paintings. Music. Books. Even in cartoons, theme parks, and popular movies."

Langdon held up his Mickey Mouse watch and told her that Walt Disney had quietly dedicated his life to passing on the Grail story to future generations. Throughout his life, Disney had been called "the Modern-Day Leonardo da Vinci." Both men were ahead of their time, uniquely talented artists, members of secret groups, and, most importantly, big jokers. Like Leonardo, Walt Disney loved putting hidden messages and symbols in his art. For someone who studies symbols, watching an early Disney movie was like being hit by a flood of hints and metaphors.

Most of Disney's hidden messages were about religion, old myths, and stories of the mistreated goddess. It was no accident that Disney retold tales like Cinderella, Sleeping Beauty, and Snow White — all of which were about the imprisonment of the sacred feminine. You didn't need to know a lot about symbols to see that Snow White—a princess who fell from grace after eating a poisoned apple—was a clear reference to Eve's downfall in the Garden of Eden. Or that Sleeping Beauty's Princess Aurora—called "Rose" and hidden in the forest to protect her from the evil witch—was the Grail story for kids. Despite its corporate image, Disney still had clever, playful employees, and their artists still enjoyed adding hidden symbols to Disney products. Langdon would never forget one of his students bringing in a Lion King DVD and pausing it to show a frame where the word SEX was clearly visible, spelled out by dust particles over Simba's head. Langdon thought this was probably just a childish prank by a cartoonist rather than a smart reference to pagan human sexuality, but he had learned not to underestimate Disney's understanding of symbols. The Little Mermaid was a beautiful collection of spiritual symbols so clearly related to the goddess that it couldn't be a coincidence. When Langdon first saw The Little Mermaid, he gasped when he noticed that the painting in Ariel's underwater home was Georges de la Tour's The Penitent Magdalene — a famous tribute to Mary Magdalene — fitting decor since the movie was a collection of obvious symbolic references to the lost importance of Isis, Eve, Pisces the fish goddess, and, often, Mary Magdalene. The Little Mermaid's name, Ariel, was strongly connected to the sacred feminine and, in the Book of Isaiah, meant "the Holy City under attack." And the Little Mermaid's flowing red hair was certainly not a coincidence.

The clicking of Teabing's crutches came closer in the hallway, his pace faster than usual. When their host entered the study, he looked stern.

"You need to explain, Robert," he said coldly. "You haven't been honest with me."
