The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 87

The fireplace in Château Villette's drawing room was cold, but Collet still paced in front of it as he read the faxes from Interpol.

Not what he expected at all.

According to official records, André Vernet was a perfect citizen. No police record—not even a parking ticket. He went to prep school and the Sorbonne, earning a degree in international finance with honors. Interpol said Vernet's name sometimes appeared in the newspapers, but always positively. Apparently, he had helped design the security measures that kept the Depository Bank of Zurich a leader in electronic security. Vernet's credit card records showed he liked art books, expensive wine, and classical CDs—mostly Brahms—which he enjoyed on a very high-quality stereo system he had bought years ago.

Nothing, Collet sighed.

The only warning sign from Interpol tonight was a set of fingerprints that belonged to Teabing's servant. The chief PTS examiner was reading the report in a comfortable chair across the room.

Collet looked over. "Anything?"

The examiner shrugged. "Prints belong to Rémy Legaludec. Wanted for minor crimes. Nothing serious. He was kicked out of university for rewiring phone jacks to get free service... later, he did some petty theft, breaking and entering, and skipped out on a hospital bill for an emergency tracheotomy." He looked up, chuckling. "Peanut allergy." Collet nodded, remembering a police investigation into a restaurant that hadn't noted on its menu that its chili contained peanut oil. A customer died of anaphylactic shock at the table after one bite.

"Legaludec probably lives here to avoid getting caught." The examiner looked amused.

"His lucky night."

Collet sighed. "Okay, send this to Captain Fache." The examiner left as another PTS agent rushed in. "Lieutenant! We found something in the barn."

From the agent's worried face, Collet guessed, "A body?"

"No, sir. Something more..." He paused. "Unexpected." Rubbing his eyes, Collet followed the agent to the barn. Inside the musty, large space, the agent pointed to the center, where a wooden ladder reached up to the hayloft, leaning against its edge.

"That ladder wasn't there before," Collet said.

"No, sir. I put it there. We were dusting for prints near the Rolls when I saw the ladder on the floor. I almost ignored it, but the rungs were worn and muddy. This ladder is used often. The hayloft's height matched the ladder, so I put it up and climbed to take a look."

Collet looked up the ladder to the high hayloft. Someone goes up there often? From below, the loft looked like an empty platform, but most of it was hidden from view.

A senior PTS agent appeared at the top of the ladder, looking down. "You need to see this, Lieutenant," he said, waving Collet up with a gloved hand. Nodding, Collet walked to the ladder and grabbed the bottom rungs. The ladder was old and got narrower as he climbed. Near the top, Collet almost slipped on a thin rung. The barn spun below him. Now alert, he kept going, reaching the top. The agent reached out, offering his wrist. Collet grabbed it and stepped onto the platform.

"It's over there," the PTS agent said, pointing into the clean loft. "Only one set of prints up here. We'll know who it is soon."

Collet squinted at the far wall in the dim light. What the hell? Against the wall was a complete computer setup—two CPUs, a flat-screen monitor with speakers, many hard drives, and a sound console with its own power supply.

Why would anyone work up here? Collet went toward the equipment. "Have you checked the system?"

"It's a listening post."

Collet spun around. "Surveillance?"

The agent nodded. "Very advanced surveillance." He pointed to a long table covered with electronic parts, manuals, tools, wires, soldering irons, and other electronic components. "Someone really knows what they're doing. A lot of this equipment is as good as our own. Tiny microphones, photoelectric recharging cells, high-capacity memory chips. He's even got some of those new nano drives."

Collet was impressed.

"Here's a complete system," the agent said, handing Collet a device about the size of a pocket calculator. A foot-long wire hung from it, with a stamp-sized piece of thin foil at the end. "The base is a high-capacity hard disk audio recording system with a rechargeable battery. That foil strip is both a microphone and a photoelectric recharging cell."

Collet knew them well. These foil microphones that used photocells had been a major innovation a few years ago. Now, a hard disk recorder could be placed behind a lamp, for example, with its foil microphone shaped to fit the lamp's base and colored to match. If the microphone got a few hours of sunlight each day, the photocells would keep recharging the system. Bugs like this could listen forever.

"How does it send the information?" Collet asked.

The agent pointed to an insulated wire running from the back of the computer, up the wall, and through a hole in the barn roof. "Simple radio wave. Small antenna on the roof." Collet knew these recording systems were usually placed in offices. They were voice-activated to save disk space, and recorded bits of conversation during the day, sending compressed audio files at night to avoid being detected. After sending the files, the hard drive erased itself and got ready to do it again the next day.

Collet now looked at a shelf holding hundreds of audio cassettes, all labeled with dates and numbers. Someone has been very busy. He turned back to the agent. "Do you know who's being bugged?"

"Well, Lieutenant," the agent said, walking to the computer and starting a program. "It's the strangest thing...."
