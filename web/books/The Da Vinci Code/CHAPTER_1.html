The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 1

Robert Langdon woke up slowly.

A telephone was ringing in the dark—a thin, strange ring. He reached for the lamp and turned it on. Looking around, he saw a fancy bedroom with old-style furniture, painted walls, and a huge bed. Where am I?

The robe hanging on the bed had the letters: HOTEL RITZ PARIS. Slowly, he started to remember.

Langdon answered the phone. "Hello?"

"Monsieur Langdon?" a man said. "I hope I didn't wake you?" Confused, Langdon looked at the clock. It was 12:32 A.M. He had only been asleep for an hour, but he felt exhausted.

"This is the front desk, sir. Sorry to bother you, but someone is here to see you. He says it's important."

Langdon still felt unclear. A visitor? He saw a crumpled flyer on the table.

THE AMERICAN UNIVERSITY OF PARIS is proud to present AN EVENING WITH ROBERT LANGDON PROFESSOR OF RELIGIOUS SYMBOLOGY, HARVARD UNIVERSITY

Langdon groaned. Tonight's lecture—a slide show about pagan symbols hidden in the stones of Chartres Cathedral—had probably bothered some conservative people in the audience. Most likely, some religious expert had followed him home to start an argument.

"I'm sorry," Langdon said, "but I'm very tired and—"

"Mais, monsieur," the concierge insisted, lowering his voice to an urgent whisper. "Your guest is an important man."

Langdon didn't doubt it. His books on religious paintings and cult symbols had made him a reluctant celebrity in the art world, and last year Langdon's fame had increased greatly after he was involved in a well-known event at the Vatican. Since then, the endless stream of self-important historians and art lovers arriving at his door seemed never-ending.

"If you would be so kind," Langdon said, trying to remain polite, "could you take the man's name and number, and tell him I'll try to call him before I leave Paris on Tuesday? Thank you." He hung up before the concierge could complain.

Sitting up now, Langdon frowned at his bedside Guest Relations Handbook, whose cover read: SLEEP LIKE A BABY IN THE CITY OF LIGHTS. SLUMBER AT THE PARIS RITZ. He turned and gazed tiredly into the full-length mirror across the room. The man staring back at him was a stranger—disheveled and weary.

You need a vacation, Robert.

The past year had been hard on him, but he didn't like seeing the proof in the mirror. His usually bright blue eyes looked tired and dull tonight. Dark stubble covered his strong jaw and dimpled chin. The gray around his temples was spreading, going further into his thick black hair. His female colleagues said the gray made him look more attractive in an intellectual way, but Langdon knew better.

If Boston Magazine could see me now.

Last month, to Langdon's dismay, Boston Magazine had named him one of the ten most interesting people in Boston—a strange honor that led to endless jokes from his Harvard colleagues. Tonight, three thousand miles from home, the award had come back to bother him at the lecture he was giving.

"Ladies and gentlemen..." the hostess announced to a packed room at the American University of Paris's Pavilion Dauphine, "Our guest tonight needs no introduction. He is the author of many books: The Symbology of Secret Sects, The Art of the Illuminati, The Lost Language of Ideograms, and when I say he wrote the book on Religious Iconology, I mean it. Many of you use his textbooks in class."

The students in the crowd nodded eagerly.

"I was going to introduce him by sharing his impressive resume. However..." She looked playfully at Langdon, who was sitting onstage. "Someone in the audience has just given me a much more... intriguing introduction."

She held up a copy of Boston Magazine.

Langdon cringed. Where did she get that?

The hostess started reading parts of the silly article, and Langdon felt himself sinking in his chair. Thirty seconds later, the crowd was smiling, and the woman didn't seem to want to stop. "And Mr. Langdon's refusal to talk about his unusual role in last year's Vatican meeting certainly makes him more intriguing." The hostess teased the crowd. "Would you like to hear more?"

The crowd applauded.

Somebody stop her, Langdon begged as she dove back into the article.

"Although Professor Langdon might not be considered classically handsome like some of our younger winners, this forty-something academic has plenty of scholarly charm. His captivating presence is highlighted by an unusually low, baritone voice, which his female students describe as 'chocolate for the ears.'"

The hall burst into laughter.

Langdon forced a strained smile. He knew what was coming—some silly line about

"Harrison Ford in Harris tweed"—and because he had thought it was finally safe to wear his Harris tweed and Burberry turtleneck this evening, he decided to act.

"Thank you, Monique," Langdon said, standing up too soon and guiding her away from the podium. "Boston Magazine clearly has a talent for fiction." He turned to the audience with an embarrassed sigh. "And if I find out who gave them that article, I'll have you deported."

The crowd laughed.

"Well, folks, as you all know, I'm here tonight to talk about the power of symbols..."

The ringing of Langdon's hotel phone broke the silence again.

He answered the phone with a groan. "Yes?"

It was the concierge, just as he thought. "Mr. Langdon, I'm very sorry to bother you again. I'm calling to let you know that your guest is on his way to your room. I wanted to warn you." Langdon was now fully awake. "You sent someone to my room?"

"I'm sorry, sir, but a man like this... I couldn't stop him."

"Who is he?"

But the concierge had hung up.

Almost at once, someone banged hard on Langdon's door.

Feeling unsure, Langdon got out of bed, his toes sinking into the soft carpet. He put on the hotel bathrobe and went to the door. "Who is it?"

"Mr. Langdon? I need to talk to you." The man's English had an accent—a harsh, commanding tone. "My name is Lieutenant Jerome Collet. Direction Centrale Police Judiciaire." Langdon stopped. The Judicial Police? The DCPJ was like the U.S. FBI. Leaving the chain on, Langdon opened the door a crack. The face looking back at him was pale and gaunt. The man was very thin, wearing a blue uniform that looked official.

"May I come in?" the agent asked.

Langdon paused, feeling uneasy as the stranger's pale eyes examined him. "What's this about?"

"My captain needs your help with something personal."

"Now?" Langdon asked, surprised. "It's after midnight."

"Are you the one who was supposed to meet with the Louvre's curator tonight?" Langdon felt a sudden worry. He and the respected curator Jacques Saunière had planned to have drinks after Langdon's talk, but Saunière never arrived. "Yes. How did you know?"

"We saw your name in his schedule."

"I hope everything is okay?"

The agent sighed heavily and slipped a Polaroid picture through the small opening in the door. When Langdon saw it, his body froze.

"This picture was taken less than an hour ago. Inside the Louvre." As Langdon looked at the strange image, his initial disgust and shock turned into anger. "Who would do this?"

"We were hoping you could help us figure that out, since you know about symbols and were meeting with him."

Langdon stared at the picture, his horror now mixed with fear. The image was awful and very strange, giving him an uneasy feeling of déjà vu. Just over a year ago, Langdon had received a picture of a dead body and a similar request for help. A day later, he almost died in Vatican City. This photo was completely different, but something about the situation felt disturbingly familiar.

The agent looked at his watch. "My captain is waiting, sir." Langdon barely heard him. He was still staring at the picture. "This symbol here, and the way his body is so strangely..."

"In place?" the agent asked.

Langdon nodded, feeling a chill as he looked up. "I can't imagine who would do this to someone." The agent looked grim. "You don't understand, Mr. Langdon. What you see in this photograph..." He paused. "Monsieur Saunière did that to himself."
