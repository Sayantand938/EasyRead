The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 46

Silas lay still on the canvas mat in his room, letting the whip marks on his back dry in the air. Tonight's second session with the Discipline had left him lightheaded and weak. He still wore the cilice belt, and he could feel blood running down his inner thigh. But he couldn't justify taking the strap off.

I have failed the Church.

Even worse, I have failed the bishop.

Tonight was meant to be Bishop Aringarosa's salvation. Five months ago, the bishop had returned from a meeting at the Vatican Observatory, where he had learned something that changed him deeply. Aringarosa was depressed for weeks before finally sharing the news with Silas.

"But this is impossible!" Silas had exclaimed. "I can't believe it!"

"It is true," Aringarosa said. "Unbelievable, but true. In only six months." The bishop's words scared Silas. He prayed for help, and even then, his trust in God and The Way never faded. Only a month later, things cleared up miraculously, and hope appeared.

Divine intervention, Aringarosa had called it.

The bishop seemed hopeful for the first time. "Silas," he whispered, "God has given us a chance to protect The Way. Our fight, like all fights, will require sacrifice. Will you be a soldier of God?"

Silas knelt before Bishop Aringarosa—the man who had given him a new life—and said, "I am a lamb of God. Guide me as you wish." When Aringarosa explained the opportunity, Silas knew it could only be God's work. Amazing fate! Aringarosa put Silas in touch with the man who had suggested the plan—a man who called himself the Teacher. Although the Teacher and Silas never met in person, each time they spoke on the phone, Silas was amazed by the Teacher's deep faith and great power. The Teacher seemed to know everything, with eyes and ears everywhere. Silas didn't know how the Teacher got his information, but Aringarosa trusted the Teacher greatly and told Silas to do the same. "Do as the Teacher tells you," the bishop told Silas. "And we will win." Win. Silas now stared at the bare floor, fearing they had lost. The Teacher had been tricked. The keystone was a clever trap. And with the trick, all hope was gone. Silas wished he could call Bishop Aringarosa and warn him, but the Teacher had cut off all direct contact tonight. For our safety.

Finally, overcoming great fear, Silas crawled to his feet and found his robe on the floor. He took his cell phone from the pocket. Hanging his head in shame, he dialed.

"Teacher," he whispered, "we've lost everything." Silas truthfully told the man how he had been tricked.

"You lose your faith too easily," the Teacher replied. "I have just received news. Very unexpected and welcome. The secret lives. Jacques Saunière passed on information before he died. I will call you soon. Our work tonight is not yet finished."
