The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 76

Langdon could tell Sophie was still disturbed from describing her experience of Hieros Gamos. He was amazed to have heard it. Not only had Sophie witnessed the ritual, but her own grandfather had been the leader... the Grand Master of the Priory of Sion. It was impressive. Da Vinci, Botticelli, Isaac Newton, Victor Hugo, Jean Cocteau... Jacques Saunière.

"I don't know what else I can tell you," Langdon said softly. Sophie's eyes were a deep green now, tearful. "He raised me like his own daughter." Langdon now understood the emotion that had been growing in her eyes as they spoke. It was regret. Distant and deep. Sophie Neveu had rejected her grandfather and was now seeing him in a completely different light.

Outside, dawn was coming fast, its red glow spreading from the right side of the plane. The earth was still black beneath them.

"Food, my dears?" Teabing rejoined them with a flourish, presenting several cans of Coke and a box of old crackers. He apologized for the limited food as he handed it out. "Our friend the monk isn't talking yet," he said, "but give him time." He bit into a cracker and eyed the poem. "So, my lovely, any progress?" He looked at Sophie. "What is your grandfather trying to tell us here? Where is this headstone? This headstone praised by Templars." Sophie shook her head and remained silent.

While Teabing again studied the verse, Langdon opened a Coke and turned to the window, his thoughts full of images of secret rituals and unbroken codes. A headstone praised by Templars is the key. He took a long sip from the can. A headstone praised by Templars. The cola was warm. The fading night seemed to disappear quickly, and as Langdon watched the change, he saw a shimmering ocean stretch out beneath them. The English Channel. It wouldn't be long now.

Langdon hoped the daylight would also bring understanding, but the brighter it became outside, the further he felt from the truth. He heard the rhythms of poetry and chanting, Hieros Gamos and sacred rites, blending with the rumble of the jet. A headstone praised by Templars.

The plane was over land again when an idea struck him. Langdon set down his empty can of Coke hard. "You won't believe this," he said, turning to the others. "The Templar headstone—I figured it out."

Teabing's eyes widened. "You know where the headstone is?" Langdon smiled. "Not where it is. What it is." Sophie leaned in to hear.

"I think the headstone refers to an actual stone head," Langdon explained, enjoying the excitement of academic discovery. "Not a grave marker."

"A stone head?" Teabing asked, surprised.

Sophie looked just as confused.

"Leigh," Langdon said, turning, "during the Inquisition, the Church accused the Knights Templar of many wrongdoings, right?"

"Correct. They made up all sorts of charges. Sexual sins, disrespecting the cross, devil worship—quite a list."

"And on that list was the worship of fake idols, right? Specifically, the Church said the Templars secretly held ceremonies where they prayed to a carved stone head... the pagan god—"

"Baphomet!" Teabing exclaimed. "My heavens, Robert, you're right! A headstone praised by Templars!"

Langdon quickly told Sophie that Baphomet was a pagan god of fertility, linked to the creative power of reproduction. Baphomet's head looked like a ram or goat, a common symbol of having children. The Templars honored Baphomet by circling a stone copy of his head and chanting prayers.

"Baphomet," Teabing chuckled. "The ceremony celebrated the creative magic of sex, but Pope Clement convinced everyone that Baphomet's head was actually the devil's. The Pope used the head of Baphomet as the key piece of evidence against the Templars." Langdon agreed. The modern idea of a horned devil called Satan likely came from Baphomet and the Church's attempts to turn the horned fertility god into a symbol of evil. The Church clearly succeeded, but not completely. Traditional American Thanksgiving tables still have pagan, horned fertility symbols. The cornucopia, or "horn of plenty," honors Baphomet's fertility and comes from the story of Zeus being fed by a goat whose horn broke off and magically filled with fruit. Baphomet also appears in group photos when someone jokingly puts two fingers behind a friend's head to make horns. Few of these pranksters know that they're actually suggesting their victim has a high sperm count.

"Yes, yes," Teabing said excitedly. "Baphomet must be what the poem is talking about. A headstone praised by Templars."

"Okay," Sophie said, "but if Baphomet is the headstone praised by Templars, we have a new problem." She pointed to the dials on the cryptex. "Baphomet has eight letters. We only have room for five."

Teabing grinned broadly. "My dear, this is where the Atbash code becomes useful."
