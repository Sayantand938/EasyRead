The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 21

The Mona Lisa.

For a moment, standing in the exit stairwell, Sophie forgot about leaving the Louvre. Her shock at the anagram was as strong as her embarrassment at not solving it herself. Sophie's skill with complicated codes had made her miss simple word games, but she knew she should have seen it. She knew anagrams well, especially in English.

When she was young, her grandfather often used anagrams to improve her English spelling. Once, he wrote the word "planets" and told Sophie that sixty-two other English words of different lengths could be made using those same letters. Sophie spent three days with a dictionary until she found them all.

"I can't believe," Langdon said, looking at the printout, "how your grandfather made such a complex anagram so quickly before he died."

Sophie knew the answer, and it made her feel even worse. I should have seen this!

She remembered that her grandfather—a wordplay fan and art lover—had enjoyed making anagrams of famous artworks when he was young. One of his anagrams even caused trouble when Sophie was little. While being interviewed by an American art magazine, Saunière had shared his dislike for the modern Cubist movement by saying that Picasso's painting Les Demoiselles d'Avignon was a perfect anagram of vile meaningless doodles. Picasso's fans were not happy.

"My grandfather probably made this Mona Lisa anagram a long time ago," Sophie said, looking at Langdon. And tonight he had to use it as a quick code. Her grandfather's message was clear and unsettling.

Leonardo da Vinci!

The Mona Lisa!

Why his last words to her mentioned the famous painting, Sophie didn't know, but she could only imagine one reason. A worrying one.

Those weren't his last words....

Was she meant to see the Mona Lisa? Had her grandfather left her a message there? It seemed quite possible. After all, the famous painting was in the Salle des Etats—a private room only reached from the Grand Gallery. Sophie now saw that the doors to this room were only twenty meters from where her grandfather had died.

He could easily have seen the Mona Lisa before he died.

Sophie looked back at the emergency stairs, feeling unsure. She knew she should get Langdon out of the museum at once, but something told her not to. Remembering her first visit to the Denon Wing as a child, Sophie thought that if her grandfather had a secret for her, Da Vinci's Mona Lisa was the perfect place to share it.

"She's just a little further," her grandfather had whispered, holding Sophie's small hand as he led her through the empty museum after hours.

Sophie was six. She felt small as she looked up at the huge ceilings and down at the dizzying floor. The empty museum scared her, but she didn't want her grandfather to know. She set her jaw and let go of his hand.

"Up ahead is the Salle des Etats," her grandfather said as they neared the Louvre's most famous room. Despite his excitement, Sophie wanted to go home. She had seen pictures of the Mona Lisa and didn't like it. She didn't get why everyone cared so much.

"C'est ennuyeux," Sophie mumbled.

"Boring," he corrected. "French at school. English at home."

"The Louvre is not my home!" she argued.

He chuckled wearily. "You're right. Let's speak English just for kicks." Sophie frowned and kept walking. As they entered the Salle des Etats, her eyes darted around the small room and stopped on the main attraction—the center of the right wall, where a single painting hung behind a protective Plexiglas screen. Her grandfather stopped at the entrance and pointed to the painting.

"Go ahead, Sophie. Not many get to see her alone." Trying to calm her nerves, Sophie slowly walked across the room. After all she'd heard about the Mona Lisa, she felt like she was approaching someone royal. Reaching the Plexiglas, Sophie held her breath and looked up, taking it all in. Sophie wasn't sure what she expected to feel, but it definitely wasn't this. No sudden shock of amazement. No moment of awe. The famous face looked just like it did in books. She stood silently for what seemed like forever, waiting for something to happen.

"So, what do you think?" her grandfather whispered, coming up behind her. "Beautiful, right?"

"She's too small."

Saunière smiled. "You're small, and you're beautiful."

I am not beautiful, she thought. Sophie hated her red hair and freckles, and she was taller than all the boys in her class. She looked back at the Mona Lisa and shook her head. "She's even worse than in the books. Her face is... brumeux."

"Foggy," her grandfather corrected.

"Foggy," Sophie repeated, knowing the conversation wouldn't go on until she said the new word.

"That's called the sfumato style," he told her, "and it's very difficult to do. Leonardo da Vinci was better at it than anyone."

Sophie still didn't like the painting. "She looks like she knows something... like when kids at school have a secret."

Her grandfather laughed. "That's part of why she is so famous. People like to guess why she is smiling."

"Do you know why she's smiling?"

"Maybe." Her grandfather winked. "Someday I'll tell you all about it." Sophie stamped her foot. "I told you I don't like secrets!"

"Princess," he smiled. "Life is full of secrets. You can't learn them all at once."

"I'm going back up," Sophie said, her voice echoing in the stairwell.

"To the Mona Lisa?" Langdon stepped back. "Now?" Sophie thought about the risk. "I'm not a murder suspect. I'll take my chances. I need to understand what my grandfather was trying to tell me."

"What about the embassy?"

Sophie felt bad for making Langdon a fugitive and then leaving him, but she didn't see any other way. She pointed down the stairs to a metal door. "Go through that door, and follow the lit exit signs. My grandfather used to bring me down here. The signs will lead you to a security gate. It only lets you go one way and opens outward." She handed Langdon her car keys. "Mine is the red SmartCar in the employee lot, right outside this wall. Do you know how to get to the embassy?"

Langdon nodded, looking at the keys in his hand.

"Listen," Sophie said, her voice softening. "I think my grandfather may have left me a message at the Mona Lisa—some kind of clue about who killed him, or why I'm in danger." Or what happened to my family. "I have to go see."

"But if he wanted to tell you why you were in danger, why wouldn't he just write it on the floor where he died? Why this complicated game?"

"Whatever my grandfather was trying to tell me, I don't think he wanted anyone else to hear it. Not even the police." Clearly, her grandfather had done everything he could to send a private message directly to her. He had written it in code, included her secret initials, and told her to find Robert Langdon—a smart move, considering the American symbologist had figured out his code. "As strange as it may sound," Sophie said, "I think he wants me to get to the Mona Lisa before anyone else does."

"I'll come."

"No! We don't know how long the Grand Gallery will stay empty. You have to go." Langdon seemed unsure, as if his own curiosity was about to overpower his good sense and pull him back into Fache's hands.

"Go. Now." Sophie gave him a grateful smile. "I'll see you at the embassy, Mr. Langdon." Langdon looked unhappy. "I'll meet you there on one condition," he replied, his voice firm. She paused, surprised. "What's that?"

"That you stop calling me Mr. Langdon."

Sophie saw the faint hint of a smile forming on Langdon's face, and she smiled back. "Good luck, Robert."

When Langdon reached the bottom of the stairs, he smelled linseed oil and plaster dust. Ahead, a lit SORTIE/EXIT sign showed an arrow pointing down a long corridor.

Langdon stepped into the hallway.

To the right was a dark restoration studio filled with statues being fixed. To the left, Langdon saw studios like Harvard art classrooms—rows of easels, paintings, palettes, framing tools—an art production line. Walking down the hall, Langdon felt like he might wake up in his bed in Cambridge. The whole night felt like a strange dream. I'm about to run out of the Louvre... as a fugitive.

Saunière's clever anagram was still in his thoughts, and Langdon wondered what Sophie would find at the Mona Lisa... if anything. She seemed sure her grandfather wanted her to see the famous painting again. Although this made sense, Langdon was bothered by a confusing problem.

P.S. Find Robert Langdon.

Saunière had written Langdon's name on the floor, telling Sophie to find him. But why?

Just so Langdon could help her solve an anagram?

That seemed unlikely.

Saunière had no reason to think Langdon was good at anagrams. We've never even met. More importantly, Sophie said she should have solved the anagram herself. She found the Fibonacci sequence, and she probably would have figured out the message with a little more time, without Langdon's help. Sophie was supposed to solve that anagram on her own. Langdon was now more sure of this, but the conclusion left a big hole in Saunière's actions.

Why me? Langdon wondered, walking down the hall. Why did Saunière want his granddaughter to find me before he died? What does Saunière think I know?

Suddenly, Langdon stopped. Eyes wide, he took out the computer printout from his pocket. He looked at the last line of Saunière's message. P.S. Find Robert Langdon.

He stared at two letters.

P.S.

In that instant, Langdon felt Saunière's puzzling symbolism become clear. Like a clap of thunder, all his knowledge of symbols and history suddenly made sense. Everything Jacques Saunière had done tonight was suddenly perfectly clear.

Langdon's thoughts raced as he tried to understand what it all meant. Turning quickly, he stared back the way he had come.

Is there time?

He knew it didn't matter.

Without hesitation, Langdon sprinted back toward the stairs.
