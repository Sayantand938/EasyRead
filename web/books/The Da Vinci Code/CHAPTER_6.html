The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 6

After squeezing under the security gate, Robert Langdon found himself just inside the Grand Gallery. He stared into what seemed like a long, deep canyon. On each side of the gallery, bare walls rose thirty feet, disappearing into the darkness above. The reddish light from the service lights shone upward, creating an odd glow across an impressive collection of Da Vincis, Titians, and Caravaggios hanging from ceiling cables. Still lifes, religious scenes, and landscapes were displayed alongside portraits of nobles and politicians. While the Grand Gallery held the Louvre's most famous Italian art, many visitors felt the wing's most amazing feature was its famous parquet floor. Made of a striking geometric design of diagonal oak slats, the floor created a temporary optical illusion—a complex network that made visitors feel like they were floating through the gallery on a surface that changed with each step.

As Langdon's eyes started to follow the floor's design, they stopped suddenly on something unexpected lying on the floor a few feet to his left, surrounded by police tape. He turned to Fache. "Is that... a Caravaggio on the floor?"

Fache nodded without looking.

Langdon guessed the painting was worth over two million dollars, yet it was lying on the floor like a discarded poster. "What on earth is it doing on the floor!" Fache frowned, clearly unaffected. "This is a crime scene, Mr. Langdon. We haven't touched anything. The curator pulled that canvas from the wall. That's how he activated the security system." Langdon looked back at the gate, trying to imagine what had happened.

"The curator was attacked in his office, ran into the Grand Gallery, and activated the security gate by pulling that painting from the wall. The gate fell immediately, blocking all access. This is the only way in or out of this gallery."

Langdon felt confused. "So the curator trapped his attacker inside the Grand Gallery?" Fache shook his head. "The security gate separated Saunière from his attacker. The killer was locked out in the hallway and shot Saunière through this gate." Fache pointed to an orange tag hanging from one of the bars on the gate they had just passed under. "The PTS team found traces of gunfire. He fired through the bars. Saunière died in here alone." Langdon pictured the photograph of Saunière's body. They said he did that himself. Langdon looked out at the huge corridor before them. "So where is his body?" Fache straightened his cross-shaped tie clip and began to walk. "As you probably know, the Grand Gallery is quite long."

Langdon thought the exact length was about fifteen hundred feet, the length of three Washington Monuments placed end to end. Equally impressive was the corridor's width, which could easily fit two passenger trains side by side. The center of the hallway had statues or large porcelain urns, which acted as dividers and kept people moving along one wall and back up the other.

Fache was silent now, walking quickly up the right side of the corridor with his eyes straight ahead. Langdon felt almost rude for rushing past so many masterpieces without even a glance.

Not that I could see anything in this lighting, he thought.

The dim red lighting unfortunately brought back memories of Langdon's last experience with similar lighting in the Vatican Secret Archives. This was the second unsettling similarity tonight to his near-death in Rome. He thought of Vittoria again. She had been out of his dreams for months. Langdon couldn't believe Rome had been only a year ago; it felt like decades. Another life. His last message from Vittoria had been in December—a postcard saying she was going to the Java Sea to continue her research in entanglement physics... something about using satellites to track manta ray migrations. Langdon never thought a woman like Vittoria Vetra could be happy living with him at a college, but their time in Rome had awakened a longing he never knew he could feel. His lifelong love of being a bachelor and the simple freedoms it allowed had been shaken somehow... replaced by an unexpected emptiness that seemed to have grown over the past year.

They continued walking quickly, but Langdon still didn't see a body. "Jacques Saunière went this far?"

"Mr. Saunière was shot in the stomach. He died very slowly, maybe over fifteen or twenty minutes. He was clearly a very strong man." Langdon turned, shocked. "It took security fifteen minutes to get here?"

"Of course not. Louvre security responded to the alarm right away and found the Grand Gallery sealed. Through the gate, they could hear someone moving around at the far end of the hall, but they couldn't see who it was. They shouted, but there was no answer. Thinking it was a criminal, they followed procedure and called the Judicial Police. We were in position within fifteen minutes. When we arrived, we lifted the barricade enough to crawl underneath, and I sent a dozen armed officers inside. They searched the length of the gallery to trap the intruder."

"And?"

"They found no one inside. Except..." He pointed farther down the hall. "Him." Langdon looked up and followed Fache's pointing finger. At first, he thought Fache was pointing to a large marble statue in the middle of the hallway. As they walked on, though, Langdon began to see past the statue. Thirty yards down the hall, a single spotlight on a portable stand shone down on the floor, creating a bright white spot in the dark red gallery. In the center of the light, like an insect under a microscope, the curator's body lay naked on the floor.

"You saw the photograph," Fache said, "so this shouldn't be a surprise." Langdon felt a deep chill as they approached the body. Before him was one of the strangest things he had ever seen.

The pale body of Jacques Saunière lay on the floor exactly as it appeared in the photograph. As Langdon stood over the body and squinted in the bright light, he reminded himself with amazement that Saunière had spent his last minutes arranging his own body in this strange way.

Saunière looked remarkably fit for a man of his age... and all of his muscles were visible. He had taken off all of his clothes, placed them neatly on the floor, and lay down on his back in the center of the wide corridor, perfectly aligned with the length of the room. His arms and legs were spread out wide, like a child making a snow angel... or, perhaps more accurately, like a man being torn apart by some invisible force.

Just below Saunière's chest, a bloody mark showed where the bullet went in. The wound hadn't bled much, leaving only a small spot of dark blood. Saunière's left index finger was also bloody, as if dipped in the wound to make his death scene even stranger. Using his own blood as ink on his bare stomach, Saunière had drawn a simple symbol: five straight lines forming a five-pointed star. The pentacle.

The bloody star, centered on Saunière's belly button, made his body look eerie. The photo Langdon saw was scary enough, but seeing it in person made Langdon feel even more uneasy.

He did this himself.

"Mr. Langdon?" Fache's dark eyes looked at him again.

"It's a pentacle," Langdon said, his voice sounding empty in the large room. "One of the oldest symbols on earth. Used over four thousand years before Christ."

"And what does it mean?"

Langdon always hesitated when asked this. Telling someone what a symbol "meant" was like telling them how a song should make them feel—it's different for everyone. A white Ku Klux Klan hood brings up images of hate and racism in the United States, but the same costume means religious faith in Spain.

"Symbols have different meanings in different situations," Langdon said. "Mostly, the pentacle is a pagan religious symbol."

Fache nodded. "Devil worship."

"No," Langdon corrected, realizing his words could have been clearer. Now, the word pagan almost meant devil worship, which is wrong. The word comes from the Latin paganus, meaning country people. "Pagans" were simply country people who kept the old, rural religions of nature worship. The Church was so afraid of those who lived in the rural villes that the harmless word for "villager"— villain —came to mean an evil person.

"The pentacle," Langdon explained, "is an ancient symbol from before Christianity that connects to the worship of Nature. People long ago saw their world as having two parts—male and female. Their gods and goddesses worked to keep these parts balanced. Like yin and yang. When male and female were in balance, the world was peaceful. When they were not, there was chaos." Langdon pointed to Saunière's stomach. "This pentacle stands for the female part of everything—an idea religious historians call the 'sacred feminine' or the 'divine goddess.' Saunière, of all people, would know this."

"Saunière drew a goddess symbol on his stomach?"

Langdon had to admit, it seemed strange. "More specifically, the pentacle represents Venus—the goddess of love, sex, and beauty."

Fache looked at the naked man and grunted.

"Early religion was based on the natural order of Nature. The goddess Venus and the planet Venus were the same. The goddess was in the night sky and was known by many names—Venus, the Eastern Star, Ishtar, Astarte—all powerful female ideas connected to Nature and Mother Earth."

Fache looked more worried now, as if he preferred the idea of devil worship. Langdon decided not to mention the pentacle's most amazing feature—the visual origin of its link to Venus. When he was a young astronomy student, Langdon was shocked to learn that the planet Venus makes a perfect pentacle in the sky every four years. The ancients were so amazed by this that Venus and her pentacle became symbols of perfection, beauty, and the repeating nature of love and sex. To honor the magic of Venus, the Greeks used her four-year cycle to plan their Olympic Games. Today, few people know that the four-year schedule of the modern Olympics still follows the cycles of Venus. Even fewer know that the five-pointed star almost became the official Olympic symbol but was changed at the last minute—its five points replaced with five interlocking rings to better show the games' spirit of togetherness and harmony.

"Mr. Langdon," Fache said suddenly. "The pentacle must also be connected to the devil. Your American horror movies make that clear."

Langdon frowned. Thanks, Hollywood. The five-pointed star was now a common image in movies about Satanic killers, usually drawn on the wall of some Satanist's apartment along with other supposed devil symbols. Langdon was always annoyed when he saw the symbol used this way; the pentacle's true beginnings were actually quite holy.

"I assure you," Langdon said, "despite what you see in the movies, the pentacle's devil meaning is historically wrong. The original female meaning is correct, but the pentacle's symbolism has been twisted over time. In this case, through violence."

"I'm not sure I understand."

Langdon glanced at Fache's crucifix, unsure how to put it. "The Church, sir. Symbols are very strong, but the early Roman Catholic Church changed the pentacle. To get rid of pagan religions and convert people to Christianity, the Church made the pagan gods and goddesses look evil, turning their holy symbols into something bad."

"Go on."

"This is common when things are changing," Langdon continued. "A new power will take old symbols and make them look bad to erase their meaning. The pagan symbols lost to Christian symbols. Poseidon's trident became the devil's pitchfork, the wise woman's hat became a witch's hat, and Venus's pentacle became a sign of the devil." Langdon paused. "Sadly, the U.S. military has also twisted the pentacle. Now it's our main symbol of war. We paint it on our fighter jets and put it on our generals' shoulders." So much for the goddess of love and beauty.

"Interesting." Fache nodded at the spread-eagle body. "And how the body is positioned? What do you think of that?"

Langdon shrugged. "The position just makes the reference to the pentacle and the sacred feminine stronger."

Fache looked confused. "I'm sorry?"

"Repeating. Showing a symbol again is the easiest way to make its meaning stronger. Jacques Saunière put himself in the shape of a five-pointed star." If one pentacle is good, two is better. Fache looked at the five points of Saunière's arms, legs, and head as he ran a hand through his slick hair again. "Interesting analysis." He paused. "And the nudity?" He seemed disgusted as he said the word, repulsed by the sight of an old man's body. "Why did he take off his clothes?"

Good question, Langdon thought. He'd been wondering the same thing since he saw the Polaroid. His best guess was that a naked body was another sign of Venus—the goddess of sex. Modern culture has erased much of Venus's link to physical relationships, but you can still see a bit of Venus's original meaning in the word "venereal." Langdon decided not to go there.

"Mr. Fache, I can't tell you why Mr. Saunière drew that symbol on himself or put himself in this position, but I can tell you that a man like Jacques Saunière would see the pentacle as a sign of the female deity. Art historians and symbologists know that this symbol is connected to the sacred feminine."

"Fine. And the use of his own blood as ink?"

"Obviously he had nothing else to write with."

Fache was silent a moment. "Actually, I think he used blood so the police would follow certain forensic steps."

"I'm sorry?"

"Look at his left hand."

Langdon's eyes followed the curator's pale arm to his left hand but saw nothing. Unsure, he walked around the body and crouched down, now noticing in surprise that the curator was holding a large, felt-tipped marker.

"Saunière was holding it when we found him," Fache said, leaving Langdon and walking to a portable table covered with investigation tools, cables, and electronic gear. "As I told you," he said, searching the table, "we haven't touched anything. Do you know this kind of pen?"

Langdon knelt down to see the pen's label.

STYLO DE LUMIERE NOIRE.

He looked up in surprise.

The black-light pen, also called a watermark pen, was a special marker originally made for museums, art restorers, and forgery experts to put invisible marks on objects. The pen used a noncorrosive, alcohol-based ink that glowed only under black light. Now, museum workers used these pens daily to mark paintings needing repair with invisible "tick marks" on their frames.

As Langdon stood, Fache turned off the spotlight. The gallery went dark.

Briefly blinded, Langdon felt unsure. Fache's dark shape appeared, lit in bright purple. He came closer with a portable light, surrounded by a violet glow.

"As you know," Fache said, his eyes glowing in the violet light, "police use black lights to find blood and other evidence at crime scenes. So you can imagine our surprise..." He quickly pointed the light at the body.

Langdon looked down and jumped back, shocked.

His heart raced as he saw the strange sight glowing on the floor. The curator's last words were written in glowing handwriting, shining purple beside his body. As Langdon stared at the shimmering words, he felt even more lost in the mystery of the night.

Langdon read the message again and looked at Fache. "What does this mean?" Fache's eyes shone white. "That, sir, is what you are here to figure out."

Nearby, in Saunière's office, Lieutenant Collet was back at the Louvre. He was sitting at the curator's large desk, working with an audio console. Except for a strange, robot-like doll of a medieval knight staring at him from the corner of the desk, Collet felt at ease. He adjusted his headphones and checked the recording system. Everything was ready. The microphones worked perfectly, and the sound was clear.

The moment of truth, he thought.

Smiling, he closed his eyes and got comfortable, ready to enjoy the rest of the conversation being recorded in the Grand Gallery.
