The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 85

Look for the orb that should be on his grave.

Each carved knight in the Temple Church lay on his back, head on a stone block. Sophie felt cold. The poem's "orb" made her think of her grandfather's basement at night.

Hieros Gamos. The orbs.

Sophie wondered if the ritual happened here. The round room seemed perfect for such a pagan act. A stone bench went around a bare floor in the center. A theater in the round, Robert called it. She pictured the room at night, filled with masked people, chanting by torchlight, watching a "sacred communion" in the room's center.

She pushed the image away and went with Langdon and Teabing to the first knights. Teabing wanted a careful search, but Sophie felt eager and walked ahead, quickly looking at the five knights on the left.

Looking closely at these tombs, Sophie saw what was the same and different. Each knight lay on his back, but three had straight legs, while two had crossed legs. This didn't seem to matter for the missing orb. Looking at their clothes, Sophie saw that two knights wore tunics over their armor, while the other three wore long robes. Again, no help. Sophie looked at the only other clear difference—their hand positions. Two knights held swords, two prayed, and one had arms at his side. After looking at the hands for a long time, Sophie shrugged, seeing no sign of a missing orb.

Feeling the cryptex in her pocket, she looked back at Langdon and Teabing. They were moving slowly, still at the third knight, with no luck either. Not wanting to wait, she turned to the second group of knights. As she walked across the open space, she quietly said the poem she now knew by heart.

In London lies a knight a Pope buried.

His work caused a Holy anger.

You seek the orb that should be on his tomb.

It speaks of rosy flesh and a woman's womb.

When Sophie arrived at the second group of knights, she found it was like the first. They all lay in different poses, wearing armor and swords. That is, all except the tenth and last tomb.

Hurrying over to it, she stared down.

No pillow. No armor. No tunic. No sword.

"Robert? Leigh?" she called, her voice echoing in the chamber. "Something's missing over here."

Both men looked up and quickly walked toward her.

"An orb?" Teabing called excitedly. His crutches clicked quickly as he hurried across the room. "Are we missing an orb?"

"Not really," Sophie said, frowning at the tenth tomb. "We seem to be missing a whole knight." Both men arrived beside her and looked down in confusion at the tenth tomb. Instead of a knight lying out in the open, this tomb was a sealed stone casket. The casket was trapezoid-shaped, narrower at the feet, wider at the top, with a pointed lid.

"Why isn't this knight shown?" Langdon asked.

"Interesting," Teabing said, rubbing his chin. "I'd forgotten about this strange thing. It's been years since I was here."

"This coffin," Sophie said, "looks like it was made at the same time and by the same artist as the other nine tombs. So why is this knight in a coffin instead of out in the open?" Teabing shook his head. "It's one of the church's mysteries. As far as I know, no one has ever found an answer for it."

"Hello?" the altar boy said, arriving with a worried look. "Sorry to be rude, but you said you wanted to scatter ashes, and yet you seem to be looking around." Teabing glared at the boy and turned to Langdon. "Mr. Wren, it seems your family's money doesn't buy you as much time as it used to, so maybe we should get the ashes out and get on with it." Teabing turned to Sophie. "Mrs. Wren?"

Sophie played along, pulling the vellum-wrapped cryptex from her pocket.

"Now then," Teabing snapped at the boy, "could you give us some privacy?" The altar boy didn't move. He was watching Langdon closely now. "You look familiar." Teabing sighed. "Maybe because Mr. Wren comes here every year!" Or maybe, Sophie now worried, because he saw Langdon on TV at the Vatican last year.

"I've never met Mr. Wren," the altar boy said.

"You're mistaken," Langdon said politely. "I think we met briefly last year. Father Knowles didn't introduce us properly, but I recognized your face as we came in. I know this is an intrusion, but if you could give me a few more minutes, I've traveled a long way to scatter ashes among these tombs." Langdon spoke his lines with Teabing's convincing tone. The altar boy's face looked even more doubtful. "These aren't tombs."

"I'm sorry?" Langdon said.

"Of course they're tombs," Teabing said. "What are you talking about?" The altar boy shook his head. "Tombs hold bodies. These are statues. Stone tributes to real men. There are no bodies under these figures."

"This is a crypt!" Teabing said.

"Only in old history books. People used to think this was a crypt, but the 1950s renovation proved it wasn't." He turned to Langdon. "And I'm sure Mr. Wren knows that. His family discovered that." An uneasy silence filled the air.

It was broken by a door slamming in the annex.

"That must be Father Knowles," Teabing said. "Maybe you should check?" The altar boy looked unsure but walked back to the annex, leaving Langdon, Sophie, and Teabing looking at each other sadly.

"Leigh," Langdon whispered. "No bodies? What's he talking about?" Teabing looked upset. "I don't know. I always thought... this has to be the place. I don't think he knows what he's talking about. It doesn't make sense!"

"Can I see the poem again?" Langdon asked.

Sophie took the cryptex from her pocket and carefully gave it to him. Langdon unwrapped the vellum, holding the cryptex as he read the poem.

"Yes, the poem definitely talks about a tomb. Not a statue."

"Could the poem be wrong?" Teabing asked. "Could Jacques Saunière have made the same mistake I just did?"

Langdon thought about it and shook his head. "Leigh, you said it yourself. Templars, the Priory's soldiers, built this church. I think the Grand Master of the Priory would know if there were knights buried here."

Teabing looked shocked. "But this place is perfect." He turned back to the knights.

"We must be missing something!"

Walking into the side room, the altar boy was surprised to see no one there. "Father Knowles?" I know I heard the door, he thought, walking until he could see the entrance. A thin man in a tuxedo was standing near the door, scratching his head and looking lost. The altar boy sighed, annoyed that he had forgotten to lock the door after letting the others in. Now some poor guy had wandered in off the street, probably looking for directions to a wedding. "I'm sorry," he called out, walking past a large pillar, "we're closed." A rustle of fabric came from behind him, and before the altar boy could turn, his head jerked back, a strong hand covering his mouth, muffling his scream. The hand over the boy's mouth was snow-white, and he smelled alcohol.

The neat man in the tuxedo calmly pulled out a very small revolver, pointing it right at the boy's forehead.

The altar boy felt his groin get hot and realized he had wet himself.

"Listen carefully," the man in the tuxedo whispered. "You will leave this church quietly, and you will run. You will not stop. Do you understand?"

The boy nodded as much as he could with the hand over his mouth.

"If you call the police..." The man in the tuxedo pressed the gun to his skin. "I will find you." The next thing the boy knew, he was running across the courtyard outside, planning to run until he couldn't anymore.
