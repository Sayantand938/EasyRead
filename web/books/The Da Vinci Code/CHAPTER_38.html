The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 38

Sophie looked closely at Langdon in the back of the taxi. He's joking. "The Holy Grail?" Langdon nodded, looking serious. "Holy Grail is what Sangreal literally means. The word comes from the French Sangraal, which became Sangreal, and then was split into two words, San Greal."

Holy Grail. Sophie was surprised she hadn't seen the connection in the words right away. Even so, Langdon's idea still didn't make sense to her. "I thought the Holy Grail was a cup. You just told me the Sangreal is a set of papers that tells some big secret."

"Yes, but the Sangreal papers are only half of what the Holy Grail really is. They are buried with the Grail itself... and tell what it really means. The papers gave the Knights Templar so much power because they revealed the true nature of the Grail."

The true nature of the Grail? Sophie felt even more confused. She had always thought the Holy Grail was the cup Jesus drank from at the Last Supper, and that Joseph of Arimathea later used to catch His blood when He died on the cross. "The Holy Grail is the Cup of Christ," she said. "It couldn't be simpler, right?"

"Sophie," Langdon whispered, leaning closer, "according to the Priory of Sion, the Holy Grail is not a cup at all. They say the Grail story—the one about a chalice —is actually a clever way of saying something else. That is, the Grail story uses the chalice as a symbol for something else, something much more powerful." He paused. "Something that fits perfectly with everything your grandfather has been trying to tell us tonight, including all his symbolic references to the sacred feminine."

Still not sure, Sophie could tell from Langdon's patient smile that he understood her confusion, but his eyes were still serious. "But if the Holy Grail isn't a cup," she asked, "what is it?" Langdon knew this question was coming, but he still wasn't sure how to answer it. If he didn't explain the history behind it, Sophie would just be confused—the same way his editor looked a few months ago when Langdon gave him a draft of the book he was working on.

"This book says what?" his editor had asked, surprised, putting down his wineglass and looking at him over his lunch. "You can't be serious."

"Serious enough to have spent a year researching it."

Jonas Faukman, a well-known editor in New York, tugged nervously at his goatee. Faukman had probably heard a lot of crazy book ideas in his career, but this one seemed to have shocked him.

"Robert," Faukman finally said, "don't misunderstand. I love your work, and we've had a great time together. But if I agree to publish an idea like this, people will be protesting outside my office for months. Besides, it will ruin your reputation. You're a Harvard historian, for God's sake, not someone just trying to make a quick buck. Where could you possibly find enough solid evidence to support a theory like this?"

With a quiet smile Langdon pulled a piece of paper from the pocket of his tweed coat and handed it to Faukman. The page listed a bibliography of over fifty titles—books by well-known historians, some modern, some hundreds of years old—many of them academic bestsellers. All the book titles suggested the same idea Langdon had just proposed. As Faukman read down the list, he looked like a man who had just found out the earth was actually flat. "I know some of these authors. They're... real historians!"

Langdon grinned. "As you can see, Jonas, this isn't just my theory. It's been around for a long time. I'm simply adding to it. No book has yet looked at the legend of the Holy Grail from a symbolic point of view. The visual evidence I'm finding to support the theory is, well, incredibly convincing."

Faukman was still staring at the list. "My God, one of these books was written by Sir Leigh Teabing—a British Royal Historian."

"Teabing has spent much of his life studying the Holy Grail. I've met with him. He was actually a big inspiration to me. He believes it, Jonas, along with all of the others on that list."

"You're telling me all of these historians actually believe..." Faukman swallowed, apparently unable to say the words.

Langdon grinned again. "The Holy Grail is probably the most sought-after treasure in human history. The Grail has created legends, wars, and lifelong searches. Does it make sense that it's just a cup? If so, then surely other relics should create similar or greater interest—the Crown of Thorns, the True Cross of the Crucifixion, the Titulus—and yet, they don't. Throughout history, the Holy Grail has been the most special." Langdon grinned. "Now you know why." Faukman was still shaking his head. "But with all these books written about it, why isn't this theory more well-known?"

"These books can't possibly compete with hundreds of years of accepted history, especially when that history is supported by the biggest bestseller of all time."

Faukman's eyes went wide. "Don't tell me Harry Potter is actually about the Holy Grail."

"I was referring to the Bible."

Faukman cringed. "I knew that."

"Laissez-le!" Sophie's shouts sliced through the air inside the taxi. "Put it down!" Langdon jumped as Sophie leaned over the seat and yelled at the driver. Langdon saw the driver clutching his radio and speaking into it. Sophie turned and plunged her hand into Langdon's tweed jacket. Before Langdon knew it, she yanked out the pistol, swung it around, and pressed it to the back of the driver's head. The driver instantly dropped his radio, raising his one free hand.

"Sophie!" Langdon choked. "What the hell—"

"Arrêtez!" Sophie commanded the driver.

Trembling, the driver obeyed, stopping the car and putting it in park.

Just then, Langdon heard the metallic voice of the taxi company's dispatcher coming from the dashboard. "... qui s'appette Agent Sophie Neveu... " the radio crackled. "Et un Américain, Robert Langdon..."

Langdon's muscles turned rigid. They found us already?

"Descendez," Sophie demanded.

The trembling driver kept his arms over his head as he got out of his taxi and took a few steps backward.

Sophie had rolled down her window and now aimed the gun outside at the bewildered cabbie.

"Robert," she said quietly, "take the wheel. You're driving." Langdon wasn't going to argue with a woman holding a gun. He got out of the car and back in behind the wheel. The driver was shouting curses, his arms still up in the air.

"Robert," Sophie said from the back seat, "I think you've seen enough of our magic forest?" He nodded. More than enough.

"Good. Drive us out of here."

Langdon looked at the car's controls and hesitated. Damn. He reached for the stick shift and clutch. "Sophie? Maybe you—"

"Go!" she yelled.

Outside, some prostitutes were walking over to see what was happening. One woman was making a call on her cell phone. Langdon pressed the clutch and moved the stick into what he hoped was first gear. He touched the accelerator, testing the gas.

Langdon released the clutch. The tires screeched as the taxi shot forward, swerving wildly and sending the gathering crowd diving for cover. The woman with the cell phone jumped into the woods, barely avoiding being hit.

"Doucement!" Sophie said, as the car jerked down the road. "What are you doing?"

"I tried to warn you," he shouted over the sound of grinding gears. "I drive an automatic!"
