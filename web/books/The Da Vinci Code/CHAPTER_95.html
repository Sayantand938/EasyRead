The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 95

Langdon hadn't looked away from the computer since the search started. Five minutes. Only two hits. Both wrong.

He was starting to worry.

Pamela Gettum was in the next room, making hot drinks. Langdon and Sophie had asked if there might be some coffee along with the tea Gettum offered. Judging by the microwave beeps, Langdon thought they were about to get instant Nescafe.

Finally, the computer pinged.

"Sounds like you got another," Gettum called. "What's the title?" Langdon looked at the screen.

Grail Allegory in Medieval Literature: A Study of Sir Gawain and the Green Knight.

"Allegory of the Green Knight," he called back.

"No good," Gettum said. "Not many mythological green giants buried in London." Langdon and Sophie waited patiently for two more unlikely results. When the computer pinged again, the offering was unexpected.

DIE OPERN VON RICHARD WAGNER

"The operas of Wagner?" Sophie asked.

Gettum peeked back in the doorway, holding a packet of instant coffee. "That seems like an odd pairing. Was Wagner a knight?"

"No," Langdon said, feeling suddenly interested. "But he was a well-known Freemason. Like Mozart, Beethoven, Shakespeare, Gershwin, Houdini, and Disney." Many books had been written about the links between the Masons and the Knights Templar, the Priory of Sion, and the Holy Grail. "I want to look at this one. How do I see the whole thing?"

"You don't want the whole thing," Gettum called. "Click on the title. The computer will show where your keywords appear, along with some text before and after for context." Langdon didn't understand what she meant, but he clicked anyway.

A new window popped up.

...mythological knight named Parsifal who...

...symbolic quest for the Grail that could be seen as...

...the London Philharmonic in 1855...

Rebecca Pope's opera book "Diva's...

...Wagner's tomb in Bayreuth, Germany...

"Wrong Pope," Langdon said, disappointed. Still, he was amazed how easy the system was to use. The keywords with context were enough to remind him that Wagner's opera Parsifal was a tribute to Mary Magdalene and the bloodline of Jesus Christ, told through the story of a young knight searching for truth.

"Just be patient," Gettum urged. "It's about numbers. Let the machine run." Over the next few minutes, the computer found more Grail references, including a text about troubadours —France's famous wandering singers. Langdon knew it was no accident that the words minstrel and minister had the same origin. The troubadours were the traveling servants or "ministers" of the Church of Mary Magdalene, using music to spread the story of the sacred feminine among ordinary people. Even now, the troubadours sang songs praising "our Lady"—a mysterious and beautiful woman to whom they promised themselves forever.

Eagerly, he checked the hypertext but found nothing.

The computer pinged again.

KNIGHTS, KNAVES, POPES, AND PENTACLES: THE HISTORY OF THE HOLY GRAIL

THROUGH TAROT

"Not surprising," Langdon said to Sophie. "Some of our keywords have the same names as cards." He reached for the mouse to click on a link. "I'm not sure if your grandfather ever mentioned it when you played Tarot with him, Sophie, but this game is a 'simple lesson' in the story of the Lost Bride and how she was controlled by the evil Church." Sophie looked at him, not believing it. "I had no idea."

"That's the point. By teaching through a game with hidden meanings, the followers of the Grail hid their message from the Church." Langdon often wondered how many card players knew that their four suits—spades, hearts, clubs, diamonds—were Grail symbols from Tarot's four suits of swords, cups, scepters, and pentacles. Spades were Swords — The blade. Male.

Hearts were Cups — The chalice. Feminine.

Clubs were Scepters — The Royal Line. The flowering staff.

Diamonds were Pentacles — The goddess. The sacred feminine.

Four minutes later, as Langdon started to worry they wouldn't find what they were looking for, the computer found something else.

The Gravity of Genius: Biography of a Modern Knight.

"Gravity of Genius?" Langdon asked Gettum. "Bio of a modern knight?" Gettum poked her head around the corner. "How modern? Please don't say it's about Sir Rudy Giuliani. I thought that one missed the point."

Langdon wasn't sure about the newly knighted Sir Mick Jagger himself, but this wasn't the time to discuss British knighthood politics. "Let's take a look." Langdon pulled up the hypertext keywords.

... honorable knight, Sir Isaac Newton...

... in London in 1727 and...

... his tomb in Westminster Abbey...

... Alexander Pope, friend and colleague...

"I guess 'modern' means different things," Sophie said to Gettum. "It's an old book. About Sir Isaac Newton."

Gettum shook her head in the doorway. "No good. Newton was buried in Westminster Abbey, the heart of English Protestantism. There's no way a Catholic Pope was there. Cream and sugar?" Sophie nodded.

Gettum waited. "Robert?"

Langdon's heart was pounding. He looked away from the screen and stood up. "Sir Isaac Newton is our knight."

Sophie remained seated. "What are you talking about?"

"Newton is buried in London," Langdon said. "His work created new sciences that angered the Church. And he was a Grand Master of the Priory of Sion. What more could we want?"

"What more?" Sophie pointed to the poem. "How about a knight a Pope buried? You heard Ms. Gettum. Newton was not buried by a Catholic Pope."

Langdon reached for the mouse. "Who said anything about a Catholic Pope?" He clicked on the "Pope" link, and the complete sentence appeared.

Sir Isaac Newton's burial, attended by kings and nobles, was led by Alexander Pope, friend and colleague, who gave a moving

eulogy before sprinkling dirt on the tomb.

Langdon looked at Sophie. "We got the right Pope on our second try. Alexander." He paused.

"A. Pope."

In London lies a knight A. Pope buried.

Sophie stood up, looking shocked.

Jacques Saunière, the master of hidden meanings, had shown once again that he was a truly clever man.
