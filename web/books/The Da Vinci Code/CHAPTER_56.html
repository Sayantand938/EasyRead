The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 56

Sophie looked at Teabing for a long time, then turned to Langdon. "The Holy Grail is a person?" Langdon nodded. "A woman, actually." Seeing Sophie's blank face, Langdon knew they had lost her. He remembered feeling the same way when he first heard it. It wasn't until he understood the symbols behind the Grail that the link to women made sense.

Teabing seemed to think the same thing. "Robert, maybe now's the time for the symbologist to explain?" He went to a side table, found a piece of paper, and put it in front of Langdon. Langdon took a pen from his pocket. "Sophie, do you know the modern symbols for male and female?" He drew the common male symbol and female symbol .

"Of course," she said.

"These," he said softly, "aren't the original symbols for male and female. Many people wrongly think the male symbol comes from a shield and spear, and the female symbol is a mirror showing beauty. But the symbols started as old symbols for the planet-god Mars and planet-goddess Venus. The original symbols are much simpler." Langdon drew another symbol on the paper.

"This symbol is the original symbol for male," he told her. "A basic phallus."

"Straight to the point," Sophie said.

"Indeed," Teabing added.

Langdon continued. "This symbol is called the blade, and it stands for aggression and manhood. In fact, this exact phallus symbol is still used today on military uniforms to show rank."

"Indeed." Teabing grinned. "The more penises you have, the higher your rank. Boys will be boys." Langdon winced. "Moving on, the female symbol, as you might imagine, is the exact opposite." He drew another symbol on the page. "This is called the chalice."

Sophie glanced up, looking surprised.

Langdon could see she understood. "The chalice," he said, "looks like a cup or bowl, and more important, it looks like a woman's womb. This symbol represents femininity, womanhood, and fertility." Langdon looked directly at her now.

"Sophie, legend says the Holy Grail is a chalice—a cup. But calling the Grail a chalice is really a way to hide the true meaning of the Holy Grail. The legend uses the chalice as a metaphor for something much more important."

"A woman," Sophie said.

"Exactly." Langdon smiled. "The Grail is the ancient symbol for womanhood, and the Holy Grail represents the sacred feminine and the goddess, which has now been lost, almost completely wiped out by the Church. The power of the female and her ability to create life was once very sacred, but it threatened the rise of the Church, which was mostly male. So the sacred feminine was made to seem evil and impure. It was man, not God, who came up with the idea of 'original sin,' where Eve ate the apple and caused the downfall of humanity. Woman, once the sacred giver of life, was now the enemy."

"I should add," Teabing chimed in, "that this idea of woman as the giver of life was the basis of ancient religion. Childbirth was seen as mystical and powerful. Sadly, Christian philosophy decided to steal the female's creative power by ignoring the facts of biology and making man the Creator. Genesis tells us that Eve was created from Adam's rib. Woman became an extension of man. And a sinful one at that. Genesis was the beginning of the end for the goddess."

"The Grail," Langdon said, "symbolizes the lost goddess. When Christianity arrived, the old pagan religions didn't disappear easily. Legends of brave quests for the lost Grail were actually stories of secret quests to find the lost sacred feminine. Knights who said they were "searching for the chalice" were speaking in code to protect themselves from a Church that had oppressed women, banished the Goddess, burned those who didn't believe, and forbidden the pagan worship of the sacred feminine."

Sophie shook her head. "I'm sorry, when you said the Holy Grail was a person, I thought you meant it was an actual person."

"It is," Langdon said.

"And not just anyone," Teabing blurted, jumping up excitedly. "A woman who held a secret so big that, if it got out, it could destroy the basis of Christianity!"

Sophie looked stunned. "Is this woman famous in history?"

"Very." Teabing grabbed his crutches and pointed down the hall. "If we go to the study, I'd be glad to show you Da Vinci's painting of her."

Two rooms away, in the kitchen, the servant Rémy Legaludec stood silently watching TV. The news was showing pictures of a man and woman... the same two people Rémy had just given tea to.
