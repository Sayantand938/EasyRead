The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 83

Langdon's Mickey Mouse wristwatch showed almost seven-thirty when he stepped out of the Jaguar limousine onto Inner Temple Lane with Sophie and Teabing. The three walked through a maze of buildings to a small courtyard outside the Temple Church. The rough stone gleamed in the rain, and doves cooed in the structures above.

London's ancient Temple Church was built entirely of Caen stone. A striking, round building with an imposing front, a central tower, and a nave sticking out on one side, the church looked more like a military fort than a place of worship. Dedicated on February 10, 1185, by Heraclius, Patriarch of Jerusalem, the Temple Church survived eight centuries of political chaos, the Great Fire of London, and World War I, only to be heavily damaged by German bombs in 1940. After the war, it was restored to its original, stark beauty.

The simplicity of the circle, Langdon thought, admiring the building for the first time. The architecture was rough and basic, more like Rome's sturdy Castel Sant'Angelo than the elegant Pantheon. The boxy addition sticking out to the right was an ugly sight, but it didn't hide the original pagan shape of the main building.

"It's early on a Saturday," Teabing said, hobbling toward the entrance, "so I'm guessing we won't have any services to worry about."

The church's entrance was a recessed stone alcove with a large wooden door. To the left of the door, looking out of place, hung a bulletin board covered with concert schedules and religious service announcements.

Teabing frowned as he read the board. "They don't open to visitors for a couple of hours." He moved to the door and tried it. The door didn't move. Putting his ear to the wood, he listened. After a moment, he pulled back, a sly look on his face as he pointed to the bulletin board.

"Robert, check the service schedule, will you? Who is in charge this week?"

Inside the church, an altar boy was almost done vacuuming the communion kneelers when he heard a knock on the sanctuary door. He ignored it. Father Harvey Knowles had his own keys and wasn't due for a couple of hours. The knocking was probably a curious tourist or a beggar. The altar boy kept vacuuming, but the knocking continued. Can't you read? The sign on the door clearly said that the church didn't open until nine-thirty on Saturday. The altar boy stayed with his chores.

Suddenly, the knocking turned into loud banging, as if someone were hitting the door with a metal rod. The young man turned off his vacuum cleaner and marched angrily toward the door. Unlatching it from the inside, he swung it open. Three people stood in the entryway. Tourists, he grumbled. "We open at nine-thirty."

The large man, clearly the leader, walked forward with metal crutches. "I am Sir Leigh Teabing," he announced, his accent very British. "As you surely know, I am with Mr. and Mrs. Christopher Wren the Fourth." He stepped aside, gesturing to the attractive couple behind him. The woman had gentle features and rich red hair. The man was tall, dark-haired, and looked somewhat familiar.

The altar boy didn't know what to say. Sir Christopher Wren was the Temple Church's most famous supporter. He had made all the repairs possible after the Great Fire. He had also been dead since the early 1700s. "Um... nice to meet you?"

The man on crutches frowned. "Good thing you're not a salesman, you're not very convincing. Where is Father Knowles?"

"It's Saturday. He doesn't come in until later."

The disabled man's frown deepened. "Some gratitude. He promised he would be here, but it seems we'll do it without him. It won't take long."

The altar boy still blocked the doorway. "I'm sorry, what won't take long?" The visitor's eyes narrowed, and he leaned forward, whispering as if to spare everyone embarrassment. "Young man, you must be new here. Every year Sir Christopher Wren's descendants bring a bit of the old man's ashes to scatter in the Temple sanctuary. It's part of his will. No one likes making the trip, but what can we do?" The altar boy had been there for a couple of years but had never heard of this. "It would be better if you waited until nine-thirty. The church isn't open yet, and I haven't finished vacuuming." The man on crutches glared. "Young man, the only reason there's anything left of this building for you to vacuum is because of the gentleman in that woman's pocket."

"I'm sorry?"

"Mrs. Wren," the man on crutches said, "would you please show this rude young man the container of ashes?"

The woman paused, then, as if waking up, reached into her sweater pocket and took out a small cylinder wrapped in cloth.

"There, you see?" the man on crutches snapped. "Now, you can either grant his dying wish and let us sprinkle his ashes in the sanctuary, or I tell Father Knowles how we've been treated." The altar boy hesitated, knowing Father Knowles valued church tradition... and, more importantly, his bad temper when anything made this historic church look bad. Maybe Father Knowles had simply forgotten they were coming. If so, it was riskier to turn them away than to let them in. After all, they said it would only take a minute. What could it hurt?

When the altar boy stepped aside to let the three people pass, he could have sworn Mr. and Mrs. Wren looked just as confused by all of this as he was. Uncertain, the boy returned to his chores, watching them out of the corner of his eye.

Langdon had to smile as the threesome moved deeper into the church.

"Leigh," he whispered, "you lie entirely too well." Teabing's eyes twinkled. "Oxford Theatre Club. They still talk of my Julius Caesar. I'm certain nobody has ever performed the first scene of Act Three with more dedication." Langdon glanced over. "I thought Caesar was dead in that scene." Teabing smirked. "Yes, but my toga tore open when I fell, and I had to lie on stage for half an hour with my 'thing' hanging out. Even so, I never moved a muscle. I was brilliant, I tell you." Langdon cringed. Sorry I missed it.

As the group moved through the rectangular annex toward the archway leading into the main church, Langdon was surprised by the stark simplicity. Although the altar layout resembled that of a standard Christian chapel, the furnishings were bare and cold, lacking any traditional decoration. "Bleak," he whispered.

Teabing chuckled. "Church of England. Anglicans take their religion without any frills. Nothing to distract from their misery."

Sophie motioned through the vast opening that gave way to the circular section of the church. "It looks like a fortress in there," she whispered.

Langdon agreed. Even from here, the walls looked unusually strong.

"The Knights Templar were warriors," Teabing reminded, the sound of his aluminum crutches echoing in this spacious area. "A religious military group. Their churches were their strongholds and their banks."

"Banks?" Sophie asked, glancing at Leigh.

"Heavens, yes. The Templars invented the idea of modern banking. For European nobles, traveling with gold was dangerous, so the Templars allowed nobles to deposit gold in their nearest Temple Church and then withdraw it from any other Temple Church across Europe. All they needed was proper documentation." He winked. "And a small fee. They were the original ATMs." Teabing pointed toward a stained-glass window where the bright sun was shining through a white-clad knight riding a rose-colored horse. "Alanus Marcel," Teabing said, "Master of the Temple in the early twelve hundreds. He and his successors actually held the Parliamentary chair of Primus Baro Angiae."

Langdon was surprised. "First Baron of the Realm?"

Teabing nodded. "The Master of the Temple, some say, had more power than the king." As they got to the circular room, Teabing glanced at the altar boy, who was vacuuming far away. "You know," Teabing whispered to Sophie, "they say the Holy Grail was once kept in this church overnight while the Templars moved it. Can you imagine the four chests of Sangreal papers sitting here with Mary Magdalene's coffin? It gives me chills." Langdon felt chills too as they entered the circular room. He looked around at the room's pale stone wall, seeing carvings of gargoyles, demons, monsters, and suffering faces, all looking in. Under the carvings, a single stone bench went around the whole room.

"Theater in the round," Langdon whispered.

Teabing raised a crutch, pointing to the far left and then to the far right. Langdon had already noticed them.

Ten stone knights.

Five on the left. Five on the right.

Lying flat on the floor, the carved, life-sized figures looked peaceful. The knights wore full armor, shields, and swords, and the tombs made Langdon feel like someone had snuck in and covered the knights in plaster while they were sleeping. All of the figures were worn, but each was clearly different—different armor parts, different leg and arm positions, faces, and markings on their shields. In London lies a knight a Pope interred.

Langdon felt shaky as he moved into the circular room.

This had to be the place.
