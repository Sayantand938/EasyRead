The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 7

The simple living space inside the Church of Saint-Sulpice was on the second floor, to the left of the choir balcony. It was a two-room place with a stone floor and just a few things in it. Sister Sandrine Bieil had lived there for over ten years. The convent nearby was officially where she lived, but she liked the quiet of the church better and had made herself comfortable upstairs with a bed, phone, and hot plate.

As the church's caretaker, Sister Sandrine was in charge of all the nonreligious stuff—general upkeep, hiring staff and guides, locking up the building at night, and ordering things like communion wine and wafers. Tonight, she was asleep in her small bed when her phone rang loudly. Tired, she picked it up.

"Sister Sandrine. Church of Saint-Sulpice."

"Hello, Sister," the man said in French.

Sister Sandrine sat up. What time is it? She knew her boss's voice, but he had never woken her up in fifteen years. The priest was very religious and always went home to bed right after mass.

"I'm sorry if I woke you, Sister," the priest said, sounding tired and worried. "I need you to do me a favor. I just got a call from an important American bishop. Maybe you know him? Manuel Aringarosa?"

"The head of Opus Dei?" Of course I know him. Everyone in the Church does. Aringarosa's conservative group had become very powerful lately. They became important quickly in 1982 when Pope John Paul II surprisingly made them a "personal prelature of the Pope," officially approving all their practices. People thought it was strange that Opus Dei's rise happened the same year the rich group supposedly put almost a billion dollars into the Vatican's bank—officially called the Institute for Religious Works—saving it from a bad bankruptcy. In another move that made people suspicious, the Pope put the founder of Opus Dei on the "fast track" to becoming a saint, speeding up the usual long waiting period for sainthood to only twenty years. Sister Sandrine couldn't help but think that Opus Dei's good standing in Rome was questionable, but you didn't argue with the Holy See.

"Bishop Aringarosa called to ask me a favor," the priest told her, sounding anxious. "One of his members is in Paris tonight...."

As Sister Sandrine listened to the strange request, she became more and more confused. "I'm sorry, are you saying this visiting Opus Dei member can't wait until morning?"

"I'm afraid not. His plane leaves very early. He's always dreamed of seeing Saint-Sulpice."

"But the church is much more interesting during the day. The sunlight through the opening, the changing shadows on the sundial—this is what makes Saint-Sulpice special."

"Sister, I agree, but I would really appreciate it if you could let him in tonight. He can be there at… let's say one o'clock? That's in twenty minutes."

Sister Sandrine frowned. "Of course. I'd be happy to." The abbé thanked her and hung up.

Confused, Sister Sandrine stayed in her warm bed for a moment, trying to wake up fully. Her sixty-year-old body didn't wake up as quickly as it used to, though tonight's phone call had certainly gotten her attention. Opus Dei had always made her uncomfortable. Besides the group's commitment to the strange practice of physical self-punishment, their views on women were very outdated. She was shocked to hear that female members were forced to clean the men's dorms for free while the men were at mass; women slept on hard floors, while the men had straw mats; and women had to do extra acts of self-punishment… all as extra punishment for original sin. It seemed like Eve's bite of the apple of knowledge was a debt women were destined to pay forever. Sadly, while most of the Catholic Church was slowly improving when it came to women's rights, Opus Dei threatened to undo that progress. Even so, Sister Sandrine had her orders. Swinging her legs off the bed, she stood slowly, feeling the cold stone floor on her bare feet. As the cold spread through her body, she felt a sudden worry. Women's intuition?

As a follower of God, Sister Sandrine had learned to find peace in the quiet voices of her own soul. Tonight, however, those voices were as silent as the empty church around her.
