The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 64

Teabing sat on the divan, holding the wooden box on his lap and admiring the lid's detailed inlaid Rose. Tonight is the strangest and most magical night of my life.

"Lift the lid," Sophie whispered, standing over him, beside Langdon. Teabing smiled. Don't rush me. After searching for this keystone for over a decade, he wanted to savor every moment. He ran his palm across the wooden lid, feeling the texture of the inlaid flower.

"The Rose," he whispered. The Rose is Magdalene is the Holy Grail. The Rose is the compass that guides the way. Teabing felt foolish. For years he had traveled to cathedrals and churches all over France, paying for special access, examining hundreds of archways beneath rose windows, searching for an encrypted keystone. La clef de voûte — a stone key beneath the sign of the Rose. Teabing slowly unlatched the lid and raised it.

As his eyes finally saw the contents, he knew instantly it had to be the keystone. He was staring at a stone cylinder, made of interconnecting lettered dials. The device seemed surprisingly familiar to him.

"Designed from Da Vinci's diaries," Sophie said. "My grandfather made them as a hobby." Of course, Teabing realized. He had seen the sketches and blueprints. The key to finding the Holy Grail lies inside this stone. Teabing lifted the heavy cryptex from the box, holding it gently. Although he had no idea how to open the cylinder, he sensed his own destiny lay inside. In moments of failure, Teabing had questioned whether his life's quest would ever be rewarded. Now those doubts were gone forever. He could hear the ancient words... the foundation of the Grail legend:

Vous ne trouvez pas le Saint-Graal, c'est le Saint-Graal qui vous trouve. You do not find the Grail, the Grail finds you.

And tonight, incredibly, the key to finding the Holy Grail had walked right through his front door.

While Sophie and Teabing sat with the cryptex and talked about the vinegar, the dials, and what the password might be, Langdon carried the rosewood box across the room to a well-lit table to get a better look at it. Something Teabing had just said was now running through Langdon's mind. The key to the Grail is hidden beneath the sign of the Rose.

Langdon held the wooden box up to the light and examined the inlaid symbol of the Rose. Although his knowledge of art did not include woodworking or inlaid furniture, he had just recalled the famous tiled ceiling of the Spanish monastery outside of Madrid, where, three centuries after it was built, the ceiling tiles began to fall out, revealing sacred texts scrawled by monks on the plaster beneath.

Langdon looked again at the Rose.

Beneath the Rose.

Sub Rosa.

Secret.

A noise in the hall behind him made Langdon turn. He saw only shadows. It was likely Teabing's servant. Langdon turned back to the box. He ran his finger over the smooth edge, wondering if he could remove the Rose, but it was too well-made. He doubted even a razor could fit between the Rose and the carved space it sat in.

Opening the box, he looked at the inside of the lid. It was smooth. But as he moved it, he saw what looked like a small hole in the center of the lid. Langdon closed the lid and looked at the Rose from above. No hole. It doesn't go all the way through.

Setting the box on the table, he saw some papers with a paper clip. Taking the clip, he went back to the box, opened it, and looked at the hole again. Carefully, he straightened the paper clip and put one end in the hole. He pushed gently. It was easy. He heard something quietly fall onto the table. Langdon closed the lid to see. It was a small piece of wood, like a puzzle piece. The Rose had come out of the lid and fallen onto the desk.

Without a word, Langdon stared at the empty spot on the lid where the Rose had been. There, carved in the wood in neat writing, were four lines of text in a language he had never seen. The letters look a little like Semitic languages, Langdon thought, but I don't know the language!

A sudden movement behind him caught his attention. Unexpectedly, a hard hit to the head knocked Langdon to his knees.

As he fell, he thought he saw a pale figure standing over him, holding a gun. Then everything went black.
