The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 47

Riding in the dim cargo hold of the armored truck felt like being in solitary confinement. Langdon fought the familiar anxiety he felt in small spaces. Vernet said he would take us far from the city. Where? How far?

Langdon's legs were stiff from sitting on the metal floor, and he shifted, wincing as blood flowed back into them. He still held the strange treasure they had taken from the bank.

"I think we're on the highway now," Sophie whispered.

Langdon felt it too. The truck had paused at the bank ramp, then turned left and right for a few minutes, and now was speeding up. Below them, the tires hummed on the smooth road. He looked at the rosewood box in his arms, placed it on the floor, took off his jacket, and pulled the box closer. Sophie moved so they were sitting side by side. Langdon felt like they were two kids with a Christmas present. The inlaid rose on the warm-colored rosewood box was made of light wood, probably ash, and stood out in the dim light. The Rose. Armies, religions, and secret societies had been built on this symbol. The Rosicrucians. The Knights of the Rosy Cross.

"Go ahead," Sophie said. "Open it."

Langdon took a deep breath. He reached for the lid, admired the woodwork, and then unhooked the clasp and opened it. He had imagined many things they might find inside, but he was wrong. Inside the padded crimson silk lining was something Langdon didn't understand.

It was a polished white marble cylinder, about the size of a tennis ball can. But it was more complex than a simple stone column, and seemed to be made of many pieces. Six doughnut-sized marble disks were stacked and attached to each other within a delicate brass frame. It looked like a tubular kaleidoscope with many wheels. Each end of the cylinder had a marble cap, so you couldn't see inside. Langdon heard liquid inside, so he thought the cylinder was hollow. The engravings around the tube were as puzzling as its construction. Each of the six disks had the same series of letters—the entire alphabet. The lettered cylinder reminded Langdon of a childhood toy—a rod with lettered pieces that could be turned to spell words.

"Amazing, isn't it?" Sophie whispered.

Langdon looked up. "I don't know. What is it?"

Now there was a spark in Sophie's eye. "My grandfather used to make these as a hobby. They were invented by Leonardo da Vinci."

Even in the dim light, Sophie could see Langdon's surprise.

"Da Vinci?" he murmured, looking again at the canister.

"Yes. It's called a cryptex. According to my grandfather, the plans come from one of Da Vinci's secret notebooks."

"What is it for?"

Considering what was happening tonight, Sophie knew the answer could be important.

"It's a vault," she said. "For keeping secret information." Langdon's eyes widened even more.

Sophie explained that making models of Da Vinci's inventions was one of her grandfather's favorite hobbies. A skilled craftsman who spent hours in his workshop, Jacques Saunière enjoyed copying master craftsmen—Fabergé, various enamel artists, and the less artistic, but more useful, Leonardo da Vinci.

Even a quick look through Da Vinci's journals showed why he was known for not finishing things as much as he was known for his genius. Da Vinci had drawn plans for hundreds of inventions he never built. One of Jacques Saunière's favorite things to do was to bring Da Vinci's more unusual ideas to life—clocks, water pumps, cryptexes, and even a fully movable model of a medieval French knight, which now stood proudly on the desk in his office. Designed by Da Vinci in 1495 as a result of his early studies of the body and movement, the robot knight's inner workings had accurate joints and tendons, and was designed to sit up, wave its arms, and move its head with a flexible neck while opening and closing a realistic jaw. Sophie had always thought this armored knight was the most beautiful thing her grandfather had ever built... until she saw the cryptex in this rosewood box.

"He made me one of these when I was little," Sophie said. "But I've never seen one so fancy and big."

Langdon's eyes had never left the box. "I've never heard of a cryptex." Sophie was not surprised. Most of Leonardo's unbuilt inventions had never been studied or even named. The term cryptex might have been her grandfather's creation, a fitting name for this device that used the science of cryptology to protect information written on the scroll or codex inside. Da Vinci had been a cryptology pioneer, Sophie knew, though he was rarely given credit. Sophie's university teachers, when presenting computer encryption methods for protecting data, praised modern cryptologists like Zimmerman and Schneier but didn't mention that Leonardo had invented one of the first basic forms of public key encryption centuries ago. Sophie's grandfather, of course, had told her all about that. As their armored truck sped down the highway, Sophie explained to Langdon that the cryptex had been Da Vinci's solution to sending secure messages over long distances. In a time without telephones or e-mail, anyone wanting to send private information to someone far away had to write it down and trust a messenger to carry the letter. Unfortunately, if a messenger thought the letter might contain valuable information, he could make more money selling the information to enemies than delivering the letter properly.

Many great minds in history had invented cryptologic solutions to protect data: Julius Caesar created a code-writing system called the Caesar Box; Mary, Queen of Scots created a transposition cipher and sent secret messages from prison; and the brilliant Arab scientist Abu Yusuf Ismail al-Kindi protected his secrets with a clever polyalphabetic substitution cipher.

Da Vinci, however, avoided mathematics and cryptology for a mechanical solution. The cryptex. A portable container that could protect letters, maps, diagrams, anything at all. Once information was sealed inside the cryptex, only the person with the correct password could access it.

"We need a password," Sophie said, pointing to the lettered dials. "A cryptex works like a bicycle's combination lock. If you line up the dials in the right position, the lock slides open. This cryptex has five lettered dials. When you turn them to the right sequence, the tumblers inside line up, and the entire cylinder slides apart."

"And inside?"

"Once the cylinder slides apart, you can access a hollow center, which can hold a scroll of paper with the information you want to keep private." Langdon looked doubtful. "And you're saying your grandfather built these for you when you were younger?"

"Some smaller ones, yes. A couple of times for my birthday, he gave me a cryptex and told me a riddle. The answer to the riddle was the password to the cryptex, and once I solved it, I could open it and find my birthday card."

"A lot of work for a card."

"No, the cards always had another riddle or clue. My grandfather loved creating elaborate treasure hunts around our house, a series of clues that eventually led to my real gift. Each treasure hunt was a test of character and worth, to make sure I earned my rewards. And the tests were never easy."

Langdon looked at the device again, still skeptical. "But why not just pry it open? Or smash it?"

The metal looks fragile, and marble is a soft stone."

Sophie smiled. "Because Da Vinci is too clever for that. He made the cryptex so that if you try to break it open, the information destroys itself. Watch." Sophie reached into the box and carefully lifted out the cylinder. "Any information to be put inside is first written on a papyrus scroll."

"Not vellum?"

Sophie shook her head. "Papyrus. I know sheep's vellum lasted longer and was more common then, but it had to be papyrus. The thinner it is, the better."

"Okay."

"Before the papyrus was put into the cryptex, it was rolled around a thin glass vial." She tipped the cryptex, and the liquid inside made a gurgling sound. "A vial of liquid."

"Liquid what?"

Sophie smiled. "Vinegar."

Langdon paused for a moment and then started nodding. "Brilliant." Vinegar and papyrus, Sophie thought. If someone tried to force the cryptex open, the glass vial would break, and the vinegar would quickly dissolve the papyrus. By the time anyone got the secret message out, it would be a blob of unreadable mush.

"As you can see," Sophie told him, "the only way to get the information inside is to know the right five-letter password. And with five dials, each with twenty-six letters, that's twenty-six to the fifth power." She quickly figured out the combinations. "About twelve million possibilities."

"If you say so," Langdon said, looking like he had a million questions in his head. "What do you think is inside?"

"Whatever it is, my grandfather really wanted to keep it secret." She paused, closing the box and looking at the five-petal Rose on it. Something bothered her. "Did you say the Rose is a symbol for the Grail?"

"Exactly. In Priory symbols, the Rose and the Grail mean the same thing." Sophie frowned. "That's strange, because my grandfather always said the Rose meant secrecy. He'd hang a rose on his office door when he was on a private call and didn't want me to bother him. He told me to do the same." Sweetie, her grandfather said, instead of locking each other out, we can hang a rose — la fleur des secrets — on our door when we need privacy. This way we learn to respect and trust each other. Hanging a rose is an old Roman custom.

"Sub rosa," Langdon said. "The Romans hung a rose over meetings to show they were private. People knew that whatever was said under the rose — or sub rosa — had to stay secret."

Langdon quickly explained that secrecy wasn't the only reason the Priory used the Rose as a symbol for the Grail. Rosa rugosa, one of the oldest types of rose, had five petals and a five-sided shape, like the star of Venus, so the Rose was strongly linked to women. Also, the Rose was closely tied to "true direction" and finding your way. The Compass Rose helped travelers navigate, as did Rose Lines, the lines on maps. So, the Rose symbolized the Grail in many ways—secrecy, women, and guidance—the feminine cup and guiding star that led to secret truth.

As Langdon finished, his face suddenly looked tense.

"Robert? Are you okay?"

He stared at the rosewood box. "Sub... rosa," he gasped, looking scared and confused. "It can't be."

"What?"

Langdon slowly looked up. "Under the sign of the Rose," he whispered. "This cryptex... I think I know what it is."
