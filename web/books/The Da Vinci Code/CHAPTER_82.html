The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 82

"Fleet Street?" Langdon asked, looking at Teabing in the back of the limo. A crypt on Fleet Street? Leigh was being deliberately vague about where he thought they would find the "knight's tomb," which, according to the poem, would give them the password to open the smaller cryptex.

Teabing grinned and turned to Sophie. "Miss Neveu, let the Harvard boy try the verse again, will you?"

Sophie reached into her pocket and took out the black cryptex, wrapped in the vellum. They had all decided to leave the rosewood box and larger cryptex in the plane's safe, taking only what they needed: the smaller, easier to carry black cryptex. Sophie unwrapped the vellum and gave the sheet to Langdon.

Langdon had read the poem several times on the jet, but hadn't found any specific location. Now, reading the words again, he studied them slowly, hoping the rhythm would reveal a clearer meaning now that he was on the ground. In London lies a knight a Pope interred.

His labor's fruit a Holy wrath incurred.

You seek the orb that ought be on his tomb.

It speaks of Rosy flesh and seeded womb.

The language seemed straightforward. A knight was buried in London. A knight who worked on something that angered the Church. A knight whose tomb was missing an orb that should be there. The poem's last line— Rosy flesh and seeded womb —clearly referred to Mary Magdalene, the Rose who carried Jesus's seed.

Despite the verse seeming simple, Langdon still didn't know who this knight was or where he was buried. Also, once they found the tomb, it sounded like they would be looking for something missing. The orb that should be on his tomb?

"No ideas?" Teabing clicked his tongue, disappointed, but Langdon felt the Royal Historian liked being ahead. "Miss Neveu?"

She shook her head.

"What would you two do without me?" Teabing said. "Fine, I'll explain it. It's really easy. The first line is key. Please read it." Langdon read, " 'In London lies a knight a Pope interred.' "

"Exactly. A knight a Pope interred." He looked at Langdon. "What does that mean to you?" Langdon shrugged. "A knight buried by a Pope? A knight whose funeral a Pope led?"

Teabing laughed loudly. "Oh, that's funny. Always the optimist, Robert. Look at the second line. This knight clearly did something to anger the Church. Think again. Think about the Church and the Knights Templar. A knight a Pope interred?"

"A knight a Pope killed?" Sophie asked.

Teabing smiled and patted her knee. "Good job, my dear. A knight a Pope buried. Or killed." Langdon thought of the famous Templar round-up in 1307—unlucky Friday the thirteenth—when Pope Clement killed and buried hundreds of Knights Templar. "But there must be many graves of 'knights killed by Popes.' "

"Aha, not so!" Teabing said. "Many were burned and thrown carelessly into the Tiber River. But this poem mentions a tomb. A tomb in London. And few knights are buried in London." He paused, watching Langdon as if waiting for him to understand. Finally, he sighed.

"Robert, come on! The church built in London by the Priory's military group—the Knights Templar themselves!"

"The Temple Church?" Langdon gasped. "It has a crypt?"

"Ten of the scariest tombs you'll ever see."

Langdon had never been to the Temple Church, but he'd read about it a lot in his Priory research. It was once the center of all Templar/Priory action in the UK. The Temple Church was named after Solomon's Temple, which is where the Knights Templar got their name. They also got the Sangreal documents there, which gave them power in Rome. Stories said the knights did weird, secret rituals in the Temple Church's strange sanctuary. "The Temple Church is on Fleet Street?"

"Actually, it's just off Fleet Street on Inner Temple Lane." Teabing grinned. "I wanted to make you worry a bit before I told you."

"Thanks."

"Neither of you has ever been there?"

Sophie and Langdon shook their heads.

"I'm not surprised," Teabing said. "The church is hidden now by bigger buildings. Hardly anyone knows it's there. It's a creepy old place. The architecture is totally pagan." Sophie looked surprised. "Pagan?"

"Completely pagan!" Teabing shouted. "The church is round. The Templars ignored the usual Christian cross shape and built a completely round church to honor the sun." He raised his eyebrows playfully. "A clear message to the guys in Rome. They might as well have rebuilt Stonehenge in London."

Sophie looked at Teabing. "What about the rest of the poem?"

The historian's cheerful look disappeared. "I'm not sure. It's confusing. We need to check each of the ten tombs closely. Hopefully, one of them will clearly be missing an orb." Langdon understood how close they were. If the missing orb showed them the password, they could open the second cryptex. He struggled to picture what they might find inside. Langdon looked at the poem again. It was like an ancient crossword puzzle. A five-letter word that speaks of the Grail? On the plane, they had already tried the obvious passwords—GRAIL, GRAAL, GREAL, VENUS, MARIA, JESUS, SARAH—but the cylinder hadn't moved. Too obvious. It seemed there was another five-letter reference to the Rose's fertile source. The fact that the word was escaping someone like Leigh Teabing made Langdon think it was no ordinary Grail reference.

"Sir Leigh?" Rémy called. He was watching them in the rearview mirror through the open divider. "You said Fleet Street is near Blackfriars Bridge?"

"Yes, take Victoria Embankment."

"I'm sorry. I don't know where that is. We usually only go to the hospital." Teabing rolled his eyes at Langdon and Sophie and muttered, "Honestly, it's like watching a child sometimes. One moment. Please, have a drink and some snacks." He left them, climbing awkwardly toward the open divider to talk to Rémy.

Sophie turned to Langdon, speaking quietly. "Robert, no one knows we're in England." Langdon realized she was right. The Kent police would tell Fache the plane was empty, and Fache would think they were still in France. We are invisible. Leigh's trick had just bought them time.

"Fache won't give up easily," Sophie said. "He has too much to lose if he doesn't arrest us." Langdon had been trying not to think about Fache. Sophie had promised to do everything she could to prove Langdon's innocence once this was over, but Langdon was starting to worry it might not matter. Fache could easily be part of this plan. Although Langdon couldn't imagine the Judicial Police involved in the Holy Grail, he felt too many coincidences tonight to dismiss Fache as an accomplice. Fache is religious, and he is determined to blame me for these murders. Then again, Sophie had said that Fache might just be too eager to make the arrest. After all, the evidence against Langdon was strong. Besides Langdon's name written on the Louvre floor and in Saunière's date book, Langdon now seemed to have lied about his manuscript and then run away. At Sophie's suggestion.

"Robert, I'm sorry you're so involved," Sophie said, putting her hand on his knee. "But I'm glad you're here."

The comment seemed more practical than romantic, but Langdon felt a sudden spark of attraction between them. He gave her a tired smile. "I'm much more fun when I've slept." Sophie was quiet for a few seconds. "My grandfather asked me to trust you. I'm glad I listened to him for once."

"Your grandfather didn't even know me."

"Even so, I think you've done everything he would have wanted. You helped me find the keystone, explained the Sangreal, told me about the ritual in the basement." She stopped.

Somehow I feel closer to my grandfather tonight than I have in years. I know he would be happy about that.

In the distance, now, the London skyline began to appear through the light morning rain. Once known for Big Ben and Tower Bridge, the horizon now featured the Millennium Eye—a huge, modern Ferris wheel that rose five hundred feet and offered amazing views of the city. Langdon had tried to ride it once, but the "viewing capsules" seemed like sealed coffins, and he chose to stay on the ground and enjoy the view from the open banks of the Thames.

Langdon felt a squeeze on his knee, pulling him back, and Sophie's green eyes were on him. He realized she had been talking to him. "What do you think we should do with the Sangreal documents if we ever find them?" she whispered.

"What I think doesn't matter," Langdon said. "Your grandfather gave the cryptex to you, and you should do with it what you feel he would want done."

"I'm asking for your opinion. You obviously wrote something in that manuscript that made my grandfather trust you. He scheduled a private meeting with you. That's unusual."

"Maybe he wanted to tell me I'm completely wrong."

"Why would he tell me to find you unless he liked your ideas? In your manuscript, did you support the idea that the Sangreal documents should be revealed or stay hidden?"

"Neither. I didn't make a decision either way. The manuscript is about the symbols of the sacred feminine—tracing her images throughout history. I certainly didn't pretend to know where the Grail is hidden or whether it should ever be revealed."

"And yet you're writing a book about it, so you obviously feel the information should be shared."

"There's a big difference between talking about a possible different history of Christ, and..." He paused.

"And what?"

"And showing the world thousands of old papers as proof that the New Testament is a lie."

"But you said the New Testament is based on made-up stories." Langdon smiled. "Sophie, every faith in the world is based on made-up stories. That's what faith is—believing in something we think is true, something we can't prove. Every religion uses metaphors, allegories, and exaggerations to describe God, from the ancient Egyptians to Sunday school today. Metaphors help us understand things we can't really understand. The problem is when we start to believe our own metaphors are real."

"So you think the Sangreal documents should stay hidden forever?"

"I'm a historian. I don't want documents destroyed, and I'd like religious scholars to have more information to think about Jesus Christ's special life."

"You're arguing both sides of my question."

"Am I? The Bible is a basic guide for millions of people, just like the Koran, Torah, and Pali Canon guide people of other religions. If we could find documents that went against the holy stories of Islam, Judaism, or Buddhism, should we? Should we announce that we have proof the Buddha wasn't born from a lotus flower? Or that Jesus wasn't really born of a virgin? People who truly understand their faith know the stories are metaphors."

Sophie looked doubtful. "My Christian friends really believe that Christ walked on water, turned water into wine, and was born of a virgin."

"Exactly," Langdon said. "Religious allegory has become part of what people believe is real. And believing in that reality helps millions of people deal with life and be better."

"But it seems their reality is false."

Langdon chuckled. "No more wrong than a math expert believing in the number 'i' because it helps her solve codes."

Sophie frowned. "That's not fair."

A moment passed.

"What did you ask before?" Langdon asked.

"I forgot."

He smiled. "Always works."
