The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 4

Captain Bezu Fache moved like an angry ox, his wide shoulders back and his chin tucked in. His dark hair was slicked back, showing a sharp widow's peak that looked like the front of a ship. As he walked, his dark eyes seemed to burn the ground, showing a fierce focus that matched his reputation for strictness.

Langdon followed the captain down the marble staircase into the atrium under the glass pyramid. They passed two armed police guards with machine guns. The message was clear: No one enters or leaves without Captain Fache's permission.

Going underground, Langdon felt uneasy. Fache's presence was not friendly, and the Louvre felt like a tomb at this hour. The staircase was lit by soft lights in each step, like in a dark movie theater. Langdon heard his footsteps echo off the glass above. He looked up and saw the faint mist from the fountains fading against the transparent roof.

"Do you like it?" Fache asked, nodding up with his chin. Langdon sighed, too tired to play games. "Yes, your pyramid is magnificent." Fache grunted. "A scar on the face of Paris."

Strike one. Langdon knew his host was hard to please. He wondered if Fache knew that this pyramid, built at President Mitterrand's request, had exactly 666 panes of glass—a strange detail that conspiracy theorists often discussed, claiming 666 was the number of Satan.

Langdon decided not to mention it.

As they went deeper into the underground foyer, the large space slowly came into view. Built fifty-seven feet below ground, the Louvre's new 70,000-square-foot lobby stretched out like a vast cave. Made of warm ocher marble to match the Louvre's honey-colored stone above, the hall was usually full of sunlight and tourists. But tonight, it was empty and dark, giving the space a cold, tomb-like feel.

"What about the museum's regular security staff?" Langdon asked.

"In quarantine," Fache replied, sounding as if Langdon were questioning his team's honesty. "Clearly, someone got in tonight who wasn't supposed to. All Louvre night guards are in the Sully Wing being questioned. My own agents have taken over museum security for the night."

Langdon nodded, hurrying to keep up with Fache.

"How well did you know Jacques Saunière?" the captain asked.

"Actually, not at all. We never met."

Fache looked surprised. "Tonight was supposed to be your first meeting?"

"Yes. We planned to meet at the American University reception after my lecture, but he never arrived."

Fache scribbled some notes in a small book. As they walked, Langdon caught a glimpse of the Louvre's lesser-known pyramid— La Pyramide Inversée —a huge upside-down skylight that hung from the ceiling like a stalactite in a nearby part of the mezzanine. Fache led Langdon up a short flight of stairs to the entrance of an arched tunnel, above which a sign read: DENON. The Denon Wing was the most famous of the Louvre's three main sections.

"Who asked for tonight's meeting?" Fache asked suddenly. "You or him?" The question seemed strange. "Mr. Saunière did," Langdon replied as they entered the tunnel. "His secretary contacted me a few weeks ago by email. She said the curator had heard I was lecturing in Paris this month and wanted to talk about something with me while I was here."

"Talk about what?"

"I don't know. Art, I guess. We have similar interests."

Fache looked doubtful. "You have no idea what your meeting was about?" Langdon did not. He'd been curious then but hadn't felt right asking for details. The respected Jacques Saunière was known for his privacy and gave very few meetings; Langdon was thankful just to meet him.

"Mr. Langdon, can you at least guess what our murder victim wanted to talk about with you on the night he was killed? It might help."

The directness of the question made Langdon uneasy. "I really can't guess. I didn't ask. I was honored to be contacted. I admire Mr. Saunière's work. I use his books often in my classes."

Fache wrote that down in his book.

The two men were now halfway up the Denon Wing's entry tunnel, and Langdon could see the two escalators at the far end, both still.

"So you had things in common?" Fache asked.

"Yes. I've spent most of the last year writing a book that covers Mr. Saunière's main area of knowledge. I wanted to get his thoughts on it." Fache looked up. "Sorry?"

The saying didn't seem to translate. "I wanted to hear what he thought about the topic."

"I see. And what is the topic?"

Langdon paused, unsure how to explain. "It's about the symbols of goddess worship—the idea of female holiness and the art and symbols related to it."

Fache ran a thick hand through his hair. "And Saunière knew about this?"

"Nobody knew more."

"I see."

Langdon felt Fache did not see at all. Jacques Saunière was known as the world's leading expert on goddess symbols. Saunière was very interested in objects related to fertility, goddess worship, Wicca, and the sacred feminine. Also, during his twenty years as curator, Saunière had helped the Louvre gather the largest collection of goddess art in the world—double axes from the priestesses' ancient Greek temple in Delphi, gold serpent wands, hundreds of Tjet symbols that looked like small standing angels, sistrum rattles used in ancient Egypt to ward off evil spirits, and many statues showing Horus being nursed by the goddess Isis.

"Maybe Jacques Saunière knew about your book?" Fache suggested. "And he called the meeting to offer help with it."

Langdon shook his head. "Actually, nobody knows about my book yet. It's still a draft, and I haven't shown it to anyone except my editor."

Fache was quiet.

Langdon did not add the reason he hadn't shown the book to anyone else. The three-hundred-page draft—called Symbols of the Lost Sacred Feminine —suggested some very strange interpretations of well-known religious symbols that would likely cause controversy.

Now, as Langdon neared the still escalators, he stopped, noticing Fache was no longer next to him. Turning, Langdon saw Fache standing a few steps back at a service elevator.

"We'll take the elevator," Fache said as the doors opened. "As you know, the gallery is quite a walk."

Although Langdon knew the elevator would make the long, two-story climb to the Denon Wing faster, he didn't move.

"Is something wrong?" Fache was holding the door, looking impatient. Langdon sighed, glancing longingly back at the open escalator. Nothing's wrong at all, he told himself, walking back toward the elevator. As a boy, Langdon had fallen down an old well and almost died struggling in the water in the small space for hours before being rescued. Since then, he'd had a strong fear of enclosed spaces—elevators, subways, squash courts. The elevator is perfectly safe, Langdon kept telling himself, never believing it. It's a small metal box hanging in a closed shaft! Holding his breath, he stepped into the elevator, feeling the familiar rush of adrenaline as the doors closed. Two floors. Ten seconds.

"You and Mr. Saunière," Fache said as the elevator started moving, "you never spoke at all? Never wrote to each other? Never mailed anything to each other?"

Another strange question. Langdon shook his head. "No. Never." Fache tilted his head, as if making a mental note of that. Saying nothing, he stared straight ahead at the shiny doors. As they went up, Langdon tried to focus on anything but the four walls around him. In the reflection of the shiny elevator door, he saw the captain's tie clip—a silver cross with thirteen black stones. Langdon was a little surprised. The symbol was known as a crux gemmata —a cross with thirteen gems—a Christian symbol for Christ and His twelve apostles. Langdon hadn't expected the captain of the French police to show his religion so openly. But then, this was France; Christianity wasn't just a religion here, but something people were born into.

"It's a crux gemmata," Fache said suddenly.

Startled, Langdon looked up and saw Fache watching him in the reflection. The elevator stopped with a jerk, and the doors opened.

Langdon quickly stepped out into the hallway, wanting the open space of the Louvre galleries with their high ceilings. But the world he stepped into was not what he expected.

Surprised, Langdon stopped.

Fache glanced over. "I assume, Mr. Langdon, you've never seen the Louvre after hours?" I guess not, Langdon thought, trying to get his bearings.

Usually brightly lit, the Louvre galleries were surprisingly dark tonight. Instead of the usual bright white light from above, a soft red glow seemed to come from the baseboards—patches of red light spreading onto the tile floors. As Langdon looked down the dark corridor, he realized he should have expected this. Almost all major galleries used red service lighting at night—low-level, non-intrusive lights that helped staff move around while keeping the paintings in relative darkness to reduce fading from too much light. Tonight, the museum felt almost oppressive. Long shadows were everywhere, and the high vaulted ceilings seemed like a low, black void.

"This way," Fache said, turning sharply right and heading through a series of connected galleries.

Langdon followed, his eyes slowly getting used to the dark. All around, huge oil paintings began to appear like photos developing in a giant darkroom... their eyes watching him as he moved through the rooms. He could smell the familiar scent of museum air—a dry, purified smell with a slight hint of carbon—created by industrial dehumidifiers that ran constantly to fight the damaging carbon dioxide breathed out by visitors. High on the walls, the visible security cameras sent a clear message: We see you. Do not touch anything.

"Any of them real?" Langdon asked, pointing to the cameras.

Fache shook his head. "Of course not."

Langdon wasn't surprised. Video surveillance in museums this big was too expensive and didn't work well. With so many galleries to watch, the Louvre would need hundreds of technicians just to watch the feeds. Most large museums now used "containment security." Instead of keeping thieves out, keep them in. Containment started after hours. If someone stole a piece of art, exits would seal around that gallery, and the thief would be trapped before the police arrived.

The sound of voices echoed down the marble hallway ahead. The noise seemed to be coming from a large alcove up ahead on the right. A bright light shone into the hallway.

"Office of the curator," the captain said.

As he and Fache got closer to the alcove, Langdon looked down a short hallway into Saunière's fancy study—warm wood, Old Master paintings, and a huge antique desk with a two-foot-tall model of a knight in armor on it. Several police agents were in the room, talking on phones and taking notes. One was sitting at Saunière's desk, typing on a laptop. The curator's private office had become the DCPJ's temporary command center for the evening.

"Messieurs," Fache called out, and the men turned. "Do not disturb us under any circumstances. Understood?"

Everyone inside the office nodded.

Langdon had seen enough DO NOT DISTURB signs on hotel doors to understand the captain's orders. Fache and Langdon were not to be bothered under any circumstances. Leaving the small group of agents behind, Fache led Langdon farther down the dark hallway. Thirty yards ahead was the entrance to the Louvre's most popular section—la Grande Galerie—a seemingly endless corridor that held the Louvre's most valuable Italian masterpieces. Langdon already knew that this was where Saunière's body was; the Grand Gallery's famous parquet floor had been easy to recognize in the Polaroid.

As they got closer, Langdon saw the entrance was blocked by a huge steel grate that looked like something medieval castles used to keep out attacking armies.

"Containment security," Fache said, as they approached the grate. Even in the darkness, the barrier looked like it could stop a tank. Reaching it, Langdon peered through the bars into the dimly lit spaces of the Grand Gallery.

"After you, Mr. Langdon," Fache said.

Langdon turned. After me, where?

Fache pointed toward the floor at the base of the grate.

Langdon looked down. In the darkness, he hadn't noticed. The barrier was raised about two feet, leaving a tight space underneath.

"This area is still off limits to Louvre security," Fache said. "My team from Police Technique et Scientifique has just finished their investigation." He pointed to the opening. "Please slide under." Langdon stared at the narrow crawl space at his feet and then up at the massive iron grate. He's kidding, right? The barrier looked like a guillotine waiting to crush anyone who tried to get through. Fache grumbled something in French and checked his watch. Then he dropped to his knees and crawled his large frame underneath the grate. On the other side, he stood up and looked back through the bars at Langdon.

Langdon sighed. Placing his palms flat on the polished parquet, he lay on his stomach and pulled himself forward. As he slid underneath, the back of his Harris tweed jacket snagged on the bottom of the grate, and he bumped the back of his head on the iron.

Very smooth, Robert, he thought, struggling and then finally pulling himself through. As he stood up, Langdon was beginning to think it was going to be a very long night.
