The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 44

"Ten digits," Sophie said, her code-breaking instincts kicking in as she looked at the printout. 13-3-2-21-1-1-8-5

Grand-père wrote his account number on the Louvre floor!

When Sophie first saw the mixed-up Fibonacci sequence on the floor, she thought it was just to get DCPJ to call in their code experts and get Sophie involved. Later, she understood the numbers also helped decode the other lines—a sequence mixed up... a number puzzle. Now, completely surprised, she saw the numbers meant even more. They were almost definitely the final key to opening her grandfather's secret safe-deposit box.

"He loved hidden meanings," Sophie said, turning to Langdon. "He loved things with many layers. Codes inside codes."

Langdon was already heading to the electronic stand near the conveyor belt. Sophie took the computer printout and followed.

The stand had a keypad like a bank ATM. The screen showed the bank's cross-shaped logo. Next to the keypad was a triangle-shaped hole. Sophie quickly put her key into the hole.

The screen changed right away.

ACCOUNT NUMBER: _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _ _

The cursor blinked. Waiting.

Ten digits. Sophie read the numbers from the printout, and Langdon typed them in. ACCOUNT NUMBER: 1332211185

When he had typed the last digit, the screen refreshed again. A message in several languages appeared. English was at the top.

CAUTION:

Before you press the enter key, please check that your account number is correct.

For your own security, if the computer does not recognize your account number, this system will automatically shut down.

"Fonction terminer," Sophie said, frowning. "Looks like we only get one try." Standard ATM machines allowed users three attempts to type a PIN before taking their bank card. This was obviously no ordinary cash machine.

"The number looks right," Langdon confirmed, carefully checking what they had typed and comparing it to the printout. He pointed to the ENTER key. "Go ahead." Sophie reached her index finger toward the keypad, but hesitated, a strange thought now occurring to her.

"Go ahead," Langdon urged. "Vernet will be back soon."

"No." She pulled her hand away. "This isn't the right account number."

"Of course it is! Ten digits. What else would it be?"

"It's too random."

Too random? Langdon couldn't disagree more. Every bank told its customers to pick random PINs so nobody could guess them. Surely clients here would be told to pick their account numbers at random.

Sophie deleted what she had typed and looked up at Langdon, her gaze confident.

"It's too much of a coincidence that this supposedly random account number could be rearranged to make the Fibonacci sequence."

Langdon saw her point. Earlier, Sophie had rearranged this account number into the Fibonacci sequence. What were the chances of that?

Sophie was at the keypad again, entering a different number, as if she remembered it. "Plus, with my grandfather's love of symbols and codes, it makes sense he would have picked an account number that meant something to him, something easy to remember." She finished typing and smiled slyly. "Something that seemed random... but wasn't." Langdon looked at the screen.

ACCOUNT NUMBER: 1123581321

It took him a second, but when Langdon saw it, he knew she was right. The Fibonacci sequence.

1-1-2-3-5-8-13-21

When the Fibonacci sequence was made into one ten-digit number, it was almost impossible to recognize. Easy to remember, but seemingly random. A smart ten-digit code that Saunière would never forget. Also, it perfectly explained why the mixed-up numbers on the Louvre floor could be put back together to make the famous sequence.

Sophie reached down and pressed the ENTER key.

Nothing happened.

At least, nothing they could see.

At that moment, below them, in the bank's huge underground vault, a robotic claw started moving. Sliding on a track attached to the ceiling, the claw went to find the right spot. On the cement floor below, hundreds of identical plastic crates were lined up on a large grid... like rows of small coffins in an underground tomb. Stopping over the right spot on the floor, the claw reached down, an electric eye checking the bar code on the box. Then, with computer precision, the claw grabbed the heavy handle and lifted the crate straight up. New gears started, and the claw moved the box to the other side of the vault, stopping over a still conveyor belt. Carefully, the retrieval arm set the crate down and pulled back.

Once the arm was out of the way, the conveyor belt started moving....

Upstairs, Sophie and Langdon let out a breath of relief when they saw the conveyor belt move. Standing by the belt, they felt like tired travelers at baggage claim, waiting for a mysterious piece of luggage with unknown contents.

The conveyor belt came into the room on their right through a narrow opening under a door that could slide up. The metal door slid up, and a huge plastic box appeared, coming out of the depths on the rising conveyor belt. The box was black, heavy plastic, and much bigger than she thought. It looked like an air-freight pet carrier without any airholes.

The box rolled to a stop right in front of them.

Langdon and Sophie stood there, quiet, staring at the strange container. Like everything else about this bank, this crate was industrial—metal clasps, a bar code sticker on top, and a strong, molded handle. Sophie thought it looked like a giant toolbox. Without delay, Sophie unhooked the two buckles facing her. Then she looked over at Langdon. Together, they raised the heavy lid and let it drop back.

Stepping forward, they looked down into the crate.

At first, Sophie thought the crate was empty. Then she saw something. Sitting at the bottom of the crate. A single item.

The polished wooden box was about the size of a shoebox and had fancy hinges. The wood was a shiny, deep purple with a strong grain. Rosewood, Sophie realized. Her grandfather's favorite. The lid had a beautiful design of a rose inlaid in it. She and Langdon exchanged puzzled looks. Sophie leaned in and grabbed the box, lifting it out.

My God, it's heavy!

She carried it carefully to a large table and set it down. Langdon stood beside her, both of them staring at the small treasure chest her grandfather had apparently sent them to get. Langdon stared in wonder at the lid's hand-carved inlay—a five-petal rose. He had seen this type of rose many times. "The five-petal rose," he whispered, "is a Priory symbol for the Holy Grail."

Sophie turned and looked at him. Langdon could see what she was thinking, and he was thinking it too. The size of the box, the obvious weight of its contents, and a Priory symbol for the Grail all seemed to point to one unbelievable idea. The Cup of Christ is in this wooden box. Langdon again told himself it was impossible.

"It's a perfect size," Sophie whispered, "to hold... a chalice." It can't be a chalice.

Sophie pulled the box toward her across the table, getting ready to open it. As she moved it, though, something unexpected happened. The box made a strange gurgling sound.

Langdon looked again, surprised. Is there liquid inside?

Sophie looked just as confused. "Did you hear that too...?"

Langdon nodded, puzzled. "Liquid."

Reaching out, Sophie carefully unfastened the clasp and lifted the lid.

The thing inside was like nothing Langdon had ever seen. But one thing was obvious to both of them right away. This was definitely not the Cup of Christ.
