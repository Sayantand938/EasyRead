The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 10

Silas sat in the black Audi the Teacher had gotten for him and looked at the large Church of Saint-Sulpice. Lights below made the church's two bell towers look like strong guards above the building. On each side, dark rows of supports stuck out like the ribs of a beautiful animal.

The ungodly used a house of God to hide their keystone. Once again, the brotherhood had shown their famous ability to trick and lie. Silas was eager to find the keystone and give it to the Teacher so they could get back what the brotherhood had taken from the believers a long time ago.

That will make Opus Dei so powerful.

Silas parked the Audi on the empty Place Saint-Sulpice and sighed, telling himself to focus on what he needed to do. His back still hurt from the physical pain he had put himself through earlier, but the pain was nothing compared to the suffering he felt before Opus Dei had saved him.

Still, the memories bothered him.

Let go of your hate, Silas told himself. Forgive those who hurt you. Looking up at the stone towers of Saint-Sulpice, Silas fought the feeling... the force that often pulled his mind back to the past, trapping him in the prison that had been his world as a young man. The memories of suffering came as they always did, like a storm to his senses... the smell of rotting cabbage, the stink of death, human urine and feces. The cries of hopelessness against the strong wind of the Pyrenees and the quiet sobs of forgotten men. Andorra, he thought, feeling his muscles tense.

It was in that empty and lonely place between Spain and France, shivering in his stone cell, wanting to die, that Silas had been saved.

He didn't know it then.

The light came long after the storm.

His name wasn't Silas then, though he couldn't remember the name his parents had given him. He'd left home at seven. His father, a big, drunk dockworker, would beat his mother because he was angry about having a son with albinism, blaming her for the boy's shameful condition. When the boy tried to protect her, he was beaten badly too.

One night, they had a terrible fight, and his mother never woke up. The boy stood over her body and felt crushing guilt for letting it happen. This is my fault!

As if controlled by a demon, the boy walked to the kitchen and grabbed a butcher knife. Like in a trance, he went to the bedroom where his father lay passed out drunk. Without a word, the boy stabbed him in the back. His father cried out and tried to turn over, but his son stabbed him again and again until the apartment was silent. The boy ran from home but found the streets of Marseilles just as cruel. His strange looks made him an outcast among the other young runaways, and he had to live alone in the basement of a crumbling factory, eating stolen fruit and raw fish from the docks. His only friends were old, torn magazines he found in the trash, and he taught himself to read. He grew stronger over time. When he was twelve, another runaway—a girl twice his age—made fun of him in the street and tried to steal his food. The girl was beaten almost to death. When the police pulled the boy off her, they told him he had to leave Marseilles or go to juvenile prison.

The boy moved down the coast to Toulon. After a while, the pitying looks on the streets turned to fear. The boy had grown into a strong young man. When people walked by, he could hear them whispering to each other. A ghost, they'd say, eyes wide with fear as they stared at his white skin. A ghost with the devil's eyes!

And he felt like a ghost... see-through... drifting from port to port. People seemed to look right through him.

At eighteen, in a port town, he was caught by two crewmen while trying to steal a case of cured ham from a cargo ship. The sailors who started hitting him smelled of beer, just like his father had. The memories of fear and hate rose up like a monster from the deep. The young man broke the first sailor's neck with his bare hands, and only the arrival of the police saved the second sailor from the same fate.

Two months later, in chains, he arrived at a prison in Andorra.

You're as white as a ghost, the inmates mocked as the guards led him in, naked and cold. Look at the ghost! Maybe the ghost will float right through these walls!

Over twelve years, his body and soul wasted away until he knew he'd become see-through.

I am a ghost.

I am weightless.

Yo soy un espectro... palido coma una fantasma... caminando este mundo a solas. One night the ghost woke up to the screams of other prisoners. He didn't know what unseen force was shaking the floor he slept on, or what powerful hand was trembling the stone walls of his cell, but as he jumped up, a large rock fell onto the spot where he had been sleeping. Looking up to see where the stone came from, he saw a hole in the shaking wall, and beyond it, a sight he had not seen in over ten years: the moon. Even while the earth still shook, the ghost found himself crawling through a narrow tunnel, stumbling out into a wide open view, and falling down a bare mountainside into the woods. He ran all night, always downward, lightheaded with hunger and exhaustion. Barely conscious, he found himself at dawn in a clearing where train tracks cut across the forest. Following the rails, he moved on as if in a dream. Seeing an empty freight car, he crawled in for shelter and rest. When he woke up, the train was moving. How long? How far?

A pain was growing in his stomach. Am I dying? He slept again. This time he woke up to someone yelling, hitting him, throwing him out of the freight car. Bloody, he wandered the edge of a small village, looking for food but finding none. Finally, his body too weak to take another step, he lay down by the side of the road and lost consciousness.

The light came slowly, and the ghost wondered how long he had been dead. A day? Three days? It didn't matter. His bed was soft like a cloud, and the air around him smelled sweetly of candles. Jesus was there, staring down at him. I am here, Jesus said. The stone has been rolled away, and you are born again.

He slept and woke up. Confusion clouded his thoughts. He had never believed in heaven, and yet Jesus was watching over him. Food appeared beside his bed, and the ghost ate it, almost able to feel his body getting stronger. He slept again. When he woke up, Jesus was still smiling down, speaking. You are saved, my son. Blessed are those who follow my path. Again, he slept.

It was a scream of pain that startled the ghost from his sleep. His body jumped out of bed, stumbled down a hallway toward the sounds of shouting. He went into a kitchen and saw a large man hitting a smaller man. Without knowing why, the ghost grabbed the large man and threw him backward against a wall. The man ran away, leaving the ghost standing over the body of a young man in priest's robes. The priest had a badly broken nose. Lifting the bloody priest, the ghost carried him to a couch.

"Thank you, my friend," the priest said in clumsy French. "The collection money is tempting for thieves. You speak French in your sleep. Do you also speak Spanish?" The ghost shook his head.

"What is your name?" he continued in broken French.

The ghost could not remember the name his parents had given him. All he heard were the mocking words of the prison guards.

The priest smiled. "No hay problema. My name is Manuel Aringarosa. I am a missionary from Madrid. I was sent here to build a church for the Obra de Dios."

"Where am I?" His voice sounded empty.

"Oviedo. In northern Spain."

"How did I get here?"

"Someone left you at my door. You were sick. I fed you. You've been here many days." The ghost looked closely at the young man who cared for him. It had been years since anyone had been kind to him.

"Thank you, Father."

The priest touched his bleeding lip. "I am the one who is thankful, my friend." When the ghost woke up the next morning, his mind felt clearer. He looked up at the cross on the wall above his bed. Even though it no longer spoke to him, he felt a peaceful feeling near it. He sat up and was surprised to see a newspaper clipping on his bedside table. The article was in French and a week old. As he read the story, he became scared. It said that an earthquake in the mountains had destroyed a prison and let many dangerous criminals escape. His heart started to race. The priest knows who I am! The feeling he had was one he hadn't felt in a long time: shame, guilt, and the fear of being caught. He jumped out of bed. Where can I run?

"The Book of Acts," a voice said from the doorway.

The ghost turned, startled.

The young priest smiled as he came in. His nose was clumsily bandaged, and he held out an old Bible. "I found one in French for you. The chapter is marked." Hesitantly, the ghost took the Bible and looked at the chapter the priest had marked: Acts 16.

The verses told of a prisoner named Silas, who was naked and beaten in his cell, singing hymns to God. When the ghost reached Verse 26, he gasped in surprise.

"... And suddenly, there was a great earthquake, so that the prison's base shook, and all the doors opened."

His eyes shot up at the priest.

The priest smiled warmly. "From now on, my friend, if you have no other name, I shall call you Silas."

The ghost nodded blankly. Silas. He had been given flesh. My name is Silas.

"It's time for breakfast," the priest said. "You will need your strength if you are to help me build this church."

Twenty thousand feet above the Mediterranean, Alitalia flight 1618 bounced in turbulence, causing passengers to shift nervously. Bishop Aringarosa barely noticed. His thoughts were with the future of Opus Dei. Eager to know how plans in Paris were progressing, he wished he could phone Silas. But he could not. The Teacher had seen to that.

"It is for your own safety," the Teacher had explained, speaking in English with a French accent. "I know enough about electronic communications to know they can be intercepted. The results could be disastrous for you."

Aringarosa knew he was right. The Teacher seemed an exceptionally careful man. He had not revealed his own identity to Aringarosa, and yet he had proven himself a man well worth obeying. After all, he had somehow obtained very secret information. The names of the brotherhood's four top members! This had been one of the coups that convinced the bishop the Teacher was truly capable of delivering the astonishing prize he claimed he could unearth.

"Bishop," the Teacher had told him, "I have made all the arrangements. For my plan to succeed, you must allow Silas to answer only to me for several days. The two of you will not speak. I will communicate with him through secure channels."

"You will treat him with respect?"

"A man of faith deserves the best."

"Excellent. Then I understand. Silas and I will not speak until this is over."

"I do this to protect who you are, who Silas is, and what I've put into this."

"What you've put into this?"

"Bishop, if your own eagerness to know what's happening gets you in jail, then you won't be able to pay me my fee."

The bishop smiled. "A good point. We want the same thing. Godspeed." Twenty million euro, the bishop thought, now looking out the plane's window. The sum was about the same number of U.S. dollars. A small price for something so powerful. He felt sure again that the Teacher and Silas would succeed. Money and faith were strong motivators.
