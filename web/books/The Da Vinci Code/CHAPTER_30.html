The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 30

Security warden Claude Grouard fumed as he stood over his captive in front of the Mona Lisa. This bastard killed Jacques Saunière! Saunière had been like a father to Grouard and his security team.

Grouard wanted nothing more than to pull the trigger and shoot Robert Langdon in the back. As senior warden, Grouard was one of the few guards who carried a loaded weapon. He reminded himself that killing Langdon would be a generous fate compared to the misery Bezu Fache and the French prison system would inflict.

Grouard yanked his walkie-talkie off his belt and tried to radio for backup. All he heard was static. The extra security in this room always messed with the guards' communications. I have to move to the doorway. Still aiming his weapon at Langdon, Grouard began backing slowly toward the entrance. On his third step, he saw something that made him stop.

What the hell is that!

An odd mirage was appearing near the center of the room. A silhouette. Was there someone else in the room? A woman was moving through the darkness, walking quickly toward the far left wall. In front of her, a purplish beam of light swung back and forth across the floor, as if she were searching for something with a colored flashlight.

"Qui est là?" Grouard demanded, his adrenaline spiking again. He suddenly didn't know where to aim his gun or which way to move.

"PTS," the woman replied calmly, still scanning the floor with her light. Police Technique et Scientifique. Grouard was sweating now. I thought all the agents were gone!

He now recognized the purple light as ultraviolet, like a PTS team's, but he couldn't understand why DCPJ would be looking for evidence in here.

"Your name!" Grouard yelled, his gut telling him something was wrong. "Answer!"

"It's me," the voice answered calmly in French. "Sophie Neveu." Somewhere in the back of Grouard's mind, the name clicked. Sophie Neveu? That was Saunière's granddaughter, wasn't it? She used to visit as a kid, but that was years ago. It couldn't be her! And even if it was Sophie Neveu, that wasn't a reason to trust her; Grouard had heard about the bad falling-out between Saunière and his granddaughter.

"You know me," the woman said. "And Robert Langdon didn't kill my grandfather. Trust me."

Warden Grouard wasn't buying that. I need help! He tried his walkie-talkie again but only got static. The entrance was still twenty yards behind him, and Grouard started backing up slowly, keeping his gun aimed at the man on the floor. As Grouard moved back, he saw the woman across the room raising her UV light and looking closely at a large painting on the far side of the Salle des Etats, right across from the Mona Lisa. Grouard gasped, realizing which painting it was.

What is she doing?

Across the room, Sophie Neveu felt cold sweat on her forehead. Langdon was still spread out on the floor. Hang on, Robert. Almost there. Knowing the guard wouldn't shoot them, Sophie focused on the task, scanning the area around one masterpiece—another Da Vinci. But the UV light showed nothing. Not on the floor, walls, or the canvas. There has to be something here!

Sophie was sure she had understood her grandfather's intentions. What else could he mean?

The painting was five feet tall. Da Vinci's scene showed a strangely posed Virgin Mary with Baby Jesus, John the Baptist, and the Angel Uriel on rocks. When Sophie was a kid, she always visited this painting with her grandfather after seeing the Mona Lisa.

Grandfather, I'm here! But I don't see it!

Behind her, Sophie heard the guard trying to radio for help again. Think!

She pictured the message scrawled on the protective glass of the Mona Lisa. How dark the tricks of man. The painting before her had no protective glass to write on, and Sophie knew her grandfather would never have ruined this masterpiece by writing on the painting itself. She paused. At least not on the front. Her eyes shot upward, following the long cables hanging from the ceiling that held the canvas.

Could that be it? Grabbing the left side of the carved wood frame, she pulled it toward her. The painting was large, and the backing bent as she swung it away from the wall. Sophie squeezed her head and shoulders in behind the painting and raised the black light to check the back. It only took seconds to know she was wrong. The back of the painting was pale and blank. There was no purple text here, only the faded brown backside of old canvas and—

Wait.

Sophie's eyes stopped on a strange glint of shiny metal stuck near the bottom edge of the frame. The object was small, partly stuck in the crack where the canvas met the frame. A shimmering gold chain hung from it.

To Sophie's surprise, the chain was attached to a familiar gold key. The large, carved head was in the shape of a cross and had an engraved seal she had not seen since she was nine years old. A fleur-de-lis with the initials P.S. Right then, Sophie felt like her grandfather was whispering in her ear. When the time comes, the key will be yours. Her throat tightened as she realized that her grandfather, even in death, had kept his promise. This key opens a box, his voice was saying, where I keep many secrets.

Sophie now understood that the whole point of tonight's word game had been this key. Her grandfather had it with him when he was killed. Not wanting it to fall into the hands of the police, he hid it behind this painting. Then he created a clever treasure hunt to make sure only Sophie would find it.

"Au secours!" the guard yelled.

Sophie grabbed the key from behind the painting and slipped it deep in her pocket with the UV penlight. Peeking out from behind the canvas, she could see the guard was still trying hard to reach someone on the walkie-talkie. He was moving backward toward the entrance, still pointing the gun at Langdon.

"Au secours!" he shouted again into his radio.

Static.

He can't transmit, Sophie realized, remembering that tourists with cell phones often got frustrated in here when they tried to call home to brag about seeing the Mona Lisa. The extra surveillance wiring in the walls made it nearly impossible to get a signal unless you stepped out into the hall. The guard was backing quickly toward the exit now, and Sophie knew she had to act immediately. Gazing up at the large painting behind which she was partially hidden, Sophie realized that Leonardo da Vinci, for the second time tonight, was there to help.

Another few meters, Grouard told himself, keeping his gun pointed.

"Stop! Or I'll destroy it!" the woman's voice echoed across the room. Grouard glanced over and stopped in his tracks. "My God, no!" Through the reddish haze, he could see that the woman had actually lifted the large painting off its cables and propped it on the floor in front of her. At five feet tall, the canvas almost entirely hid her body. Grouard's first thought was to wonder why the painting's trip wires hadn't set off alarms, but of course the artwork cable sensors had yet to be reset tonight. What is she doing!

When he saw it, his blood went cold.

The canvas started to bulge in the middle, the fragile outlines of the Virgin Mary, Baby Jesus, and John the Baptist beginning to distort.

"No!" Grouard screamed, frozen in horror as he watched the priceless Da Vinci stretching. The woman was pushing her knee into the center of the canvas from behind! "NO!" Grouard wheeled and aimed his gun at her but instantly realized it was an empty threat. The canvas was only fabric, but it was utterly impenetrable—a six-million-dollar piece of body armor. I can't put a bullet through a Da Vinci!

"Put down your gun and radio," the woman said in calm French, "or I'll put my knee through this painting. I think you know how my grandfather would feel about that." Grouard felt dizzy. "Please... no. That's Madonna of the Rocks!" He dropped his gun and radio, raising his hands over his head.

"Thank you," the woman said. "Now do exactly as I tell you, and everything will work out fine."

Moments later, Langdon's pulse was still pounding as he ran beside Sophie down the emergency stairwell toward the ground level. Neither of them had said a word since leaving the trembling Louvre guard lying in the Salle des Etats. The guard's pistol was now clutched tightly in Langdon's hands, and he couldn't wait to get rid of it. The weapon felt heavy and dangerously foreign. Taking the stairs two at a time, Langdon wondered if Sophie had any idea how valuable a painting she had almost ruined. Her choice in art seemed strangely relevant to tonight's adventure. The Da Vinci she had grabbed, much like the Mona Lisa, was famous among art historians for its many hidden pagan symbols.

"You chose a valuable hostage," he said as they ran.

"Madonna of the Rocks," she replied. "But I didn't pick it; my grandfather did. He left me a little something behind the painting."

Langdon looked surprised. "What!? But how did you know which painting? Why Madonna of the Rocks?"

"So dark the con of man." She smiled, pleased with herself. "I missed the first two anagrams, Robert. I wasn't going to miss the third."
