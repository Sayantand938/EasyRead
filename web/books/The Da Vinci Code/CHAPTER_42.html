The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 42

The Depository Bank of Zurich was a twenty-four-hour vault bank offering a full range of modern, anonymous services in the tradition of Swiss numbered accounts. With offices in Zurich, Kuala Lumpur, New York, and Paris, the bank had recently expanded to offer anonymous computer code protection and faceless digital backup. But the main part of their business was their oldest and simplest service—the anonymous storage—blind drop services, also known as anonymous safe-deposit boxes. Clients could store anything from stock certificates to valuable paintings anonymously, through layers of high-tech privacy, and withdraw items at any time, also anonymously.

As Sophie parked the taxi, Langdon looked at the building's harsh design and felt the Depository Bank of Zurich was not a fun place. The building was a windowless rectangle made entirely of dull steel. Like a huge metal brick, it sat back from the road with a fifteen-foot-tall, neon cross glowing over the front.

Switzerland's reputation for secret banking had become one of its most profitable exports. Places like this had become controversial in the art world because they were perfect for hiding stolen art, sometimes for years, until things cooled down. Because deposits were protected from police searches by privacy laws and were linked to numbered accounts instead of names, thieves could relax knowing their stolen goods were safe and untraceable.

Sophie stopped the taxi at a large gate blocking the bank's driveway—a concrete ramp that went under the building. A video camera pointed at them, and Langdon felt this camera was real, unlike the ones at the Louvre. Sophie rolled down the window and looked at the electronic stand on the driver's side. An LCD screen had instructions in seven languages, with English at the top: INSERT KEY.

Sophie took the gold, laser-etched key from her pocket and looked back at the stand. Below the screen was a triangular hole.

"I bet that fits," Langdon said.

Sophie lined up the key's triangular part with the hole and slid it in until it disappeared. The key didn't need to be turned. The gate immediately began to open. Sophie took her foot off the brake and rolled down to a second gate and stand. The first gate closed behind them, trapping them like a ship in a lock.

Langdon didn't like feeling trapped. Hopefully this second gate works too. This second stand had the same instructions.

INSERT KEY.

When Sophie put in the key, the second gate opened right away. Soon after, they were driving down the ramp into the building's depths.

The private garage was small and dark, with room for about twelve cars. At the far end, Langdon saw the building's main entrance. A red carpet ran across the concrete floor, inviting people to a huge door that looked like it was made of solid metal.

That's confusing, Langdon thought. Welcome, but stay away. Sophie parked the taxi near the entrance and turned off the engine. "You should leave the gun here."

Gladly, Langdon thought, sliding the gun under the seat. Sophie and Langdon got out and walked up the red carpet toward the metal door. The door had no handle, but on the wall next to it was another triangular keyhole. There were no instructions this time.

"Keeps out the dummies," Langdon said.

Sophie laughed, looking worried. "Here we go." She put the key in the hole, and the door swung open with a low hum. Sophie and Langdon looked at each other and went inside. The door slammed shut behind them.

The entrance hall of the Depository Bank of Zurich had decorations as grand as any Langdon had ever seen. While most banks were happy with regular shiny marble and granite, this one had chosen metal and rivets all over the walls.

Who decorated this place? Langdon wondered. A steel company?

Sophie looked just as scared as she looked around the lobby.

The gray metal was everywhere—the floor, walls, counters, doors, even the lobby chairs seemed to be made of molded iron. Still, the effect was impressive. The message was clear: You are walking into a vault.

A large man behind the counter looked up as they entered. He turned off the small television he was watching and greeted them with a pleasant smile. Despite his huge muscles and visible gun, his speech was as polite as a Swiss bellhop.

"Bonsoir," he said. "How may I help you?"

The greeting in two languages was the latest hospitality trick of European hosts. It assumed nothing and allowed the guest to reply in whichever language was easier. Sophie replied in neither. She simply laid the gold key on the counter in front of the man. The man glanced down and immediately stood up straighter. "Of course. Your elevator is at the end of the hall. I will let someone know you are on your way."

Sophie nodded and took her key back. "Which floor?"

The man gave her an odd look. "Your key tells the elevator which floor." She smiled. "Ah, yes."

The guard watched as the two newcomers walked to the elevators, put in their key, got on the lift, and disappeared. As soon as the door had closed, he grabbed the phone. He was not calling to let anyone know they were here; there was no need for that. A vault greeter had already been alerted automatically when the client's key was inserted outside at the entry gate. Instead, the guard was calling the bank's night manager. As the line rang, the guard switched the television back on and stared at it. The news story he had been watching was just ending. It didn't matter. He got another look at the two faces on the television.

The manager answered. "Oui?"

"We have a situation down here."

"What's happening?" the manager demanded.

"The French police are chasing two people tonight."

"So?"

"They just walked into our bank."

The manager cursed softly. "Okay. I'll call Monsieur Vernet now." The guard hung up and made another call. This one to Interpol.

Langdon was surprised the elevator was going down, not up. He didn't know how many floors they'd gone below the Zurich Depository Bank before the door opened. He didn't care. He was glad to be out of the elevator.

A host was already there to meet them, moving quickly. He was old and kind, wearing a neat suit that looked strange—an old-fashioned banker in a modern world.

"Bonsoir," the man said. "Good evening. Please, follow me." He turned and walked quickly down a narrow metal hall without waiting for an answer.

Langdon and Sophie walked down halls, past large rooms full of flashing computers.

"Voici," their host said, stopping at a steel door and opening it. "Here you are." Langdon and Sophie entered a different world. The small room looked like a fancy hotel lounge. The metal was gone, replaced by rugs, dark wood furniture, and soft chairs. On the large desk, two glasses sat next to an open bottle of Perrier, still bubbling. A pewter coffee pot steamed nearby.

Typical, Langdon thought. Only the Swiss.

The man smiled knowingly. "I guess this is your first time visiting us?" Sophie hesitated and then nodded.

"I understand. Keys are often passed down in families, and first-time users are always unsure what to do." He pointed to the table of drinks. "This room is yours to use as long as you like."

"You said keys are sometimes inherited?" Sophie asked.

"Yes. Your key is like a Swiss bank account, often passed down through generations. For our gold accounts, the shortest safe-deposit box rental is fifty years. Paid in advance. So, we see a lot of family changes."

Langdon stared. "Did you say fifty years?"

"At least," their host replied. "Of course, you can rent for much longer, but unless other arrangements are made, if there's no activity on an account for fifty years, the contents of the safe-deposit box are automatically destroyed. Should I explain how to access your box?" Sophie nodded. "Please."

Their host gestured around the fancy room. "This is your private viewing room. Once I leave, you can spend as much time as you need in here to look at and change the contents of your safe-deposit box, which arrives... over here." He led them to the far wall where a wide conveyor belt curved into the room, looking a bit like a baggage claim carousel.

"You put your key in that slot there...." The man pointed to a large electronic stand facing the conveyor belt. The stand had a familiar triangular hole. "Once the computer confirms your key, you enter your account number, and your safe-deposit box will be brought up robotically from the vault below for you to examine. When you're done, you place the box back on the conveyor belt, insert your key again, and the process is reversed. Because it's all automated, your privacy is guaranteed, even from the bank staff. If you need anything, just press the call button on the table in the center of the room." Sophie was about to ask a question when a telephone rang. The man looked surprised and a little embarrassed. "Excuse me, please." He walked over to the phone, which was on the table next to the coffee and Perrier.

"Oui?" he answered.

He frowned as he listened. "Oui... oui... d'accord." He hung up and gave them an uneasy smile. "I'm sorry, I have to leave now. Make yourselves at home." He hurried toward the door.

"Excuse me," Sophie called. "Could you explain something before you go? You said we need an account number?"

The man stopped at the door, looking pale. "Of course. Like most Swiss banks, our safe-deposit boxes are linked to a number, not a name. You have a key and a personal account number that only you know. Your key is only half of your identification. Your personal account number is the other half. Otherwise, if you lost your key, anyone could use it."

Sophie hesitated. "And if my benefactor didn't give me an account number?" The banker's heart pounded. Then you shouldn't be here! He gave them a calm smile. "I'll have someone help you. He'll be here soon." Leaving, the banker closed the door behind him and turned a heavy lock, trapping them inside.

Across town, Collet was standing in the Gare du Nord train terminal when his phone rang. It was Fache. "Interpol got a tip," he said. "Forget the train. Langdon and Neveu just went into the Paris branch of the Depository Bank of Zurich. I want your men there immediately."

"Any clues yet on what Saunière was trying to tell Agent Neveu and Robert Langdon?" Fache's tone was cold. "If you arrest them, Lieutenant Collet, then I can ask them myself." Collet understood. "Twenty-four Rue Haxo. Right away, Captain." He hung up and radioed his men.
