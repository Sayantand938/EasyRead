The Da Vinci Code

CHAPTER 65

Sophie Neveu, despite working in law enforcement, had never had a gun pointed at her until tonight. It was almost unbelievable that the gun she was now staring at was held in the pale hand of a huge albino with long white hair. He looked at her with red eyes that seemed scary and unreal. He was wearing a wool robe tied with a rope, like a priest from the Middle Ages. Sophie had no idea who he was, but she suddenly felt that Teabing's idea that the Church was behind this might be right.

"You know why I'm here," the monk said, his voice empty. Sophie and Teabing were sitting on the couch, arms raised as the attacker had told them. Langdon was on the floor, moaning. The monk's eyes went straight to the keystone on Teabing's lap.

Teabing's voice was strong. "You won't be able to open it."

"My Teacher is very smart," the monk replied, moving closer, the gun moving between Teabing and Sophie.

Sophie wondered where Teabing's servant was. Didn't he hear Robert fall?

"Who is your teacher?" Teabing asked. "Maybe we can pay you off."

"The Grail is worth more than money." He came closer.

"You're bleeding," Teabing said calmly, pointing to the monk's right ankle where blood was running down his leg. "And you're limping."

"So are you," the monk replied, pointing to the metal crutches next to Teabing. "Now, give me the keystone."

"You know about the keystone?" Teabing asked, sounding surprised.

"Never mind what I know. Stand up slowly, and give it to me."

"Standing is hard for me."

"Exactly. I don't want anyone making sudden moves."

Teabing slid his right hand through one of his crutches and grabbed the keystone in his left. Stumbling to his feet, he stood up straight, holding the heavy cylinder in his left hand, and leaning unsteadily on his crutch with his right.

The monk came within a few feet, keeping the gun aimed right at Teabing's head. Sophie watched, feeling helpless as the monk reached out to take the cylinder.

"You will not succeed," Teabing said. "Only the worthy can unlock this stone."

God alone judges who is worthy, Silas thought.

"It's quite heavy," the man on crutches said, his arm shaking now. "If you don't take it soon, I'm afraid I'll drop it!" He swayed dangerously.

Silas quickly stepped forward to take the stone, and as he did, the man on crutches lost his balance. The crutch slid out from under him, and he started to fall sideways to his right. No! Silas reached out to save the stone, lowering his weapon as he did. But the keystone was moving away from him now. As the man fell to his right, his left hand swung backward, and the cylinder fell from his palm onto the couch. At the same moment, the metal crutch that had been sliding out from under the man seemed to speed up, swinging in a wide arc through the air toward Silas's leg. Sharp pain shot through Silas's body as the crutch hit his cilice perfectly, crushing the barbs into his already raw flesh. Collapsing, Silas crumpled to his knees, causing the belt to cut even deeper. The pistol fired with a loud roar, the bullet hitting harmlessly in the floorboards as Silas fell. Before he could raise the gun and fire again, the woman's foot hit him right under the jaw.

At the bottom of the driveway, Collet heard the gunshot. The muffled pop sent panic through his veins. With Fache on the way, Collet had already given up any hope of getting personal praise for finding Langdon tonight. But Collet would be damned if Fache's ego got him in front of a Ministerial Review Board for careless police work.

A weapon was fired inside a private home! And you waited at the bottom of the driveway?

Collet knew the chance for a quiet approach was long gone. He also knew if he stood still for another second, his whole career would be over by morning. Looking at the estate's iron gate, he made his decision.

"Tie on, and pull it down."

In the distant corners of his foggy mind, Robert Langdon had heard the gunshot. He'd also heard a scream of pain. Was it his? A jackhammer was drilling a hole into the back of his head. Somewhere nearby, people were talking.

"Where the devil were you?" Teabing was yelling.

The manservant hurried in. "What happened? Oh my God! Who is that? I'll call the police!"

"Bloody hell! Don't call the police. Make yourself useful and get us something to tie up this monster."

"And some ice!" Sophie called after him.

Langdon drifted off again. More voices. Movement. Now he was sitting on the divan. Sophie was holding an ice pack to his head. His skull ached. As Langdon's vision finally started to clear, he found himself staring at a body on the floor. Am I imagining things? The large body of an albino monk lay tied up and gagged with duct tape. His chin was split open, and the robe over his right thigh was soaked with blood. He too seemed to be just now waking up.

Langdon turned to Sophie. "Who is that? What... happened?"

Teabing hobbled over. "You were rescued by a knight with an Excalibur made by Acme Orthopedic."

Huh? Langdon tried to sit up.

Sophie's touch was shaky but gentle. "Just take a moment, Robert."

"I'm afraid," Teabing said, "that I've just shown your friend the upside of my disability. It seems everyone underestimates you."

From the couch, Langdon looked down at the monk and tried to picture what had happened.

"He was wearing a cilice," Teabing explained.

"A what?"

Teabing pointed to a bloody strip of barbed leather on the floor. "A Discipline belt. He wore it on his thigh. I aimed carefully."

Langdon rubbed his head. He knew about Discipline belts. "But how... did you know?" Teabing grinned. "Christianity is what I study, Robert, and some groups are very open about their beliefs." He pointed his crutch at the blood soaking the monk's cloak.

"As it were."

"Opus Dei," Langdon whispered, remembering recent news stories about several well-known Boston businessmen who belonged to Opus Dei. Worried coworkers had wrongly and publicly claimed the men wore Discipline belts under their suits. But the three men did not. Like many in Opus Dei, these men were at the

"supernumerary" level and didn't practice any physical self-punishment. They were devoted Catholics, loving fathers, and active members of their community. The media quickly moved past their faith to focus on the shocking practices of the stricter "numerary" members... like the monk on the floor.

Teabing examined the bloody belt. "Why would Opus Dei want the Holy Grail?"

Langdon was too out of it to think about it.

"Robert," Sophie said, by the wooden box. "What's this?" She held the small Rose inlay he took from the lid.

"It hid writing on the box. It might tell us how to open the keystone." Before Sophie and Teabing could speak, a wave of blue police lights and sirens appeared at the bottom of the hill and moved up the long driveway.

Teabing frowned. "Friends, we have a choice to make, and quickly."
