Project Hail Mary

Chapter 26

I sat in my cell, staring at the wall.

It wasn’t a dirty jail cell or anything. If anything, it looked like a college dorm room. Painted brick walls, desk, chair, bed, private bathroom, and so on. But the door was steel and the windows had bars. I wasn’t going anywhere.

Why did the Baikonur launch site have a jail cell ready? I don’t know. Ask the Russians.

The launch was today. Soon, some strong guards would come through that door with a doctor. He’d inject me with something, and that would be the last time I saw Earth.

Almost as if planned, I heard the door being unlocked. Someone with more courage might have seen that as a chance. Rush the door and maybe get past the guards. But I had given up hope of escape long ago. What could I do? Run into the Kazakhstan desert and take my chances?

The door opened and Stratt walked in. The guards closed the door behind her.

“Hey,” she said.

I glared at her from my bed.

“The launch is on schedule,” she said. “You’ll be leaving soon.”

“Whoopee.”

She sat in the chair. “I know you won’t believe this, but it wasn’t easy for me to do this to you.”

“Yeah, you’re really sentimental.”

She ignored the jab. “Do you know what I studied in college? What my degree was in?”

I shrugged.

“History. I was a history major.” She tapped her fingers on the desk. “Most people think I studied science or business. Or communications. But no. It was history.”

“Doesn’t seem like you.” I sat up on my bunk. “You don’t spend much time looking back.”

“I was eighteen and had no idea what to do. I majored in history because I didn’t know what else to do.” She smirked. “Hard to picture me like that, huh?”

“Yeah.”

She looked out the barred window toward the launchpad in the distance. “But I learned a lot. I actually liked it. People today…they don’t know how good they have it. The past was constant misery for most people. And the further back you go, the worse it was.”

She stood and wandered around the room. “For fifty thousand years, until the industrial revolution, human life was about one thing: food. Every culture spent most of its time, energy, people, and resources on food. Hunting, gathering, farming, storing, distributing…it was all about food.

“Even the Roman Empire. Everyone knows about the emperors, the armies, and the conquests. But what the Romans really invented was a great system for getting farmland and moving food and water.”

She walked to the other side of the room. “The industrial revolution made farming mechanical. Since then, we’ve focused on other things. But that’s only been the last two hundred years. Before that, most people spent most of their lives directly involved in food production.”

“Thanks for the history lesson,” I said. “But I’d like my last moments on Earth to be a little more pleasant, if you don’t mind. So…could you leave?”

She ignored me. “Leclerc’s Antarctica nukes bought us some time. But not much. And we can only dump so many chunks of Antarctica into the ocean before the sea-level rise and ocean-biome death cause more problems than Astrophage. Remember what Leclerc told us: Half the world's population will die.”

“I know,” I muttered.

“No, you don’t know,” she said. “Because it gets much worse.”

“Worse than half of humanity dying?”

“Of course,” she said. “Leclerc’s estimate assumes all countries will work together to share resources and ration food. But do you think that will happen? Do you think the United States—the most powerful military force ever—will sit by while half their people starve? How about China, a nation of 1.3 billion people that’s always close to famine even when things are good? Do you think they’ll just leave their weaker neighbors alone?”

I shook my head. “There’ll be wars.”

“Yes. There’ll be wars. Fought for the same reason most wars were fought long ago: food. They’d use religion or honor or whatever as an excuse, but it was always about food. Land to grow it on and people to work that land.

“But that’s not all,” she said. “Because once the desperate, starving countries start invading each other for food, the food made will go down. Ever heard of the Tai Ping rebellion? It was a civil war in China in the 1800s. Four hundred thousand soldiers died fighting. And twenty million people died from the famine that followed. The war messed up farming, see? That’s how huge these things are.”

She wrapped her arms around herself. I’d never seen her look so weak. “Starvation. Chaos. Famine. Everything needed to make and get food, and everything needed for war. All of society will fall apart. There’ll be plagues too. Many of them. Everywhere. Because the hospitals and doctors will be overwhelmed. Diseases that were once easy to control will spread.”

She turned to face me. “War, famine, disease, and death. Astrophage is the end of the world. The Hail Mary is all we have now. I’ll do anything to give it even the smallest chance to work.”

I lay down on my bunk and faced away from her. “Whatever helps you sleep.”

She walked back to the door and knocked. A guard opened it. “Anyway. I just wanted you to know why I’m doing this. I had to tell you.”

“Go to hell.”

“Oh, I will, trust me. You three are going to Tau Ceti. The rest of us are going to hell. Or, more likely, hell is coming here.”

Yeah? Well, hell’s coming back to you, Stratt. In the form of me. I am hell.

I mean…I don’t know what I’ll say to her. But I definitely plan to say stuff. Mean stuff.

I’m eighteen days into my almost four-year journey. I’m just now reaching Tau Ceti’s heliopause—the edge of the star’s strong magnetic field. At least, the edge of where it’s strong enough to block fast-moving interstellar radiation. From now on, the radiation hitting the ship will be much higher.

Doesn’t matter to me. I’m surrounded by Astrophage. But it’s interesting to see the radiation sensors rise and rise. It’s progress, at least. But in the big picture, I’m on a long road trip and my current status is “just walking out the front door.”

I’m bored. I’m alone in a spaceship with not much to do.

I clean and organize the lab again. I might come up with some research ideas for either Astrophage or Taumoeba. Heck, I could write some papers while I’m traveling home. Oh, and there’s the intelligent alien I hung out with for a couple of months. I might want to write some things down about him too.

I do have a huge collection of video games. I have every piece of software that was available when they built the ship. I’m sure they can keep me busy for a while.

I check the Taumoeba farms. All ten are doing fine. I feed them Astrophage sometimes, just to keep them healthy and breeding. The farms copy Venus’s atmosphere, so as the Taumoeba generations pass, they’ll get even better at living on Venus. After four years of this, by the time I drop them off on the planet, they’ll be well suited for it.

And yes, I’ve already decided I’ll drop them off. Why not?

I have no idea what kind of world I’ll be returning to. Thirteen years have passed on Earth since I left, and they’ll experience another thirteen before I get back. Twenty-six years. All my students will be adults. I hope they all survive. But I have to admit…some probably won’t. I try not to think about it.

Anyway, once I get back to my solar system, I might as well stop by Venus and drop off the Taumoeba. I’m not sure how I’ll release it, but I have some ideas. The easiest is just to make a ball of Astrophage filled with Taumoeba and throw it at Venus. The Astrophage will protect the Taumoeba from the heat when entering the atmosphere, and then the Taumoeba will be free. They’ll have a feast. Venus must be full of Astrophage by now, and Taumoeba can get to work as soon as they find it.

I check my food supply. I’m still on track. I have three more months of real food packs left, and then it’s coma food from then on.

I don’t want to go back into a coma. I have the genes to survive it, but so did Yáo and Ilyukhina. Why risk dying if I don’t have to?

Also, I can’t be completely sure I set the course correctly. I think it’s right, and every time I check, I’m still heading home. But what if something goes wrong while I’m in a coma? What if I wake up and I’m a light-year away from the solar system?

But with the isolation, loneliness, and bad food, I might be willing to take those risks eventually. We’ll see.

Speaking of loneliness, I start thinking about Rocky. My only friend now. Really. He’s my only friend. I didn’t have much of a social life when things were normal. Sometimes I’d have dinner with other teachers and staff at the school. I’d have a beer with old friends from college on a Saturday night sometimes. But because of time moving differently in space, when I get home, they’ll all be much older than me.

I liked Dimitri. He was probably my favorite of the Hail Mary group. But who knows what he’s doing now? Russia and the United States might be at war. Or they might be allies in a war. I have no idea.

I climb the ladder to the control room. I sit in the pilot’s seat and open the navigation panel. I shouldn’t do this, but it’s become a habit. I turn off the spin drives and float. I don’t feel gravity anymore, but I barely notice. I’m used to it.

With the spin drives off, I can use the Petrovascope safely. I look around in space for a bit—I know where to look. I find it quickly. The small light of the Petrova frequency. The Blip-A’s engines. If I were close to that light, my ship would be vaporized.

I’m on one side of the system, and he’s on the other. Tau Ceti itself looks like a lightbulb in the distance. But I can still see the Blip-A’s engine flare clearly. Using light as fuel releases an incredible amount of power.

Maybe we could use that later. Maybe Earth and Erid can talk by sending out huge bursts of Petrova light, thanks to Astrophage. I wonder how much light it would take to make a flash that you could see from 40 Eridani. We could talk in Morse code or something. They have a copy of Wikipedia now. They'd figure out what we're doing when they saw the flashes.

Still, our "conversation" would be slow. 40 Eridani is sixteen light-years from Earth. So if we sent a message like "Hey, how's it going?" it would be thirty-two years before we got their answer.

I stare at the little dot of light on the screen and sigh. I’ll be able to track him for a while. I know where his ship will be at any time. He’ll use the exact flight plan I gave him. He trusts my science as much as I trust his engineering. But after a few months, the Petrovascope won’t be able to see the light anymore. Not because the light is too weak—it’s a very sensitive instrument. It won’t be able to see him because our speeds relative to each other will cause the light from his engines to shift to a redder color. It won’t be the Petrova wavelength anymore when it gets to me.

What? Would I do a crazy amount of math about relativity to figure out our speeds relative to each other at any time, as I see it, and then use those calculations to find out when the light from his engines will disappear from the Petrovascope’s view? Just so I know how much longer I can see my friend in the distance? Wouldn’t that be kind of sad?

Yeah.

Okay, my sad little daily routine is over. I turn off the Petrovascope and turn on the spin drives again.

—

I check my shrinking supply of real food. I’ve been traveling for thirty-two days now. According to my calculations, fifty-one days from now I’ll have to rely completely on coma slurry.

I go to the dormitory. “Computer, give me a sample of the coma food.”

The mechanical arms reach into their supply area, grab a bag of white powder, and drop it on the bunk.

I pick up the bag. Of course it’s a powder. Why would they include the liquid for long-term storage? The Hail Mary's water system is a closed loop. Water goes in, comes out, is purified, and reused.

I take the package to the lab, open it, and pour some powder into a beaker.

I add a little water, stir it, and it turns into a milky-white liquid. I sniff it. It doesn’t smell like anything. So I take a sip.

It’s hard, but I don't spit it out. It tastes like aspirin. That nasty pill taste. I’m going to have to eat this Bitter Pill Chow™ every meal for years.

Maybe a coma isn’t so bad.

I set the beaker aside. I’ll deal with that later. For now, I’ll work on the beetles.

I have four little Taumoeba farms, from Rocky. Each is a steel-like capsule, small enough to hold in my hand. I say “steel-like” because it’s an Eridian steel alloy humans haven't made yet. It’s much harder than our metal alloys, but not harder than diamond tools.

We debated the mini-farm casing. Xenonite seemed like the obvious choice. But how would Earth scientists open it? Our tools couldn’t cut it. The only option would be extreme heat, which could harm the Taumoeba inside.

I suggested a xenonite container with a lid, clamped down like a pressure door. I’d leave instructions on the USB stick for safe opening. Rocky rejected that. No matter how good the seal, it wouldn't be perfect. Over the two years, enough air could leak out and suffocate the Taumoeba. He insisted on a single, sealed container. Probably a good idea.

So we picked Eridian steel. It’s strong, doesn’t rust, and lasts a long time. Earth can cut it with a diamond saw. And hey, they'll probably study it to learn how to make it themselves. Everyone wins!

His plan for the farms was simple. Inside, there’s a living group of Taumoeba and a Venus-like air. Also, there’s a coil of very thin steel tubing filled with Astrophage. The Taumoeba can only reach the outside layer, so they have to eat their way down the tube, which is about 20 meters long. Some simple tests show that will feed the small Taumoeba group for several years. As for their waste—it'll just sit in their own waste. The container will gain methane and lose carbon dioxide over time, but it doesn’t matter. Though it’s small for humans, it’s a huge, gigantic space to the tiny microbes inside.

The beetles have been a priority for me. I want them ready to launch right away. Just in case something bad happens to the Hail Mary. But I don’t want to launch them unless it’s really important. The closer we are to Earth when they launch, the better chance they have of getting there safely.

Besides installing the mini-farms, I also have to refill the little guys. I’d used almost half their fuel when they were used as engines for the Hail Mary. But they only need 60 kilograms of Astrophage each to be full. That’s hardly anything compared to my supply of Astrophage made on Eridia.

The hardest part is opening the beetle’s fuel tank. Like everything else here, it wasn’t meant to be reused. It’s like refilling a disposable lighter. It’s just not made for that. It’s completely sealed. I have to clamp it in the mill and use a 6-millimeter drill bit to get in…it’s complicated. But I’m getting better at it.

I finished John and Paul yesterday. Today I’m working on Ringo and, if I have time, George. George will be the easiest. I don’t need to refuel him—I never used him as an engine. I just have to attach the mini-farm.

Figuring out where to put the mini-farm was another issue. Even though it’s small, it’s too big to fit inside the little probe. So I glue it to the bottom. Then I spot-weld a small weight to the top of the beetle. The computer inside cares a lot about where the probe’s center of mass is. It’s easier to add a weight than to completely change the guidance system.

Which brings us to the weight.

The extra weight of the farm makes the beetles weigh a kilogram more than they should. That’s okay. I remember many meetings with Steve Hatch discussing the design. He’s a strange guy, but he’s a great rocket scientist. The beetles know where they are in space by looking at the stars, and if they have less fuel than they should, they slow down their acceleration as needed.

In short: They’ll get home. It’ll just take a little longer. I did the math and it’s a small difference in Earth time. Though the beetles will experience several extra months during the trip than originally planned.

I go to the supply cabinet and pull out the BOCOA (big container of Astrophage). It’s a metal bin with wheels that blocks light. There are several hundred kilograms of Astrophage in there, and I’m in 1.5 g’s of gravity. That’s why I added the wheels. You’d be amazed what you can do with a machine shop and really wanting not to drag heavy stuff around.

I hold the handle with a towel because it’s so hot. I wheel it over to the lab table, sit in the chair, and get ready for the careful refueling. I get the plastic syringe ready. With it, I can squirt 100 milliliters of Astrophage into that 6-millimeter hole each time. That’s about 600 grams. In total, I have to do it about two hundred times per beetle.

I open the BOCOA and—

“Ugh!” I wince and pull away from the container. It smells awful.

“Uh…” I say. “Why does it smell like that?”

Then it hits me. I know that smell. It’s the smell of dead, rotting Astrophage.

The Taumoeba are loose again.
