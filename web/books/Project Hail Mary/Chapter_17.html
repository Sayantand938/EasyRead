Project Hail Mary

Chapter 17

I wake up with Rocky looking at me.

It happens every morning now. But it never stops being weird.

How do I know that a five-sided creature with no eyes is “looking” at me? I just know. Something in the way he is.

“You awake,” he says.

“Yeah.” I get out of bed and stretch. “Food!”

The arms reach up and give me a hot box. I open it and take a look. Looks like eggs and sausage.

“Coffee.”

The arms hand me a cup of coffee like they’re supposed to. It’s kind of cool that the arms will hand me a cup when there’s gravity, but a pouch when there isn’t. I’ll remember this when I write the Hail Mary ’s review.

I look at Rocky. “You don’t have to watch me sleep. It’s okay.”

He turns to a worktable in his part of the dorm. “Eridian culture rule. Must watch.” He picks up a device and fiddles with it.

Ah, the c-word. “Culture.” We have an unspoken agreement that cultural things just have to be accepted. It ends any small argument. “Do it my way because that’s how I was raised,” basically. We haven’t had any cultural clashes…yet.

I eat my breakfast and drink my coffee. Rocky doesn’t say anything to me during that time. He never does. Eridian courtesy.

“Trash,” I say.

The arms collect my empty cup and meal container.

I go up to the control room and sit in the pilot’s seat. I bring up the telescope view on the main screen. Planet Adrian is in the center. I’ve been watching it get bigger for the past ten days. The closer we get, the more I respect Rocky’s astronomy skills. All of his observations on its motion and mass have been perfect.

Hopefully his gravity calculation is right too. Or we’ll have a very short and painful attempt to orbit.

Adrian is a pale-green planet with thin white clouds in the upper atmosphere. I can’t see the ground at all. Again, I’m amazed at the software that must be in this ship’s computers. We are spinning around as we fly through space. But the image on-screen is perfectly still.

“We’re getting close,” I say. Rocky is two floors below me, but I speak normally. I know he can hear it just fine.

“You know air yet, question?” Rocky calls out. Just as I know his hearing ability, he knows my hearing limitations.

“I’ll try again now,” I say.

I switch to the Spectrometer screen. The Hail Mary has been very reliable in almost every way, but you can’t expect perfection. The spectrometer has been acting up. I think it’s the digitizer. I’ve been trying it every day, and it keeps saying it can’t get enough data to analyze.

I focus on Adrian and try again. The closer we get, the more reflected light we’ll get, and maybe it’ll be enough for the spectrometer to tell me what Adrian’s atmosphere is made of.

ANALYZING…

ANALYZING…

ANALYZING…

ANALYSIS COMPLETE.

“It worked!” I say.

“Worked, question?!” Rocky says, his voice an octave higher than normal. He hurries through his tunnels to the control-room bulb. “What is Adrian air, question?”

I read the results off the screen. “Looks like it’s…91 percent carbon dioxide, 7 percent methane, 1 percent argon, and the rest are tiny amounts of other gases. It’s a pretty thick atmosphere too. Those are all clear gases, but I can’t see the planet’s surface.”

“You can usually see a planet's surface from space, right?”

“If the atmosphere lets light pass through, yes.”

“Human eyes are amazing. I’m jealous.”

“Well, not amazing enough. I can’t see Adrian’s surface. When the air gets really dense, light can't pass through it. But that's not important. The methane—that’s strange.”

“Explain.”

“Methane doesn’t last long. Sunlight breaks it down quickly. So why is there methane?”

“Geology makes methane. Carbon dioxide plus minerals plus water plus heat equals methane.”

“Yes. Possible,” I say. “But there’s a lot of methane. Eight percent of a very dense atmosphere. Can geology create that much?”

“Do you have another idea?”

I rub the back of my my neck. “No. Not really. But it is strange.”

“Difference is science. You think about difference. Make theory. You is science human.”

“Yes. I’ll think about it.”

“How long until orbit, question?”

I switch to the Navigation console. We’re right on course, and the orbital-insertion burn is scheduled for twenty-two hours from now. “Just under one day,” I say.

“Excitement,” he says. “Then we sample Astrophage at Adrian. You ship sampler working well, question?”

“Yes,” I say, with no way to know if I’m telling the truth. There’s no reason for Rocky to know I only partly understand how my own ship works.

I flip through the science instruments until I land on the controls for the External Collection Unit. I look at the diagram on the screen. It’s simple enough. The sampler is a rectangular box. When activated, it will pivot up to be at a right angle to the hull. Then, doors on both sides of the rectangle will open up. Inside, there’s a bunch of sticky resin—ready to catch anything that flies in.

That’s it. Flypaper. Fancy space flypaper, but just flypaper.

“After collection, how sample enter ship, question?”

Simple doesn’t mean easy. As far as I can tell, there’s no automated system to do anything with the sample. “I have to go get it.”

“Humans are amazing. You leave ship.”

“Yeah, I guess.”

Eridians never bothered to invent spacesuits. Why would they? Space has no sensory input for them. It would be like a human with scuba gear diving into an ocean of black paint. There’s just no reason to do it. Eridians use hull robots for EVA work. The Hail Mary doesn’t have one of those, so any EVA work has to be done by me.

“Amazing is wrong word,” he says. “Amazing is a compliment. Better word is ♫♪♫♪ .”

“What’s that mean?”

“It is when person does not act normal. Danger to self.”

“Ah,” I say, adding the new chord into my language database. “Crazy. My word for that is ‘crazy.’”

“Crazy. Humans are crazy.”

I shrug.

“Darn it!” I said.

“Watch your language!” came the voice over the radio. “Seriously, though, what happened?”

The sample vial fell slowly from my hand to the bottom of the pool. It took several seconds to fall 3 feet but, wearing this EVA suit at the bottom of the world’s largest swimming pool, I had no chance of grabbing it.

“I dropped vial number three.”

“Okay,” said Forrester. “That’s three vials so far. We’re going to have to work on the clamp tool.”

“Might not be the tool. Might just be me.”

The tool in my clumsy gloved hand was not perfect, but still pretty clever. It turned the awkward movements of an EVA suit glove into precise control at the other end. All I had to do was squeeze a trigger with my index finger and the clamp tightened by 2 millimeters. If I squeezed a different trigger with my middle finger, it would rotate up to 90 degrees clockwise. My ring and pinkie fingers made it tilt forward up to 90 degrees.

“Stand by, I’m checking the video,” said Forrester.

NASA’s Neutral Buoyancy Lab at Johnson Space Center was an amazing feat of engineering. The gigantic swimming pool was big enough to fit a full-size model of the International Space Station inside. They used it to train astronauts on zero-g movement while in EVA suits.

After many meetings (that I had to go to), the microbiology people convinced Stratt that the mission needed tools made just for it. She agreed, but only if none of them were crucial to the mission. She insisted that all the important things be regular products with millions of hours of testing by customers.

And, because I was her science helper, I had to test the IVME kit.

IVME was an acronym that stood for four words that shouldn't be together: "in vacuum microbiology equipment." Astrophage lives in space. We could study them on Earth in our atmosphere, but we wouldn't fully understand how they worked until we studied them in vacuum and zero gravity. The crew of the Hail Mary would need these tools.

I stood in a corner of the NBL, with the huge ISS behind me. Two scuba divers floated nearby, ready to save me if something went wrong.

NASA had put a metal lab table in the pool for me. The biggest challenge wasn't making equipment that worked in vacuum—though they had to completely change how pipettes worked because there's no suction in space. The problem was the clumsy EVA gloves the user had to wear. Astrophage might like vacuum, but human bodies definitely don't.

But hey, at least I was learning a lot about how Russian EVA suits worked.

Yes, Russian. Not American. Stratt listened to several experts, and they all agreed that the Russian Orlan EVA suit was the safest and most reliable. So that's what the mission would use.

"Okay, I see what happened," Forrester said through the headset. "You told the clamp to tilt, but it released instead. The wires inside must be tangled. I'll be right there. Can you come up and bring the clamp with you?"

"Sure." I waved to the two divers and pointed up. They nodded and helped me to the surface.

I was lifted out of the pool by a crane and placed on the deck nearby. Several techs came and helped me out of the suit. It was pretty easy—I just stepped out of the back. I love these suits that open like a cocoon.

Forrester came from the control room next door and picked up the tool. “I’ll make some adjustments and we can try again in a couple of hours. I got a call while you were in the pool; you’re needed in Building 30. Shapiro and DuBois have a couple-hour break while they reset the flight-control simulators. No rest for the wicked. Stratt wants you over there training them on Astrophage.”

“Copy that, Houston,” I said. The world might have been ending, but being at NASA’s main campus was too cool for me not to be excited.

I left the NBL and walked to Building 30. They would have sent a car if I’d asked, but I didn’t want one. It was only a ten-minute walk. Besides, I loved walking around in my country’s space history.

I walked in, through security, and onward to a small conference room they’d set up. Martin DuBois, in his blue flight uniform, stood and shook my hand. “Dr. Grace. Good to see you again.”

His careful paperwork and notes were laid out in front of him. Annie Shapiro’s messy notes and crumpled papers were scattered on the table next to him, but her seat was empty.

“Where’s Annie?” I asked.

He sat back down. Even while seated, he kept a stiff, perfect posture. “She had to use the restroom. She should be back shortly.”

I sat down and opened my backpack. “You know, you can call me Ryland. We’re all PhDs here. I think first names are fine.”

“I’m sorry, Dr. Grace. That is not how I was raised. However, you may call me Martin if you wish.”

“Thanks.” I pulled out my laptop and turned it on. “How have you been lately?”

“I’m fine, thank you. Dr. Shapiro and I are now having sex.”

I paused. “Um. Okay.”

“I thought I should tell you.” He opened his notebook and put a pen down. “We shouldn’t keep secrets in the main team.”

“Right, right,” I said. “I mean. It shouldn’t matter. You’re the main scientist and Annie’s the backup. There’s no way you’d both be on the mission. But…I mean…your relationship…”

“Yes, you’re right,” DuBois said. “I’m going on a suicide mission in less than a year. And if I can’t go, she will. We know this, and we know this relationship will end in death.”

“These are sad times,” I said.

He put his hands together. “Dr. Shapiro and I don’t see it that way. We’re having a lot of sex.”

“Yeah, okay, I don’t need to know—”

“We don’t need condoms either. She’s on birth control and we’ve both had complete medical checkups for the program.”

I typed on my computer, hoping he’d talk about something else.

“It’s nice.”

“I’m sure it is.”

“Anyway, I thought you should know.”

“Yeah, no, sure.”

The door opened, and Annie trotted in.

“Sorry! Sorry! I had to pee. Like…really bad,” said the world’s smartest and most accomplished microbiologist. “I really had to go!”

“Welcome back, Dr. Shapiro. I’ve told Dr. Grace about our sexual relationship.”

I put my head in my hands.

“Cool,” said Annie. “Yeah, we’ve got nothing to hide.”

“Anyway,” said DuBois, “if I remember the last lesson correctly, we were working on the cellular biology within Astrophage mitochondria.”

I cleared my throat. “Yes. Today, I’ll be discussing the Astrophage’s Krebs cycle. It’s the same as what we find in Earth mitochondria, but with one extra step—”

Annie raised her hand. “Oh, sorry. One more thing—” She turned to DuBois. “Martin, we have about fifteen minutes of free time after this lesson and before our next training. Want to meet in the bathroom down the hall and have sex?”

“I agree to that,” said DuBois. “Thank you, Dr. Shapiro.”

“Okay, cool.”

They both looked at me, ready for their lesson. I waited a few seconds to make sure they were done sharing, but they seemed ready. “Okay, so the Krebs cycle in Astrophage has a different—wait. Do you call her Dr. Shapiro while having sex?”

“Of course. That’s her name.”

“I kind of like it,” she said.

“I regret asking,” I said. “Now, the Krebs cycle…”

Rocky’s data about Planet Adrian was spot on. It’s 3.93 times Earth’s mass and has a radius of 10,318 kilometers (almost double Earth’s). It’s moving along around Tau Ceti with an average orbital velocity of 35.9 kilometers per second. Plus, he had the planet's position correct to within 0.00001 percent. That data was all I needed to figure out the needed thrust.

It’s a good thing those numbers were right. If they hadn’t been, there would have been some serious problems when the orbital insertion went wrong. Maybe even some dying.

Of course, to use the spin drives at all, I had to take us out of centrifuge mode.

Rocky and I float in the control room, he in his ceiling bulb and me in the pilot’s seat. I watch the camera-feed screen with a stupid grin on my face.

I’m at another planet! I shouldn’t be this excited. I’ve been at another star for the past several weeks. But that’s kind of complex. Tau Ceti is pretty much like the sun. It’s bright, you can’t get too close to it, and it even emits the same general range of frequencies. For some reason, being at a new planet is much more exciting.

The wispy clouds of Adrian drift by beneath us. Or, more accurately, the wispy clouds barely move at all and we zoom by overhead. Adrian has a higher gravity than Earth, so our orbital velocity is just over 12 kilometers per second—far more than what’s needed to orbit Earth.

The pale-green planet that I’ve been watching for eleven days has a lot more detail now that we’re on top of it. It’s not just green. There are dark and light bands of green wrapping around it. Just like Jupiter and Saturn. But unlike those two gas-giant leviathans, Adrian is a rocky world. Thanks to Rocky’s notes, we know the radius and mass, which means we know its density. And it’s far too dense to be just gas. There’s a surface down there, I just can’t see it.

Man, what I wouldn’t give for a lander!

Realistically, it wouldn’t do me any good. Even if I had some way of landing on Adrian, the atmosphere would crush me dead. It’d be like landing on Venus. Or Erid, for that matter. Heck, in that case, I wish Rocky had a lander. The pressure down there might not be too much for an Eridian.

Speaking of Erid, Rocky’s calibrating some kind of device in his control-room bubble. It looks almost like a gun. I don’t think we’ve started a space war, so I assume it’s something else.

He holds the device in one hand, taps it with another, and uses two more to hold a flat panel connected to the device by a short cable. He uses his last hand to hold onto a grip.

He tweaks the device with something like a screwdriver, and suddenly the panel lights up. It was completely flat, but now it has texture. He waves the gun part left and right, and the patterns on the screen move with him.

“Success! It works!”

I lean over the pilot’s seat to get a better view. “What is it?”

“Wait.” He points the gun part at my external camera screen. He adjusts a few controls, and the pattern on the rectangle forms a circle. Looking closer, I see some parts of the circle are higher than others. It looks like a raised map.

“This device hear light. Like human eye.”

“Oh. It’s a camera.”

“♫♪♫,” he says quickly. Now we know the word "camera."

“It analyze light and show as texture.”

“Oh, and you can feel that texture?” I say. “Cool.”

“Thank you.” He attaches the camera to the wall of lights and adjusts it to face my main screen. “What wavelengths of light can humans see?”

“All wavelengths between 380 nanometers and 740 nanometers.” Most people don’t know that right away. But most people aren’t junior high teachers with huge charts of the visible spectrum on their classroom walls.

“I understand,” he says. He adjusts a few knobs on his device. “Now I ‘see’ what you see.”

“You’re an amazing engineer.”

He waves a claw, brushing it off. “No. Camera is old. Display is old. Both were on my ship for science. I only changed them to use inside.”

I think Eridians are very modest. Or, Rocky is someone who can’t accept a compliment.

He points to the circle on his display. “This is Adrian?”

I check the exact area of Adrian he’s pointing at, then look at my screen. “Yes, and that part is ‘green.’ ”

“I have no word for this.”

Of course the Eridian language has no words for colors. Why would it? I never thought of colors as strange. But if you’ve never heard of them, I guess they’re pretty weird. We have names for frequency ranges in the electromagnetic spectrum. Then again, my students all have eyes and they were still amazed when I told them “x-rays,” “microwaves,” “Wi-Fi,” and “purple” were all just wavelengths of light.

“Okay, then,” I say.

“Yes, yes. I call this color: middle-rough. My display is smooth for high-frequency light. Rough for low-frequency light. This color is middle-rough.”

“Got it,” I say. “And yes, green is right in the middle of the wavelengths humans can see.”

“Good, good,” he says. “Is sample ready?”

We’ve been in orbit for about a day now, and I turned on the sampler as soon as we got here. I switch to the External Collection Unit screen. It says it's working perfectly and even shows how long it’s been open: 21 hours and 17 minutes.

“Yeah, I guess.”

“You get.”

“Ugh,” I groan. “EVAs are so much work!”

“Lazy human. Go get!”

I laugh. He sounds a little different when he’s joking. It took me a while to figure it out. It’s like…it’s in the timing between words. They don’t have the same rhythm. I can’t really explain it, but I know it when I hear it.

From the External Collection Unit screen, I tell the sampler to close its doors and go back to its flat shape. The panel says it’s done, and I check with the hull cameras.

I climb into the Orlan EVA suit, go into the airlock, and cycle it.

Adrian is even more gorgeous in person. I stay out on the hull staring at the huge world for several minutes. Stripes of dark and light green cover the orb, and the reflected light from Tau Ceti is simply breathtaking. I could stare at it for hours.

I probably got to do this with Earth too. I wish I could remember. Man, I really wish I could remember that. It must have been just as beautiful.

“You out long time,” Rocky’s voice comes through the headset. “You are safe, right?”

I set up the EVA panel to always play my radio feed over speakers in the control room. Plus, I taped a headset microphone to Rocky’s control-room bulb and made it voice-activated. All he has to do is talk and it broadcasts.

“I’m looking at Adrian. It’s pretty.”

“Look later. Get sample now.”

“You’re pushy.”

“Yes.”

I climb along the hull, lit by the Adrian sun. Everything has a light-green tint. I find the sample collector right where it should be.

It’s smaller than I thought. About half a meter square. There’s a lever next to it with red and yellow stripes. The lever reads PULL LEVER TO RELEASE ECU—ПОТЯНУТЬ РЫЧАГ ЧТОБЫ ОСВОБОДИТЬ ECU—拉杆释放 ECU.

I clip a tether to a hole on the unit (probably for this), and pull the lever to the open position.

The sampler floats away from the hull.

I make my way back to the airlock, pulling the sampler. I cycle back in and take off the suit.

“All good, question?” Rocky asks.

“Yes.”

“Good!” Rocky says. “You check with science gear, question?”

“Yes. Now.” I bring up the Centrifuge panel. “Prepare for gravity.”

“Yes, gravity.” He grips handholds with three claws. “For science gear.”

Once the centrifuge spins up, I start working in the lab.

Rocky hurries into his tunnel in the lab ceiling and watches closely. Well, not "watches." Listens closely, I guess.

I put the sampler on the lab table and open one of the panels. This is the side that faced Tau Ceti. I smile at what I see.

I tilt my head back to look at Rocky. "This panel was white when we started; now it's black."

“Not understand.”

"The sampler's color changed to the color of Astrophage. We got a lot of Astrophage."

“Good good!”

Over the next two hours, I scrape everything off both halves of the sampler, putting each group in their own containers. Then I rinse each sample well with water and let the Astrophage settle to the bottom. I'm sure a lot of that sticky stuff came with the Astrophage when I scraped it off, and I want it gone.

I do a series of tests. First, I run some Astrophage through DNA-marker testing to see if they are the same as the Astrophage found on Earth. They are—at least, the markers I checked are the same.

Then I check the total amount of each sample.

“Interesting,” I say.

Rocky perks up. “What’s interesting?”

“Both sides had about the same number of creatures.”

“Not expected,” he says.

“Not expected,” I agree.

One side of the sample pointed toward Tau Ceti, and the other pointed toward Adrian. Astrophage travel to reproduce. For every eager Astrophage that goes to Adrian, two should come back. So, generally, twice as many Astrophage should go from Adrian to Tau Ceti as go the other way. But that’s not happening. The number going out is the same as the number coming in.

Rocky climbs along the tunnel on the lab roof to get a better view. “Mistake in counting? How do you count?”

“I measure the total heat given off by both samples.” It’s a certain way to know how much Astrophage there is. Each one is always 96.415 degrees Celsius. The more there are, the more heat the metal plate I put them on will absorb.

He taps two claws together. “That is good way. Number must be same. How?”

“I don’t know.” I smear some of the “returning” Astrophage (the ones going from Adrian to Tau Ceti) onto a slide. I take it to a microscope.

Rocky hurries through his tunnel to keep up. “That is what, question?”

“Microscope,” I say. “It helps me see very small things. I can see Astrophage with this.”

“Amaze.”

I look at the sample and gasp. There’s more than just Astrophage in there!

The familiar black dots of Astrophage are all over the sample. But so are clear cells, smaller bacteria-looking things, and larger amoeba-like things. There are thin things, fat things, spiral things…too many to count. Too many different kinds of things to count. It’s like looking at all the life in a drop of lake water!

“Wow!” I say. “Life! There’s a lot of life in here! Not just Astrophage. A lot of different species!”

Rocky literally bounces off the tunnel walls. “Amaze! Amaze amaze amaze!”

“Adrian isn’t just a planet,” I say. “Adrian is a planet with life, like Earth or Erid! That explains where the methane comes from. Life makes methane!”

Rocky freezes. Then he shoots straight up. I’ve never seen him raise his shell so high. “Life is also reason for population discrepancy! Life is reason!”

“What?” I say. He’s more excited than I’ve ever seen him. “How? I don’t understand.”

He taps the tunnel wall with his claw, pointing at my microscope. “Life on Adrian EATS Astrophage! Population balanced. Natural order. This explains everything!”

“Oh my God!” I gasp. My heart pounds in my chest. “Astrophage has a predator!”

There’s a whole biosphere on Adrian. Not just Astrophage. There’s even a living biosphere within the Petrova line.

This is where it all began. It has to be. How else can we explain so many different life-forms that all evolved to travel in space? They all came from the same genetic origin.

Astrophage was just one of many life-forms that evolved here. And with all life, there is variety and predation.

Adrian isn’t just a planet that Astrophage infected. It’s the Astrophage home! And it’s the home of Astrophage’s predators.

“This is amazing!” I yell. “If we find a predator…”

“We take it home!” Rocky says, much higher than usual. “It eats Astrophage, breeds, eats more Astrophage, breeds, eats more and more! Stars saved!”

“Yes!” I press my knuckles against the tunnel wall. “Fist-bump!”

“What, question?”

I knock on the tunnel again. “This. Do this.”

He copies my action against the wall across from my hand.

“Celebration!” I say.

“Celebration!”
