Project Hail Mary

Chapter 21

“We can talk about this alone,” I said. “I can meet with you one at a time.”

The three astronauts sat on a couch in front of me. I’d taken over the breakroom and locked the door for this meeting. Yáo sat in the middle, looking serious as always. DuBois was to his left, sitting up straight. Ilyukhina slouched to Yáo’s left, drinking a beer.

“No need for separate meetings,” said Yáo. “There’s no room on this mission for secrets.”

I moved in my chair. Why did Stratt send me to do this? I’m not good with people and I don’t know how to handle sensitive issues. She said something about the crew liking me more than anyone else. Why? Maybe I just seemed nice because I was usually standing next to Stratt.

Anyway, launch was just a month away and I had to get this information.

“Okay,” I said. “Who wants to start?”

DuBois raised his hand. “I can go first if that’s okay with everyone.”

“Sure.” I scribbled with my pen to make sure it worked. “So…how would you like to die?”

Yeah. Uncomfortable topic. But one we had to cover. These three were going to give their lives so the rest of us could have a chance to fight. The least we could do was help them die the way they wanted to.

DuBois handed me a crisp piece of paper. “I’ve written down my request here. I think you’ll find everything is in order.”

I took the paper. It had bullet points, charts, and some notes at the bottom. “What is all this?”

DuBois pointed to the middle of the page. “I want to die by breathing in nitrogen. All my research shows it’s one of the least painful ways to die.”

I nodded and jotted down some notes.

“That paper lists the equipment I’ll need to make sure I die. It’s well within my personal-item weight limit.”

I frowned, mostly to hide that I didn’t know what to say.

He folded his hands on his lap. “It’s just a nitrogen tank and a connector for the EVA suit. I can wear the suit and have it pump in nitrogen instead of oxygen. The feeling of suffocation comes from too much carbon dioxide in the lungs, not lack of oxygen. The suit will keep removing the carbon dioxide I breathe out, leaving only nitrogen. I’ll just get tired and maybe a little dizzy. Then I’ll pass out.”

“All right.” I tried to stay calm. “What if the EVA suit isn’t available?”

“Section four has the backup plan. If I can’t use the EVA suit, I’ll use the ship’s airlock. The airlock is big enough that carbon dioxide won’t build up enough to be unpleasant.”

“Okay.” I wrote down a few more notes. Though I didn’t really need to. His paper was very detailed. “We’ll make sure there’s a tank with plenty of nitrogen, and a backup tank just in case the first one leaks.”

“Excellent. Thank you.”

I set the paper aside. “Ilyukhina? What about you?”

She set her beer down. “I want heroin.”

Everyone looked at her. Even Yáo paled a little.

“Sorry, what?” I said.

“Heroin.” She shrugged. “I’ve been a good girl all my life. No drugs. Not much sex. I want to feel intense pleasure before I die. People die from heroin all the time. It must be really nice.”

I rubbed my temples. “You want to die…from a heroin overdose?”

“Not right away,” she said. “I want to enjoy it. Start with a normal dose that works. Get high. Addicts all say the first few times are the best. Then it gets worse. I want to feel those first few times. Then overdose when the time is right.”

“I guess…we can do that,” I said. “Dying from an overdose can be really awful, though.”

She dismissed my worry. “Have doctors figure out the best way to take it for me. The right amount to make the early times as good as possible. Then the fatal dose can have other drugs in it to make sure I die without pain.”

I wrote down her request. “Okay. Heroin. I don’t know where we’ll get it, but we’ll figure it out.”

“You have the whole world helping you,” she said. “Get a drug company to make me heroin. It can’t be that hard.”

“Right. I’m sure Stratt can call someone or something.”

I sighed. Two down, one to go. “All right. Commander Yáo? What about you?”

“I want a gun, please,” he said. “A Type-92 handgun. The usual Chinese military one. Keep the bullets in a dry, sealed plastic box for the trip.”

At least that made some sense. Quick and painless. “A gun. Got it. That’s easy.”

He looked at his crewmates. “I’ll be the last to die. If anything goes wrong with either of your plans, I’ll have the gun ready. Just in case.”

“Very thoughtful,” said DuBois. “Thank you.”

“Don’t shoot me if I look like I’m enjoying myself,” Ilyukhina said.

“Understood,” said Yáo. He turned back to me. “Is that all?”

“Yeah,” I said, already standing. “This has been really awkward, thanks. I’m going to…go somewhere else now.”

—

I toss and turn in bed. The burns on my arm hurt more than ever. The painkillers barely help. I’m starting to wonder if I can find Ilyukhina’s heroin.

I won’t, I won’t. But I definitely would if this were still a mission to die.

Focus on that. This isn’t a mission to die anymore. If I do things right, I save the world and go home.

The pain gets a little better. It comes and goes. When I can, I’ll look at whatever books I have on burns. I’d at least like to know when it’ll stop hurting.

Tap.

“Huh?” I mumble.

Tap.

I look toward the noise. It’s Rocky tapping on the airlock wall.

“Rocky!” I fall out of my bunk, rolling onto my right side as I land. I scramble along the floor to the airlock wall. “Rocky, buddy! Are you okay?!”

I hear a low thrum from inside him.

“I don’t understand. Speak louder.”

“Sick…” he mumbles.

“Yeah, you’re sick. You came into my air. Of course you’re sick! You almost died!”

He tries to lift himself from the floor, then falls back down. “How did I get back here, question?”

“I moved you.”

He taps the ground with a claw, annoyed. “You touch my air, question?”

“A little, yeah.”

He points to my left arm. “Skin on arm not smooth. Hurt, maybe?”

I guess he can see right through the bandages with his sonar. Must be pretty bad under there. I kind of thought it would be, but now I know. “Yeah. But I’ll be okay.”

“You hurt self to save me. Thanks.”

“You did the same thing. Is your radiator okay? You were on fire and full of soot.”

“It getting better.” He points to the soot all along the wall and floor. “This from inside me, maybe?”

“Yes.”

“How it leave me, maybe?”

I feel proud for a moment. Why shouldn’t I? It was hard, and I did it. I point to the steel box on the airlock wall, now covered three times over. “I made something to blow air at you. I aimed at your radiator things, and all that bad stuff came out.”

He’s quiet for a moment. Then, still a little unsteady, he says, “How long that stuff inside me, maybe?”

I think back on the day. “About…two days.”

“You almost kill me.”

“What?! How?! I blew all the soot out of your radiator!”

He shifts a little. “Black stuff not soot. My body makes it. It covers damage while body fixes itself.”

“Oh…” I say. “Oh no…”

I didn’t blow soot out of his radiator. I blew the scabs off his wounds! “I’m so sorry! I was trying to help.”

“It’s okay. If you did it sooner I’d die. But I healed enough before you did it. Taking it off hurt a little. Thanks.”

I put my head in my hands. “Sorry,” I say again.

“Don’t say sorry. You saved me when you put me here. Thank thank thank.” He tries to stand again, but only gets up for a second before falling. “I am weak. I will heal.”

I step back and sit on my bunk. “Would you be more comfortable in zero g? I can turn off the centrifuge.”

“No. Gravity helps heal.” He moves his legs into a sort of bed for his shell to rest on. Probably a comfy sleeping position. “Sample container safe, right?”

“Yes. It’s in the lab now. I created an Adrian-like space in a sealed container and put some Astrophage in with the sample. I'll check on it later.”

“Good,” he says. “Human eyes very useful.”

“Thanks,” I say. “But my human brain wasn’t so useful. I can’t get the sample out of the container.”

He tilts his shell slightly. “You sealed the sample and can’t open it, right?”

“Yes.”

“You’re usually not dumb. Why dumb now?”

“Humans are dumb when we need sleep and take pain medicine. I’m tired and medicated right now.”

“You should sleep.”

I stand up. “I will soon. But first, I have to fix our orbit. It’s not very good right now.”

“Fix orbit while dumb. Good plan.”

I laugh. “New word: ‘sarcasm.’ You say the opposite of what you mean to make a point. Sarcasm.”

He says the word for “sarcasm” in his language.

—

Between exhaustion and drugs, I sleep like a baby. I wake up feeling a million times better, but my burns feel a million times worse. I look at the bandages. They’re new.

Rocky is at his workbench, messing with his tools. He’s cleaned up his area. It looks brand new. “You are awake, yes?”

“Yeah,” I say. “How are you feeling? Are you healing?”

He wiggles a claw . “Need much more healing. But some healing complete. Cannot move much.”

I drop my head back on the pillow. “Same.”

“Robot arms did things to you arm while you sleep.”

I point to the bandages. “It changed the cloth. Changing the cloth helps people heal.”

He pokes at his latest invention with various tools.

“What’s that?”

“I go to lab to see device that store Adrian life. I made device now to collect sample from inside and not let you air in.” He holds up a large box. “Put you vacuum chamber in this. Close this. This make Adrian air inside.”

He opens the top and points to a couple of hinged rods. “Control these from outside. Gather sample. Seal you device. Open my device. Have sample. Do human science with sample.”

“Smart,” I say. “Thanks.”

He gets back to work.

I lie in my bunk. There are a lot of things I want to do, but I need to take it slow. I can’t risk another “stupid day” like yesterday. I almost ruined the sample and killed Rocky. I’m smart enough now to know I’m stupid. That’s progress.

“Computer: coffee!”

After a minute, the arms hand me a cup of coffee.

“Hey,” I say, sipping my coffee. “How come we hear the same sounds?”

He keeps working on the parts inside his device. “Useful thing. Both change over time. Not surprising.”

“Yeah, but why the same frequencies? Why don’t you hear much higher sounds than I can? Or much lower?”

“I do hear much higher and much lower sounds.”

Didn’t know that. But I should have guessed. It’s how an Eridian mostly senses things. Of course he’ll hear more than I do. That still leaves one question, though.

“Okay, but why is there overlap? Why don’t we hear completely different sound ranges?”

He puts the tool down, but keeps working on his device with two hands. With the free hand, he scrapes his workbench. “You hear this, question?”

“Yes.”

“That is sound of predator coming for you. That is sound of prey running away. Sound of things touching things very important. Changed over time to hear.”

“Ah! Yes.”

It’s clear now that he mentions it. Sounds—voices, instruments, birds—can all be very different. But the sound of things hitting each other won’t change much from planet to planet. Two rocks clanging on Earth will sound the same as on Erid. So we’re all made to hear it.

“Better question,” he says. “Why we think same speed, question?”

I turn onto my side. “We don’t think at the same speed. You do math way faster than I can. And you remember things perfectly. Humans can’t do that. Eridians are smarter.”

He grabs another tool and starts working again. “Math is not thinking. Math is steps. Memory is not thinking. Memory is storage. Thinking is thinking. Problem, answer. You and me think same speed. Why, question?”

“Hmm.”

I think about it. It’s a good question. Why isn’t Rocky a thousand times smarter than me? Or a thousand times dumber?

“Well…I have an idea why we’re about the same intelligence. Maybe.”

“Explain.”

“Intelligence helps us beat the other animals on our planet. But evolution is lazy. Once a problem is solved, the trait stops changing. So you and me, we’re both just smart enough to be smarter than our planet’s animals.”

“We are much much smarter than animals.”

“We’re as smart as evolution made us. So, we’re just smart enough to be able to control our planets.”

He thinks it over. “I agree. But why did Earth intelligence evolve to be the same as Erid intelligence?”

“Our intelligence comes from animal intelligence. So, what does animal intelligence come from? How smart do animals need to be?”

“Smart enough to spot danger or food in time to react.”

“Yes, exactly!” I say. “But how long is that time? How long does an animal have to react? How long will it take the danger to kill the animal, or the prey to escape? I think it’s about gravity.”

“Gravity, you ask?” He puts the device down completely. He’s paying full attention now.

“Yeah! Think about it. Gravity decides how fast an animal can run. Higher gravity means more time on the ground, so faster movement. I think animal intelligence has to be faster than gravity.”

“Interesting idea,” Rocky says. “But Erid has twice Earth’s gravity. You and I are equally smart.”

I sit up on my bed. “I bet our gravities are so close, in space terms, that the intelligence needed is almost the same. If we met a creature from a planet with one-hundredth of Earth’s gravity, I bet it would seem pretty dumb to us.”

“Maybe,” he says. He goes back to his gadget. “Another thing we have in common: You and I would both die for our people. Why? Evolution doesn’t like death.”

“It’s good for the species,” I say. “If people are willing to sacrifice themselves, the species is more likely to survive.”

“Not all Eridians willing to die for others.”

I chuckle. “Not all humans either.”

“You and me are good people,” Rocky says.

“Yeah.” I smile. “I suppose we are.”

Nine days until launch.

I paced around my room. It was pretty empty, but I didn’t mind. The portable unit was like a small mobile home with a kitchenette. Better than most people got. The Russians were busy setting up dozens of temporary shelters a few miles from Baikonur Cosmodrome. But then, I guess we were all busy lately.

Anyway, I’d barely used my bed since we’d arrived. There just always seemed to be some new issue or problem. Nothing big. Just…issues.

The Hail Mary was finished. Over 2 million kilograms of spacecraft and fuel in a good, steady orbit—four times the mass of the International Space Station, and built in one-twentieth the time. The press used to track the total cost, but around $10 trillion, they stopped. It just didn’t matter. It wasn’t about using resources wisely anymore. It was Earth versus Astrophage, and no cost was too great.

ESA astronauts had been on the ship for the last few weeks, testing it out. The test crew reported about five hundred problems that we’d been fixing for the last few weeks. None of them were critical.

It was really happening. The Hail Mary was going to launch in nine days.

I sat at the table that was my desk and looked through papers. I signed some and set others aside for Stratt to see tomorrow. How did I become an administrator? We all have to accept changes in our lives, I guess. If this was my role, then so be it.

I put the papers down and looked out the window. The Kazakhstani steppes were flat and bare. People usually don’t build launch sites near anything important. For obvious reasons.

I missed my kids.

Dozens of them. Hundreds, really, over a school year.

They didn’t curse at me or wake me up at night. Their fights were usually over quickly, either by a teacher's handshake or detention. And, a little selfish, but here it is: They admired me. I missed being respected like that.

I sighed.

My kids would have a hard time even if the mission worked. It would take thirteen years for the Hail Mary to reach Tau Ceti, and (if the crew found a solution) another thirteen years for the beetles to return to us. That’s over twenty-five years before we would even know what to do. My kids wouldn’t be kids anymore when it was over.

“Onward,” I mumbled, and grabbed the next problem report. Why was it on paper instead of just an email? Because Russians do things a certain way and it’s easier to work with them than to complain about it.

The report was from the ESA crew about problems in Slurry Pump Fourteen of the medical feeding transport system. Pump Fourteen was only part of the backup system and it was still 95 percent effective. But there was no reason to put up with that. We still had 83 kilograms of unused launch mass. I made a note to include a spare slurry pump—it was only 250 grams. The crew could install it before leaving orbit.

I set the paper aside and saw a brief flash out my window. Probably a jeep driving on the dirt road that led to the temporary shelters. I got headlights through my window from time to time. I ignored it.

The next paper in my stack was all about potential balance issues. The Hail Mary kept its center of mass along its long axis by pumping Astrophage around as needed. But we still wanted to keep things as balanced as possible anyway. The ESA crew had rearranged several supply bags in the storage compartment to better balance—

The window shattered as a deafening explosion shook the room. Glass shards nicked my face as a shockwave knocked me clean out of my chair.

After that: silence.

And then: sirens in the distance.

I got to my knees, and then to my feet. I opened and closed my mouth a few times to pop my ears.

I stumbled to the door and opened it. The first thing I noticed was that the small triplet of steps that once led to my door were several feet away. Then I saw the freshly disturbed earth between the steps and my door and I understood what happened.

The steps are anchored into the ground with four-by-fours sunk deep like fence posts. My portable has no such support.

My whole house moved, but the stairs didn't.

“Grace?! Are you okay?!” It was Stratt’s voice. Her portable radio was next to mine.

“Yeah!” I say. “What the heck was that?!”

“I don’t know,” she said. “Hold on.”

Soon, I saw a flashlight moving around. She came to me in a bathrobe and boots, already talking on her walkie-talkie. “Eto Stratt. Chto sluchylos’?” she demanded.

“Vzryv v issledovatel’skom tsentre,” came the reply.

“The research center blew up,” she said.

Baikonur was a place where rockets launched, but it also had research buildings. They weren’t labs, more like classrooms. Astronauts usually spent a week at Baikonur before a launch, wanting to study and get ready until the last day.

“Oh God,” I said. “Who was there? Who was there?!”

She pulled some papers from her robe pocket. “Hold on, hold on…” She flipped through them, dropping each one as she looked at the next. I knew what they were—I’d seen them every day for a year. Schedule charts, showing where everyone was and what they were doing at all times.

She stopped at the page she needed. She actually gasped. “DuBois and Shapiro. They’re supposed to be there doing Astrophage experiments.”

I put my hands on my head. “No! Please, no! The research center is five kilometers away. If the blast did this much damage here—”

“I know, I know!” She turned on her walkie-talkie again. “Prime crew—I need your locations. Report in.”

“Yáo here,” came the first reply. “In my bunk.”

“Ilyukhina here. At the officers’ bar. What was that explosion?”

Stratt and I waited for the reply we hoped for.

“DuBois,” she said. “DuBois! Report in!”

Silence.

“Shapiro. Dr. Annie Shapiro. Report in!”

More silence.

She took a deep breath and let it out. She clicked the walkie-talkie on one more time. “Stratt to transport—I need a jeep to take me to Ground Control.”

“Copy,” came the reply.

The next few hours were, honestly, chaos. The entire base was locked down for a while and everyone’s ID was checked. For all we knew, some doomsday cult wanted to wreck the mission. But nothing seemed wrong.

Stratt, Dimitri, and I sat in the bunker. Why were we in a bunker? The Russians weren’t taking any chances. It didn’t seem like a terrorist attack, but they were protecting the important people just in case. Yáo and Ilyukhina were off in another bunker. The other science leaders were in other bunkers too. Spread everyone out so there’s no single attack that would work. There was a dark reason for it. Baikonur was built during the Cold War, after all.

“The research buildings are a crater,” said Stratt. “And there’s still no sign of DuBois or Shapiro. Or the fourteen other staff that worked there.”

She pulled up pictures on her phone and showed them to us.

The photos showed total destruction. The area was lit up with bright lights the Russians had put up and the place was full of rescue workers. Though there was nothing for them to do.

Almost nothing was left. No debris, little wreckage. Stratt swiped through photo after photo. Some were close-ups of the ground. Round, shiny beads were all over the place. “What’s up with the beads?” she said.

“Metal condensate,” Dimitri said. “It means metals were turned into gas, then became liquid like raindrops.”

“Jesus,” she said.

I sighed. “The only thing in those labs that could make enough heat to vaporize metal is Astrophage.”

“I agree,” said Dimitri. “But Astrophage doesn't just 'explode.' How could this happen?”

Stratt looked at her wrinkled schedule. “According to this, DuBois wanted more practice with Astrophage-powered electrical generators. Shapiro was there to watch and help.”

“That doesn't make sense,” I said. “Those generators use a very small amount of Astrophage to make electricity. Not nearly enough to blow up a building.”

She put her phone down. “We’ve lost our main and backup science experts.”

“This is a nightmare,” said Dimitri.

“Dr. Grace, I want a short list of possible replacements.”

I stared, shocked. “Are you made of stone?! Our friends just died!”

“Yes, and everyone else will die if we don’t make this mission happen. We have nine days to find a replacement science expert.”

I teared up. “DuBois…Shapiro…” I sniffled and wiped my eyes. “They’re dead. They’re dead…oh God…”

Stratt slapped me. “Wake up!”

“Hey!”

“Cry later! Mission first! Do you still have that list of candidates who can resist comas from last year? Start looking through it. We need a new science specialist. And we need them now!”

—

“Collecting sample now…” I say.

Rocky watches me from his tunnel in the lab ceiling. His device works perfectly. The clear xenonite box has valves and pumps that let me control the environment inside. The vacuum chamber is inside with its lid open. The box even controls the temperature, keeping it a chilly minus 51 degrees Celsius.

Rocky scolded me for leaving the sample at (human) room temperature for so long. He had a lot to say about it. We had to add “reckless,” “idiot,” “foolish,” and “irresponsible” to our shared vocabulary so he could fully express his opinion.

There was another word he used a lot, but he wouldn’t tell me what it meant.

Three days off the painkillers, and I’m a lot smarter than I was. At least he understands that much—I wasn’t just some stupid human. I was a human with enhanced stupidity.

Rocky wouldn't give me the box I needed until I slept three times without using drugs. My arm really hurts, but he has a point.

Rocky got better in that time, too. I don't know what's happening inside him. He looks the same, but he's moving much better. Not full-speed, though. Me neither. We're both hurting, really.

We agreed to keep the gravity at one-half g.

I open and close the claws in the box a few times. "Look at me. I'm an Eridian now."

“Yes. Very Eridian. Hurry and get sample.”

"You're no fun." I grab the cotton swab and bring it to a glass slide. I rub it on the slide, leaving a smear, then put it back in the vacuum chamber. I seal the chamber, put the slide in a clear container, and seal the box.

"Okay. That should do it." I turn the valves to let my air in, then open the box. The slide is safe in its container. The galaxy's smallest spaceship. At least, for any Adrian life that might be there.

I walk to the microscope station.

Rocky follows in the tunnel above. “You sure you can see light that small?”

"Yes. It's old technology. Very old." I put the container on the tray and adjust the lenses. The container is clear enough to see through.

“Okay, Adrian, what do you have for me?” I put my face to the eyepieces.

The most obvious thing is the Astrophage. As usual, they’re jet-black, absorbing all light. That’s expected. I adjust the backlight and focus. And I see microbes everywhere.

One of my favorite experiments with the kids is to have them look at a drop of water. A drop of water, ideally one from a puddle outside, will be full of life. It always goes over well, except for the occasional kid who then refuses to drink water for a while.

“Lots of life in here,” I say. “Different kinds.”

“Good. Expected.”

Of course there would be. Any planet with life will have it everywhere. That’s my theory, at least. Evolution is extremely good at filling every corner of the ecosystem.

Right now I’m looking at hundreds of unique life-forms, never before seen by humans. Each one an alien race. I can’t help but smile. Still, I have work to do.

I pan around until I find a good clump of Astrophage. If there’s a predator to be found, it’ll be where the Astrophage is. Otherwise it’d be a pretty bad predator.

I flick on the microscope’s internal camera. The image appears on a little LCD screen. I adjust the screen and set it recording.

“This could take time,” I say. “Need to see interaction between— whoa! ”

I push my face back to the microscope for a better look. It only took seconds before the Astrophage was attacked. Am I incredibly lucky, or is this life-form just that aggressive?

Rocky scurried back and forth above me. “What, question? What happen, question?”

The monster lunges toward the clump of Astrophage. It’s a shapeless blob, like an amoeba. It presses against its much smaller prey and starts to surround the entire clump by oozing around both sides.

The Astrophage wriggle. They know something is wrong. They try to escape, but it’s too late. They can only sputter a short distance before they stop. Normally, Astrophage can accelerate to near light speed in seconds, but these can’t. Maybe the monster excreted a chemical that disables them somehow?

The encirclement is complete, and the Astrophage are surrounded. A few seconds later, the Astrophage become cell-like in appearance. No longer featureless black, their organelles and membranes are clearly visible in the microscope’s light. They have lost their ability to absorb heat and light energy.

They’re dead.

“Got it!” I say. “I found the predator! It ate Astrophage right in front of me!”

“Found!” Rocky cheers. “Isolate.”

“Yes, I’ll isolate it!” I say.

“Happy happy happy!” he says. “Now you name.”

I grab a nano-pipette from the supply. “I don’t get it.”

“Earth culture. You find. You name. What is the predator’s name, question?”

“Oh,” I say. I’m not feeling creative right now. This is too exciting to distract me. It’s an amoeba from Tau Ceti. “Taumoeba, I guess.”

Taumoeba. The savior of Earth and Erid.

Hopefully.

—

I should have a bolo tie. Maybe a cowboy hat. Because I’m a rancher now. And I’m running about 50 million Taumoeba on my ranch.

Once I got a few Taumoeba from the Adrian air sample, Rocky built a breeder tank and we let them get to work. It’s just a xenonite box full of Adrian air and a few hundred grams of Astrophage.

As far as we can tell, Taumoeba is very tough when it comes to temperature changes. Good thing, too, because I let it sit at room temperature that one day.

Drugs are bad.

Looking back, it’s clear why they handle temperature well. They live in a negative-51-degree-Celsius place and eat Astrophage, which is always 96.415 degrees Celsius. Everyone loves a hot meal, right?

And they breed like crazy! I gave them a ton of Astrophage to eat. It’s like adding yeast to sugar water. But instead of making alcohol, we’re making more Taumoebas. Now that we have enough to test, I can get started.

If you put a goat on Mars, what happens? It dies right away (and badly). Goats didn’t grow up to live on Mars. So, what happens if you put a Taumoeba on a planet other than Adrian?

That’s what I want to know.

Rocky watches from his tunnel above the main worktable as I make a new atmosphere in my vacuum chamber.

“No have oxygen, question?” he asked.

“No oxygen.”

“Oxygen dangerous.” He’s been a little nervous since his insides caught fire.

“I breathe oxygen. It’s okay.”

“Can explode.”

I pull my goggles off and look up at him. “There’s no oxygen in this experiment. Calm down.”

“Yes. Calm.”

I get back to work. I turn a valve to let a little gas into the vacuum chamber. I check the pressure gauge to make sure that—

“Confirm again: No oxygen, right?”

I jerk my head up to glare at him. “It’s just carbon dioxide and nitrogen! Only carbon dioxide and nitrogen! Nothing else! Don’t ask me again!”

“Yes. Won't ask again. Sorry.”

Can’t blame him, I guess. Being on fire sucks.

We have two planets to deal with here. No, not Earth and Erid. Those are just the planets we live on. The planets we care about right now are Venus and Threeworld. That’s where Astrophage is breeding out of control.

Venus, of course, is the second planet in my solar system. It’s about Earth’s size with a thick carbon-dioxide atmosphere.

Threeworld is the third planet in Rocky’s home system. At least, that’s what I call it. The Eridians don’t have a name for it, not even in their language. They just call it “Planet Three.” They didn’t have ancient people looking up at stars and naming them after gods. They only found the other planets in their system a few hundred years ago. But I don’t want to keep saying “Planet Three,” so I named it Threeworld.

The hardest part about working with aliens and saving humanity is constantly having to name things.

Threeworld is a very small planet—only about the size of Earth’s moon. But unlike our moon, Threeworld has an atmosphere. How? I have no idea. The gravity on the surface is only 0.2 g’s, which shouldn’t be enough. But somehow, Threeworld holds on to a thin atmosphere. According to Rocky, it’s 84 percent carbon dioxide, 8 percent nitrogen, 4 percent sulfur dioxide, and some other gases. The air pressure is less than 1 percent of Earth’s.

I check the readings and nod. I look closely at the experiment. I’m proud of this idea.

There’s a thin layer of Astrophage on a glass plate. I put it there by shining IR light through the glass and drawing the Astrophage from the other side. It’s the same way the spin drive works. The result is a smooth layer of Astrophage that’s only one cell thick.

Then I put Taumoeba on the slide. As they eat the Astrophage, the slide will become more transparent. It’s much easier to measure light than count tiny organisms.

“Okay…the chamber has the same atmosphere as Venus’s upper atmosphere. As close as I can get it.”

I think the Astrophage breeding zone depends on the air pressure. They have to slow down from almost light speed when they enter the planet’s atmosphere. But they’re so small that it doesn’t take long, and they absorb all the heat that’s created.

So, Astrophage stop moving when the air is 0.02 atmospheres thick. That’s what we’ll use as our standard pressure. The atmosphere on Venus is 0.02 atmospheres thick at about 70 kilometers up, and the temperature there is about minus 100 degrees Celsius (thanks to all the information I have!). So that’s the temperature I set the Venus experiment to. Rocky’s temperature control works perfectly, even at very low temperatures.

“Good. Now Threeworld.”

“What’s the temperature of Threeworld’s air at 0.02 atmosphere altitude?”

“Minus eighty-two degrees Celsius.”

“Okay, thanks,” I say. I go to the next chamber. It has the same setup of Astrophage and Taumoeba. I add the right gases to copy Threeworld’s atmosphere and temperature at the 0.02-atmosphere pressure. I get the information from Rocky’s perfect memory. It’s like Venus or Adrian. Mostly carbon dioxide with some other gases mixed in. No surprise—Astrophage go for the most CO2 they can find.

It’s lucky these planets aren’t full of helium or something. I don’t have any of that. But carbon dioxide? That’s easy. My body makes it. And nitrogen? Thanks to DuBois and how he died, there’s plenty aboard.

Threeworld does have some sulfur dioxide, though. Four percent of the atmosphere. It’s enough that I had to include it, so I made some. The lab has many chemicals, but no sulfur dioxide gas. But, it has sulfuric acid in liquid form. I got some copper tubing from a broken freezer coil and used it to help make the sulfur dioxide I needed. It worked great.

“Okay, Threeworld’s done,” I say. “We’ll wait an hour and check the results.”

“We have hope,” says Rocky.

“Yes, we have hope,” I say. “Taumoeba are very tough. They can live in a near vacuum, and they seem fine in extreme cold. Maybe Venus and Threeworld are livable for them. They’re good enough for Taumoeba’s prey, so why not for Taumoeba?”

“Yes. Things are good. All is good!”

“Yeah. For once, everything’s going great.”

Then the lights go out.
