Project Hail Mary

Chapter 15

It’s been a few hours. But I have to know. How does he change the tunnel?

He needs huge air pressure to live. My ship can’t take that. And he can’t be in a vacuum. So how does he make changes?

I hear clinks and clanks from the other side of the airlock. This time I’ll find out!

I enter the airlock and look through the window. The Blip-A’s hull robot has taken off the old tunnel and is putting on a new one.

Oh. Well. That’s a letdown.

The old tunnel floats off into space—its use is over, it seems. The robot puts the new tunnel in place and uses xenonite glue along the edge of the Blip-A’s hull.

How did Eridians fly a ship that went near the speed of light without computers? Guessed their way? They’re good at doing math in their heads. Maybe they never had to invent computers. But still. No matter how good they are at math, there are limits.

The clunking stops. I peek out the window again. The tunnel is fully installed.

It looks like the old tunnel, but it has a much bigger airlock part. Almost the whole divider wall is a cabinet big enough to hold Rocky with room left. But it’s not big enough to hold me. I guess I won’t be visiting the Blip-A anytime soon.

“Hmph,” I say. I try not to let it bother me, but come on. He gets to see an alien spaceship. Why don’t I get to see one?

Rocky’s side of the tunnel no longer has the network of gripping bars. Instead, there is a metal strip running along the length of the tunnel. It goes into the divider airlock and further onto my side of the tunnel. It leads right up to my airlock door.

Across from the metal strip is something that looks like a pipe. It’s made of the same dull alien browns and tans as the tunnel wall. And it’s square. It also runs the length of the tunnel.

With a whoosh , Rocky’s side of the tunnel fills with fog. Then a second whoosh fills my side. That’s what the pipe was for, I guess. Sending the right air to both sides. I’m glad Rocky has oxygen to work with.

The Blip-A door opens, and Rocky comes out, inside his geodesic ball. He’s wearing something like overalls with a bandolier across the bottom of his shell. The AC unit is on his back. Two of his hands hold metal blocks. The other three are free. One of them waves to me. I wave back.

The spaceball (what else should I call it?) floats into the airlock and then sticks to the metal plate.

“What?” I say. “How…”

Then I see it. The ball didn’t magically move. Those blocks Rocky is holding are magnets. Pretty strong ones, I guess. And the metal strip is magnetic. Probably iron. He rolls the ball along the metal line and into the divider airlock. He moves metal controls through the alien shell with his magnets. It’s amazing to watch.

After some hissing and the sound of pumps, he pushes a plate away, which opens the door on my side of the airlock. From there, he rolls along the metal line to my door. I open it.

“Hello!”

“Hello!”

“So…do I carry you around? Is that the plan?”

“Yes. Carry. Thank.”

I carefully grab the ball, worried it might be hot. But it isn’t. Among other things, xenonite is a great insulator. I pull him through and into the ship.

Rocky is heavy. Much heavier than I thought he would be. If there were gravity, I probably couldn’t lift him at all. As it is, he has a lot of inertia. It takes a lot of effort to pull him along. It’s like pushing a motorcycle that won't start. Seriously—he’s as heavy as a motorcycle.

I shouldn’t be surprised. He told me all about his biology and how it uses metals. Heck, his blood is mercury. Of course he’s heavy.

“You are very heavy,” I say. I hope he doesn’t think I mean, “Hey, fatty! Go on a diet!”

“My mass is one hundred sixty-eight kilograms,” he says.

Rocky weighs over 300 pounds!

“Wow,” I say. “You weigh a lot more than me.”

“What is your weight, question?”

“Maybe eighty kilograms.”

“Humans have very little weight!” he says.

“I’m mostly water,” I say. “Anyway. This is the control room. I operate the ship from here.”

“I understand.”

I push him ahead of me down the tunnel to the lab. He skitters around within his ball. He tends to shift around when he’s looking at something new. I think it helps him get a better “view” of things with his sonar. Kind of like a dog tilting its head to get more information about a sound.

“This is my lab,” I say. “All the science happens here.”

“Good good good room!” he squeals. His voice is a full octave higher than normal. “Want to understand everything!”

“I’ll answer any questions you have,” I say.

“Later. More rooms!”

“More rooms!” I say dramatically.

I push him into the dormitory. I move us slowly so he can see everything from the middle of the room. “I sleep here. Well, I used to. Then you made me sleep in the tunnel.”

“You sleep alone, really?”

“Yes.”

“I also sleep alone a lot. Very sad.”

He just doesn’t get it. Being afraid to sleep alone is probably natural for him. Interesting…that might have started their pack behavior. And a pack behavior is needed for a species to become smart. That strange (to me) sleep habit could be why I’m talking to Rocky right now!

Yeah, that wasn't very scientific. Lots of things probably made them intelligent. The sleep thing is likely just one part. But hey, I’m a scientist. I have to make theories!

I open a panel to the storage area and push his ball partly inside. “This is a small room for storage.”

“I understand.”

I pull him back out. “That’s all the rooms. My ship is much smaller than yours.”

“Your ship has much science!” he says. “Show me things in the science room, okay?”

“Sure.”

I take him back up to the lab. He moves around in the ball, looking at everything. I float us to the center of the room and hold onto the edge of the table.

I push the ball against the lab table. I think it’s steel, but I’m not sure. Most lab tables are. Let’s check.

“Use your magnets,” I say.

He pushes one of his magnets against the pentagon side touching the table. With a clunk the magnet sticks. He’s now held in place.

“Good!” he says. He uses his magnets on each side to roll across the table and back. It’s not pretty, but it works. At least I don’t have to hold him still.

I move away from the table and float to the edge of the room. “There’s a lot here. What do you want to know about first?”

He starts to point one way, then stops. Then he picks something else, but stops there too. Like a kid in a candy shop. Finally, he picks the 3-D printer. “That. What is that, okay?”

“It makes small things. I tell the computer what shape to make, and it tells this machine how to do it.”

“I see it make small thing, question?”

“It needs gravity.”

“That’s why your ship rotates, question?”

“Yes!” I say. Wow, he’s fast. “The rotation makes gravity for science stuff.”

“Your ship can’t rotate with the tunnel attached.”

“Right.”

He thinks it over.

“Your ship has more science than mine. Better science. I bring my things into your ship. Release tunnel. You spin your ship for science. You and I will study how to kill Astrophage together. Save Earth. Save Erid. This is a good plan, question?”

“Uh…yes! Good plan! But what about your ship?” I tap his xenonite bubble. “Human science can’t make xenonite. Xenonite is stronger than anything humans have.”

“I’ll bring materials to make xenonite. Can make any shape.”

“Okay,” I say. “Do you want to get your stuff now?”

“Yes!”

I went from being the “only space explorer left” to “a guy with a weird new roommate.” This should be interesting.

“Have you met Dr. Lamai?” Stratt asked.

I shrugged. “I’ve met so many people lately, I really can’t remember.”

The ship had a sick bay for the crew. This was a special medical center in the second hangar.

Dr. Lamai put her hands together and slightly bowed her head. “It’s nice to meet you, Dr. Grace.”

“Thanks,” I said. “Um, you too.”

“I’ve put Dr. Lamai in charge of all the medical stuff for the Hail Mary, ” Stratt said. “She was the main scientist for the company that made the coma technology we’re going to use.”

“Nice to meet you,” I said. “So you’re from Thailand, I guess?”

“Yes,” she said. “The company didn’t make it, sadly. Because the technology only works on one in every seven thousand people, so it’s not very useful for business. I’m very glad that my research might still help humanity.”

“That’s putting it lightly,” said Stratt. “Your technology might save humanity.”

Lamai looked away. “You’re too kind.”

She led us into her lab. A dozen areas each had slightly different experiment setups, each connected to a monkey that was unconscious.

I looked away. “Do I have to be here?”

“You’ll have to excuse Dr. Grace,” Stratt said. “He’s a bit…sensitive about some things.”

“I’m fine,” I said. “I know animal testing is needed. I just don’t like to watch it.”

Lamai said nothing.

“Dr. Grace,” Stratt said. “Stop being rude. Dr. Lamai, please update us.”

Lamai pointed to metal arms above the closest test monkey. “We created these automated stations to monitor and care for coma patients when we thought we'd have tens of thousands. That didn't happen.”

“Do they work?” Stratt asked.

“Our first design wasn't meant to be totally on its own. It would handle the basics, but if it faced a problem it couldn't fix, a doctor would be called.”

She walked by the line of sleeping monkeys. “We're making good progress on the fully automated version. This device is run by advanced software from Bangkok. It will care for a coma patient. It watches their health, gives them needed medical care, feeds them, checks their fluids, and so on. It’s still better to have a real doctor, but this is a good backup.”

“Are they a type of artificial intelligence?” Stratt asked.

“No,” Lamai said. “We don't have time to build a complex AI. This is just a set of rules. Very complicated, but not AI. We need to test it in many ways and know exactly how it acts and why. We can’t do that with an AI.”

“I see.”

She pointed to diagrams on the wall. “Our biggest discovery also destroyed our company. We found the genes that show who can resist long-term comas. We can find them with a simple blood test. And, as you know, when we tested this on people, we found that very few actually have these genes.”

“But couldn’t you still help those people?” I asked. “I know it’s only one in seven thousand, but it’s a start, right?”

Lamai shook her head. “Sadly, no. This isn’t a necessary treatment. There’s no urgent medical reason to be unconscious during chemotherapy. It even adds a bit of risk. So there wouldn't be enough people wanting it to keep a company going.”

Stratt rolled up her sleeve. “Check my blood for the genes. I want to know.”

Lamai was briefly surprised. “A-Alright, Ms. Stratt.” She went to a rolling cart and got a blood-draw kit. Someone this important wasn’t used to doing basic medical tasks. But Stratt was Stratt.

Still, Lamai was good at her job. She got the needle into Stratt quickly and on the first try. The blood went into the tube. When the blood draw was done, Stratt rolled down her sleeve. “Grace, you’re next.”

“Why?” I asked. “I’m not offering.”

“To show others,” she said. “I want everyone on this project, even those barely involved, to get tested. Astronauts are rare, and only one in seven thousand will be coma-resistant. We might not have enough good candidates. We need to be ready to include more people.”

“It’s a suicide mission,” I said. “It’s not like we’ll have people begging, ‘Pick me! Please pick me!’ ”

“Actually, we do have that,” Stratt said.

Lamai poked my arm. I looked away. I get a little sick when I see my blood going into a tube. “What do you mean, we have that?”

“We’ve already had tens of thousands of people volunteer. All knowing it’s a one-way trip.”

“Wow,” I said. “How many of them are crazy or want to die?”

“Probably a lot. But there are hundreds of astronauts with experience on the list too. Astronauts are brave people, ready to risk their lives for science. Many of them are willing to give their lives for humanity. I admire them.”

“Hundreds,” I say. “Not thousands. We’ll be lucky if even one of those astronauts is good enough.”

“We’re already counting on a lot of luck,” said Stratt. “Might as well hope for more.”

Soon after college, my girlfriend Linda moved in with me. The relationship only lasted eight more months and was a complete failure. But that’s not important right now.

When she moved in, I was shocked by how much random junk she felt she needed to bring into our small apartment. Box after box of stuff she had collected over decades of never throwing anything away.

Linda had very little compared to Rocky.

He’s brought in so much stuff we don’t have places to put it all.

Almost the whole dormitory is filled with duffel bags made of a canvas-like stuff. They're in random, muddy colors. When how things look doesn't matter, you just get whatever colors come out of the factory. I don't even know what's in all of them. He doesn't explain. Every time I think we might be done, he brings more bags in.

Well, I say "he" brings them in, but it's me. He hangs out in his ball, stuck to the wall with magnets, while I do all the work. Again, this is a lot like Linda.

"This is a lot of stuff," I say.

“Yes yes,” he says. “I need these things.”

“A lot of stuff.”

“Yes yes. Understand. Things in tunnel is last things.”

“Okay,” I grumble. I float back to the tunnel and grab the last few soft boxes. I move them through the cockpit and lab down to the dormitory. I find a place to cram them. There's very little space left. I vaguely wonder how much weight we just added to my ship.

I manage to keep the area near my bunk clear. And there's a spot on the floor that Rocky picked out as his sleeping place. The rest of the room is a crazy mess of soft boxes taped to each other, the wall, the other bunks, and anything else that will keep them from floating around.

“Are we done?”

“Yes. Now detach tunnel.”

I groan. “You made the tunnel. You take it off.”

“How I take off tunnel, question? Me inside ball.”

“Well, how do I do it? I don’t understand xenonite.”

He made a turning motion with two of his arms. “Turn tunnel.”

“Okay, okay.” I grab my EVA suit. “I’ll do it. Jerk.”

“No understand last word.”

“Not important.” I climb into the suit and close the rear flap.

Rocky is surprisingly good at doing things with a couple of magnets from inside a ball.

Each of his duffel bags has a metal plate on it. He can climb along the pile and move things around as needed. Sometimes, a bag he’s using to hold on to comes loose, and he floats away. When that happens, he calls me, and I put him back.

I hold on to my bunk and watch him do his thing. “Okay, step one. Taking astrophage samples.”

“Yes, yes.” He holds his hands out and moves one around the other. “Planet moves around Tau. Astrophage goes there from Tau. Same at Eridani. Astrophage uses carbon dioxide to make more astrophage there.”

“Yes,” I say. “Did you get a sample?”

“No. My ship had a tool for this. But the tool broke.”

“You couldn’t fix it?”

“The tool didn’t just stop working. It broke. It fell off the ship during the trip. The tool is gone.”

“Oh! Wow. Why did it fall off?”

He wiggles his shell. “Don’t know. Many things break. My people made the ship very quickly. No time to make sure everything worked well.”

Problems with quality because of deadlines: a problem all over the galaxy.

“I tried to replace it. Failed. Tried. Failed. Tried. Failed. I put the ship in the path of the Astrophage. Maybe some will stick to the hull. But the robot on the hull can’t find any. Astrophage is very small.”

His shell slumps down. His elbows are higher than his breathing holes. He sometimes lowers his shell when sad, but I’ve never seen him lower it this much.

His voice gets deeper. “Fail fail fail. I repair things, not do science. The smart science people died.”

“Hey…don’t think like that…” I say.

“Don’t understand.”

“Uh…” I pull myself over to his bags. “You’re alive. And you’re here. And you haven’t quit.”

But his voice is still low. “I try so many times. Fail so many times. Not good at science.”

“I am,” I say. “I’m a science human. You’re good at making and fixing things. We’ll figure this out together.”

He raises his shell a bit. “Yes. Together. You have thing to get Astrophage, yes?”

The External Collection Unit. I remember it from my first day in the control room. I didn’t think about it much then, but that must be it. “Yes. I have a thing for this.”

"Relief! I tried for so long. So many times. Failed." He's quiet for a moment. "I spent much time here. Much time alone."

"How long were you here alone?"

He pauses. "I need new words."

I pull my laptop off the wall. We run into new words every day, but it's happening less and less. That's something.

I start the frequency analyzer and bring up my dictionary. "Ready."

"Seven thousand seven hundred and seventy-six seconds is ♩♫♩♪♪. Erid rotates once in one ♩♫♩♪♪."

I immediately know the number. I figured it out when I was studying Rocky's clock. 7,776 is six to the fifth power. It's exactly how many Eridian seconds it takes for an Eridian clock to reset to all zeroes. They divided their day into a very convenient (to them) number of seconds. I can follow that.

"Eridian day." I enter it into my dictionary. "A planet rotating once is a 'day.'"

"I understand," he says.

"Erid circles Eridani once every 198.8 Eridian days. 198.8 Eridian days is ♫♩♪♫♪."

“Year,” I say, and say it again as I understand. “A planet circling a star once is one year. So, that's an Eridian year.”

“We use Earth units, or you'll get confused. How long is an Earth day? And how many Earth days in an Earth year?”

“One Earth day is 86,400 seconds. One Earth year is 365.25 Earth days.”

“Understood,” he says. “I have been here forty-six years.”

“Forty-six years?!” I gasp. “Earth years?!”

“I have been here forty-six Earth years, yes.”

He’s been stuck in this system longer than I’ve been alive.

“How…how long do Eridians live?”

He wiggled a claw. “Average is six hundred eighty-nine years.”

“Earth years?”

“Yes,” he says a little sharply. “Always Earth units. You're not good at math, so always Earth units.”

I can’t even speak for a moment.

“How many years have you been alive?”

“Two hundred ninety-one years.” He pauses. “Yes. Earth years.”

Wow. Rocky is older than the United States. He was born around the same time as George Washington.

He’s not even that old for his species. There are old Eridians who were alive when Columbus discovered North America (which was already inhabited).

“Why are you so surprised?” Rocky asks. “How long do humans live?”
