Project Hail Mary

Chapter 10

They knocked, so I should knock back. I know the wall will be hot, so I tap it quickly.

I knock three times, like they did.

No answer right away. I study the hex wall. About forty hexes, and each looks different. Maybe different materials? I feel like I should do something, but what?

Are they watching? I don’t see a camera.

I point to my airlock. I don’t know if they can see me or understand. I push off the wall and go back to the airlock, then open the inner door. Why not? The pressure is the same. It’s okay to leave it open. If the tunnel loses pressure, the air will slam the door shut and I’ll live.

I go to the lab and pack a bag with some important things, then go back to the tunnel.

First, I tape LED lamps along the tunnel, pointing at the hex wall. Now I can see better. I use my handheld x-ray spectrometer to scan a hex. It’s xenonite, like the cylinders they sent.

Almost.

The trace elements are a bit different. Interesting. Maybe xenonite is like steel—many recipes? I check the next hex. Another unique mix.

Best guess: Different types of xenonite work best in different situations. They didn't know what my air was like, so they want to test different compounds against it. When I leave the tunnel, they’ll check the hexes to see which one does best.

That means I should leave the tunnel. Should I lower the pressure on my side for them? Seems polite. I could do it easily—I'd just tell the airlock to cycle. It would think, "Wow, there's a lot of air in me today!" but would just keep pumping until it was empty.

But then again, maybe they can sample the air on this side. If so, I should leave it here, right?

I decide to leave it as is. They probably have a way to sample it. If I were building this tunnel, that’s what I’d do, and they seem smart.

I turn back toward the airlock, but something catches my eye. Movement!

I quickly look back at the hex wall. Nothing's changed. But I could have sworn I saw something move. Some of the hexes are shiny—I probably just saw my reflection.

Wait…

One hex looks different. Why?

It’s near the tunnel wall. Not very noticeable. I float over to get a better look.

“Holy cow!” I say.

This hex is clear! All the others are cloudy, but this one is like glass! I grab a lamp from the wall and hold it up to the hex. I press my head against the hot wall to get a better look.

Light shines through to the other side. I can see the tunnel walls beyond. Either their side has no air, or their air is clear. Either way, nothing is blocking or blurring my view.

Suddenly, a rock hits the other side of the hex. It stays there, just inches from me. It’s roughly triangular, a dark brown color, with rough, jagged edges, like a spear tip from a caveman.

Have I found cavemen in space?

Stop being stupid, Ryland.

Why did they put a rock there? Is it sticky? Are they trying to block my view? If so, they’re failing. The little triangle is only a couple of inches wide at its thickest, and the hex is a good 8 inches across.

And it gets weirder. Now the rock is bending at joints, and two similar rocks do the same. Then there's a longer rock attached to them that—

That’s not a rock. It’s a claw! A claw with three fingers!

I need to see more! I press my face against the hex. It burns, but I don’t pull away. It hurts, and it’ll probably leave a mark. I should get a camera from the lab, but no one would think of that now.

I groan as my face hurts, but I get a better view.

The alien’s claw—er…I’ll call it a hand. That’s less scary. The alien’s hand has three triangular fingers, each with joints. Knuckles, I guess. They can close up into a raindrop shape or widen out to a sort of three-legged starfish.

The skin is weird. It looks like brownish-black rock. It’s uneven and bumpy, like someone carved the hand out of granite and hasn’t smoothed it yet. Natural armor, maybe? Like a turtle shell but less neat?

There’s an arm too. I can barely see it from this angle, no matter how hard I press my face into the Hot Wall of Pain. But there’s an arm leading away from the hand. I mean, there’d have to be, right? Not just a magic floating hand.

I can’t take the pain anymore. I pull my head away. I feel my face. It’s pretty raw, but there aren’t any blisters.

Tap-tap-tap.

The alien is tapping the clear hex with a finger. So I flick it with my finger three times.

It taps the hex again, three times. So I tap again as well.

Then comes something creepy. The cla— hand— retreats and returns with an object and holds it against the clear hexagon. Whatever it is, it’s small. I drift closer to the wall for a better look. The heat warms my face.

The object is xenonite, of course. It’s about a half-inch high and finely detailed. It looks like a doll. But it has an oversized head and really thick arms and legs—

“Oh!”

It’s me. I’m a tiny Russian Orlan-MKS2 EVA suit. That’s all they’ve seen of me so far.

Another hand appears. I have two hands, so it makes sense they do too. The second hand holds a model of the Hail Mary. It seems to be the same size as the little me. The hands then push the little me into the little Hail Mary’s airlock.

It’s clear. It’s saying, Go back into your ship.

I give a thumbs-up. The alien lets Mini-Me and the Hail Mary model float away. Then it twists a hand into something like a thumbs-up. It’s just two fingers curled into a ball with the third pointing up. At least it’s not the middle one.

I go back to the Hail Mary and close the airlock door.

I’m panting and excited. I can’t believe that just happened.

That’s an alien. I just saw an alien. Not just an alien ship, but an alien being. I mean—just his claw—er…hand. But still.

I say “his hand,” but maybe it’s her hand. Or some other pronoun I don’t know. They might have seventeen sexes, for all I know. Or none. No one talks about the hard parts of first contact: pronouns. I’ll use “he” for now, because it seems rude to call a thinking being “it.”

Also, until I hear otherwise, his name is Rocky.

Okay, now what? Rocky told me to go back into my ship. So I did.

I feel kind of dumb. There’s a lot of science I should be doing, right?

I peek through the airlock window. My lamps are still taped to the walls in the tunnel, and I can see some…changes.

The hex wall is gone. Just plain gone. I can see all the way to the Blip-A’s hull. And there’s a hull robot attached to it, reaching out and doing stuff with its little robot hands.

And yeah, its hands look like Rocky’s hands, generally. Three fingers. About the same size as Rocky’s hands. Probably controlled with something like a Nintendo Power Glove inside the ship.

Man, I’m old.

The robot is very interested in my lamps. Heck, I would be too. Those are alien artifacts with alien technology. Sure, they’re just lights, but they’re alien lights to my Eridian friends over there. Probably the most exciting scientific find of their history. The robot arm puts them in a little space on the Blip-A hull, and a latch closes. I bet those are going to be the most studied lamps ever.

I’m glad they got to have that moment of discovery, but they took my light source away. I can hear the occasional clunk, but it’s pitch-dark in there.

That’s interesting itself. I’m not an alien from 40 Eridani, but if I were working with a remote-controlled robot, I’d have a camera on it somewhere and a light to see what I was doing. But they don’t need that. They don’t need light.

Wait a minute. What they see might be totally different from what we see. Humans only see a small part of all the light out there. We changed to see the light that's most common on Earth. Maybe Eridians changed to see different light. The room could be full of infrared or ultraviolet light, and I wouldn't see anything.

Hmm. A robot. Why a robot? They had a living thing there a few minutes ago—my boy Rocky. Why switch him out with a robot?

Vacuum.

They probably took all the air out of the tunnel. They have a piece of my hull—they know it's made of aluminum and about how thick it is. Maybe they aren't sure if my ship can handle outside pressure. Or maybe their air messes with aluminum.

So they keep the tunnel empty, which means they have to use a robot.

I feel like Sherlock Holmes. All I saw was "nothing," and I figured out a bunch of stuff! Stuff that's just a guess and with nothing to prove it, but stuff anyway!

I could get another lamp—the lab has a few more. I could shine it in there to see what Robo-Rocky is doing. But I'll find out soon enough. And I don't want to be in some other part of the ship if something cool happens.

Just as I'm thinking that, something cool happens.

Knock-knock-knock.

No, that’s not creepy at all. Being in a spaceship twelve light-years from home and having someone knock on the door is totally normal.

Okay, now I need another lamp. I rush down to the lab to grab one, then back up to the control room. I cycle the airlock without putting on the EVA suit. I turn the manual vent valves on both doors of the airlock to repressurize the tunnel. It works as expected. The seal is still good out there.

I open the outer door and float in, lamp in hand.

The hex wall is gone—replaced by a solid wall of clear material. And on the other side of that wall is Rocky.

He’s a spider. A big spider.

I turn to run. But my rational brain takes over.

“Easy…easy…they’re friendly,” I say to myself. I turn back and look at the scene.

Rocky is smaller than a human. He’s about the size of a Labrador. He has five legs coming out from a central shell-like thing. The shell, roughly a pentagon, is 18 inches across and half as thick. I don’t see eyes or a face anywhere.

Each leg has a joint in the middle—I’ll call it an elbow. Each leg (or arm?) ends in a hand. So he’s got five hands. Each hand has the triangular fingers I saw closely last time. All five hands look the same. I don’t see any “front” or “back” to him. He seems to be symmetrical with five sides.

He wears clothing. The legs are bare, showing the rocklike skin, but there’s cloth on the shell. Like a shirt with five armholes. I don’t know what the shirt is made of, but it looks thicker than human clothing. It’s a dull greenish-brown, with uneven shading.

The top of the shirt has a large open hole. Like the neck of a human’s T-shirt. That hole is smaller than the shell. So he must put the shirt on by pulling it down and sliding the arms through the holes. Like a human’s shirt.

But there’s no neck or head to fit through that hole on top. Just a hard, rocky pentagon that sticks up a little from the rough skin.

On his side of the tunnel, there are handles and latticework on the walls. He casually holds onto a couple of bars with two of his hands. I guess when you have five hands, zero g isn’t that hard. Just use a hand or two to stay in place and use the other three to do stuff.

For me, the tunnel is kind of small. But for him, it’s absolutely spacious.

He waves to me with a free arm. He knows one human greeting and he plans to use it.

I wave back. He waves again. I shake my head. No more waving.

He pivots his “shoulders” to rotate his shell back and forth. He “shook his head” as much as he could. I wonder how we’re going to stop this game of “Eridian See, Eridian Do,” but he takes care of that for me.

He taps the clear wall three times with a finger, then keeps the finger pointing. Is he…pointing?

I follow the line and wow, there’s stuff in the tunnel with me! They left me a present!

I can be forgiven for not noticing. Seeing an alien kind of distracted me from the small collection of objects on the tunnel wall.

“All right,” I say. “Let’s see what you left me.”

“♩♫♪♪♫,” says Rocky.

My jaw drops. Yes, I’m in zero g. It still drops.

There was no clear way he said the sounds. Just notes. Like whale song. Except not really like whale song, because there were several at once. Whale chords, I guess. And he was responding to me. That means he can hear too.

And importantly, the sounds were in my hearing range. Some of the notes were low, some of them high. But definitely able to be heard. That alone is amazing when I think about it. He’s from a different planet, and a totally different family tree of life, but we ended up with sound ranges that work together.

On top of all that, he thought my noises deserved a response.

“You have a language!” I say. “How do you have a language?! You don’t have a mouth!”

“♫♫♩,” Rocky explains.

Thinking clearly, you can’t make spaceships without a civilization, and you can’t have a civilization without being able to talk to each other. So of course they have language. It’s interesting that they talk with sound, like humans do. Coincidence? Maybe not. Maybe that’s just the easiest way for that to happen through evolution.

“♪.” Rocky points to the objects they left me.

“Right, right,” I say. The whole language thing is way more interesting to me, and I’d rather look into that. But for now, Rocky wants to know what I think of his gifts.

I float over to the objects. They're attached to the wall with my own tape.

The objects are two spheres. Each has a raised image on it. One has the Hail Mary and the other has the Blip-A.

I pull the Hail Mary ball off the tape. It’s not warm. In fact, the tunnel isn’t warm anymore. Interesting. Maybe they saw I like things cooler and made it more comfortable for me.

There’s a rattle from inside the ball. I shake it and listen. More rattling.

I find a seam. I twist the top and bottom of the ball against each other and they turn. Left-handed screw, of course.

I look to Rocky to see if he approves. He has no face, so no expression. He just floats there, watching me. Well, not watching…no eyes. Actually, wait. How does he know what I'm doing? He clearly knows—he waved and stuff. He must have eyes somewhere. I probably just don’t see them.

I turn back to the sphere. I pull the two halves apart and inside is…a bunch more little spheres.

I sigh. This makes even more questions.

The little beads float out and drift across my view. They’re not separate. They’re connected by little strings. Like a complicated necklace. I spread it out as much as I can.

They look like—for lack of a better word—beaded handcuffs. Two circles of beads connected by a small thread. Each circle has eight beads on it. The connecting thread has none. This seems very planned. But I have no idea what it means.

Maybe the other ball—the one with the Blip-A picture on it—will tell me more. I let the handcuffs float and take the Blip-A ball off the wall. I shake it and hear a lot of rattling inside. I unscrew the two halves and another set of beads comes out.

Unlike the handcuffs, there’s only one ring in this thing. And it has seven beads, not eight. Also, it has three strings sticking out of the circle, each going to a single bead. It's like a necklace with decorations hanging off it.

There’s more inside. I shake the model and another necklace floats out. I look at it and it’s just like the one I just looked at. I keep shaking and more necklaces come out. They’re all the same. I gather them up and put them in my pockets.

“This reminds me of something…” I hit my head. “What does this remind me of…?”

Rocky taps his shell with a claw. I know he’s just copying me, but it feels like he’s saying, Think, dummy!

What would I tell my students now?

Why did I suddenly think of my students? I saw a picture of my classroom. A quick memory. I’m holding a model of a molecule and explaining—

“Molecules!” I grab the handcuffs and show them to Rocky. “These are molecules! You’re trying to tell me something about chemistry!”

“♫♪♫♫♪.”

But wait. These are strange molecules. They don’t make sense. I look at the handcuffs. Nothing makes a molecule like this. Eight atoms on one side, eight on the other, and connected by…what? Nothing? The string isn’t even coming off a bead. It’s just connecting the two circles.

“Atoms!” I say. “The beads are protons. So the circles of beads are atoms. And the little connectors are chemical bonds!”

“Okay, if that’s the case…” I hold up the handcuffs and count everything again. “Then this is two atoms, each with eight protons, connected to each other. Element number eight is oxygen. Two oxygens. O2! And it was in the Hail Mary ball.”

I hold it toward Rocky. “You clever fellow, this is my atmosphere!”

I grab the other set of beads. “So your atmosphere is…seven protons connected to three individual atoms with one proton each. A nitrogen attached to three hydrogens. Ammonia! Of course it’s ammonia! You breathe ammonia!”

That explains the strong smell on all of the little presents they left me. Remaining traces of their air.

My smile fades. “Yikes. You breathe ammonia?”

I count all the little ammonia necklaces they gave me. I only got one O2 molecule, but he gave me twenty-nine ammonias.

I think about it for a moment.

“Oh,” I say. “I get it. I see what you’re saying.”

I look to my alien counterpart. “You have twenty-nine times more atmosphere than I do.”

Wow. Two things I notice right away: First, Eridians live under immense pressure. Like—similar to being a thousand feet underwater on Earth. Secondly, xenonite is incredible. I’m not sure how thick that wall is—half an inch, maybe? Less? But it’s holding back about 28 times the pressure of Earth's atmosphere. And it’s just a big, flat panel with no extra support (the worst shape for holding pressure). Their whole ship is made of big flat panels. That material must be incredibly strong. No wonder I couldn’t bend or break the pieces they sent before.

Our environments are not remotely compatible. I’d die in seconds on his side of the tunnel. And I bet he wouldn’t survive in one twenty-ninth of his normal pressure with no ammonia.

Okay, that's fine. We have sound and we can use gestures. That’s a good way to start talking.

I pause to think about all this. It’s amazing. I have an alien friend, and we’re chatting! I’m so excited! But I’m also exhausted. I’m so tired I can barely think. I haven’t slept in two days. There’s always been some big event happening. I can’t stay awake forever. I need sleep.

I hold up one finger. The “wait a minute” sign. Hopefully he remembers it from last time. He holds up one of his fingers too.

I hurry back to the ship and down to the lab. There’s an analog clock on the wall. Because every lab needs one. It’s hard, but I get it off the wall and hold it. I also grab a dry-erase marker from the workstation.

I go back through the control room to the Tunnel of Aliens. Rocky is still there. He seems happier to see me. How do I know? I don’t. He just moved a bit and seems more alert.

I show him the clock. I turn the time-set dial on the back, to show him how the hands move. He makes a circle with his hand. He understands!

I set the clock to 12:00. Then I use the marker to draw a line from the center to the twelve and another from the center to the two. I’d rather sleep eight hours, but I don’t want to make Rocky wait too long. I’ll take a two-hour nap. “I’ll come back when the clock looks like this,” I say. As if he’d understand.

“♩♪♫.” He gestures. He reaches out with two hands and grabs…nothing. Then he pulls the nothing toward him.

“What?”

He taps the wall and points to the clock, then does it again. Does he want the clock closer to the wall?

I push the clock closer. This seems to excite him. He does the gesture faster. I move it further forward. The clock is almost touching the wall now. He does the gesture one more time, but slower this time.

Now, I have no idea what he wants. So I just push the clock against the wall. It’s touching now. He raises his hands and shakes them a little. Weird jazz hands. Is that good?

Okay, I hope he knows I’ll be back in two hours. I turn to leave but hear tap-tap-tap right away.

“Whaaat?” I say.

“♪♪♫♪,” he says, pointing to the clock. It moved a little away from the wall. He doesn’t like that.

“Um, okay,” I say. I pull a loop of tape off the wall, unloop it, and rip it in half. I use the two halves to tape the left and right sides of the clock to the clear wall.

Rocky gives me the jazz hands signal again. I think it means “yes” or “I like this.” Like nodding.

I turn to leave again, but tap-tap-tap!

I spin around once more. “Dude, I just want a darn nap!”

He holds up a finger, using my own sign language against me. Now I have to wait! I guess that’s fair. I hold up my finger to show I understand.

He opens a round door leading into his ship. It’s the right size for an Eridian—I would have a hard time squeezing through if I tried. He disappears inside, leaving the door open. I’d love to know what’s beyond the door, but I can’t see anything. It’s totally dark in there.

Hmm. Interesting. It is completely dark in his ship. That door probably leads to an airlock. But even an airlock would have some lights in it, wouldn’t it?

Rocky didn’t have any problem getting around. But I know he can see—he responds to my gestures. This makes my earlier idea about Eridian vision seem more likely: I think they see a different part of the light spectrum than humans do. Maybe they see only infrared or only ultraviolet. That airlock might be perfectly lit up as far as Rocky’s concerned, but I can’t see a thing. Likewise, my lights are completely useless to him.

I wonder if we see any of the same light. Maybe red (the color with the longest wavelength that humans can see) is “♪♫♩,” the shortest wavelength they can see. Or something. Might be worth checking. I should bring a rainbow of lights and find out if he can—oh, he’s back.

Rocky bounces into the tunnel and spider-walks along the rails to the dividing wall. He’s incredibly graceful. Either he’s very experienced in zero g, or Eridians are just really good at climbing around. They have five hands with opposable fingers, and he’s an interstellar traveler, so it’s probably a little bit of both.

With one hand, he holds up a device for me to see. It’s…I don’t know what it is.

It’s a cylinder (man, these people like cylinders), a foot long and maybe 6 inches wide. I can see that his grip squishes the casing a little bit. It’s made of a soft material, like foam rubber. The cylinder has five square windows in a row. Inside each window is a shape. I think they might be letters. But they’re not just ink on paper. They’re on a flat surface, but the symbols stick out about an eighth of an inch.

“Huh,” I say.

The symbol on the right spins away to be replaced by another. A couple of seconds later, it happens again. Then again.

“It’s a clock!” I say. “I showed you a clock, so you showed me a clock!”

I point to my clock, still taped to the wall, and then to his. He does the jazz hands with two of the hands he’s not using. I do jazz hands back.

I watch the Eridian clock for a while. Rocky holds it still for me to see. The symbols—numbers, probably—change in the right window. They’re on a rotor, like an old digital clock from home. After a bit, the rotor to the left of it changes one position. Aha!

It seems the right rotor changes every two seconds, or a little more. It shows six different symbols before repeating: “ℓ,” “I,” “V,” “λ,” “+,” and “V,” in that order. When it reaches “ℓ,” the next rotor to the left moves one step. After about a minute, that second rotor goes through all the symbols, and when it reaches “ℓ,” the third rotor from the right moves.

They seem to read left to right—like English. A nice coincidence, but not that strange. There are only four options: left to right, right to left, top to bottom, or bottom to top. So there was a 1 in 4 chance we’d be the same.

So his clock is easy for me to read. And it works like a car odometer. “ℓ” is clearly their 0. So “I” is 1, “V” is 2, “λ” is 3, “+” is 4, and “V” is 5. What about 6 through 9? They don’t exist. After “V” we go back to “ℓ.” Eridians use base six.

Of all the things I teach, number bases are the hardest for students to really get. There’s nothing special about the number 10. We have ten digits because we have ten fingers. It's simple. Rockies have three fingers per hand and only use two hands when counting (they keep the other three feet/hands on the ground to stay steady). So they have six fingers to work with.

“I like you, Rocky! You’re a genius!”

And he is! With this, Rocky showed me:

  • How Eridian numbers work (base six)
  • How Eridian numbers are written (ℓ, I, V, λ,+, V)
  • How Eridians read information (left to right)
  • How long an Eridian second is

I hold up a finger and hurry back into the ship for my stopwatch. I come back and time Rocky’s clock. I start the timer just as the third rotor changes. The right rotor keeps clicking over about every two seconds, and every six clicks, the next rotor moves one. This will take a while, but I want the count to be as correct as possible. It takes about a minute and a half for the third rotor to move just one click. I expect to be here for ten minutes or so. But I plan to watch the whole time.

Rocky gets bored. At least, I think that’s what’s happening. He starts fidgeting, then lets the clock float near the divider wall. Then he wanders around his side of the tunnel. I’m not sure if he’s doing anything special. He opens a door to his ship, starts to climb through, then stops. He seems to think about it, then changes his mind. He closes the door. He doesn’t want to leave while I’m still here. Maybe I’ll do or say something interesting.

“♪♪♩,” he says.

“I know, I know,” I say. I hold up a finger.

He holds up his finger, then goes back to slowly bouncing from wall to wall. Zero-g pacing.

Finally, the third rotor completes a full lap and I stop my timer. Total time: 511.0 seconds. I don’t have a calculator, and I’m too excited to go back to the ship to get one. I pull out a pen and do long division on the palm of my hand. One Eridian second is 2.366 Earth seconds.

I circle the answer on my palm and stare at it. I add a few exclamation points nearby because I think they fit.

I know it doesn’t seem like much, but this is a huge deal. Rocky and I are astronauts. If we’re going to talk, we’re going to talk science. And just like that, Rocky and I have set up a basic unit of time. Next up: length and mass!

No, actually. Next up—a nap. I’m so tired. I pull my clock off the wall, circle the “2” with my dry-erase marker—just to be as clear as possible, then tape it back in place. I wave. He waves back. Then I go back for a nap.

—

This is ridiculous. How can I expect to sleep? How could anyone in this situation? I’m still trying to understand what’s happening. There’s an alien out there.

And it’s killing me that I can’t find out what he knows about Astrophage. But you can’t talk about complex science with someone using only gestures. We need a shared language, even if it's basic.

I just need to keep doing what I’m doing. Work on science communication. The verbs and nouns of physics. It’s the one set of ideas we’re sure to share—physical laws are the same everywhere. And once we have enough words to actually talk about science, we’ll start talking about Astrophage.

And in “VVℓλI” Eridian seconds I’ll be talking to him again. How the heck can a guy sleep at a time like this? There’s no way I can just—
