Project Hail Mary

Chapter 4

Okay.

I think it’s time I really took a look at these screens!

How am I in another solar system?! That doesn’t even make sense! What star is that, anyway?! Oh my God, I am so going to die!

I hyperventilate for a while.

I remember what I tell my students: If you’re upset, take a deep breath, let it out, and count to ten. It dramatically reduced the number of tantrums in my classroom.

I take a breath. “One…two…thr—this isn’t working! I’m going to die!”

I hold my head in my hands. “Oh God. Where am I?”

I search the monitors for anything I can understand. There’s no lack of information—there’s too much. Each screen has a label on the top. “Life Support,” “Airlock Status,” “Engines,” “Robotics,” “Astrophage,” “Generators,” “Centrifuge”—wait a minute. Astrophage?

I check the Astrophage panel closely.

REMAINING: 20,906 KG

CONSUMPTION RATE: 6.045 G/S

What’s far more interesting than those numbers is the picture below. It shows what I think is the Hail Mary. My first good look at what this ship is like.

The top of the ship is a cylinder with a pointy end. It looks just like a rocket. Since the control room has tapered, cone-shaped walls, it must be at the very front. Below me is the lab. In the picture, that room says “Lab.” Below that is the room I woke up in.

The one with my dead friends.

I sniff and wipe a tear. No time for that now. I push it away and keep looking at the picture. That room is called “Dormitory.” Okay, so this picture matches what I’ve seen. And it’s good to know the real names of things. Under the dormitory is a much shorter room, maybe 1 meter tall, called “Storage.” Aha! There must be a panel in the floor I didn’t see. I’ll check that out later.

But there’s more. A lot more. Under the storage area, there’s an area called “Cable Faring.” No idea what that is or why it’s there. Below that, the ship widens, and there seem to be three cylinders as wide as my little area. They’re next to each other. I bet they built this ship in space, and the biggest thing they could launch was about 4 meters wide.

The three cylinders—I think they’re about 75 percent of the ship’s total size—are called “Fuel.”

The fuel area is split into nine smaller cylinders. I tap one just to see what happens, and it shows a screen for that fuel bay. It says ASTROPHAGE: 0.000 KG. It also has a button that says “Jettison.”

Well, I don’t know why I’m here or what this is all about, but I don’t want to press any button that says Jettison.

It’s probably not as exciting as it looks. These are fuel tanks. If the fuel is used up, the ship can drop the tank to be lighter and make the remaining fuel last longer. It’s the same reason rockets launching from Earth have multiple stages.

It’s odd that the ship didn’t automatically drop them when they were empty. I close the window and go back to the main ship map.

Under each of those large fuel areas is a shape like a trapezoid labeled “Spin Drive.” I’ve never heard that name before, but since it’s in the back of the ship and has the word “drive” in it, I guess it’s the engine.

Spin drive…spin drive…I close my eyes and try to think about it….

—

Nothing happens. I can’t just pull up memories whenever I want. I’m not quite able to do that yet.

I look at the diagram more closely. Why is there 20,000 kilograms of Astrophage on this ship? I have a strong feeling I know. It’s the fuel.

And why not? Astrophage can move itself with light and has amazing energy-storage ability. It’s had billions of years to evolve and get good at it. Just like a horse uses energy better than a truck, Astrophage uses energy better than a spaceship.

Okay, that explains why there’s a lot of Astrophage on the ship. It’s fuel. But why put a picture of the ship on this screen? That’s like putting a blueprint of a car on its gas gauge.

Interestingly, the diagram doesn’t really care about the rooms. It doesn’t even show what’s inside them—just a label for each one and that’s it. However, the diagram is very focused on the hull and the back of the ship.

I see red pipes going from the fuel areas to the spin drives. Probably how fuel gets to the engines. But I also see the pipes all along the hull of the ship. And they cut across the Cable Faring area. So the Astrophage fuel is mostly in the fuel tank, but also kept in a layer all around the hull.

Why do that?

Oh, and there are temperature readings everywhere. I guess temperature is important because the readings are every few meters along the hull. And every single one of them reads 96.415°c.

Hey, I know that temperature. I know that exact temperature! Where do I know it from? Come on, brain… come on…

—

96.415°c, the display read.

“Huh,” I said.

“What is it?” Stratt said immediately.

It was my second day in the lab. Stratt still wanted me to be the only one to look at Astrophage—at least for now. She dropped her tablet on the table and came to the observation-room window. “Something new?”

“Kind of. The normal temperature of an Astrophage is 96.415 degrees Celsius.”

“That’s pretty hot, isn’t it?”

“Yeah, almost the temperature water boils,” I said. “It would kill anything living on Earth. But for something that likes being near the sun, who knows?”

“So what’s important about it?”

“I can’t make them hotter or colder.” I pointed to the experiment I’d set up in the fume hood. “I put some Astrophage in ice-cold water for an hour. When I took them out, they were 96.415 degrees Celsius. Then I put some in a lab furnace at one thousand degrees. Again, when I took them out: 96.415 degrees.”

Stratt paced next to the window. “Maybe they’re really good at keeping heat in?”

“I thought of that, so I did another experiment. I took a tiny drop of water and put a few Astrophage in it. After a few hours, the whole drop was 96.415 degrees. The Astrophage heated up the water, so heat can get out of it.”

“What does that mean?” she asked.

I tried to scratch my head, but the plastic suit stopped me. “Well, we know they have a lot of energy inside. I think they use it to keep their body temperature steady. Like we do.”

“A warm-blooded tiny organism?” she said.

I shrugged. “Looks like it. Hey, how much longer will I be the only one working on this?”

“Until you stop finding new things.”

“One guy alone in a lab? That’s not how science is done,” I said. “Hundreds of people all over the world should be working on this.”

“You’re not the only one who thinks that,” she said. “Three different leaders have called me today.”

“Then let other scientists help!”

“No.”

“Why not?”

She looked away for a moment, then back at me through the window. “Astrophage is a microbe from space. What if it can infect people? What if it’s deadly? What if protective suits and gloves aren’t enough?”

I gasped. “Wait! Am I a test subject? I’m a test subject!”

“No, it’s not like that,” she said.

I stared at her.

She stared at me.

I stared at her.

“Okay, it’s exactly like that,” she said.

“Dang it!” I said. “That’s just not cool!”

“Don’t be dramatic,” she said. “I’m just being careful. Think about what would happen if I sent Astrophage to the smartest people on Earth and it killed them. We’d instantly lose the very people we need most right now. I can’t risk that.”

I frowned. “This isn’t some dumb movie, Stratt. Diseases change slowly over time to attack certain hosts. Astrophage has never even been on Earth before. There’s no way it can ‘infect’ humans. Besides, it’s been a few days and I’m not dead. So send it to the real scientists.”

“You are a real scientist. And you’re making progress as fast as anyone else would. There’s no reason for me to risk other lives while you’re getting it done on your own.”

“Are you kidding?” I said. “With a couple hundred people working on this, we’d make much more progress on—”

“Also, most deadly diseases need at least three days to start showing.”

“Ah, there it is.”

She walked back to her table and picked up her tablet. “The rest of the world will get their chance later. But for now, it’s just you. At least tell me what those things are made of. Then we can talk about sharing it with other scientists.”

She resumed reading her tablet. The conversation was over. And she’d ended it with a zinger. Despite my best efforts, I still had no idea what Astrophage was made of.

They blocked every type of light I tried. Visible, infrared, ultraviolet, x-ray, microwaves…I even put some Astrophage in a radiation-proof container and hit it with gamma rays from Cesium-137 (this lab has everything). I called it the “Bruce Banner Test.” I liked that name. Anyway, even gamma rays couldn’t get through those little guys. That's like shooting a .50-caliber bullet at a piece of paper and it bounces off. It just doesn’t make sense.

I went back to the microscope, feeling grumpy. The little dots were still on the slide where they’d been for hours. This was my control group. The ones I hadn’t hit with different light sources. “Maybe I’m thinking about this too much…” I muttered.

I looked around the lab supplies until I found what I needed: nanosyringes. They were rare and expensive, but the lab had them. They were basically tiny needles. Small and sharp enough to poke microorganisms. You could pull mitochondria out of a living cell with one of those.

Back to the microscope. “Okay, you little punks. I’ll admit you’re radiation-proof. But what if I stab you in the face?”

Normally, a nanosyringe would be controlled by special equipment. But I just wanted to stab something and didn’t care about breaking the tool. I grabbed the part that would normally connect to the control machine and moved the needle into view under the microscope. They’re called nanosyringes, but they’re actually about 50 nanometers wide. Still, the needle was tiny compared to the big 10-micron Astrophage—only about one two-thousandth of its width.

I poked an Astrophage with the needle, and what happened next was completely unexpected.

First, the needle went in. No question about that. Despite being resistant to light and heat, Astrophage was no better at handling sharp objects than any other cell, it seemed.

As soon as I made a hole in it, the whole cell turned clear. It wasn't just a plain black dot anymore, but a cell with organelles and everything else I, as a microbiologist, wanted to see. Just like that. It was like flipping a switch.

And then it died. The broken cell wall just gave way and completely fell apart. The Astrophage went from being a solid round object to a slowly spreading puddle with no edge. I grabbed a regular needle from a nearby shelf and sucked up the goo.

“Yes!” I said. “I killed one!”

“Good for you,” Stratt said, not looking up from her tablet. “First human to kill an alien. Just like Arnold Schwarzenegger in Predator. ”

“Okay, I know you’re trying to be funny, but that Predator died by setting off a bomb on purpose. The first human to actually kill a Predator was Michael Harrigan—played by Danny Glover—in Predator 2.”

She looked at me through the window for a moment, then shook her head and rolled her eyes.

“The thing is, I can finally find out what Astrophage is made of!”

“Really?” She put the tablet down. “Killing it did the trick?”

“I think so. It’s not black anymore. Light is getting through. Whatever strange effect was blocking it isn’t anymore.”

“How did you do it? What killed it?”

“I got through the outer cell membrane with a nanosyringe.”

“You poked it with a stick?”

“No!” I said. “Well. Yes. But it was a scientific poke with a very scientific stick.”

“It took you two days to think of poking it with a stick.”

“You…be quiet.”

I took the needle to the spectroscope and squirted the Astrophage goop onto the platform. Then I sealed the chamber and started the analysis. I bounced from one foot to the other like a little kid while I waited for the results.

Stratt craned her neck to watch me. “So what’s this you’re doing now?”

“It’s the atomic-emission spectroscope,” I said. “I told you about it earlier—it sends x-rays into a sample to energize the atoms, then watches the wavelengths that come back. Didn’t work at all when I tried it on the live Astrophage, but now that the magic light-stopping properties are gone, things should work like normal.”

The machine beeped.

“All right! Here we go! Time to find out what chemicals are in a life-form that doesn’t use water!” I read the LCD screen. It showed all the peaks and the elements they represented. I stared at the screen silently.

“Well?” Stratt said. “Well?!”

“Um. There’s carbon and nitrogen…but most of the sample is hydrogen and oxygen.” I sighed and plopped down in the chair next to the machine. “There are twice as many hydrogen atoms as oxygen atoms.”

“What’s wrong?” she asked. “What does that mean?”

“It’s water. Astrophage is mostly water.”

Her mouth fell open. “How? How can something that lives on the surface of the sun have water?”

I shrugged. “Probably because it keeps its internal temperature at 96.415 degrees Celsius no matter what.”

“What does this all mean?” she asked.

I put my head in my hands. “It means every scientific paper I ever wrote is wrong.”

Well. That's a blow.

But I wasn't happy in that lab anyway. And they must have found someone smarter than me, because here I am: at another star in a ship powered by Astrophage.

So why am I the one out here? All I did was prove that what I always believed was wrong.

I guess I’ll think about that later. For now, I want to know what star that is. And why we built a ship to bring people here.

All important questions, for sure. But right now, there’s a whole part of the ship that I haven’t checked out yet.

Storage.

Maybe I can find something to wear other than this makeshift toga.

I climb down the ladder to the lab, and then farther down into the dormitory.

My friends are still there. Still dead. I try not to look at them.

I look around the floor for any sign of a hidden door. Nothing. So I get down and crawl around. Finally, I see it—a very thin line marking a square right under my male crewmate’s bed. I can’t even get my fingernail into the line, it’s so thin.

There were all kinds of tools in the lab. I’m sure there’s a flat screwdriver I could use to open this. Or…

“Hey computer! Open this access panel.”

“Specify aperture to open.”

I point to the panel. “This. This thing. Open it.”

“Specify aperture to open.”

“Uh…open aperture to supply room.”

“Unsealing supply room,” says the computer.

There’s a click, and the panel lifts up a few inches. A rubber seal around the line gets torn in the process. I couldn’t see it when the panel was closed, it was so tight. I’m glad I didn’t try to pry it open. It would have been a pain.

I pull the bits of the seal off the panel, and the panel comes loose. I shake it a bit before realizing I have to turn it. Once I turn it 90 degrees, it comes off, and I put it aside. I poke my head into the room below and see a bunch of soft, white cubes. I guess that makes sense. Packing stuff in soft containers lets you fit more things into the room.

Just like the control room diagram showed, the storage area is about a meter high, and packed with those soft containers. I'd have to move a bunch just to get in—if I wanted to. I guess I will eventually. It looks a bit cramped, to be honest, like a crawlspace under a house.

I grab the closest package and pull it up through the opening.

The package is held together by Velcro straps. I pull them apart, and the container unfolds like a Chinese takeout box. Inside are a bunch of uniforms.

Jackpot! Though it's not really a surprise. Whoever packed this probably planned it carefully. They knew the crew would want uniforms right after waking up, so they put them in the first bag. There are at least a dozen uniforms in the package, each in vacuum-sealed plastic bags. I open one randomly.

It’s a light-blue, one-piece jumpsuit. Astronaut clothes. The fabric is thin but feels comfortable. On the left shoulder is the Hail Mary mission patch, the same design I saw in the control room. Below it is the Chinese flag. The right shoulder has a white patch with a blue chevron triangle inside a wreath and the letters “CNSA.” I recognize it right away, being a nerd. It’s the Chinese National Space Agency logo.

There’s a name tag over the left breast pocket. It reads 姚—the same character I saw in the Hail Mary mission logo. It’s pronounced Yáo.

How do I know—? Of course I know. Commander Yáo. He was our leader. I can see his face now, young and striking, with determined eyes. He knew how important the mission was and the weight on his shoulders. He was ready. He was strict but fair. And you knew—you just knew—he’d give his life in a second for the mission or his crew.

I pull out another uniform, much smaller than the commander’s. The mission patch is the same, but there’s a Russian flag below it. The right shoulder has a tilted red chevron in a ring. It’s the symbol of Roscosmos—the Russian space agency. The name patch reads ИЛЮХИНА, another name from the logo. This was Ilyukhina’s uniform.

Olesya Ilyukhina. She was hilarious. She could have you laughing in thirty seconds. She just had a fun, contagious personality. Yáo was serious, Ilyukhina was easygoing. They argued about it sometimes, but even Yáo liked her. I remember when he finally laughed at one of her jokes. You can’t be serious all the time.

I stand up and look at the bodies. No longer a stern commander, no longer a cheerful friend, just two empty shells that once held souls but now barely look human. They deserve more than this. They deserve a burial.

The container has several outfits for each of us. I finally find mine. They're just as I pictured. A Hail Mary mission patch with a U.S. flag, a NASA logo on the right, and a name tag that says GRACE.

I put on my jumpsuit. After more searching, I find footwear. They're not really shoes. Just thick socks with rubber soles—grippy booties. I guess that's all we need for the mission. I put them on, too.

Then I start the sad job of dressing my dead crewmates. The jumpsuits look too big on their thin, dry bodies. I even put the booties on. Why not? It's our uniform. And a traveler should be buried in uniform.

I start with Ilyukhina. She's so light. I carry her on my shoulder up the ladders to the control room. I set her on the floor and open the airlock. The spacesuit inside is big and in the way. I move it, piece by piece, into the control room and put it on the pilot's chair. Then I put Olesya into the airlock.

The airlock controls are easy to understand. The air pressure and the outer door are controlled from the panel in the control room. There's even a Jettison button. I close the door and start the jettison process.

It starts with a buzzing alarm, flashing lights, and a voice counting down. There are three Abort switches inside the airlock. Anyone in there can easily stop the process.

When the countdown ends, the airlock loses pressure to 10 percent of an atmosphere (according to the screen). Then it opens the outer door. With a whoosh, Olesya is gone. The ship's speed makes her body drift away.

"Olesya Ilyukhina," I say. I don't know her religion or if she had one. I don't know what she would have wanted me to say. But I'll remember her name. "I commend your body to the stars." It feels right. Maybe cheesy, but it makes me feel better.

Next, I carry Commander Yáo to the airlock. I put him inside, seal it, and jettison him the same way.

"Yáo Li-Jie," I say. I don't know how I remembered his first name. It just came to me. "I commend your body to the stars."

The airlock cycles and I am alone. I was alone before, but now I am truly alone. The only living person for light-years, at least.

What do I do now?

The kids all sat at their desks, ready for science class.

Michael spoke up. “The substitute teacher was so booooring.”

“Well, I’m not,” I said. I picked up four plastic bins from the corner. “Today we’re going to look at rocks! Okay, maybe that is a little boring.”

A chuckle from the kids.

“You’re going to split into four teams, and each team will get a bin. You have to sort the rocks into igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic. The first team to finish—and get every rock in the right place—gets beanbags.”

“Can we pick our own teams?” Trang asked, excited.

“No. That just causes problems. Because kids are animals. Terrible, terrible animals.”

Everyone laughed.

“Teams will be in alphabetical order. So the first team is—”

Abby raised her hand. “Mr. Grace, can I ask something?”

“Sure.”

“What’s going on with the sun?”

The whole class suddenly paid much closer attention.

“My dad says it’s not a big deal,” Michael said.

“My dad says it’s a government conspiracy,” said Tamora.

“Okay…” I set the bins down and sat on the edge of my desk. “So…basically, you know how there’s algae in the ocean, right? Well, there’s a kind of space algae growing in the sun.”

“Astrophage?” said Harrison.

I almost slipped off the desk. “Wh-Where did you hear that word?”

“That’s what they’re calling it now,” said Harrison. “The president said it in a speech last night.”

I’d been so isolated in that lab I didn’t even know the president had given a speech. And wow. I invented that word for Stratt the day before. In that time it got from her to the president to the media.

Wow.

“Okay, yes. Astrophage. And it’s growing on the sun. Or near it. People aren’t sure.”

“So what’s the problem?” Michael asked. “Algae in the ocean doesn’t hurt us. Why would algae on the sun?”

I pointed to him. “Good question. Thing is, Astrophage is starting to absorb a lot of the sun’s energy. Well, not a lot. Just a small percentage. But that means Earth gets a little less sunlight. And that can cause real problems.”

“So it’ll be a little colder? Like a degree or two?” Abby asked. “What’s the big deal?”

“You guys know about climate change, right? How our CO2 has messed up the environment?”

“My dad says that’s not real,” said Tamora.

“Well, it is,” I said. “Anyway. All the environmental problems we have from climate change? They happened because the world’s average temperature went up one and a half degrees. That’s it. Just one and a half degrees.”

“How much will this Astrophage stuff change Earth’s temperature?” asked Luther.

I stood and paced slowly in front of the class. “We don’t know. But if it grows as fast as algae, experts are saying Earth’s temperature could drop ten to fifteen degrees.”

“What’ll happen?” Luther asked.

“It’ll be bad. Very bad. A lot of animals—entire species—will die out because it’s too cold where they live. The ocean water will cool down, too, and it might cause the whole food chain to collapse. So even things that could live through the lower temperature will starve because the things they eat all die off.”

The kids stared at me, amazed. Why hadn’t their parents explained this to them? Probably because they didn’t get it themselves.

Besides, if I got a nickel every time I wanted to smack a kid’s parents for not teaching them even the simplest things…well…I’d have enough nickels to put in a sock and smack those parents with it.

“Animals are going to die too?!” Abby asked, horrified.

Abby rode horses in competitions and spent most of her time at her grandfather’s dairy farm. Kids often don't really grasp human suffering. But animal suffering is different.

“Yes, I’m sorry, but a lot of farm animals will die. And it’s worse than that. Crops will fail. We won't have enough food. When that happens, society often falls apart and—” I stopped. They were just kids. Why was I saying so much?

“How—” Abby started. I’d never seen her speechless. “How long before this happens?”

“Climate scientists think it’ll happen within the next thirty years,” I said.

Right away, all the kids relaxed.

“Thirty years?” Trang laughed. “That’s forever!”

“It’s not that long…” I said. But to twelve- and thirteen-year-olds, thirty years seemed like a million.

“Can I be on Tracy’s team for the rock-sorting assignment?” asked Michael.

Thirty years. I looked at their young faces. In thirty years they’d all be in their early forties. They would suffer the most. And it wouldn’t be easy. These kids were going to grow up in a perfect world and be thrown into a terrible nightmare.

They were the generation that would live through the Sixth Extinction Event.

I felt a cramp in my stomach. I was looking at a room full of children. Happy children. And there was a good chance some of them would actually die of hunger.

“I…” I stuttered. “I have to go do something. Forget the rock assignment.”

“What?” asked Luther.

“Do…study hall. This is study hall for the rest of the hour. Just do homework from other classes. Stay in your seats and work quietly until the bell rings.”

I left the room without another word. I almost collapsed in the hall from the shakes. I went to a drinking fountain and splashed water on my face. Then I took a deep breath, got some self-control back, and jogged to the parking lot.

I drove fast. Way too fast. I ran red lights. I cut people off. I never do any of that, but that day was different. That day was…I don’t even know.

I screeched into the lab parking lot and left my car parked crookedly.

Two U.S. Army soldiers were at the doors to the complex. Just as they had been the previous two days while I’d been working there. I stormed past them.

“Should we have stopped him?” I heard one ask the other. I didn’t care what the response was.

I stomped into the observation room. Stratt was there, as always, reading her tablet. She looked up and I saw real surprise on her face.

“Dr. Grace? What are you doing here?”

Beyond her, through the windows, I saw four people in protective suits working in the lab.

“Who are they?” I asked, pointing at the window. “And what are they doing in my lab?”

“I don’t like your tone,” she said.

“I don’t care.”

“And it’s not your lab. It’s my lab. Those technicians are gathering the Astrophage.”

“What are you going to do with it?”

She held her tablet under her arm. “Your dream is coming true. I’m dividing up the Astrophage and sending it to thirty different labs around the world. Everywhere from CERN to a CIA bioweapons facility.”

“The CIA has a biowea—?” I started. “Never mind. I want to do more work on this.”

She shook her head. “You’ve done your part. We thought it was life without water. Turns out it wasn’t. You proved that. And since no alien burst out of you, we can say the test phase is over too. So you’re done.”

“No, I’m not done. There’s a lot more to learn.”

“Of course there is,” she said. “And I have thirty labs all waiting to start on it.”

I stepped forward. “Leave some Astrophage here. Let me keep working on it.”

She stepped forward as well. “No.”

“Why not?!”

“According to your notes, there were one hundred and seventy-four living Astrophage cells in the sample. And you killed one yesterday, so we’re down to a hundred and seventy-three.”

She pointed to her tablet. “Each of these labs—huge, national labs—will get five or six cells each. That’s it. We’re down to that little. Those cells are the one hundred and seventy-three most important things on Earth right now. Our study of them will decide if humanity survives.”

She paused and spoke a little more softly. “I understand. You spent your whole life trying to prove that life doesn’t need water. Then, unbelievably, you get some actual alien life and it turns out to need water. That’s hard. Get over it and get back to your life. I’ve got it from here.”

“I’m still a microbiologist who spent his career creating ideas about alien life. I’m useful and have skills almost no one else has.”

“Dr. Grace, I can’t afford to leave samples here just to make you feel better.”

“Feel better?! This isn’t about me! It’s about my children!”

“You don’t have children.”

“Yes, I do! Dozens of them. They come to my class every day. And they’re all going to end up in a Mad Max nightmare if we don’t fix this. Yeah, I was wrong about the water. I don’t care about that. I care about those kids. So give me some gosh-darned Astrophage! ”

She stepped back and pursed her lips. She looked to the side, thinking it over. Then she turned back to me. “Three. You can have three Astrophage.”

I unclenched my muscles. “Okay.” I breathed a little. I didn’t realize how tense I’d been. “Okay. Three. I can work with that.”

She typed on her tablet. “I’ll keep this lab open. It’s all yours. Come back in a few hours and my people will be gone.”

I was already halfway into a containment suit. “I’m getting back to work now. Tell your people to stay out of my way.”

She glared at me but didn’t say anything further.

I have to do this for my kids.

I mean…they’re not my kids. But they’re my kids.

I look at the screens in front of me. I need to think this through.

My memory is unreliable. It seems okay, but it’s not complete. Instead of waiting for a moment of sudden realization where I remember everything, what can I figure out now?

Earth is in trouble. The sun is infected with Astrophage. I’m in a spaceship in another solar system. This ship was hard to build and it had an international crew. This is an interstellar mission—something that should be impossible with our technology. So, humanity put a lot of time and effort into this mission, and Astrophage was the missing piece that made it possible.

There’s only one reason for this: There’s a solution to the Astrophage problem here. Or a possible solution. Something good enough to spend a lot of resources on.

I check the screens for more info. Mostly they seem to be the usual things you’d find on a spaceship. Life support, navigation, that kind of thing. One screen is labeled “Beetles.” The next screen over says—

Wait, beetles?

Okay, I don’t know if it matters, but I need to find out if there are a lot of beetles on this ship. That’s something I need to know.

The screen is split into four parts, each showing almost the same thing: a small diagram and some text. The diagrams each show a rounded, oval shape with a pointed head and a trapezoid on the back. If you tilt your head and squint, it might look like a beetle. Each beetle also has a name above it: “John,” “Paul,” “George,” and “Ringo.”

Yeah, I get it. I’m not laughing, but I get it.

I randomly pick one beetle, John, and examine it closely.

John is no insect. I’m pretty sure it’s a spaceship. The trapezoid in the back is labeled “Spin Drive,” and the entire oval part is labeled “Fuel.” The little head has a “Computer” label and a “Radio” label.

I look closer. The Fuel info box says ASTROPHAGE: 120KG—TEMP: 96.415°c. The Computer box says LAST MEMORY CHECK: 3 DAYS AGO. 5 TB FUNCTIONING CORRECTLY. And the Radio info just says STATUS: 100%.

It’s an unmanned probe. Something small, I guess. The total mass of the fuel is just 120 kilograms. That’s not much. But a little Astrophage is very powerful. There aren’t any scientific instruments labeled. What’s the purpose of an unmanned ship with nothing on board?

Wait…what if the 5 terabytes of storage is the ship’s purpose?

A realization hits me.

“Oh. Crap,” I say.

I’m out in space. I’m in another star system. I don’t know how much Astrophage it took to get here, but it was probably a lot. Sending a ship to another star probably took a ridiculous amount of fuel. Sending that ship to another star and bringing it back would take ten times as much fuel.

I check the Astrophage panel to refresh my memory.

REMAINING: 20,862 KG

CONSUMPTION RATE: 6.043 G/S

The consumption rate used to be 6.045 grams per second. So it's gone down a little. And the fuel amount went down too. Basically, as the fuel is used up, the ship gets lighter, so it needs less fuel per second to keep the same acceleration. Okay, that makes sense.

I don't know the Hail Mary's exact weight, but to push it at 1.5 times the force of gravity with just a few grams of fuel per second…Astrophage is incredible.

Anyway, I don't know exactly how the consumption rate will change (I could figure it out, but it's complicated). So I'll just guess it's about 6 grams per second. How long will the fuel last?

It's nice to have a jumpsuit on. It has pockets for little things. I still haven't found a calculator, so I do the math with a pen and paper. In total, I'll run out of fuel in about forty days.

I don't know what star that is, but it's not the sun. And there's no way to get from another star to Earth in just forty days of accelerating at 1.5 g's. It probably took years to get here from Earth—which might be why I was in a coma. Interesting.

Anyway, this means one thing: The Hail Mary isn't going home. This is a one-way trip. And I think these beetles are how I'm supposed to send information back to Earth.

I don't have a radio powerful enough to send signals light-years away. I don't know if that's even possible. So instead, I have these little "beetle" ships with 5 terabytes of information each. They'll fly back to Earth and send their data. There are four of them in case one fails. I should probably put copies of my findings in each one and send them all home. If one makes it, Earth is saved.

I’m on a suicide mission. John, Paul, George, and Ringo get to go home, but my long and winding road ends here. I must have known all this when I signed up. But to my memory-lost brain this is new information. I’m going to die out here. And I’m going to die alone.
