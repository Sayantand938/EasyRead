Project Hail Mary

Chapter 2

Okay, breathe. Let's not freak out. Yes, the gravity is too strong. Start there and think of reasonable answers.

I could be in a centrifuge. It would have to be pretty big. But with Earth's gravity giving 1 g, you could have these rooms at an angle going around a track or on the end of a long arm or something. Spin that around, and the total force of the spin plus Earth's gravity could be 15 meters per second per second.

Why would someone build a huge centrifuge with hospital beds and a lab in it? I don't know. Could you even do that? How big would it have to be? And how fast would it spin?

I think I know how to figure it out. I need a good accelerometer. Dropping things off a table and timing them is okay for rough guesses, but it's only as good as my reaction time on the stopwatch. I need something better. And only one thing will work: a small piece of string.

I look through the lab drawers.

After a few minutes, I have half the drawers open and have found almost every kind of lab supply except string. I'm about to quit when I finally find a spool of nylon thread.

"Yes!" I pull off a few feet of thread and bite it off. I tie a loop on one end and tie the other end around the tape measure. The tape measure will be the "weight" in this test. Now I just need something to hang it from.

I look up at the hatch over my head. I climb up the ladder (easier now) and put the loop over the latch handle. Then I let the tape measure pull the string tight.

I have a pendulum.

The cool thing about pendulums is that the time it takes to swing back and forth—the period—stays the same, no matter how big the swing. If it has a lot of energy, it swings farther and faster, but the period is still the same. This is how mechanical clocks keep time. The period depends on only two things: the length of the pendulum and gravity.

I pull the pendulum to the side. I let it go and start the timer. I count each swing back and forth. It’s not exciting. I almost fall asleep, but I keep going.

When I reach ten minutes, the pendulum is hardly moving, so I decide that’s enough. In total, it swung 346 times in ten minutes.

Now for phase two.

I measure from the hatch handle to the floor. It’s a little over two and a half meters. I go back downstairs to the “bedroom.” Again, the ladder is easy. I feel much better now. That food really helped.

“What’s your name?” the computer asks.

I look at my sheet toga. “I am the great philosopher Pendulus!”

“Incorrect.”

I hang the pendulum on a robot hand near the ceiling. I hope it stays still for a while. I estimate the distance from the robot hand to the ceiling—about a meter. My pendulum is now four and a half meters lower than before.

I do the experiment again. Ten minutes on the stopwatch, and I count the swings. The result: 346 swings. Same as upstairs.

Gosh.

Here's the thing: in a centrifuge, the farther you are from the middle, the stronger the force. So, if I were in a centrifuge, the "gravity" down here would be stronger than upstairs. But it isn't. At least, not enough to change how many times the pendulum swings.

But what if I'm in a really big centrifuge? One so big that the force difference between here and the lab is so small it doesn't change the number of swings?

Let's see…the formula for a pendulum…and the formula for the force of a centrifuge…wait, I don't have the actual force, just a swing count, so there’s a one-over-x thing involved…this is actually a very helpful problem!

I have a pen, but no paper. That’s okay—I have a wall. After a lot of "crazy prisoner scribbling on a wall"–type stuff, I have my answer.

Let's say I'm on Earth and in a centrifuge. That would mean the centrifuge adds to the force already here from Earth. According to my math (and I showed all my work!), that centrifuge would need a 700-meter radius (which is almost half a mile) and would be spinning at 88 meters per second—almost 200 miles per hour!

Hmm. I mostly think in metric when doing science. Interesting. Most scientists do, though, right? Even scientists who grew up in America.

Anyway, that would be the biggest centrifuge ever…and why would anyone build it? Plus, it would be super loud. Zooming through the air at 200 miles per hour? At the very least there’d be some shaking here and there, not to mention a lot of wind noise. I don’t hear or feel anything like that.

This is getting strange. Okay, what if I'm in space? There wouldn't be shaking or wind resistance, but the centrifuge would have to be bigger and faster because there’s no gravity to help.

More math, more graffiti on the wall. The radius would have to be 1,280 meters—close to a mile. Nothing that big has ever been built for space.

So I’m not in a centrifuge. And I’m not on Earth.

Another planet? But there isn’t any planet, moon, or asteroid in the solar system with this much gravity. Earth is the biggest solid thing in the whole system. Sure, the gas giants are larger, but unless I’m in a balloon floating in Jupiter’s winds, there’s nowhere I could be to feel this force.

How do I know all that space stuff? I just know it. It feels natural—like information I always use. Maybe I’m an astronomer or a planetary scientist. Maybe I work for NASA or ESA or—

I met Marissa every Thursday night for steak and beer at Murphy’s on Gough Street. Always at six p.m., and because the staff knew us, always at the same table.

We’d met almost twenty years ago in grad school. She dated my roommate back then. Their relationship (like most in grad school) was a mess and they broke up within three months. But she and I became good friends.

When the host saw me, he smiled and pointed to our usual table. I walked through the corny decorations to Marissa. She had a couple of empty glasses in front of her and a full one in her hand. Looks like she’d started early.

“Pre-gaming, huh?” I said, sitting down.

She looked down and played with her glass.

“Hey, what’s wrong?”

She took a sip of whiskey. “Rough day at work.”

I signaled the waiter. He nodded but didn’t even come over. He knew I wanted a rib-eye, medium, mashed potatoes on the side, and a pint of Guinness. Same thing I ordered every week.

“How rough could it be?” I asked. “Easy government job with the DOE. You probably get, like, twenty days off a year? All you have to do is show up and you get paid, right?”

Again, no laugh. Nothing.

“Oh, come on!” I said. “What’s got you so down?”

She sighed. “You know about the Petrova line?”

“Sure. Interesting mystery. I’m guessing solar radiation. Venus doesn’t have a magnetic field, but positive particles might go there because it’s electrically neutral—”

“No,” she said. “It’s something else. We don’t know what. But it’s something…else. But whatever. Let’s eat steak.”

I snorted. “Come on, Marissa, tell me. What is wrong?”

She thought about it. “Why not? You’ll hear it from the president in about twelve hours anyway.”

“The president?” I said. “Of the United States?”

She took another sip of whiskey. “Have you heard of Amaterasu? It’s a Japanese solar probe.”

“Sure,” I said. “JAXA has been getting some great data from it. It’s really cool, actually. It’s in a solar orbit, about halfway between Mercury and Venus. It has twenty different instruments that—”

“Yeah, I know. Whatever,” she said. “According to their data, the sun’s output is decreasing.”

I shrugged. “So? Where are we in the solar cycle?”

She shook her head. “It’s not the eleven-year cycle. It’s something else. JAXA considered the cycle. There’s still a downward trend. They say the sun is 0.01 percent less bright than it should be.”

“Okay, interesting. But hardly worth three whiskeys before dinner.”

She pursed her lips. “That’s what I thought. But they’re saying that value is increasing. And the speed of the increase is increasing. It’s some sort of exponential loss that they caught very, very early thanks to their probe’s super sensitive instruments.”

I leaned back in the booth. “I don’t know, Marissa. Finding an exponential progression that early seems really unlikely. But okay, let’s say the JAXA scientists are right. Where’s the energy going?”

“The Petrova line.”

“Huh?”

“JAXA studied the Petrova line carefully and says it’s getting brighter as the sun dims. Somehow, the Petrova line is stealing energy from the sun.”

She took some papers from her purse and put them on the table. They were full of graphs and charts. She sorted through them, found the one she wanted, and slid it to me.

The x-axis said “time,” and the y-axis said “luminosity loss.” The line was definitely exponential.

“This can’t be right,” I said.

“It is,” she said. “The sun’s output will drop one percent in nine years. In twenty years, it’ll be five percent. This is bad. Really bad.”

I stared at the graph. “That means an ice age. Like…soon. An instant ice age.”

“Yeah, at least. And crop failures, mass starvation…I don’t know what else.”

I shook my head. “How can the sun change so fast? It’s a star. Stars don’t change this fast. Changes take millions of years, not dozens. You know that.”

“No, I don’t know that. I used to know that. Now I only know the sun’s dying,” she said. “I don’t know why and I don’t know what we could do about it. But I know it’s dying.”

“How…” I frowned.

She finished her drink. “President speaks to the nation tomorrow morning. I think they’re working with other world leaders to all announce at the same time.”

The waiter set down my Guinness. “Here you go, sir. The steaks should be out soon.”

“I need another whiskey,” Marissa said.

“Make it two,” I added.

I blink. Another memory.

Was it true? Or is that just a random memory of me talking to someone who believed in a fake doomsday theory?

No. It’s real. I’m terrified just thinking about it. And it’s not just a sudden terror. It’s a cozy, comfortable terror that’s always with me. I’ve felt it for a long time.

This is real. The sun is dying. And I’m caught up in it. Not just as someone who lives on Earth and will die with everyone else—I’m actively involved. I feel responsible.

I still don’t remember my own name, but I remember random bits of information about the Petrova problem. They call it the Petrova problem. I just remembered that.

My subconscious has priorities. And it’s desperately telling me about this. I think my job is to solve the Petrova problem.

…in a small lab, wearing a bedsheet toga, with no idea who I am, and no help other than a mindless computer and two mummified roommates.

My vision blurs. I wipe my eyes. Tears. I can’t…I can’t remember their names. But…they were my friends. My comrades.

Only now do I realize I’ve been facing away from them the whole time. I’ve done everything I can to keep them out of sight. Scrawling on the wall like a madman with the corpses of people I cared about right behind me.

But now the distraction is over. I turn to look at them.

I sob. It comes without warning. I remember bits and pieces all at once. She was funny—always ready with a joke. He was professional and never lost his nerve. I think he was military and he was definitely our leader.

I fall to the floor and put my head in my hands. I can’t hold anything back. I cry like a child. We were a lot more than friends. And “team” isn’t the right word either. It’s stronger than that. It’s…

It’s on the tip of my tongue…

Finally, the word pops into my head. It had to wait until I wasn’t paying attention to sneak in.

Crew. We were a crew. And I’m all that’s left.

This is a spacecraft. I know that now. I don’t know how it has gravity but it’s a spaceship.

Things start to make sense. We weren’t sick. We were in suspended animation.

But these beds aren’t magical “freeze chambers” like in the movies. There’s no special technology here. I think we were in medically induced comas. Feeding tubes, IVs, constant medical care. Everything a body needs. Those arms probably changed sheets, kept us rotated to prevent bedsores, and did everything ICU nurses normally do.

And we were kept fit. Electrodes all over our bodies to stimulate muscle movement. Lots of exercise.

But in the end, comas are dangerous. Very dangerous. Only I survived, and my brain is a mess.

I walk over to the woman. I actually feel better, looking at her. Maybe it’s a sense of closure, or maybe it’s just the calmness that comes after a crying fit.

The mummy has no tubes attached. No monitoring equipment at all. There’s a small hole in her leathery wrist. That’s where the IV was when she died, I guess. So the hole never healed.

The computer must have deleted everything when she died. Waste not, want not, I guess. No use wasting resources on dead people. More for the living.

More for me, in other words.

I take a deep breath and let it out. I have to be calm. I have to think clearly. I remembered a lot just then—my crew, some of what they were like, that I’m on a spaceship (I’ll freak out about that later). The point is I’m getting more memories back, and they’re coming sort of when I want them instead of randomly. I want to focus on that, but the sadness is just so strong.

“Eat,” says the computer.

A panel in the center of the ceiling opens up, and a food tube drops out. One of the robot arms catches it and places it on my bed. The label reads DAY 1—MEAL 2.

I’m not in the mood to eat, but my stomach growls as soon as I see the tube. Whatever I’m feeling, my body has needs.

I open the tube and squirt goop into my mouth.

I have to admit: It’s another amazing flavor. I think it’s chicken with a little vegetable. There’s no texture, of course—it’s basically baby food. And it’s a little thicker than my earlier meal. It’s all about getting my stomach used to solid food again.

“Water?” I say between mouthfuls.

The ceiling panel opens again, this time with a metal cylinder. An arm brings it to me. Text on the shiny container reads DRINKING WATER. I unscrew the top and, sure enough, there’s water in there.

I take a sip. It’s room temperature and tastes flat. It’s probably distilled and without minerals. But water’s water.

I finish the rest of my meal. I haven’t had to use a bathroom yet but I will eventually. I’d rather not pee on the floor.

“Toilet?” I say.

A wall panel spins around to show a metal toilet. It’s right there in the wall, like in a prison cell. I take a closer look. It has buttons and stuff on it. I think there’s a vacuum pipe in the bowl. And there’s no water. I think this might be a zero-g toilet changed to work in gravity. Why do that?

“Okay, uh…hide toilet.”

The wall swivels around again. The toilet is gone.

All right. I’m well fed. I’m feeling a little better about things. Food will do that.

I need to focus on good things. I’m alive. Whatever killed my friends, it didn’t kill me. I’m on a spaceship—I don’t know the details, but I know I’m on a ship and it seems to be working right.

And my mental state is getting better. I’m sure of it.

I sit cross-legged on the floor. It’s time to take action. I close my eyes and let my mind wander. I want to remember something—anything—on purpose. I don’t care what. But I want to start it. Let’s see what I get.

I start with what makes me happy. I like science. I know it well. I get excited by the little experiments I’ve been doing. And I’m in space. So maybe I can think about space and science and see what ideas come to me….

I took the very hot spaghetti TV dinner out of the microwave and hurried to my couch. I peeled off the plastic top to let the steam out.

I turned the TV sound on and listened to the live broadcast. Several coworkers and a few friends had asked me to watch this with them, but I didn’t want to spend the whole evening answering questions. I just wanted to watch quietly.

It was the most watched event ever. More than the moon landing. More than any World Cup Final. Every TV channel, streaming service, news website, and local station was showing the same thing: NASA’s live broadcast.

A reporter stood with an older man in the viewing area of a flight-control room. Behind them, men and women in blue shirts focused on their computer screens.

“This is Sandra Elias,” said the reporter. “I’m here at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena, California. I’m here with Dr. Browne, who is the head of Planetary Sciences for NASA.”

She turned to the scientist. “Doctor, what’s happening now?”

Browne cleared his throat. “We got confirmation about ninety minutes ago that ArcLight successfully entered orbit around Venus. Now we’re just waiting for the first set of data.”

It had been a crazy year since JAXA's announcement about the Petrova problem. But study after study backed up what they found. Time was running out, and the world needed to know what was happening. That's how Project ArcLight started.

The situation was scary, but the project itself was amazing. The science geek in me couldn’t help but be excited.

ArcLight was the most expensive unmanned spacecraft ever made. The world needed answers and couldn’t wait around. Usually, if you asked a space agency to send a probe to Venus in less than a year, they’d laugh at you. But it’s amazing what you can do when money is no object. The United States, European Union, Russia, China, India, and Japan all helped pay for it.

“Tell us about going to Venus,” the reporter said. “What makes it so difficult?”

“The main problem is fuel,” said Browne. “There are specific times when traveling between planets uses the least amount of fuel, but we were nowhere near a good time to go from Earth to Venus. So, we had to put a lot more fuel in orbit just to get ArcLight there in the first place.”

“So it’s just bad timing, then?” the reporter asked.

“I don’t think there’s ever a good time for the sun to get dimmer.”

“Good point. Please continue.”

“Venus moves very fast compared to Earth, which means it takes more fuel just to catch up. Even when things are ideal, it actually takes more fuel to get to Venus than it does to get to Mars.”

“Amazing. Amazing. Now, Doctor, some people have asked, ‘Why bother with the planet? The Petrova line is huge, going all the way from the sun to Venus. Why not somewhere in between?’ ”

“Because the Petrova line is thickest there—as thick as the whole planet. And we can use the planet’s gravity to help us. ArcLight will orbit Venus twelve times while gathering samples of whatever the Petrova line is made of.”

“And what do you think that material is?”

“We have no idea,” said Browne. “None at all. But we might know soon. After ArcLight finishes this first orbit, it should have enough material for its lab.”

“And what can we expect to learn tonight?”

“Not much. The lab is pretty simple. Just a strong microscope and an x-ray tool. The main goal here is to bring samples back. It’ll be another three months for ArcLight to return with those samples. The lab is there to get some data in case something goes wrong on the way back.”

“Good planning as always, Dr. Browne.”

“It’s what we do.”

A cheer came from behind the reporter.

“I’m hearing—” She paused for the sound to quiet down. “I’m hearing that the first orbit is done and the data is coming in now….”

The main screen in the control room changed to a black-and-white picture. It was mostly gray, with black dots here and there.

“What are we looking at, Doctor?” the reporter asked.

“This is from the internal microscope,” said Browne. “It’s magnified ten thousand times. Those black dots are about ten microns across.”

“Are those dots what we’ve been looking for?” she asked.

“We can’t be sure,” said Browne. “They could just be dust. Any large gravity source like a planet will have a cloud of dust surrounding—”

“What the fuck?!” someone shouted in the background. Several flight controllers gasped.

The reporter chuckled. “Everyone’s excited here at JPL. We’re live, so we apologize for any—”

“Oh my God!” said Browne.

More images appeared on the main screen. One after another. All almost the same.

Almost.

The reporter looked at the images on-screen. “Are those particles…moving?”

The images, playing one after another, showed the black dots changing shape and moving around in their space.

The reporter cleared her throat and said what many would call the understatement of the century: “They look a bit like microbes, don’t you think?”

“Telemetry!” Dr. Browne called out. “Is the probe shaking?”

“Already checked,” said someone. “No shaking.”

“Are they all moving in the same direction?” he asked. “Could something outside be causing it? Like magnetism? Static electricity?”

The room went silent.

“Anyone?!” said Browne.

I dropped my fork right into my spaghetti.

Is this really alien life? Am I really this lucky?! To be alive when humanity first finds life from another planet?!

Wow! I mean—the Petrova problem is still scary but…wow! Aliens! This could be aliens! I couldn’t wait to talk about this with the kids tomorrow—

“Angular anomaly,” the computer says.

“Darn it!” I say. “I was almost there! I almost remembered myself!”

“Angular anomaly,” the computer repeats.

I unfold myself and get to my feet. From what I can tell, the computer seems to understand some of what I say. Like Siri or Alexa. So I’ll talk to it like them.

“Computer, what is an angular anomaly?”

“Angular anomaly: a critical object is not at the expected angle by at least 0.01 radians.”

“What object is anomalous?”

“Angular anomaly.”

Not much help. I'm on a ship, so it must be a navigation problem. That can't be good. How would I even steer this thing? I don’t see anything that looks like spaceship controls—not that I really know what those look like. But all I’ve found so far is a “coma room” and a lab.

That other hatch in the lab—the one that goes farther up—that must be important. This is like being in a video game. Explore the area until you find a locked door, then look for the key. But instead of searching bookshelves and trash cans, I have to search my mind. Because the “key” is my own name.

The computer's not being unreasonable. If I can't remember my own name, I probably shouldn't be allowed into important areas of the ship.

I climb onto my bunk and lie on my back. I keep an eye on the robot arms above, but they don't move. I guess the computer thinks I can take care of myself for now.

I close my eyes and focus on that flash of memory. I can see bits and pieces of it in my mind. Like looking at an old, damaged photo.

I'm in my house…no…apartment. I have an apartment. It's tidy, but small. There's a picture of the San Francisco skyline on one wall. Not helpful. I already know I lived in San Francisco.

There's a Lean Cuisine microwave meal on the coffee table in front of me. Spaghetti. The heat isn't even yet, so there are pockets of nearly frozen noodles next to burning hot ones. But I'm taking bites anyway. I must be hungry.

I'm watching NASA on TV; I see all that stuff from my previous flash of memory. My first thought is…I'm thrilled! Could it be alien life? I can't wait to tell the kids!

I have kids? This is a single man's apartment with a single man eating a single man's meal. I don't see anything feminine at all. There's nothing to suggest a woman in my life. Am I divorced? Gay? Either way, there's no sign that children live here. No toys, no pictures of kids on the wall or mantel, nothing. And the place is way too clean. Kids make a mess of everything. Especially when they start chewing gum. They all go through a gum phase—at least, a lot of them do—and they leave it everywhere.

How do I know that?

I like kids. Huh. Just a feeling. But I like them. They’re cool. They’re fun to be around.

So I’m a single man in my thirties, who lives alone in a small apartment, I don’t have any kids, but I like kids a lot. I don’t like where this is headed…

A teacher! I’m a schoolteacher! I remember now!

Oh, thank God. I’m a teacher.
