Chapter 13

“Mr. Easton, I don’t think we need to be searched,” said Stratt.

“I think you do,” said the head prison guard. His thick New Zealand accent sounded friendly, but there was an edge to it. This man had built his career on not tolerating nonsense.

“We’re exempt from all—”

“Stop,” Easton said. “No one gets in or out of Pare without a full search.”

Auckland Prison, which the locals called “Pare” for some reason, was New Zealand’s only maximum-security prison. The only way in was covered with security cameras and a micro-scanner for all visitors. Even the guards went through the detector on their way in.

Easton’s assistant and I stood to the side while our bosses argued. He and I looked at each other and shrugged. A small group of underlings with stubborn bosses.

“I’m not turning over my Taser. I can call your prime minister if you like,” Stratt said.

“Sure,” said Easton. “She’ll tell you the same thing I’m about to tell you now: We don’t allow weapons anywhere near those animals in there. Even my own guards only have batons. There are some rules we don’t change. I’m fully aware of your power, but it has limits. You’re not magical.”

“Mr. E—”

“Torch!” Easton said, holding out his hand.

His assistant handed over a small flashlight. He clicked it on. “Please open your mouth wide, Ms. Stratt. I need to check for anything hidden.”

Whoa boy. I stepped forward before this got any worse. “I’ll go first!” I opened my mouth wide.

Easton shined the light into my mouth and looked this way and that. “You’re clear.”

Stratt just glared at him.

He held the flashlight at the ready. “I can get a female guard in here and order a much more detailed search if you like.”

For a few seconds, she did nothing. Then she pulled her Taser from its holster and handed it over.

She must have been tired. I’d never seen her give up on a power trip before. Though, I also hadn’t seen her get into a pointless argument before either. She had a lot of authority and wasn’t afraid to use it when needed, but she usually wasn’t one to argue when a simple solution was available.

Soon, guards escorted Stratt and me through the cold, gray walls of the prison.

“What the heck is wrong with you?” I said.

“I don’t like little dictators in their little kingdoms,” she said. “It drives me crazy.”

“You can give a little sometimes.”

“I’m out of patience, and the world is running out of time.”

I held up a finger. “No, no, no! You can’t just say ‘I’m saving the world’ every time you’re a jerk.”

She thought about it. “Yeah, okay. You might be right.”

We followed the guards down a long hallway to the Maximum Security Unit.

“Maximum security seems like too much,” she said.

“Seven people died,” I reminded her. “Because of him.”

“It was an accident.”

“It was criminal carelessness. He deserves what he got.”

The guards led us around a corner. We followed. The place was a maze.

“Why bring me here?”

“Science.”

“Of course.” I sighed. “I don’t like this.”

We entered a bare room with only a metal table. A prisoner in an orange jumpsuit sat on one side. He was balding, maybe in his late forties or early fifties. He was handcuffed to the table. He didn’t look dangerous.

Stratt and I sat across from him. The guards closed the door.

The man looked at us. He tilted his head, waiting for someone to talk.

“Dr. Robert Redell,” Stratt said.

“Call me Bob,” he said.

“I’ll call you Dr. Redell.” She took a file from her briefcase and read it. “You’re serving a life sentence for seven counts of manslaughter.”

“That’s why they say I’m here, yes,” he said.

I spoke up. “Seven people died on your rig because you were careless. Seems like a good reason for you to be here.”

He shook his head. “Seven people died because the control room didn’t follow the rules and turned on a main pumping station while workers were still in the reflector tower. It was a terrible accident, but it was an accident.”

“Explain it to us,” I said. “If the deaths at your solar farm weren’t your fault, why are you here?”

“Because the government thinks I stole millions of dollars.”

“And why do they think that?” I asked.

“Because I stole millions of dollars.” He moved his chained wrists to a better position. “But that had nothing to do with the deaths. Nothing!”

“Tell me about your blackpanel power idea,” Stratt said.

“Blackpanel?” He frowned. “It was just an idea. I emailed it without my name.”

Stratt rolled her eyes. “Do you really think email sent from a prison computer lab is anonymous?”

He looked away. “I don’t know computers. I’m an engineer.”

“I want to hear more about blackpanel,” she said. “And if I like what I hear, it could shorten your jail time. So start talking.”

He perked up. “Well…I mean…okay. What do you know about solar thermal power?”

Stratt looked at me.

“Uh,” I said. “It’s when you have a lot of mirrors that reflect sunlight to the top of a tower. If you get a few hundred square meters of mirror focusing all that sunlight onto a single point, you can heat up water, boil it, and run a turbine.”

I turned to Stratt. “But that’s not new. There’s a working solar thermal power plant in Spain right now. If you want to know about it, talk to them.”

She silenced me with a hand motion. “And that’s what you were making for New Zealand?”

“Well,” he said. “New Zealand paid for it. But the idea was to power Africa.”

“Why would New Zealand pay a lot of money to help Africa?” I asked.

“Because we’re nice,” Redell said.

“Wow,” I said. “I know New Zealand is pretty cool but—”

“And it was going to be a New Zealand company making money from the power,” Redell said.

“There it is.”

He leaned forward. “Africa needs roads, power, and things like that. To do that, they need power. And they have nine million square kilometers of land no one uses that gets sunlight all day long. The Sahara Desert is just there , ready to give them everything. All we had to do was build the power plants!”

He flopped back in his chair. “But every local government wanted money. Bribes, payoffs, you name it. You think I stole a lot? That’s nothing compared to the bribes I had to pay just to build a solar plant in the middle of nowhere.”

“And then?” Stratt said.

He looked at his shoes. “We built a small plant—one square kilometer of mirrors. All of it focused on a big metal drum full of water on top of a tower. Boil the water, run a turbine, you know how it works. I had a crew checking the drum for leaks. When anyone’s in the tower, the mirrors are supposed to be pointed away. But someone in the control room turned on the whole system when they thought they were starting a fake test.”

He sighed. “Seven people. All dead right away. At least they didn’t suffer. Much. Someone had to pay. The people who died were all New Zealanders, and so am I. So the government came after me. It was a joke of a trial.”

“And the stealing?” I said.

He nodded. “Yeah, that came up in the trial too. But I would have gotten away with it if the project had worked. I’m not to blame here. I mean, yeah, stealing money, okay, I’m guilty of that. But I didn’t kill those people. Not by being careless or anything else.”

“Where were you when the accident happened?” Stratt said.

He paused.

“Where were you?” she repeated.

“I was in Monaco. On a vacation.”

“You’d been there for three months on that vacation. Gambling away your stolen money.”

“I…have a gambling problem,” he said. “I admit that. I mean, gambling debt made me steal in the first place. It’s a sickness.”

“And what if you had been doing your job instead of going on a spree for three months? What if you’d been there the day the accident happened? Would the accident still have happened?”

His expression was answer enough.

“Okay,” Stratt said. “Now we’re past the excuses and lies. You’re not going to convince me you’re an innocent victim. And now you know that. So let’s move on: Tell me about blackpanels.”

“Yeah, okay.” He calmed down. “I’ve worked in energy my whole life, so Astrophage is really interesting to me. A storage method like that—man, if it weren’t hurting the sun, it would be the best thing to ever happen to us.”

He moved in his seat. “Nuclear reactors, coal plants, solar thermal plants…they all do the same thing: Heat water to make steam, then use the steam to power a turbine. But with Astrophage, we don’t need any of that. It turns heat directly into stored energy. And it doesn’t need much heat. Just anything above 96.415 degrees.”

“We know that,” I said. “I’ve been using heat from a nuclear reactor to grow Astrophage for months.”

“How much did you get? A few grams? My idea can get you a thousand kilograms a day. In a few years, you’ll have enough for the whole Hail Mary mission. It’ll take you longer to build the ship anyway.”

“Okay, I’m listening,” I said. Of course, Stratt hadn’t told me anything about this "blackpanel" thing.

“Get a square of metal foil. Any metal will work. Anodize it until it’s black. Don’t paint it—anodize it. Put clear glass over it with a one-centimeter gap. Seal the edges with brick, foam, or something that insulates well. Then put it in the sun.”

“Okay, what will that do?”

“The black foil will absorb sunlight and get hot. The glass will keep the heat in—heat can only escape through the glass, which is slow. It’ll get to a temperature over one hundred degrees Celsius.”

I nod. “And at that temperature, you can grow Astrophage.”

“Yes.”

“But it would be ridiculously slow,” I said. “If you had a one-square-meter box and perfect weather…say, one thousand watts of sunlight per square meter…”

“It’s about half a microgram per day,” he said. “More or less.”

“That’s nowhere near ‘a thousand kilograms’ per day.”

He smiled. “It just depends on how many square meters you make.”

“You’d need two trillion square meters to get a thousand kilograms per day.”

“The Sahara Desert is nine trillion square meters.”

My jaw dropped.

“That was quick,” said Stratt. “Explain.”

“Well,” I said. “He wants to cover part of the Sahara Desert with black panels. Like…a quarter of the whole Sahara Desert!”

“It’d be the biggest thing humans ever made,” he said. “You could easily see it from space.”

I glared at him. “And it would destroy the environment of Africa and probably Europe.”

“Not as much as the coming ice age will.”

Stratt held up her hand. “Dr. Grace. Would it work?”

I fidgeted. “Well, I mean…it seems like a good idea. But I don’t know if we can actually do it. This isn’t like building a building or a road. We’re talking about literally trillions of these things.”

Redell leaned in. “That’s why I designed the blackpanels to be made entirely out of foil, glass, and ceramics. All materials we have plenty of here on Earth.”

“Wait,” I said. “How do the Astrophage reproduce in this situation? Your blackpanels will help them, sure, and they’ll be ready to reproduce. But there are a lot of steps they need to take when they reproduce.”

“Oh, I know.” He smirked. “We’ll have a static magnet in there to give them a magnetic field to follow—they need that to start their migration. Then we’ll have a small IR filter on one part of the glass. It’ll only let the CO2 IR light through. The Astrophage will go there to reproduce. Then, after dividing, they’ll head toward the glass because that’s where the sun is. We’ll have a small pinhole somewhere in the side of the panel for air to get in and out. It’ll be slow enough that it doesn’t cool down the panel, but fast enough to replace the CO2 used by Astrophage while reproducing.”

I opened my mouth to protest, but I couldn’t find anything wrong with it. He’d thought it all through.

“Well?” said Stratt.

“As a reproduction system it’s horrible,” I said. “Way less efficient and far lower yield than my system on the carrier’s reactor. But he didn’t design it to be efficient. He designed it to be easily expanded.”

“That’s right,” he said. He pointed to Stratt. “I hear you have total power over almost the whole world right now.”

“That’s an overstatement,” she said.

“Not by much, though,” I said.

Redell went on. “Can you get China to focus their factories on making blackpanels? Not just them but nearly every industrial country on Earth? That’s what it would take.”

She pressed her lips together. After a moment, she said, “Yes.”

“And can you tell the damn crooked government leaders in North Africa to stay out of the way?”

“That part will be simple,” she said. “When this is all done, those governments will keep the blackpanels. They’ll be the industrial-energy superpower of the world.”

“See, there we go,” he said. “Save the world and permanently raise Africa out of poverty while we’re at it. Of course, this is all just a theory. I have to develop the blackpanel and make sure we can make a lot of them. I’d need to be in a lab instead of prison.”

Stratt thought it over. Then she stood.

“Okay. You’re with us.”

He pumped his fist.

I wake up in my bed, which is stuck to the tunnel wall. The first night was a mess with duct tape. After that, I learned that epoxy glue works well on xenonite, so I could add a couple of anchor points and put the mattress up right.

I sleep in the tunnel every night now. Rocky wants me to. And, about every eighty-six hours, Rocky sleeps in the tunnel and wants me to watch. Well, he’s only slept three times so far, so I don't know much about when he's awake. But he’s been pretty regular about it.

I stretch my arms and yawn.

“Good Morning,” Rocky says.

It’s completely dark. I turn on the lamp next to the bed.

Rocky has a whole workshop on his side of the tunnel. He’s always changing or fixing something. It seems like his ship always needs work. Right now, he's holding a long metal thing with two of his hands and using two more to poke at the insides with thin tools. His last hand holds a handle on the wall.

“Mornin’,” I say. “I’m going to eat. I’ll be back.”

Rocky waves without really paying attention. “Eat.”

I float down to the dorm for my morning routine. I eat a prepackaged breakfast (scrambled eggs with pork sausage) and drink a bag of hot coffee.

It’s been a few days since I last cleaned up, and I can smell myself. Not good. So I wash myself at the sponge-bath station and get clean coveralls. With all this technology, I haven’t seen a way to clean clothes. So I soak them in water and put them in the lab freezer for a while. It kills the germs, and those cause the smell. Fresh, but not really clean, clothes.

I put on the coveralls. I’ve decided today is the day. After a week of improving our language skills, Rocky and I are ready to have real conversations. I can even understand him without looking at the translation about a third of the time now.

I float back to the tunnel, finishing my coffee.

Okay. Finally I think we have the right words for this. Here goes.

I clear my throat. “Rocky. I am here because Astrophage makes Sol sick but doesn’t make Tau Ceti sick. Are you here for the same reason?”

Rocky puts the device and his tools on his chest strap and climbs along the support rails to the divider. Good. He understands this is serious.

“Yes. No understand why Tau not sick but Eridani is sick. If Astrophage no leave Eridani, my people die.”

“Same!” I say. “Same same same! If Astrophage keeps infecting Sol, all the humans will die.”

“Good. Same. You and I will save Eridani and Sol.”

“Yes yes yes!”

“Why did other humans on your ship die, question?” Rocky asks.

Oh. So we’re going to talk about that?

I rub the back of my head. “We, uh…we slept all the way here. Not a normal sleep. A special sleep. A dangerous sleep, but we had to. My crewmates died, but I didn’t. Just luck.”

“Bad,” he says.

“Bad. Why did the other Eridians die?”

“I don’t know. Everyone got sick. Then everyone died.” His voice shakes. “I’m not sick. I don’t know why.”

“Bad,” I say with a sigh. “What kind of sick?”

He thinks for a moment. “I need a word. Small life. One thing. Like Astrophage. Eridian body made of many many of these.”

“Cell,” I say. “My body has many cells too.”

He says the Eridian word for “cell,” and I add the sounds to my growing dictionary.

“Cell,” he says. “My crew have trouble with cells. Many cells die. Not infection. Not hurt. No reason. But not me. Never me. Why, I wonder? I don't know.”

Each cell in the sick Eridians died? That sounds awful. It also sounds like radiation sickness. How can I explain that? I shouldn’t have to. If they travel in space, they should know about radiation. We don’t have a word for it yet. Let’s fix that.

“I need a word: hydrogen atoms moving very fast. Very, very fast.”

“Hot gas.”

“No. Faster than that. Very, very, very fast.”

He wiggles his shell. He’s confused.

I try something else. “Space has very, very, very fast hydrogen atoms. They move almost as fast as light. Stars made them a long, long, long time ago.”

“No. Space has no mass. Space is empty.”

Oh boy. “No, that’s wrong. There are hydrogen atoms in space. Very, very fast hydrogen atoms.”

“Understood.”

“You didn’t know that?”

“No.”

I stare in shock.

How can a civilization develop space travel without ever discovering radiation?

“Dr. Grace,” she said.

“Dr. Lokken,” I said.

We sat across from each other at a small steel table. It was a tiny room, but big for an aircraft carrier. I wasn’t sure what it was for, and its name was in Chinese. But I thought it was where the navigator looked at maps…?

“Thank you for seeing me,” she said.

“No problem.”

We usually avoided each other. We went from “annoyed” to “very annoyed.” I was as much to blame as she was. We started badly in Geneva and never got better.

“I don’t think this is needed.”

“Me neither,” I said. “But Stratt said you had to show me this. So, here we are.”

“I have an idea. I want your opinion.” She took out a file and gave it to me. “CERN will release this paper next week. This is a draft. But I know people there, so they let me see it early.”

I opened the folder. “Okay, what’s it about?”

“They learned how Astrophage stores energy.”

“Really?!” I gasped. Then I cleared my throat. “Really?”

“Yes, and frankly it’s amazing.” She pointed to a graph on the first page. “Basically, it’s neutrinos.”

“Neutrinos?” I shook my head. “How the heck…”

“I know. It doesn’t make a lot of sense. But there’s a big burst of neutrinos every time they kill an Astrophage. They even took samples to the IceCube Neutrino Observatory and poked them in the main detector pool. They got a ton of hits. Astrophage can only hold neutrinos if it’s alive, and there are a lot inside.”

“How does it make neutrinos?”

She flipped a few pages in the paper and pointed to another chart. “This is more your thing than mine, but microbiologists have confirmed Astrophage has a lot of free hydrogen ions—just raw protons with no electron—zooming around just inside the cell membrane.”

“Yeah, I remember reading about that. It was a group in Russia that figured that out.”

She nodded. “CERN is pretty sure that, somehow, when those protons crash into each other really fast, their energy turns into two neutrinos that go in opposite directions.”

I leaned back, confused. “That is really strange. Mass doesn't usually just 'happen' like that.”

She wiggled her hand. “Not exactly. Sometimes gamma rays, when they get close to an atom's nucleus, will suddenly become an electron and a positron. It’s called ‘pair production.’ So it happens. But we’ve never seen neutrinos made that way.”

“That’s kind of cool. I never got too into atomic physics. I’ve never heard of pair production before.”

“It’s a thing.”

“Okay.”

“Anyway,” she said, “there’s a lot of complex stuff about neutrinos I won’t get into—there are different kinds and they can even change from one kind to another. But the main point is this: They’re an extremely tiny particle. Their mass is like one twenty-billionth the mass of a proton.”

“Waaaaaait,” I said. “We know Astrophage is always 96.415 degrees Celsius. Temperature is just how fast the particles inside are moving. So we should be able to calcu—”

“Calculate how fast the particles inside are moving,” she said. “Yes. We know how fast the protons move on average. And we know their mass, which means we know their energy. I know what you’re thinking and the answer is yes. It matches up.”

“Wow!” I put my hand on my forehead. “That’s amazing!”

“Yes. It is.”

That was the answer to the question we'd been asking for so long: Why is Astrophage’s critical temperature what it is? Why not hotter? Why not colder?

Astrophage makes neutrinos in pairs by smashing protons together. For the reaction to work, the protons need to hit each other with more energy than the energy of two neutrinos. If you start with the mass of a neutrino and work backward, you know how fast those protons have to hit each other. And when you know how fast the particles in an object are moving, you know its temperature. To have enough energy to make neutrinos, the protons have to be 96.415 degrees Celsius.

“Oh man,” I said. “So any heat energy above the critical temperature will just make the protons hit harder.”

“Yes. They’ll make neutrinos and have extra energy. Then they bump into other protons, and so on. Any heat energy above the critical temperature quickly turns into neutrinos. But if it drops below critical temperature, the protons are too slow, and neutrino production stops. The result: You can’t get it hotter than 96.415 degrees. Not for long, anyway. And if it gets too cold, the Astrophage uses stored energy to heat back up to that temperature—just like any other warm-blooded animal.”

She gave me a moment to let that all sink in. CERN really delivered. But a couple of things still bothered me.

“Okay, so it makes neutrinos,” I said. “How does it turn them back into energy?”

“That’s the easy part,” she said. “Neutrinos are what we call Majorana particles. This means a neutrino is its own antiparticle. Basically, every time two neutrinos collide, it’s a matter-antimatter interaction. They cancel each other out and become photons. Two photons, actually, with the same wavelength and going opposite directions. And since a photon’s wavelength is based on its energy…”

“The Petrova wavelength!” I exclaimed.

She nodded. “Yes. A neutrino’s mass energy is exactly the same as the energy in one photon of Petrova-wavelength light. This paper is truly groundbreaking.”

I rested my chin on my hands. “Wow…just wow. I guess the only question left is how does an Astrophage keep neutrinos inside?”

“We don’t know. Neutrinos routinely pass through the entire planet Earth without hitting a single atom—they’re just that small. Well, it’s more about quantum wavelengths and the chances of collision. But basically, neutrinos are famously hard to interact with. But for some reason, Astrophage has what we call ‘super cross-sectionality.’ That’s just a fancy term meaning nothing can quantum-tunnel through it. It goes against every law of particle physics we thought we knew, but it’s been proven again and again.”

“Yeah.” I tapped my finger on the table. “It absorbs all wavelengths of light—even wavelengths that should be too large to interact with it.”

“Yes,” she said. “It also collides with all matter that tries to get by, no matter how unlikely that collision should be. Anyway, as long as an Astrophage is alive, it shows this super cross-sectionality. And that leads nicely to what I wanted to talk to you about.”

“Oh?” I said. “There’s more?”

“Yes.” She took a drawing of the Hail Mary hull from her bag. “This is what I need you for: I’m trying to protect the Hail Mary from radiation.”

I perked up. “Of course! Astrophage will block it all!”

“Maybe,” she said. “But I need to know how space radiation works to be sure. I know the basics, but not the specifics. Please explain it to me.”

I folded my arms. “Well, there’s two types, really. High-energy particles from the sun, and GCRs that are just always around.”

“Start with the solar particles,” she said.

“Sure. Solar particles are just hydrogen atoms from the sun. Sometimes a magnetic storm on the sun can make it shoot out a lot of them. Other times it’s calmer. And lately, the Astrophage infection has been taking so much energy from the sun that magnetic storms are less common.”

“Horrifying,” she said.

“I know. Did you hear that global warming has been almost reversed?”

She nodded. “Humanity’s carelessness with our environment accidentally gave us an extra month by pre-heating the planet.”

“We fell in poop and came out smelling like roses,” I said.

She laughed. “I haven’t heard that one. We don’t have that saying in Norwegian.”

“You do now.” I smiled.

She looked down at the ship’s design—a little faster than I thought was needed, but oh well.

“How fast do these solar particles travel?” she asked.

“About four hundred kilometers per second.”

“Good. We can ignore them.” She scribbled a note on the paper. “The Hail Mary will be moving away faster than that within eight hours. They won’t be able to catch up, much less do any damage.”

I whistled. “It’s really amazing what we’re doing. I mean…jeez. Astrophage would be the best thing ever if it weren’t, you know, destroying the sun.”

“I know,” she said. “Now, tell me about GCRs.”

“Those are harder,” I said. “It means—”

“Galactic cosmic rays,” she said. “But they're not really cosmic rays, are they?”

“Right. They’re just hydrogen ions—protons. But they’re going much faster. Almost the speed of light.”

“Why call them cosmic rays if they aren't electromagnetic at all?”

“People used to think they were. The name stayed.”

“Do they come from one place?”

“No, they come from all directions. Supernovas make them, and they've happened everywhere. We're constantly surrounded by GCRs from all directions. They're a big problem for space travel. But not anymore!”

I leaned in to see her drawing again. It showed a cut-away of a ship's side. There was a tiny gap between two layers. “Will you fill that space with Astrophage?”

“That’s the idea.”

I studied the drawing. “You want fuel in the hull? Isn’t that risky?”

“Only if it sees CO2 light. Without CO2, it’s harmless. And it’ll be dark between the walls. Dimitri wants to mix Astrophage with thin oil to move it to the engines easily. I want to cover the hull with that mix.”

I pinched my chin. “It could work. But Astrophage can be killed by physical damage. You can kill one by poking it with a sharp nanostick.”

“Yes, that’s why I asked CERN to do some secret experiments for me as a favor.”

“Wow. CERN will just do whatever you want? Are you, like, a tiny Stratt or something?”

She chuckled. “Old friends and contacts. Anyway, they found that even particles moving close to light speed can’t get past Astrophage. And none of them seem to kill it either.”

“That actually makes a lot of sense,” I said. “It evolved to live on the surface of stars. They must get hit by energy and very fast-moving particles all the time.”

She pointed to a close-up picture of Astrophage canals. “The entire radiation load will be stopped. All we need is a layer of Astrophage liquid thick enough to make sure there’s always an Astrophage cell in the way of any incoming particles. One millimeter should be more than enough. Plus, we don’t waste any mass. We’ll be using the fuel itself as insulation. And if the crew needs that last little bit of Astrophage, well, consider it a backup.”

“Hmm…a ‘backup’ that could power New York City for twenty thousand years.”

She looked at the diagram, then back to me. “You did all that math in your head?”

“Eh, I had some shortcuts. We’re dealing with such huge amounts of energy here, I tend to think in ‘New York City years’ of energy, which is about one-half of one gram of Astrophage.”

She rubbed her temples. “And we need to make two million kilograms of it. If we make a mistake along the way…”

“We’ll save Astrophage the trouble of destroying humanity by destroying it ourselves,” I say. “Yeah. I think about that a lot.”

“So, what do you think?” she said. “Is this a terrible idea, or could it work?”

“I think it’s brilliant.”

She smiled and looked away.
