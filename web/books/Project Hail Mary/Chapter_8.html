Project Hail Mary

Chapter 8

By the time we got to Geneva, I had no idea what day it was.

The computer predictions for the Astrophage breeder didn’t match what was actually happening. But I had managed to grow almost six grams of Astrophage. The aircraft carrier’s reactor couldn’t make enough heat to speed up the reaction any more. Stratt kept hinting they’d get a heat source that could keep up, but nothing had happened yet.

I typed on my computer even as the fancy private jet stopped at the gate. Stratt had to tell me to stop working.

Three hours later, we waited in a conference room.

Always a conference room. My life was just going from one conference room to another. This one was nicer than most, at least. With nice wood walls and a cool mahogany table. It was really something.

Stratt and I didn’t talk. I worked on heat stuff while she typed on her laptop doing who-knows-what. We were around each other enough.

Finally, a serious-looking woman came in and sat across from Stratt.

“Thank you for meeting me, Ms. Stratt,” she said with a Norwegian accent.

“No need to thank me, Dr. Lokken,” she said. “I don’t want to be here.”

I looked up from my laptop. “You are? I thought you scheduled this.”

She didn’t take her eyes off the Norwegian. “I scheduled it because I had six different world leaders on the phone at the same time bothering me to do it. I finally gave in.”

“And you are…?” Lokken asked me.

“Ryland Grace.”

She actually backed away. “ The Ryland Grace? Author of ‘An Analysis of Water-Based Assumptions and Recalibration of Expectations for Evolutionary Models’?”

“Yeah, got a problem with that?” I said.

Stratt half smiled at me. “You’re famous.”

“Infamous,” said Lokken. “His silly paper was an insult to the entire scientific community. This man works for you? Ridiculous. All his ideas about alien life were proven wrong.”

I scowled. “Hey. My point is life doesn’t have to have water to evolve. Just because we found some life that does use water, that doesn’t mean I’m wrong.”

“Of course it does. Two life-forms separately evolved to need water—”

“Independently?!” I scoffed. “Are you crazy? Do you really think something as complex as mitochondria would evolve the same way twice? This is clearly a panspermia event.”

She dismissed my statement like a fly. “Astrophage mitochondria is very different from Earth mitochondria. They clearly evolved on their own.”

“They’re ninety-eight percent the same!”

“Ahem,” said Stratt. “I don’t really understand what you’re arguing about, but can we—”

I pointed at Lokken. “This idiot thinks Astrophage evolved on its own, but it’s obvious Astrophage and Earth life are related!”

“That’s interesting, but—”

Lokken slammed the table. “How could a common ancestor have crossed interstellar space?”

“The same way Astrophage does it!”

She leaned toward me. “Then why haven’t we seen interstellar life all this time?”

I leaned toward her. “No idea. Maybe it was a one-time thing.”

“How do you explain the differences in mitochondria?”

“Four billion years of evolution going in different directions.”

“Stop,” Stratt said calmly. “I don’t know what this is…some kind of science argument? That’s not what we’re here for. Dr. Grace, Dr. Lokken, please sit down.”

I plopped into my seat and folded my arms. Lokken sat as well.

Stratt fiddled with a pen. “Dr. Lokken, you’ve been bothering governments to bother me. Over and over. Day in and day out. I know you want to be involved in Project Hail Mary, but I won’t make it a huge international mess. We don’t have time for the politics and power plays that always happen on big projects.”

“I’m not happy to be here either,” Lokken said. “I’m here, even though it’s a big problem for both of us, because this was the only way to tell you about a critical design flaw in the Hail Mary.”

Stratt sighed. “We sent out those early designs for general feedback. Not required meetings in Geneva.”

“Then consider this ‘general feedback.’ ”

“Could have been an email.”

“You would have deleted it. You have to listen to me, Stratt. This is important.”

Stratt twirled the pen around a few more times. “Well, I’m here. Go ahead.”

Lokken cleared her throat. “Tell me if I’m wrong, but the whole point of the Hail Mary is to be a lab. One we can send to Tau Ceti to find out why that star—and only that star—is safe from Astrophage.”

“That’s right.”

She nodded. “Then do you also agree that the lab on the ship is the most important thing?”

“Yes,” Stratt said. “Without it, the mission means nothing.”

“Then we have a big problem.” Lokken pulled some papers from her purse. “I have a list of the lab equipment you want on board. Spectrometers, DNA sequencers, microscopes, chemistry lab glassware—”

“I know the list,” Stratt said. “I approved it.”

Lokken dropped the papers on the table. “Most of this stuff won’t work in zero g.”

Stratt rolled her eyes. “We’ve already thought of that, of course. Companies everywhere are working on zero-g versions of this equipment right now.”

Lokken shook her head. “Do you know how much research went into making electron microscopes? Gas chromatographs? Everything else on this list? A century of science built on many failures. You just assume that making these things work in zero-g will happen right away?”

“I don’t see any way around it, unless you invented artificial gravity.”

“We have invented artificial gravity,” Lokken insisted. “A long time ago.”

Stratt looked at me. Clearly, that surprised her.

“I think she means a centrifuge,” I said.

“I know she means a centrifuge,” Stratt said. “What do you think?”

“I hadn’t thought about it before. I guess…it could work….”

Stratt shook her head. “No. That won’t work. We need to keep things simple. As simple as we can. Big, strong ship, few moving parts. The more complicated we make it, the more things can go wrong.”

“It’s worth the risk,” said Lokken.

“We’d have to add a giant weight to the Hail Mary just to make it work.” Stratt frowned. “I’m sorry, but we barely have enough energy to make the Astrophage for the current weight limit. We can’t just double it.”

“Wait. We have enough energy to make all the fuel? When did that happen—?” I said.

“You don’t need to add weight,” Lokken said. She pulled another paper from her purse and slapped it down on the table. “If you take the current design, cut it in half between the crew area and the fuel tanks, the two sides will have a good weight balance for a centrifuge.”

Stratt peered at the diagram. “You put all the fuel on the same side. That’s two million kilograms.”

“No.” I shook my head. “The fuel would be used up.”

They both looked at me.

“It’s a one-way trip,” I said. “The fuel will be gone when they get to Tau Ceti. Lokken picked a cut point where the back of the ship will weigh three times as much as the front. It’s a good weight balance for a centrifuge. It could work.”

“Thank you,” said Lokken.

“How do you cut a ship in half?” asked Stratt. “How does it become a centrifuge?”

Lokken flipped the diagram over to reveal a detailed image showing a connection between the two ship halves. “Spools of Zylon cable between the crew area and the rest of the ship. We could simulate one g of gravity with a hundred meters of separation.”

Stratt pinched her chin. Had someone actually changed her mind on something?

“I don’t like complicated things…” she said. “I don’t like risk.”

“This makes things simpler and safer,” Lokken said. “The ship, the crew, the Astrophage…it’s all just there to support the lab equipment. You need equipment you can count on. Stuff that’s been used for years with tons of use in the real world. Every possible problem has already been fixed. If you have one g of gravity—to make sure they’re in the environment they were made for—you get all that reliability.”

“Hmm,” said Stratt. “Grace? What do you think?”

“I…I think it’s a good idea.”

“Really?”

“Yeah,” I said. “I mean, we already have to build the ship to handle four years of constant speeding up at one and a half g’s or so. It’s going to be pretty strong.”

She looked at Lokken’s drawing for a while. “Wouldn’t this make the artificial gravity in the crew area backward?”

And she was right. The Hail Mary was designed so that “down” was “toward the engines.” As the ship speeds up, the crew is pushed “down” to the floor. But inside a centrifuge, “down” is always “away from the center of the spin.” So the crew would all be pushed toward the front of the ship.

“Yes, that would be a problem.” Lokken pointed to the diagram. The cables didn’t connect right to the crew area. They connected to two big discs on each side. “The cables connect to these big hinges. The whole front half of the ship can spin 180 degrees. So when they’re using the centrifuge, the front will face in toward the other half of the ship. Inside the crew area, the force of gravity will be away from the front—same as when the engines are firing.”

Stratt thought about it. “This is a pretty complex machine and you’ll be splitting the ship in two. You really think this is less risky?”

“Less risky than using brand-new equipment that hasn’t been tested enough. Trust me, I’ve used sensitive equipment most of my career,” I said. “It’s picky and fragile even when things are perfect.”

Stratt picked up her pen and tapped it on the table several times. “Okay. We’ll do it.”

Lokken smiled. “Excellent. I’ll write up a paper and send it along to the UN. We can form a committee—”

“No, I said we’ll do it.” Stratt stood up. “You’re with us now, Dr. Lokken. Pack a bag and meet us at Genève Aéroport. Terminal 3, private plane called Stratt.”

“What? I work for ESA. I can’t just—”

“Yeah, don’t worry,” I said. “She’s going to call your boss or your boss’s boss or whatever and have you assigned to her. You just got drafted.”

“I…I didn’t mean to design it myself,” Lokken argued. “I only meant to point out—”

“I never said you volunteered,” Stratt said. “It’s not voluntary at all.”

“You can’t just force me to work for you.”

But Stratt was already walking out of the room. “Meet us at the airport in one hour or I’ll have the Swiss Gendarmerie drag you there in two hours. Your call.”

Lokken stared at the door, stunned, then back to me.

“You get used to it,” I said.

—

The ship is a centrifuge! I remember now!

That’s why there’s a strange area called “Cable Faring.” That’s where the spools and Zylon cables are. The ship can break in half, turn the crew area around, and spin.

That turning-around part—that’s the odd ring I saw on the hull during my spacewalk! I remember the design now. It has two big hinges, letting the crew area turn around before the centrifuge starts.

It’s a lot like Apollo spacecraft. The lunar lander was attached below the command module at launch, but they’d separate, turn the command module around, and reconnect with the lander on their way to the moon. It’s one of those things that seems silly but is the best way to fix a problem.

I float back to the cockpit and check screens on different consoles. When each one isn’t what I want, I go to the next. Finally, I find it. The “Centrifuge” screen. It was hidden as a subpanel in the Life Support screen.

It looks easy enough. There are yaw, pitch, and roll readouts, showing the ship’s current state, like the Navigation panel. Another readout is labeled “Crew Compartment Angle”—that must be the turning-around part. Each one reads “0° per second.”

Below those is a button labeled “Engage Centrifuge Sequence.” Underneath that are numbers for rotational acceleration rate, final speed, spooling rate, estimated g-force at the lab floor, four screens for spool status (I guess there are four spools, two on each side), which emergency plans to use if there’s a problem, and more stuff I won’t pretend to understand. The important thing is all those readouts already have values.

Gotta love computers. They do all the thinking so you don’t have to.

I check out the emergency mode. It just says “Spin Down.” I tap it, and a menu pops up. The choices are: “Spin Down,” “Halt All Spools,” and one in red called “Separate.” I don’t want to do that one. I think “Spin Down” will slowly slow the ship’s spin if something’s wrong. Sounds good, so I’ll leave it on that.

I’m about to turn on the centrifuge, but I stop. Is everything secure? Is it okay to suddenly have a lot of force on the ship? I ignore the thought. The ship sped up constantly for years. It should be fine with a little centrifuge, right?

Right?

Like many astronauts before me, I trust the engineers who built this. Dr. Lokken, I guess. Hope she knew what she was doing.

I hit the button.

At first, nothing. I wonder if I even hit it right, or if I just missed the screen like I do on my phone sometimes.

Then, the alert goes off all over the ship. The loud triple beep repeats every few seconds. No one on the crew could miss that. A last warning, I guess, in case they weren't paying attention.

Above me, the Petrovascope screen locks. That confirms the ship's engines use Astrophage. It makes sense, but I wasn’t sure until now.

The beeping stops, and nothing happens. Then I see I’m closer to the Nav panel than before. I floated to the edge of the room. I put my arm out to stop myself and get back to where I was. Then I float toward the Nav panel again.

“Ohhh,” I say.

It’s begun. I’m not moving toward the Nav panel. The whole cockpit is moving toward me. The ship is starting to spin.

Everything shifts and changes direction. That's because as the ship spins, the crew area is also turning. This could get tricky.

“Uh…right!” I push off the wall and into the pilot seat.

I tilt. Or, rather, the room tilts. No, that doesn’t make sense. Nothing tilts. The ship is spinning faster and faster. It’s also speeding up the acceleration. Also, the front half of the ship has separated from the back, and it’s turning around those two big hinges. When it’s done, the nose will be pointing in toward the back half of the ship. All of this is happening at the same time, so the forces I’m feeling are really strange. Very complicated stuff, but also not my problem. It’s up to the computer to handle.

I watch the Centrifuge panel. The pitch rate reads 0.17° per second. Another readout labeled “Component Separation” reads 2.4 meters. There’s a little beep and the “Crew Compartment Angle” readout blinks. It shows 180°. I assume this whole process was planned out to reduce shock to the system and/or crew.

I feel a slight pressure on my butt as the seat pushes against me. The change is very smooth. I just…feel more and more gravity in what feels like a tilting room. It’s a strange feeling.

I know, logically, that I’m in a ship spinning around. But there are no windows to see out of. Only screens. I check the telescope screen that’s still pointed at the Blip-A. The stars in the background don’t move. It’s adjusting for my rotation somehow and canceling it out. That bit of software was probably hard to do, considering the camera probably isn’t at the exact center of rotation.

My arms grow heavy, so I put them on the armrests. I have to start using my neck muscles again for the first time in a while.

Five minutes after the process began, I feel a little less than normal Earth gravity. A quadruple beep announces the end of the process.

I check the Centrifuge screen. It shows a spin rate of 20.71° per second, a total distance of 104 meters, and a “Lab Gravity” of 1.00 g.

The ship diagram shows the Hail Mary split in two, the crew compartment facing the other half. The two halves are ridiculously far apart, and the whole thing spins slowly. Well, actually pretty fast, but it looks slow from that distance.

I unstrap from the chair, walk to the airlock, and open the hatch. The ammonia smell comes into the cockpit again, but not as strong as before. The alien thing is on the floor. I quickly touch it to check the temperature. It’s still warm, but not burning hot anymore. Good. It doesn’t have a heater or anything weird like that. It was just really hot at first.

I pick it up. Time to see what this thing is made of. And what’s inside.

Before leaving the cockpit, I look at the Telescope screen one last time. I don’t know why—I guess I just like to see what alien ships near me are doing.

The Blip-A spins in space. It rotates, probably at the same rate as the Hail Mary. I guess they saw me spin the centrifuge and thought it was another way to talk.

Humanity’s first misunderstanding with an intelligent alien race. Glad I could be a part of it.

—

I put the cylinder on the lab table. Where do I start? Everywhere!

I check if it's radioactive with a Geiger counter. It's not. That’s good.

I poke it with different things to feel how hard it is. It’s hard.

It looks like metal but doesn’t feel exactly like metal. I use a multimeter to see if it conducts electricity. It doesn’t. Interesting.

I get a hammer and chisel. I want a small piece of the cylinder material for the gas chromatograph—that way I’ll know what elements are in it. After a few hits with the hammer, the chisel breaks. The cylinder isn’t even scratched.

“Hm.”

The cylinder is too big for the gas chromatograph. But I find a handheld x-ray spectrometer. It looks like a UPC scanner gun. Easy to use, and it’ll give me some idea of what this thing is made of. It’s not as precise as the chromatograph, but it's better than nothing.

After a quick scan, it tells me the cylinder is made of xenon.

“What…?”

I use the spectrometer on the steel lab table to make sure it’s working right. It reports iron, nickel, chromium, and so on. Just what it should say. So I check the cylinder again and get the same weird results as before. I test it four more times but keep getting the same answer.

Why did I run the test so many times? Because those results don't make any sense. Xenon is a noble gas. It doesn’t react with anything. It doesn’t connect to anything. And it’s a gas at room temperature. But somehow it’s part of this solid thing?

And no, it’s not a tube full of xenon or anything like that. A spectrometer doesn't scan deeply. It can only tell you what's on the surface. If I pointed it at gold-plated nickel, it would say “100% gold,” because that’s all it can see. It can only tell me what the molecules on the cylinder's surface are. Apparently, they’re made of xenon.

This handheld spectrometer can’t detect elements lighter than aluminum. So there could be carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, or anything else in there. But for elements it can detect…I’m looking at pure xenon.

“How?!”

I sit down on a stool and stare at the cylinder. What a strange object. What do I even call noble gases that react with things? Un-nobles?

But being stumped has one good effect. It makes me stop frantically messing with the cylinder and just look at it. For the first time, I see a thin line going around it, about an inch from the top. I feel it with my fingernail. It’s definitely a groove of some kind. Is that a lid? Maybe it just opens.

I pick up the cylinder and try to pull off the top. It doesn’t move. On a whim, I try to unscrew it. It also doesn’t move.

But there’s no reason aliens would use the same tightening and loosening rules we do, right?

I turn the lid to the right and it turns. My heart jumps!

I keep turning. After 90 degrees I feel it unlock. I pull the two pieces apart.

Both halves have complex things inside. They look like…models of some kind? They both have very thin poles sticking up from their bases, leading to spheres of different sizes. I don’t see any moving parts, and everything seems to be made of the same strange material as the case.

I look at the bottom half first. Got to start somewhere.

A single whisker holds up…an abstract sculpture? It’s a marble-sized ball and a BB-sized ball, each held in place by thinner whiskers branching off the main vertical “trunk.” There’s also a strange curved shape connecting the tops of the two balls. This whole thing looks familiar…why…?

“Petrova line!” I say suddenly.

I’ve seen that curved shape so many times I know it by heart. My heart races.

I point to the large ball. “So you must be a star. And the little one must be a planet.”

These aliens know about Astrophage. Or, at least, they know about the Petrova line. But that doesn’t really tell me anything. They’re in a ship powered by Astrophage, so of course they know about it. And we’re chatting in a solar system that has a Petrova line, so that’s not surprising either. This could be their home system for all I know.

This is a good start, though. We were “talking” by flashing our engines. So they know I use Astrophage and that I can “see” (with help from the ship) the Petrova frequency. From that, they figured I must be able to see the Petrova line. They’re smart.

I look at the other half of the thing. Dozens of whiskers rise from the base. They’re all different lengths and each one ends in a ball less than a millimeter across. I poke a whisker with my finger and it doesn’t bend. I press harder and harder. Eventually the whole thing slides on the table. Those whiskers are stronger than something that thin should be.

I guess xenon makes pretty strong material when you get it to react with things. It bugs my sensitive scientist's heart! I try to ignore it and get back to what I’m doing.

I count thirty-one whiskers, each with its little ball at the end. While counting, I notice something special. There’s one whisker sticking up from the exact center of the disc, but unlike the others, it’s not connected to a ball. I squint to get a good look.

Instead of one ball, it’s two balls of different sizes and a curve—okay, I see. It’s a very small copy of the Petrova-line model on the other half of the thingamajig. Maybe one-twentieth the size.

And that little Petrova-line model has an even thinner line connecting it to another ball at the end of a different line. No, not quite a ball. It’s another Petrova-line model. I check the rest of the thingamajig for any more of them, but I don’t see any. Just the one in the middle and the one off to the side.

“Wait a minute…waaaaait a minute…”

I pull out the drawer with the lab computer panel. Time to use that huge amount of reference info. I find a big spreadsheet with the info I need, open it in Excel (Stratt likes products that are proven and easy to get), and do some math on it. Soon, I have the data chart I wanted. And it matches.

Stars. The little balls on the end of the lines are stars. Of course they are. What else would have a Petrova line?

But they’re not just any stars. These are specific stars. They’re all in the right places compared to each other, with Tau Ceti right in the center. The map looks a little strange. To make the balls match my data chart of star locations, I have to hold the thingamajig at an angle and turn it a bit.

But of course, all of Earth’s data is based on Earth’s orbit as the reference point. People from another planet would have a different system. But however you look at it, it means the same thing: The thingamajig is a map of the nearby stars.

Then I’m suddenly very interested in that little line connecting the center ball (Tau Ceti) to another ball. I check the matching star in my catalog: It’s called 40 Eridani. But I bet the crew of the Blip-A call it home.

That’s the message. “We’re from the 40 Eridani system. And now we’re here at Tau Ceti.”

But there’s even more to it. They’re also saying “40 Eridani has a Petrova line, just like Tau Ceti.”

I pause, letting that settle.

“Are you going through the same thing?!” I say.

Of course, they are! Astrophage is affecting all the nearby stars. These people are from a planet circling 40 Eridani, and 40 Eridani is infected, just like Earth’s sun! They have some advanced science, so they did what we did. They built a ship and came to Tau Ceti to see why it’s not dying!

“Wow!” I say.

Okay, I’m jumping to conclusions. Maybe they collect Astrophage from their Petrova lines and see it as a good thing. Maybe they created Astrophage. Maybe they just think Petrova lines are beautiful. This could mean many things. But the most likely, in my opinion, is that they’re here to find a cure.

Aliens.

Real aliens.

Aliens from the 40 Eridani system. So, I guess that makes them Eridanians? It’s hard to say, even harder to remember. Eridans? No. How about Eridians? It sounds like “iridium,” which is one of the coolest elements on the periodic table. Yeah, I’m going to call them Eridians.

And I think it’s pretty clear how I should react.

I searched the lab carefully a few days ago. There’s an electronics kit in one of the drawers. The challenge is remembering which one.

I don’t remember, of course. It takes me a while to search, and I almost swear a few times, but I find it eventually.

I don’t have any xenonite (that’s what I’m calling this weird alien stuff, and no one can stop me). But I do have solder and a soldering iron. I break off a small piece of solder, melt one end, and stick it to the Tau Ceti sphere. It sticks pretty well, which is good. You never know with xenonite.

I check, double-check, and triple-check to make sure I know which little star in the model is Sol (Earth’s sun). I solder the other side of the wire to Sol.

I look around the lab until I find some hard wax. With some poking, fire, and a little swearing, I make a really bad copy of the Petrova-line icon they sent me. I stick it onto Sol in the model. It looks okay. At least, good enough that they should understand.

I look at it. The sleek, thin lines of the xenonite whiskers are ruined by my crooked, blob-ended solder and bad wax model. It’s like someone drew with crayons on a Da Vinci, but it will have to do.

I try to screw the top and bottom of the thing back together. They don’t fit. I try again. Still doesn’t work. I remember that Eridians use left-handed screws. So I do what feels like unscrewing. The two pieces connect perfectly.

Time to give it back to them. Nicely.

Except I can’t. Not with the ship spinning like this. If I tried to go out the airlock, I’d fly off into space.

I grab the thing and go up to the control room. I strap myself into the chair and tell the ship to stop spinning.

Like before, I feel the room tilt, but this time it tilts the other way. And again, I know it’s not really tilting, it’s just how I feel the sideways movement, but whatever.

I feel the gravity lessen and the room straighten until I’m floating again. This time I’m not confused. I guess my instincts have accepted that gravity comes and goes. The operation finishes with a final “clunk” as the crew compartment realigns with the back of the ship.

I get back in the EVA suit, grab the gadget, and go out into space again. I don’t need to use tethers to cross the hull this time. I just clip my tether in the airlock.

The Blip-A has stopped spinning—probably when the Hail Mary did. And it’s still 217 meters away.

I don’t need to be a great quarterback to make this throw. I just need to launch the gadget toward the Blip-A. It’s over a hundred meters wide. I should be able to hit it.

I give the gadget a push. It floats away from me at a good speed. Maybe 2 meters per second—about a jogging pace. This is a kind of message too. I’m telling my new friends that I can handle slightly faster deliveries.

The gadget floats toward the Eridian ship and I go back into mine.

“Okay, guys,” I say. “The enemy of my enemy is my friend. If Astrophage is your enemy, I’m my friend.”

—

I watch the Telescope screen. Sometimes I look away. Sometimes I play Klondike solitaire on the Nav panel. But I always check the telescope every few seconds. A thick pair of gloves, taken from the lab earlier, tries to float away. I grab them and stuff them behind the pilot’s seat.

It’s been two hours and my alien friends haven’t said a word. Are they waiting for me to speak again? I just told them what star I came from. It’s their turn to talk, right?

Do they even understand the idea of taking turns? Or is that just a human thing?

What if Eridians live for 2 million years and waiting a century to answer is normal for them?

How am I going to get rid of this red 7 on the far-right pile? I don’t have any black 8s in my deck and—

Movement!

I turn to the Telescope screen so fast my legs swing out into the middle of the control room. There’s another cylinder coming toward me. I guess the many-armed robot threw it just now. I check the Radar screen. Blip-B is moving at over a meter per second. I only have a few minutes to get ready!

I get back into the EVA suit and use the airlock. When I open the outside door, I see the cylinder spinning. It might be the same one as before, or it might be new. And this time, it’s going straight for the airlock. I guess they saw that’s where I left and came back into the ship and decided to make it easier for me.

How thoughtful of them.

They’re accurate too. A minute later, the cylinder floats right through the middle of the open doorway. I catch it. I wave to the Blip-A and close the door. They probably don’t know what a wave is, but I felt like I should do it.

I go back to the control room and take off the EVA suit, leaving the cylinder to float near the airlock. The ammonia smell is strong, but this time I’m prepared for it.

I put the thick lab gloves on and grab the cylinder. Even through the fireproof gloves, I can feel the warmth. I know I should wait for it to cool down but I don’t want to.

It looks the same as before. I unscrew it the same left-handed way. This time, there’s no star map. Instead, it’s a model. What am I looking at here?

A single post from the base holds up an odd shape. No, two odd shapes connected by a tube. Hey, wait. One of the shapes is the Hail Mary. Oh, and the other one is the Blip-A.

The models have no detail or texture. But they’re good enough for me to recognize what they are, so they did their job. The Hail Mary is only 3 inches long, while the Blip-A is closer to 8 inches. Man, that ship is huge.

And that tube connecting them? It connects to the Hail Mary’s airlock and leads to the center of the Blip-A’s diamond-shaped segment. The tunnel is just wide enough to cover my airlock door.

They want to meet.
