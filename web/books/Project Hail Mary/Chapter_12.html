Project Hail Mary

Chapter 12

T ap-tap-tap.

The sound barely gets through to me. It’s so distant.

Tap-tap-tap.

I wake up from a sleep without dreams. “Huh?”

Tap-tap-tap.

“Breakfast,” I mumble.

The robot arms reach into a space and take out a wrapped meal. It’s like Christmas every morning here. I pull the lid off and steam rises up. There’s a breakfast burrito inside.

“Nice,” I say. “Coffee?”

“Preparing…”

I take a bite of the breakfast burrito. It’s good. All the food is good. I guess they figured if we’re going to die, we may as well eat good stuff.

“Coffee,” says the computer. A mechanical arm hands me a pouch with a straw. Like a Capri Sun for adults. Made for zero gravity.

I let the burrito float nearby and take a sip of coffee. It’s delicious, of course. It even has just the right amount of cream and sugar. That’s a very personal thing that everyone likes differently.

Tap-tap-tap.

What is that?

I check the LCD screen taped near my bunk. Rocky is in the tunnel tapping on the divider wall.

“Computer! How long was I asleep?”

“Patient was unconscious for ten hours and seventeen minutes.”

“Oh crud!”

I wriggle out of my bedding and bounce up through the ship toward the control room. I carry the burrito and coffee with me because I’m starving.

I bounce into the tunnel. “Sorry! Sorry!”

Rocky taps the divider louder than before, now that I’m here. He points to the Popsicle-stick numbers I taped to the divider and then to his clock. He makes a fist with one hand.

“I’m sorry!” I clasp my hands together as if praying. I don’t know what else to do. There’s no space symbol for begging. I don’t know if he understands, but he opens his fist.

Maybe it was a small scolding. I mean, he could have made five fists, but he only made one.

Anyway, I kept him waiting over two hours. He’s understandably upset. Hopefully this next trick will make up for it.

I hold up a finger. He does the same.

I grab my duct-taped laptops and start the sound-analysis program on one and Excel on the other. I press them against the tunnel wall and tape them there.

I pull the Popsicle-stick numbers off the divider wall. They’re as good a place to start as any. I hold up the “I” and point to it. “One,” I say. “One.”

I point to my mouth, then back to the Eridian number. “One.” Then I point to Rocky.

He points to the “I” and says “♪.”

I pause the waveform analyzer and scroll back a few seconds. “There we go…” Rocky’s word for “one” is just two notes played together. There are also many other tones and echoes in there, but the main sounds are just two notes.

I type “one” into the spreadsheet on the other computer and write down the frequencies.

“Okay…” I go back to the divider and hold up the “V” symbol. “Two,” I say.

“♪,” he says. Another one-syllable word. The oldest words in a language are usually the shortest.

This time, it’s a chord made of four different notes. I enter “two” and record the frequencies for that word.

He starts to get excited. I think he knows what I’m doing, and it makes him happy.

I hold up the “λ” and before I can even speak, he points to it and says, “♫♪.”

Great. Our first two-syllable word. I have to scroll back and forth a bit in the waveform data to get the chords right. The first syllable has two notes, and the second has five! Rocky can make at least five different notes at the same time. He must have multiple sets of vocal cords or something. Well, he has five arms and five hands. So why not five sets of vocal cords?

I don’t see a mouth anywhere. The notes are just coming from somewhere inside him. When I first heard him speak, I thought it sounded like whale song. That may have been more accurate than I thought. Whales sound like they do because they move air back and forth across their vocal cords without letting it out. Rocky may be doing the same thing.

Tap-tap-tap-tap!

“What?” I look back at him.

He points to the “λ” symbol still in my hand and then at me. Then back to the “λ” and back at me. He’s almost frantic.

“Oh, sorry,” I say. I hold the number up properly and say, “Three.”

He does jazz hands. I throw some jazz hands back.

Huh. Now that we’re talking about it…

I stand still for a moment so he’ll know there was a break in the conversation. Then I do jazz hands and say, “Yes.”

I repeat the gesture. “Yes.”

He does it back to me and says, “♫♩.”

I write down the frequencies in my laptop.

“Okay, we have ‘yes’ in our vocabulary now,” I say.

Tap-tap-tap.

I look over. Once he has my attention, he does jazz hands again and says, “♫♩.” Same chord as before.

“Yes,” I say. “We talked about this.”

He holds up a finger for a moment. Then he makes two fists and taps them together. “♪♪.”

…What?

“Ohhh,” I say. I’m a teacher. What would I teach someone who just learned ‘yes’?

“That’s ‘no.’ ”

At least I hope so.

I make fists and tap them together. “No.”

“♫♩,” he says. I check the laptop. He just said yes.

Wait. Does that mean it's not no? Is that another yes? Now I’m confused.

“No?” I ask.

“No,” he says in Eridian.

“So, ‘yes’?”

“No, yes.”

“Yes?”

“No. No.”

“Yes, yes?”

“No!” He makes a fist at me, clearly frustrated.

Enough of this back-and-forth routine. I hold up a finger.

He unclenches his fist and returns the gesture.

I type what I think is “no” into my spreadsheet. If I’m wrong, I’m wrong, and we'll figure it out later.

I hold up the “+” symbol. “Four.”

He holds up three fingers on one hand and one finger on another. “♩♩.”

I write down the frequencies.

—

Over the next several hours, we grow our shared vocabulary to several thousand words. Language is an accelerating system. The more words you know, the easier it is to explain new ones.

Talking is hard because my system for listening to Rocky is slow and awkward. I check the frequencies he makes with one laptop, then find them in my spreadsheet on the other laptop. It's not a good system. I’ve had enough.

I take an hour to write some software. I'm not a computer expert, but I know some basic programming. I write a program that takes the audio-analysis software's output and finds the words in my table. It's barely a program—more like a script. It's not efficient, but computers are fast.

Luckily, Rocky speaks with musical notes. It’s hard to make a computer turn human speech into text, but it’s easy to make a computer recognize musical notes and find them in a chart.

After that, my laptop screen shows me the English translation of what Rocky is saying as it happens. When a new word appears, I add it to my database, and the computer recognizes it from then on.

Rocky, however, doesn’t use anything to record what I’m saying or doing. No computer, no pen, no microphone. Nothing. He just listens carefully. And as far as I can tell, he remembers everything I told him. Every word. Even if I only told it to him once, hours ago. I wish my students paid attention that well!

I think Eridians have much better memories than humans.

In general, the human brain is like a bunch of software fixes put together into one unit that somehow works. Each "feature" was added as a random change that solved a specific problem to help us survive.

Basically, the human brain is a mess. Everything about evolution is messy. So, I assume Eridians are also a mix of random changes. But whatever made their brains the way they are, it gave them what we humans would call "photographic memory."

It's probably even more complex than that. Humans have a whole part of our brains for sight, and it even has its own memory storage. Maybe Eridians are just really good at remembering sounds. After all, it's their main sense.

I know it's too soon, but I can't wait. I get a vial of Astrophage from the lab supplies and take it to the tunnel. I hold it up. "Astrophage," I say.

Rocky's whole body changes. He lowers his shell a little. He grips the bars he uses to stay in place a bit tighter. "♫♪♫," he says, his voice quieter than usual.

I check the computer. It's not a word I've saved yet. It must be his word for Astrophage. I write it down in the database.

I point to the vial. “Astrophage on my star. Bad.”

“♫♩♪♫ ♫♪♫♩ ♫♪♫,” Rocky says.

The computer translates: Astrophage on my star. Bad bad bad.

Okay! Theory confirmed. He’s here for the same reason I am. I want to ask so many more questions. But we just don’t have the words. It’s so frustrating!

“♫♫ ♫♩♪♪♫ ♫♪♫,” Rocky says.

My computer shows the text: You come from where, question?

Rocky has learned the basic word order of English. I think he realized early on that I can’t easily remember things, so he uses my system instead of trying to teach me his. I probably seem pretty dumb, honestly. But some of his own grammar slips in now and then. He always ends a question with the word “question.”

“No understand,” I say.

“You star is what name, question?”

“Oh!” I say. He wants the name of my star. “Sol. My star is called ‘Sol.’ ”

“Understand. Eridian name for your star is_ ♫♪♫♪♩♩.”

I write down the new word. It’s Rocky’s word for “Sol.” Unlike two humans struggling to talk, Rocky and I can’t even say each other’s names right.

“My name for your star is ‘Eridani,’ ” I say. We really call it “40 Eridani,” but I want to keep it easy.

“Eridian name for my star is ♫♩♪♪♪.”

I add the word to the list. “Understand.”

“Good.”

I don’t need the computer to tell me that one. I’m starting to know some common words like “you,” “me,” “good,” “bad,” and so on. I’m not artistic and have a terrible ear for music. But after hearing a chord a lot, you start to remember it.

I check my watch—yes, I have a watch now. The stopwatch also tells time. It took me a while to notice. I was busy with other things.

We’ve been doing this all day and I’m tired. Do Eridians even sleep? I guess I’ll find out.

“Human bodies must sleep. Sleep is this.” I curl up and close my eyes to show what sleep is like. I fake a snore because I’m a bad actor.

I return to normal and point to his clock. “Humans sleep for twenty-nine thousand seconds.”

Besides having perfect memory, Eridians are extremely good at math. At least, Rocky is. As we worked with scientific units, it became clear that he could convert from his units to mine instantly. And he easily understands base ten.

“Many seconds…” he says. “Why be still so many seconds, question…Understand!”

He relaxes his limbs until they are limp. He curls up like a dead bug and stays still for a while. “Eridians same! ♪♫♫♪ !”

Thank goodness. I can’t imagine explaining “sleep” to someone who had never heard of it. Hey, I’m going to become unconscious and hallucinate for a while. Also, I spend a third of my time doing this. And if I can’t do it for a while, I go insane and eventually die. Don't worry.

I add his word for “sleep” to the dictionary.

I turn to leave. “I’m going to sleep now. I’ll come back in twenty-nine thousand seconds.”

“I observe,” he says.

“You observe?”

“I observe.”

“Uh…”

He wants to watch me sleep? That would be strange usually, but it's okay when you’re studying a new life-form, I guess.

“I will be still for twenty-nine thousand seconds,” I warn him. “A lot of seconds. I won’t do anything.”

“I observe. Wait.”

He goes back to his ship. Is he finally going to get something to write with? After a few minutes, he comes back with a device in one of his hands and a bag held in two more.

“I observe.”

I point to the device. “What is that?”

“♫♪♩♫.” He pulls some kind of tool out of the bag. “♫♪♩♫ not work.” He pokes the device with the tool a few times. “I change. ♫♪♩♫ work.”

I don’t bother to write down the new word. What would I call it? “Thing Rocky was holding that one time”? Whatever it is, it has a couple of wires sticking out and a hole that shows some complicated parts inside.

The object itself doesn't matter. The important thing is he’s fixing it. New word for us.

“Fix.” I say. “You fix.”

“♫♪♫♪,” he says.

I add “fix” to the dictionary. I think it’ll come up a lot.

He wants to watch me sleep. He knows it won’t be exciting, but he wants to anyway. So he brought work to keep busy.

Okay. Whatever makes him happy.

“Wait,” I say.

I go back to the ship and head to the dormitory.

I pull the mattress pad, sheets, and blanket from my bunk. I could use one of the other two bunks but…my dead friends were in them, so I don’t want to.

I bring the pad and sheets through the lab, awkwardly through the control room, and into the tunnel. I use a lot of duct tape to stick the mattress pad to the wall, then arrange the sheets and blanket.

“I sleep now,” I say.

“Sleep.”

I turn off the lights in the tunnel. It’s completely dark for me, but it doesn’t bother Rocky, who wants to watch me. It's the best of both worlds.

I wiggle into bed and try not to say good night. That would just cause more questions.

I fall asleep to the occasional clink and scrape of Rocky working on his device.

—

The next few days are the same, but not boring at all. We learn a lot more words and some grammar. Tenses, plurals, "what if" statements…language is complicated. But we’re learning it bit by bit.

Even though it’s slow, I’m learning more of his language. I don’t need the computer as much. But I still need it sometimes—it’ll take a long time to be able to do without it.

I spend an hour every day learning Eridian words. I made a simple program to pick words randomly from my spreadsheet and play the sounds with a music app. It’s a basic program, not written well, but computers are fast. I want to stop using the spreadsheet as soon as possible. I still need it all the time for now. But sometimes I understand a whole sentence without using the computer. Small steps.

Every night, I sleep in the tunnel. He watches. I don’t know why. We haven’t talked about it yet. We’ve been too busy with other things. But he really wants to watch me sleep. Even if I just want a short nap.

Today I want to tackle a very important science topic that’s been tricky for us. Mostly because we're in zero gravity.

“We need to talk about mass.”

“Yes. Kilogram.”

“Right. How do I explain a kilogram to you?” I ask.

Rocky pulls out a small ball from his bag. It’s about the size of a ping-pong ball. “I know mass of this ball. You measure. You tell me how many kilograms ball is. Then I know kilogram.”

He figured it out!

“Yes! Give me the ball.”

He grabs several support bars with different hands and puts it in the mini-airlock. After a few minutes of waiting for it to cool, I have it in my hands. It’s smooth and made of a metal. Seems pretty dense to me.

“How will I measure this?” I mutter.

“Twenty-six,” Rocky says suddenly.

“What about twenty-six?”

He points to the ball in my hand. “Ball is twenty-six.”

Oh, I get it. The ball weighs twenty-six of something. Whatever he's measuring in. Okay. All I have to do is figure out how heavy this ball is, divide by twenty-six, and tell him the answer.

“I understand. The ball weighs twenty-six.”

“No. Is not.”

I pause. “It doesn’t?”

“Is not. Ball is twenty-six.”

“I don’t understand.”

He thinks for a moment, then says, “Wait.”

He disappears into his ship.

While he’s gone, I wonder how to weigh something in zero g. It still has mass, of course. But I can’t just put it on a scale. There’s no gravity. And I can’t use the Hail Mary’s spinning gravity. The tunnel is connected to her nose.

I could make a small spinner. Big enough for the smallest lab scale I have. Spin it at a steady rate with the scale inside. Measure something I know the mass of and then measure the ball. I could figure out the ball's mass from the comparison of the two measurements.

But I’d have to build a steady spinner. How would I do that? I can spin something in the lab’s zero-g easily, but how do I spin it at a steady rate for multiple tries?

Oooh! I don’t need a steady rate! I just need a string with a mark in the middle!

I fly into the Hail Mary. Rocky will forgive me for taking off. He can probably “watch” me from wherever he is on his ship anyway.

I bring the ball to the lab. I get a piece of nylon string and tie each end to a plastic sample container. Now I have a string with little buckets at each end. I put the containers together and pull the folded string tight. I use a pen to mark the end point. That’s the exact middle of this thing.

I wave the ball back and forth to get a feel for its mass. Probably less than a pound. Less than half a kilogram.

I leave everything floating in the lab and kick my way to the dormitory.

“Water,” I say.

“Water requested,” says the computer. The metal arms give me a zero-g “sipper” of water. Just a plastic pouch with a straw that only lets water through if you open a clip. And inside is 1 liter of water. The arms always give me water a liter at a time. You have to stay hydrated if you want to save the world.

I return to the lab. I pour about half the water into a sample box and seal it. I put the half-empty sipper into one bucket and the metal ball into the other. I set the whole thing spinning in the air.

The two weights clearly aren’t equal. The uneven spin of the two connected containers shows the water side is much heavier. Good. That’s what I wanted.

I pluck it out of the air and take a sip of water. I start it spinning again. Still off-center, but not as bad.

I take more sips, do more spins, take more sips, and so on until my little device spins perfectly around the marked center point.

That means the weight of the water is equal to the weight of the ball.

I pull out the sippy. I know how dense water is—it’s 1 kilogram per liter. So all I need to know is how much water is here to know its weight and therefore the weight of the metal ball.

I get a large plastic syringe from the supplies. It can pull out 100 cc of liquid at most.

I attach the syringe to the sippy and unclip the straw. I draw out 100 cc of water, then squirt it into my “wastewater box.” I repeat this a few more times. The last syringe is only about a quarter full when I empty the bag.

Result: 325 ccs of water, which weighs 325 grams! Therefore, Rocky’s ball also weighs 325 grams.

I return to the tunnel to tell Rocky all about how smart I am.

He makes a fist at me as I enter. “You left! Bad!”

“I measured the mass! I did a very smart experiment.”

He holds up a string with beads on it. “Twenty-six.”

The beaded string is just like the ones he sent me when we talked about our atmospheres—

“Oh,” I say. It’s an atom. That’s how he talks about atoms. I count the beads. There are twenty-six in all.

He’s talking about element 26—one of the most common elements on Earth. “Iron,” I say. I point at the necklace. “Iron.”

He points at the necklace and says, “♫♩♪♫♫.” I write the word in my dictionary.

“Iron,” he says again, pointing at the necklace.

“Iron.”

He points to the ball in my hand. “Iron.”

It takes a second to sink in. Then I slap my forehead.

“You are bad.”

It was a fun experiment, but a total waste of time. Rocky was giving me all the information I needed. Or trying to, at least. I know how dense iron is, and I know how to figure out the volume of a sphere. Getting to mass from there is just a little math.

I pull a pair of calipers out of the toolkit I keep in the tunnel and measure the sphere’s diameter. It’s 4.3 centimeters. From that I figure out the volume, multiply by the density of iron, and get a much more exact mass of 328.25 grams.

“I was only off by one percent,” I grumble.

“You talk to you, question?”

“Yes! I’m talking to me.”

“Humans are unusual.”

“Yes,” I say.

Rocky stretches his legs. “I sleep now.”

“Wow,” I say. This is the first time he’s had to sleep since we met. Good. This will give me some time for lab work. But how much time?

“How long do Eridians sleep?”

“I not know.”

“You don’t know? You’re Eridian. How can you not know how long Eridians sleep?”

“Eridians not know how long sleep last. Maybe short time. Maybe long time.”

They sleep for random amounts of time. I guess there’s no rule that says sleep has to become regular. Does he at least know a possible range?

“Is there a shortest time? A longest time?”

“ Minimum is 12,265 seconds. Maximum is 42,928 seconds.”

Rocky often gives me oddly specific numbers for things that should be estimates. It took me a while to figure it out, but I finally did. He is giving me estimates. But they’re in his units and in base six. It’s easier for him to change those values to Earth seconds than to think in Earth seconds.

If I changed those values back to Eridian seconds and looked at the numbers in base six, I bet they’d be round numbers. But I’m too lazy. Why change data he already changed? He’s never been wrong about math.

Meanwhile, I have to divide by 60 twice on a calculator just to convert from one of my planet’s units to another. He’ll sleep for at least three and a half hours and at most almost twelve hours.

“I understand,” I say. I head back toward the airlock.

“You watch, ask questions?” Rocky asks.

He watched me sleep, so it’s only fair he offers to let me watch him. I’m sure Earth scientists would be thrilled to learn anything about what an Eridian sleeping looks like. But I finally have time to deeply analyze xenonite, and I’m just dying to know how xenon bonds with other elements. If I can get any of my lab equipment to work in zero g, that is.

“Not necessary.”

“You watch, ask questions?” he asks again.

“No.”

“Watch.”

“You want me to watch you sleep?”

“Yes. Want want want.”

Through unspoken agreement, a repeated word three times means great emphasis.

“Why?”

“I sleep better if you watch.”

“Why?”

He waves his arms, trying to find a way to say it. “Eridians do that.”

Eridians watch each other sleep. It’s a custom. I should be more understanding, but he made fun of me when I talked to myself. “Eridians are strange.”

“Watch. I sleep better.”

I don’t want to watch a dog-sized spider not move for hours. There’s a crew in there, right? Have one of them do it. I point to his ship. “Have another Eridian watch you.”

“No.”

“Why not?”

“I am the only Eridian here.”

My mouth hangs open. “You’re the only person on that huge ship?!”

He’s quiet for a moment, then says, “♫♩♪♫♩♪♫ ♫♪ ♩♪♫ ♫♪♫♪♩ ♫♪♩♪ ♫♩ ♪ ♫♩♪ ♫ ♩♪♫♩♪ ♫♩♪ ♫.”

Complete nonsense. Did my patched-together translation software break? I check it. No, it’s working fine. I look at the waveforms. They look like the ones I’d seen before. But they’re lower. Now that I think about it, that whole sentence sounded lower than anything Rocky has ever said before. I select the whole part in the software’s recording history and raise it an octave. The octave is a universal thing, not just for humans. It means doubling the frequency of every note.

The computer immediately translates it. “Original crew was twenty-three. Now it is only me.”

That octave-drop…I think it’s emotion.

“They…did they die?”

“Yes.”

I rub my eyes. Wow. The Blip-A had a crew of twenty-three. Rocky is the only one left, and he’s upset about it.

“Wh…er…” I stammer. “Bad.”

“Bad bad bad.”

I sigh. “I started with three crew members. Now it’s just me.” I put my hand up against the divider.

Rocky puts a claw on the divider opposite my hand. “Bad.”

“Bad bad bad,” I say.

We stay like that for a moment. “I’ll watch you sleep.”

“Good. Me sleep,” he says.

His arms relax, and he looks completely like a dead bug. He floats freely in his side of the tunnel, no longer holding on to any support bars.

“Well, you’re not alone anymore, buddy,” I say. “Neither of us are.”
