Project Hail Mary

Chapter 18

The crew of the Hail Mary sat on the couch in the break room, each with their chosen drink.

Commander Yáo had a German beer, Engineer Ilyukhina had a shockingly large glass of vodka, and Science Specialist DuBois had a glass of 2003 Cabernet Sauvignon that he had poured ten minutes earlier to let it breathe.

The break room had been hard to arrange. Stratt didn’t like anything not directly related to the mission, and an aircraft carrier didn’t have much extra space. But with over a hundred scientists from all over the world wanting a place to relax, she gave in. A small room in the corner of the hangar deck was built for this "luxury."

Dozens of people crowded into the room and watched the TV feed on the wall-mounted monitor. Without a word, the crew got to sit on the couch. The crew got every possible benefit. They were giving their lives for humanity. The least we could do was give them the best seats.

“And we’re just minutes from lift-off,” said the BBC reporter. We could have watched American, Chinese, or Russian news; it would have been the same. The wide shot of Baikonur Cosmodrome mixed with shots of the huge rocket on the launchpad.

The reporter stood in the observation room overlooking Moscow’s Mission Control Center. “Today’s launch is the ninth of sixteen for Project Hail Mary, but it may be the most important. This payload contains the cockpit, lab, and sleeping modules. Astronauts on the ISS are ready to receive the modules and will spend the next two weeks putting them on the Hail Mary ’s frame, which was built over the last several trips…”

Ilyukhina raised her vodka. “Don’t mess up my house, Roscosmos jerks!”

“Aren’t they your friends?” I asked.

“They can be both!” She roared with laughter.

The countdown appeared on the screen. Less than a minute left.

Yáo leaned forward, watching closely. It must have been difficult—a soldier used to action forced to simply watch something so important happen.

DuBois noticed Yáo’s face. “I’m sure the launch will be fine, Commander Yáo.”

“Mm,” said Yáo.

“Thirty seconds to launch,” said Ilyukhina. “I can’t wait that long.” She finished her vodka and immediately poured another.

The scientists moved closer as the countdown continued. I was pushed against the back of the couch, but I was too focused on the screen to notice.

DuBois turned to look at me. “Ms. Stratt isn’t joining us?”

“I don’t think so,” I said. “She doesn’t care about things like launches. She’s probably looking at spreadsheets in her office.”

He nodded. “Then it’s good you’re here. To represent her, in a way.”

“Me? Represent her? What makes you say that?”

Ilyukhina turned quickly to face me. “You are number two, yes? You are second in command of Project Hail Mary?”

“What? No! I’m just one of the scientists. Like all these people.” I pointed to the men and women behind me.

Ilyukhina and DuBois looked at each other and then back to me. “You really believe that?” she said.

Bob Redell spoke up behind me. “You’re not like the rest of us, Grace.”

I shrugged at him. “Of course I am. Why wouldn’t I be?”

“The thing is,” DuBois said, “you are, for some reason, special to Ms. Stratt. I thought you two were sleeping together.”

My mouth dropped open. “What?! Are you crazy?! No! No way!”

“Huh,” said Ilyukhina. “Maybe you should? She is tense. She could use a good romp.”

“Oh my God. Is that what people think?” I turned to face the scientists. Most of them looked away. “Nothing like that is happening! And I’m not her second in command! I’m just a scientist—forced into this project like the rest of you!”

Yáo turned around and stared at me for a moment. The room went silent. He didn’t talk much, so when he did, people listened.

“You are the number two,” he said. Then he turned back to the screen.

The BBC announcer counted down the last few seconds along with the timer on the screen. “Three…two…one…and we have lift-off!”

Flames and smoke were all around the rocket on the screen, and it went up into the sky. Slowly at first, then getting faster and faster.

Ilyukhina held up her glass for a few seconds and then cheered loudly. “Tower is clear! Launch is good!” She drank her vodka quickly.

“It’s only a hundred feet off the ground,” I said. “Maybe wait until it gets to orbit?”

DuBois took a sip of his wine. “Astronauts celebrate when the tower is clear.”

Yáo took a sip of his beer without saying anything.

—

“Why. Doesn’t. This. Work?!” I hit my forehead with both hands at each word.

I flop into the lab chair, feeling drained.

Rocky watches from his tunnel above. “No predator, question?”

“No predator.” I sigh.

The experiment is pretty straightforward. It’s a glass bulb filled with Adrian’s air. The air didn’t really come from Adrian, but the gas mix matches the spectrograph of its atmosphere. The pressure is very low—one-tenth of an atmosphere, like Adrian’s upper atmosphere must be.

Also in the bulb are our collected Adrian life-forms and some fresh Astrophage. I hoped that giving them a bunch of nice, juicy Astrophage would make the predator population boom, so I could separate it from the sample once it was the main cell type there.

Didn’t work.

“You are certain, question?”

I check my simple heat sensor. It’s just a thermocouple with one part stuck in ice water and the other part on the bulb. Heat comes from the Astrophage and is used up by the ice. The thermocouple's temperature tells me how much total heat the Astrophage is giving off. If the temperature drops, it means the Astrophage population shrank. But that’s not happening.

“Yeah, I’m sure,” I say. “No change in Astrophage population.”

“Maybe temperature of bulb no good. Too hot. Adrian upper atmosphere is probably much colder than you room temperature.”

I shake my head. “The temperature on Adrian shouldn’t matter. The predator needs to survive the temperature of Astrophage.”

“Ah. Yes. You are right.”

“Maybe the predator idea is wrong,” I say.

He clicks across the tunnel to the other side of the lab. He paces when he’s thinking. It’s interesting that humans and Eridians both do that. “Predators are the only answer. Maybe predators don’t live in the Petrova line. Maybe predators live lower in the atmosphere.”

I get excited. “Maybe.”

I look at the lab screen. It’s showing the view from the outside camera on Adrian. Not for any scientific reason—just because it looks cool. Right now, we’re about to cross into the day side of the planet. The light of dawn shines along a curve.

“Okay, let’s say the predator lives in the atmosphere. How high up?”

“What height is best, question? If you are predator, where do you go, question? You go to Astrophage.”

“Okay, so how high up are the Astrophage?” The question answers itself. “Ah! They breed at a certain height. Where there’s enough carbon dioxide for them to breed.”

“Yes!” He hurries back up his tunnel and stands above me. “We can find it. Easy. Use Petrovascope.”

I slam my fist into my palm. “Yes! Of course!”

Astrophage have to breed somewhere. Some level of carbon dioxide will be essential. But we don’t have to figure that out or make guesses. When an Astrophage divides, it and its offspring go back to Tau Ceti. And they use IR-light emission to do it. That means there will be a glow of Petrova-frequency light coming from all over the planet at that specific height.

“To the control room!” I say.

“Control room!” He hurries across the lab ceiling tunnel and disappears through his personal control-room entrance. I follow beside him, but I’m not quite as fast.

I climb up the ladder, take the pilot’s seat, and turn on the Petrovascope. Rocky is already in his bulb and points his camera at my main screen.

The entire screen glows red.

“What is this? No data.”

“Wait,” I say. I bring up the controls and options and start moving sliders. “We’re inside the Petrova line. There’s Astrophage all around us. Let me just change the setting to only show the brightest sources….”

It takes a lot of adjusting, but I finally get the brightness range set. What I’m left with are irregular, blotchy areas of IR light coming from Adrian.

“I think this is our answer,” I say.

Rocky leans closer to his grainy screen to "see" what I'm looking at.

"Not what I expected," I say.

I thought it would just be a simple layer of IR light at a certain height. But it’s not like that at all. The clumps are basically clouds. And they don’t line up with the thin white clouds I can see with regular light. These are, for lack of a better word, IR clouds.

Or, more accurately, clouds of Astrophage that are giving off IR light. For some reason, Astrophage multiply much more in some areas than others.

“Strange pattern,” says Rocky, agreeing with me.

“Yes. Maybe the weather affects how they multiply?”

“Maybe. Can you figure out the height, question?”

“Yes. Wait.”

I zoom and move the Petrovascope until I’m looking at an Astrophage cloud right on the edge of Adrian. The readouts show the camera’s current angle compared to the ship's axes. I write those angles down and switch to the navigation console. It tells me the ship's angle compared to the center of our orbit. With that information, and a lot of trigonometry, I can figure out the height of the Astrophage clouds.

“They’re multiplying at a height of 91.2 kilometers from the surface. The width is less than 200 meters.”

Rocky folds one of his claws over the other. I know that body language. He’s thinking. “If there are predators, they’re there.”

“Agreed,” I say. “But how do we get a sample?”

“How close can we orbit, question?”

“One hundred kilometers from the planet. Any closer and the ship will burn up in the atmosphere.”

“This is too bad,” Rocky says. “Eight point eight kilometers from where they breed. We can’t get closer, question?”

“If we hit the atmosphere at orbital speed, we die. But what if we slow down?”

“Slowing down means we can’t orbit. We fall into the air. We die.”

I lean over the armrest to look at him. “We can use the engines to keep from falling into the atmosphere. Just keep thrusting away from the planet. Lower ourselves into the atmosphere, get a sample, and then leave.”

“Won’t work. We’ll die.”

“Why won’t it work?”

Engines give off huge IR light. If used in air, air becomes ions. Explosion. Destroy ship.

I wince. “Right, of course.”

When Dimitri first tested a spin drive, it was only on for 100 microseconds, and it melted a metric ton of metallic silicon behind it. And that test drive was one-thousandth the power of the Hail Mary’s engines. Everything works fine when I’m in a vacuum. But using the engines in air would create a fireball that makes a nuclear bomb look like a firecracker.

We sit in frustrated silence for a while. The salvation of both our worlds might be just 10 kilometers below us, and we can’t get to it. There has to be a way. But how? We don’t even need to be there. We just need to get a sample of the air there. Anything, no matter how small.

Wait a minute.

“How do you make xenonite again? You mix two liquids?”

Rocky is caught off guard by the question, but he answers. “Yes. Have liquid and liquid. Mix. They become xenonite.”

“How much can you make? How much of those liquids did you bring?”

“I bring much. I use to make my zone.”

I bring up a spreadsheet and start typing in numbers. “We need 0.4 cubic meters of xenonite. Can you make that much?”

“Yes,” he says. “We need enough liquid to fill 0.61 cubic meters.”

“Okay. Then I have…an idea.” I press my fingertips together.

—

It’s a simple idea, but also dumb. But when dumb ideas work, they become genius ideas. We’ll see what happens with this one.

The Astrophage breeding grounds are 10 kilometers into Adrian’s atmosphere. I can’t fly the Hail Mary that low because the air is too thick and I’d burn up. I can’t use the engines in the atmosphere because that’ll cause a disaster and everything will blow up.

So, it’s time to go fishing. We’re going to make a 10-kilometer-long chain, put a sampling device on the end (Rocky will make that), and drag it through the atmosphere. Sounds easy, right?

Wrong.

The Hail Mary has to stay at 12.6 kilometers per second to stay in orbit. Any slower and we’ll fall and burn up. But if we drag a chain through the air at that speed—even a xenonite chain—it’ll be torn apart and vaporized.

So we have to go slower. But going slower means falling toward the planet. Unless I use the engines to keep us at the same height. But if I do that, I’d be blasting directly away from the chain and sample device. The engine exhaust will vaporize all of it.

So we’ll push at an angle. Simple as that.

It’ll look completely absurd. The Hail Mary will be angled 30 degrees from straight up, pushing upward at that angle. Below it, the chain will hang 10 kilometers straight down. The air behind the thrusters will constantly be on fire. It should be quite a sight. But it’ll be behind us, and the chain will be passing through normal air.

In the end, our sideways speed will be just over 100 meters per second. The chain can handle that speed in the thin high-altitude air, no problem. I figured it’ll only bend about 2 degrees from straight down.

Once we think we have a sample, we get out of there. What could possibly go wrong!

I’m being sarcastic.

I’m not the best at 3-D modeling, but I can make a chain link in CAD pretty well. It’s not a regular oval link, though. It’s mostly oval, but with a small opening for another link to fit into. Easy to put the links together, but very unlikely for them to come apart on their own. Especially when they’re pulled tight.

I grab a block of aluminum and put it in the milling machine.

“This will work, right?” Rocky asks from his ceiling tunnel.

“It should,” I say.

I start the milling machine, and it gets right to work. It drills out the mold for a chain link exactly as I’d planned.

I pull the piece out, dust off the aluminum bits, and hold it up to the tunnel. “How’s this?”

“Very good!” Rocky says. “We will need many many many chain links. More molds mean I can make more at once. You can make many molds, yes?”

“Well.” I look in the supply cabinet. “I don’t have much aluminum.”

“You have many things in ship you don’t use. Two beds in dormitory, for instance. Melt them, make blocks, make more molds.”

“Wow. You really go all out, don’t you?”

“No understand.”

“I’m not going to melt a bunch of stuff. How would I even do that?”

“Astrophage. Melt anything.”

“You got me there,” I say. “But no. The heat would be too much for my life support to handle. That reminds me. Why do you have so much extra Astrophage?”

He pauses. “Strange story.”

I perk up. Always ready for a strange story. He clicks along his tunnel and sits in a slightly wider spot. “Science Eridians do lots of math. Figure out trip. More fuel means faster trip. So we make lots and lots of Astrophage.”

“How’d you make so much? Earth had a very hard time making it.”

“Was easy. Put in metal balls with carbon dioxide. Put in ocean. Wait. Astrophage double, double, double. Lots of Astrophage.”

“Riiight. Because your oceans are hotter than Astrophage.”

“Yes. Earth oceans are not. Sad.”

When it comes to making Astrophage, Erid had a huge advantage. The whole planet is like a pressure cooker. Twenty-nine atmospheres at 210 degrees Celsius means water is liquid on the surface. And their oceans are far, far hotter than the temperature Astrophage needs. They just put Astrophage in the water, let it absorb heat, and grow.

I’m jealous. We had to cover the Sahara Desert to grow our Astrophage. All they had to do was throw it in the water. The stored heat energy of Erid’s oceans is amazing. A huge amount of water—much more than Earth’s total oceans—holding a temperature around 200 degrees Celsius or more. That’s a lot of energy.

And that’s why they can take a century or so to solve the problem while Earth is going to freeze in a few decades. It’s not just their air storing heat. Their oceans store even more. Huge advantage. Again.

“Science Eridians design ship and fuel needs. Journey to take 6.64 years.”

That confuses me for a moment. 40 Eridani is ten light-years away from Tau Ceti, so you can’t get from one to the other in less than ten years from Erid’s view. He must mean 6.64 years of time felt by his ship because of time slowing down.

“Strange things happen on trip. Crew sick. Die.” His voice lowers. “Now I know was radiation.”

I look down and give him a moment.

“Everyone sick. I alone to run ship. More strange things happen. Engines not work right. I am engine expert. I cannot figure out problem.”

“Your engines failed?”

“No. Not fail. Thrust normal. But speed…not increase. No can explain.”

“Huh.”

He clatters back and forth as he talks. “Then more strange: Reach halfway point earlier than should. Much earlier. I turn ship around. Thrust to slow down. But Tau get farther away. How? Still moving toward Tau but Tau moving away. Much confusion.”

“Uh-oh,” I say. A thought creeps into my head. A very disturbing thought.

“I speed up. Slow down. Much confuse. But get here. Even with all mistakes and confusion, I get here in three years. Half of time science Eridian say should be. So much confuse.”

“Oh…oh my…” I mumble.

“Much, much, much fuel left. Much more than needed. Not complaining. But confused.”

“Yeah…” I say. “Tell me this: Is time on Erid the same as time on your ship?”

He cocks his carapace. “Question makes no sense. Of course, time is the same. Time is the same everywhere.”

I put my head in my hands. “Oh boy.”

Eridians don’t know about relativistic physics.

They calculated their entire journey with Newtonian physics. They figured it all out by assuming they could just accelerate faster and faster and the speed of light wasn’t a problem.

They don’t know about time dilation. Rocky doesn’t realize that Erid experienced much more time than he did on that trip. They don’t know about length dilation. The distance to Tau Ceti will actually increase as you slow down relative to it—even if you’re still going toward it.

An entire planet of intelligent people put together a ship based on wrong scientific assumptions, and by some miracle, the sole survivor of the crew was clever enough at trial-and-error problem solving to actually get it to its destination.

And out of that major screw-up comes my salvation. They thought they’d need a whole lot more fuel. So Rocky has tons to spare.

“Okay, Rocky,” I say. “Get comfortable. I have a lot of science to explain.”

He knocked twice and peeked into my office. “Dr. Grace? Are you Dr. Grace?”

It wasn’t a large office, but any personal space was precious on an aircraft carrier. Before it became my office, the room was a storage locker for bathroom supplies. The crew had three thousand people who needed toilet paper daily. I got to use the room as my office until the next time we were in port, then they’d fill it up with more supplies.

I was about as important as toilet paper.

I looked up from my laptop. The short, slightly messy man at the door waved awkwardly.

“Yeah,” I said. “I’m Grace. You are…?”

“Hatch. Steve Hatch. University of British Columbia. Nice to meet you.”

I pointed to the folding chair in front of the folding table I used as a desk.

He shuffled in, carrying a round metal object. I’d never seen anything like it. He plopped it on my table.

I looked at the object. It was like someone had flattened a basketball, then stuck a triangle on one end and a four-sided shape on the other.

He sat in the chair and stretched his arms. “Man, that was weird. I’ve never been on a helicopter before. Have you? Well, of course you have. How else would you get here? I mean, I guess you could have used a boat, but probably not. I hear they keep the carrier far away from land in case something goes wrong during the Astrophage experiments. A boat would have been nicer, honestly, that helicopter ride almost made me puke. But I’m not complaining. I’m just happy to be here.”

“Um”—I pointed to the object on my desk—“what is this thing?”

He seemed to get even more excited. “Ah, right! That’s a beetle! Well, a practice version, anyway. My team and I think we’ve worked out most of the problems. Well, you never get all the problems worked out, but we’re ready to test the engines for real. And the university said we had to do that here on the carrier. Also the government of British Columbia said it. Oh, and the national government of Canada said it too. I’m Canadian, by the way. But don’t worry! I’m not one of those Canadians who hate America. I think you guys are all right.”

“Beetle?”

“Yeah!” He picked it up and turned the four-sided shape toward me. “This is how the Hail Mary crew will send us back the information. It’s a little spacecraft that can fly itself back to Earth from Tau Ceti. Well, from anywhere, really. That’s what my team and I have been working on for the past year.”

I peek into the four-sided shape and see a shiny, glasslike surface. “Is that a spin drive?” I asked.

“Sure is! Man, those Russians know their stuff. We just used their designs and everything worked out great. At least, I think it did. We haven’t tested the spin drive yet. The hard part is getting it to navigate and steer.”

He turned the device around and pointed the triangular head toward me. “This is where the cameras and computer are. No complicated navigation systems. It uses regular light to see the stars. It finds constellations and figures out where it is from that.” He tapped the middle of the rounded shell. “There’s a little DC generator in here. As long as we have Astrophage, we have power.”

“What can it carry?” I ask.

“Data. It’s got a backup system with more storage than anyone could use.” He knocked on the dome. It echoed slightly. “Most of this thing is fuel. It’ll need about 125 kilos of Astrophage to make the trip. Sounds like a lot but…man…twelve light-years!”

I lifted the device and held it in my hands a couple of times. “How does it turn?”

“Reaction wheels inside,” he said. “It spins them one way, the ship turns the other. Easy-peasy.”

“Interstellar navigation is ‘easy-peasy’?” I smiled.

He chuckled. “Well, for what we have to do, yeah. It has a receiver that’s always listening for a signal from Earth. When it hears that signal, it’ll send out its location and wait for instructions from the Deep Space Network. We don’t have to be super exact with the navigation. We just need it to show up within radio range of Earth. Anywhere within the orbit of Saturn or so will do.”

I nod. “And then scientists can tell it exactly how to get back. Clever.”

He shrugged. “They’ll probably do that, yeah. But they don’t have to. They’ll have it radio over all the data first thing. The information gets across. Then they can pick it up later if they want. Oh, and we’re making four of these. All we need is for one of them to survive the trip.”

I turned the beetle this way and that. It was surprisingly light. A few pounds at most. “Okay, so there are four of these. How likely is each one to survive the trip? Is there at least a little backup aboard?”

He shrugged. “Not much, no. But it doesn’t have to travel for nearly as long as the Hail Mary does. So things don’t have to last as long.”

“It’s going the same route, right?” I asked. “Why doesn’t it take the same time?”

“Because the Hail Mary can’t speed up as fast, since it has fragile humans inside. The beetle doesn’t have that problem. Everything on it is made of tough, military-grade parts that can handle a lot of force. So it gets to near-light speed much faster.”

“Oh, interesting…” I wondered if this would be a good question for my students. I quickly decided against it. The math was way too hard for any eighth grader.

“Yeah,” Hatch said. “They speed up at five hundred g’s until they’re cruising at 0.93 c. It’ll take over twelve years to get back to Earth, but for them, it’ll only feel like twenty months. Do you believe in God? Sorry for asking. I do. And I think He was pretty amazing to make relativity a thing, don’t you? The faster you go, the less time you feel. It’s like He’s asking us to explore the universe, you know?”

He stopped talking and looked at me.

“Well,” I said. “This is really amazing. Good job.”

“Thanks!” he said. “So can I have some Astrophage to test it?”

“Sure,” I said. “How much do you want?”

“How about a hundred milligrams?”

I hesitated. “Hold on. That’s a lot of energy.”

“Okay, okay. Can’t blame me for asking. How about one milligram?”

“Yeah, I can do that.”

He clapped. “Hell yeah! Astrophage headed my way!” He leaned towards me. “Isn’t it amazing? Astrophage, I mean? It’s like…the coolest thing ever! Again, God’s just giving us the future!”

“Cool?” I said. “It’s a disaster that could wipe us all out. If anything, God’s handing us the end of the world.”

He shrugged. “I mean, maybe a little. But man. Perfect for storing energy! Imagine a house powered by a battery. Like—you have a double-A battery, but full of Astrophage. That’d power your house for about a hundred thousand years. Imagine buying a car and never needing to charge it? The whole idea of power grids will disappear. And it’ll all be clean, green energy once we start growing the stuff on the moon or something. All it needs is sunlight!”

“Clean? Renewable?” I said. “Are you saying Astrophage will be… good for the environment? Because it won’t be. Even if Hail Mary finds a fix, we’re facing a mass extinction. Twenty years from now, many species on Earth will be gone. And we’re working hard to make sure humans aren’t one of them.”

He waved off my comment. “Earth’s had five mass extinctions before. And humans are smart. We’ll make it through.”

“We’ll starve!” I said. “Billions of people are going to starve.”

“Naaaah,” he said. “We’re already saving up food. We’ve got a lot of methane in the air to trap the sunlight. It’ll be all right. As long as Hail Mary works.”

I just stared at him for a moment. “You are, without a doubt, the most positive person I’ve ever met.”

He gave me a double thumbs-up. “Thanks!”

He picked up the beetle and turned to leave. “Come on, Pete, let’s get you some Astrophage!”

“Pete?” I asked.

He looked over his shoulder. “Yeah. I’m naming them after the Beatles. The British rock group.”

“You’re a fan, I guess?”

He turned back to face me. “Fan? Oh, yes. I don’t want to overstate it, but Sgt. Pepper’s Lonely Hearts Club Band is the best music ever made. I know, I know. Some people wouldn’t agree. But they’re wrong.”

“Fair enough,” I said. “But why Pete? Aren’t the Beatles named John, Paul, George, and Ringo?”

“Right. And that’s what we’ll call the ones on the Hail Mary. But this guy is for testing near Earth. I get a whole SpaceX launch just for me! Isn’t that amazing! Anyway, I named him after Pete Best—he played drums for the Beatles before Ringo.”

“Okay, I didn’t know that,” I said.

“Now you do. I’m gonna get that Astrophage now. I’ve got to make sure these beetles will be able to…‘Get Back . ’ ”

“Okay.”

He frowned. “ ‘Get Back.’ It’s a song. By the Beatles.”

“Sure. Okay.”

He turned and left. “Some people just don’t appreciate the classics.”

I was left confused. Pretty sure I wasn’t the first.
