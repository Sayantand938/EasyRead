Project Hail Mary

Chapter 7

A flood of thoughts hit me all at once: We’re not alone. This is an alien. That ship is weird, how does that work? Do they live here? Is this their star? Am I starting an interplanetary problem by wandering into alien territory?!

“Breathe,” I tell myself.

Okay, one thing at a time. What if this is another ship from Earth? One I don’t remember? Heck, it took me a few days to remember my name. Maybe Earth sent multiple ships with different designs? Like, for backup or to make it more likely that at least one of them works. Maybe that ship is the Praise Allah or the Blessings of Vishnu or something.

I look all around the control room. There are screens and controls for everything, but there’s nothing for a radio. The EVA panel has some radio controls, but that’s just for talking to crewmates when they’re outside.

If they’d sent multiple ships, surely they would have had some radio system so we could talk to each other.

Also, that ship…it’s crazy.

I go through the navigation console screens until I find the Radar panel. I’d noticed it earlier, but didn’t think much of it. I assume it’s there so I can get near asteroids or other objects and not hit them.

After a few tries, I manage to turn it on. It immediately spots the other ship and sounds an alarm. The loud noise hurts my ears.

“Whoa, whoa, whoa!” I say. I quickly look around the panel until I see a button labeled “Mute Proximity Alert.” I press it and the noise stops.

I scan the rest of the screen. There's a lot of data here, all in a window called "BLIP-A." I guess if there were multiple contacts, I'd get multiple windows. Whatever. It's all just raw numbers about the reading. Nothing cool like a 3D Star Trek scan or anything.

"Velocity" is zero. They have matched my velocity exactly. That can't be an accident.

"Range" is 217 meters. I'm guessing that's the distance to the closest part of the other ship. Or maybe the average. No, it would be the closest part. The point of this system is probably to avoid crashes.

Speaking of crashes—217 meters is a ridiculously small distance compared to the size of a solar system. There's no way this is an accident. That ship put itself here on purpose because I'm here.

Another reading, "Angular width," is 35.44 degrees. Okay, some basic math should handle this.

I bring up the Utility panel on the main screen and open the calculator app. Something 217 meters away is taking up 35.44 degrees of the view. Assuming the radar can see in all 360 degrees (it would be a pretty bad radar if it couldn't)...I type some numbers into the calculator to do an ARCTAN operation, and:

The ship is 139 meters long. Roughly.

I bring the Astrophage panel up on another screen. The little map there shows that the Hail Mary is just 47 meters long. So yeah. The alien ship is three times the size of mine. There's just no way Earth sent something that big.

And the shape. What is up with that shape? I turn my attention back to the Petrovascope (which is now just acting as a camera).

The center of the ship is diamond-shaped—a rhombus. Well, I guess it's an octahedron, really. Looks like it has eight faces, each triangular. That part alone is about the size of my ship.

The diamond is linked by three thick rods (I'm not sure what else to call them) to a wide, trapezoid-shaped base. That seems like it could be the back. And in front of the diamond is a thin stalk (I'm just making up names now) with four flat panels attached parallel to the main ship's direction. Maybe they're solar panels? The stalk goes on to a pyramid-shaped nose. A nose pyramid, I suppose.

Every part of the hull is flat. Even the "rods" have flat sides.

Why would anyone do that? Flat panels are a bad idea. I don't know who made this, but I assume they need some kind of air inside, right? Huge, flat panels are terrible for that.

Maybe this is just a probe and not a real ship. Maybe there's no air inside because there's nothing alive inside. I might be looking at an alien object instead of a ship.

Still, this is the most exciting moment in human history.

So it runs on Astrophage. That was the steady Petrova-frequency light I saw before. It's interesting that they use the same propulsion as we do. But since it's the best way to store energy, it's not surprising. When European sailors first met Asian sailors, no one was surprised they both used sails.

But the "why" is what I wonder about. Something on board (either a computer or a crew) decided to come to my ship. How did they even know I was here?

I guess they saw me the same way I saw them: the huge IR light from my engines. And since the back of my ship was facing Tau Ceti, I was shining a 540-trillion-watt flashlight at them. Depending on where they were, I might have looked even brighter than Tau Ceti itself, at least in the Petrova frequency.

So they can see the Petrova frequency. And so can I.

I go through the Spin Drive console screens until I find one called "Manual Control." When I click it, a warning box appears:

MANUAL CONTROL IS RECOMMENDED ONLY FOR EMERGENCIES. ARE YOU SURE YOU WANT TO ENTER MANUAL CONTROL MODE?

I tap “Yes.”

It brings up another box.

SECOND CONFIRMATION: TYPE “Y-E-S” TO ENTER MANUAL CONTROL MODE.

I groan and type Y-E-S.

The panel finally takes me to the Manual Control screen. It’s a bit scary. Not because it’s complex, but because it’s so simple.

There are three sliders labeled “Drive 1,” “Drive 2,” and “Drive 3,” each presently at zero. The top of each slider is labeled “107 N.” The N must mean “Newtons”—a unit of force. I guess if I put all three drives to maximum, it would give me 30 million Newtons. That’s about sixty times the thrust a jumbo jet’s engines produce during takeoff.

Science teachers know a lot of random facts.

There are a bunch more little sliders. In groups labeled “Yaw,” “Pitch,” and “Roll.” There must be little spin drives on the sides of the ship to adjust its direction. I can definitely see why it’s a bad idea to mess with this panel. One mistake and I’ll put the ship into a spin that tears it apart.

But at least they thought of that. There’s a button in the middle of the screen labeled “Zero All Rotation.” Good.

I check the Petrovascope again. Blip-A hasn’t moved. It’s on my left, a little bit ahead.

I switch the Petrovascope back to Petrova-frequency mode, and the screen turns mostly black. Like before, I can see the Petrova line in the background, blocked by Blip-A.

“Let’s see if you have anything to say…” I mutter. Spin drive 2 is in the middle of the ship. Its thrust will be straight ahead and hopefully won’t change my direction. We’ll see.

I set it to 0.1% power for one second, then back to 0.

Even with just one engine, at one one-thousandth power, for one second, the ship drifts a bit. The “Velocity” value for Blip-A on the Radar panel shows 0.086 m/s. That tiny thrust moved my ship about 8 centimeters per second.

But I don’t care about that. I care about the other ship.

I watch the Petrovascope. A drop of sweat falls from my forehead and floats away. I feel like my heart is going to jump out of my chest.

Then, the back of the ship lights up in the Petrova frequency for one second. Just like I did.

“Wow!”

I flick the drive on and off several times: three short bursts, a long one, and one more short one. There’s no message there. I just want to see what they do with it.

They were more ready this time. In seconds, the other ship repeats the pattern.

I gasp. And I smile. Then I wince. Then I smile again. This is a lot to process.

That was too fast for any probe to react. If it had remote control, the controllers would have to be at least a few light-minutes away—there’s just nothing around here that could be hiding them.

There is an intelligent life-form on that ship. I am about 200 meters from a real alien!

I mean…my ship is powered by aliens. But this new one is intelligent!

Oh my gosh! This is it! First Contact! I’m the one! I’m the one who meets aliens for the first time!

The Blip-A (that’s what I’m calling their ship for now) briefly starts its engines again. I watch closely to memorize the sequence, but it’s just a single dim light. They’re not signaling. They’re maneuvering.

I check the Radar panel. As expected, the Blip-A moves alongside the Hail Mary and holds position at 217 meters.

I switch through the Scientific panel to bring up the normal telescopic cameras again. The Petrovascope’s normal-light camera is just to orient things for the main scope itself. The telescope has much better resolution and clarity. I guess I’m too excited to think straight because it took me until now to think of it.

The image is much clearer through the main telescope. I guess it’s just an incredibly high-resolution camera, because I can still zoom in and out with no loss of clarity. I have a very good view of the Blip-A now.

The ship’s hull is a mix of gray and tan. The pattern looks random and smooth, like someone started mixing paint but stopped too soon.

I see movement in the corner of the screen. An odd-shaped thing slides along a track in the hull. It’s a stalk sticking up with five jointed “arms” coming out of the top. Each arm has a clamp-like “hand” on the end.

Only now do I see a network of tracks all over the hull.

It’s a robot. Something controlled from inside. At least, I think so. It doesn’t look like a little green man, and it definitely doesn’t look like an alien spacesuit.

Not that I know what either of those things would look like.

Yeah, I’m pretty sure it’s a hull-mounted robot. Space stations back on Earth have them. They’re a good way to do things outside your ship without having to put on a suit.

The robot moves along the hull until it gets to the spot closest to the Hail Mary. One of its little clamp hands holds a cylinder. I don’t really know how big it is, but the robot is tiny compared to the ship. I think it’s about my size or maybe smaller, but that’s just a guess.

The robot stops, reaches toward my ship, and gently lets go of the cylinder into space.

The cylinder moves slowly toward me. It’s rotating slightly, end-over-end. Not perfectly, but still very smooth.

I check the Radar panel. The Blip-A is stopped. And there’s a “Blip-B” screen now. It shows the much smaller cylinder coming closer at 8.6 centimeters per second.

Interesting. That’s the exact same speed I used for the Hail Mary a little while ago when I flashed the engine as a greeting. That can’t be an accident. They want me to have that cylinder, and they want to send it to me at a speed they know I’m used to.

“Very thoughtful of you…” I say.

These aliens are smart.

I have to think they mean well at this point. I mean, they’re really trying to say hi and be helpful. Besides, if they don't mean well, what could I do about it? Die. That’s what I’d do. I’m a scientist, not Buck Rogers.

Well, I guess I could aim the spin drives at their ship and fire them at full power, which would vaporize—you know what? I’m just not going to think about that right now.

Some quick math tells me the cylinder will take over forty minutes to get to me. I have that long to get into an EVA suit, go outside, and get ready on the hull for humanity’s first touchdown catch with an alien quarterback.

I learned a lot about the airlock when I was giving my crewmates a burial in space and—

Ilyukhina would have loved this. She would have been jumping up and down with excitement. Yáo would have been calm and steady, but he would have smiled when he thought we weren’t looking.

The tears blur my vision. With no gravity, they cover my eyes. It’s like trying to see underwater. I wipe them off and throw them across the control room. They splatter on the opposite wall. I don’t have time for this. I have an alien thingy to catch.

I unhook my belt and float over to the airlock. My mind is racing with ideas and questions. And I’m jumping to crazy, unsupported conclusions. Maybe this smart alien species invented Astrophage. Maybe they genetically engineered it to “grow” spaceship fuel. The ultimate solar power. Maybe once I explain what’s happening to Earth, they’ll have a solution.

Or maybe they’ll get on my ship and lay eggs in my brain. You never know.

I open the inner airlock door and pull out the EVA suit. Do I even know how to get into this thing? Or how to use it safely?

I unlock the Orlan-MKS2 EVA suit and open the back hatch. I turn on the main power with a switch on the belt. The suit starts up right away, and the panel on the chest says ALL SYSTEMS FUNCTIONAL—what the heck? I know exactly what to do.

We probably trained with this a lot. I know it like I know physics. It’s in my head, but I don’t remember learning it.

The Russian suit is one sealed unit. Unlike American suits where you put on the top and bottom, then deal with the helmet and gloves, the Orlan is like a onesie with a hatch in the back. You step in, close the hatch, and you’re done. It’s like an insect shedding its skin in reverse.

I open the back and wiggle into the suit. Zero gravity really helps here. I don’t have to struggle with the suit as much as I usually would. Weird. I know this is easier than before, but I don’t remember doing it before. I think I have brain damage from that coma.

I’m good enough for now. I keep going.

I get my arms and legs in the right spots. The jumpsuit is uncomfortable in the Orlan. I’m supposed to wear special underwear. I even know what it looks like, but it’s just for temperature and monitoring. I don’t have time to find it in storage. I have a date with a cylinder.

Now in the suit, I push against the airlock wall with my legs to close the back flap. When it’s close enough, a light turns green on the chest panel. I reach up with my thick gloves and press the Autoseal button.

The suit closes the opening with loud clicks. With a final “clunk,” the outer seal locks. My status board is green, and I have seven hours of air. The pressure inside is 400 hectopascals—about 40 percent of Earth’s air at sea level. That’s normal for spacesuits.

The whole process took only five minutes. I’m ready to go outside.

Interesting. I didn’t have to go through a decompression step. On space stations back home, astronauts have to spend hours in an airlock slowly getting used to the low pressure needed for the EVA suit before they can go out. I don’t have that problem. Apparently, the entire Hail Mary is at that 40 percent pressure.

Good design. The only reason space stations around Earth have a full atmosphere of pressure is in case the astronauts have to abort and return to Earth quickly. But for the Hail Mary crew…where would we go? Might as well use the low pressure all the time. It makes things easier on the hull and lets you do quick EVAs.

I take a deep breath and let it out. A soft whir comes from somewhere behind me and cool air flows along my back and shoulders. Air conditioning. It feels nice.

I grab a handhold and spin myself around. I pull the inner airlock door closed and then turn the main lever to start the cycling sequence. A pump starts up. It’s louder than I would have thought. It sounds like a motorcycle idling. I keep my hand on the lever. Pushing it back to the original position will stop the cycle and repressurize. If I see even a hint of a red light on my suit panel, I’m going to throw that lever so fast it’ll make my head spin.

After a minute, the pump grows quieter. Then quieter still. It’s probably as loud as it ever was. But with the air leaving the chamber, there’s no way for the noise to get to me other than through my feet touching the Velcro pads on the floor.

Finally, the pump stops. I’m in total silence aside from the fans inside the suit. The airlock controls show that the pressure inside is zero, and a yellow light turns green. I’m clear to open the outer door.

I grab the hatch crank, then hesitate.

“What am I doing?” I say.

Is this really a good idea? I want that cylinder so badly I’m just moving ahead without any sort of plan. Is this worth risking my life over?

Yes. Absolutely.

Okay, but is it worth risking everyone on Earth? If I mess up and die, the whole Hail Mary Project will be pointless.

Hmm.

Yes. It’s still worth it. I don’t know what these aliens are like, what they want, or what they’re planning. But they will have information. Any information, even stuff I’d rather not know, is better than nothing.

I spin the handle and open the door. Empty black space is outside. The light of Tau Ceti shines on the door. I peek out and see Tau Ceti myself. From here, it’s a little less bright than the sun on Earth.

I double-check my tether to be sure I’m attached, then I step into space.

—

I’m good at this.

I must have practiced a lot. Maybe in a water tank or something. But it feels natural to me.

I leave the airlock and clip one of my tethers to a rail on the outer hull. Always have two tethers. And always have at least one attached. That way you're never in danger of floating away from the ship. The Orlan-MKS2 is probably the best EVA suit ever, but it doesn't have a SAFER unit like NASA's EMU suit. At least with a SAFER unit you have a little thrust to get back to the ship if you drift away.

All that info rushes into my head at once. I guess I’ve spent a lot of time thinking about spacesuits. Maybe I’m the crew’s EVA expert? I don’t know.

I flip up the sun visor and look toward the Blip-A. I wish I could learn something special by seeing it up close, but it’s pretty far away. The Hail Mary’s telescope gave me a much better view. Still, there’s something…different about staring right at an alien spacecraft.

I see a flash of the cylinder. Now and then the flat ends of the slowly spinning cylinder reflect Taulight.

I’ve decided “Taulight” is a word, by the way. Light from Tau Ceti. It’s not “sunlight.” Tau Ceti isn’t the sun. So…Taulight.

I still have a good twenty minutes before the cylinder gets to the ship. I watch it for a while to guess where it’ll hit. It’d be nice to have a crewmate inside at the radar station.

It’d be nice to have a crewmate at all.

After five minutes, I have a good idea where the cylinder will hit. It’s headed for about the middle of the ship. That’s as good a place as any for aliens to aim for.

I move across the hull. The Hail Mary is pretty big. My little pressurized area is only half its length and the back half widens out to be three times as wide. Most of that will be empty now, I guess. It used to be full of Astrophage for my one-way trip here.

The hull has rails and latch points all over for EVA tethers. Tether by tether, rail by rail, I make my way toward the middle of the ship.

I have to step over a thick ring. It circles the crew area of the ship. It’s a good 2 feet thick. I don’t know what it is, but it must be pretty heavy. Weight is everything when designing spaceships, so it must be important. I’ll guess about that later.

I continue along, one hull latch at a time, until I’m roughly in the center of the hull. The cylinder gets closer. I adjust my position a bit to keep up with it. After a painfully long wait, it’s almost within reach.

I wait. No need to rush. If I grab at it too early, I might knock it off course and into space. I’d have no way to get it back. I don’t want to look dumb in front of the aliens.

Because they’re surely watching me right now. Probably counting my arms and legs, noting my size, figuring out what part they should eat first, whatever.

I let the cylinder get closer and closer. It’s moving less than 1 mile per hour. Not exactly a fast pass.

Now that it’s so close, I can guess its size. It’s not big at all. About the size and shape of a coffee can. It’s a dull gray color with spots of slightly darker gray randomly here and there. Similar to the Blip-A ’s hull, kind of. Different color but same spots. Maybe it’s a style thing. Random spots are “in” this season or something.

The cylinder floats into my arms and I grab it with both hands.

It weighs less than I expected. It’s probably hollow. It’s a container. There’s something inside they want me to see.

I hold the cylinder under one arm and use the other to deal with tethers. I hurry back to the airlock. It’s a stupid thing to do. There’s no reason to hurry and it literally puts my life in danger. One slip-up and I’d be lost in space. But I just can’t wait.

I get back into the ship, cycle the airlock, and float into the control room with my prize in hand. I open the Orlan suit, already thinking about what tests I’ll run on the cylinder. I have a whole lab to work with!

The smell hits me right away. I gasp and cough. The cylinder is bad!

No, not bad. But it smells bad. I can hardly breathe. The chemical smell is familiar. What is it? Cat pee?

Ammonia. It’s ammonia.

“Okay,” I wheeze. “Okay. Think.”

My first thought is to close the suit again. But that would just trap me in a small space with the ammonia that’s already in here. Better to let the cylinder air out in the bigger space of the ship.

Ammonia isn’t poisonous—at least, not in small amounts. And the fact that I can still breathe at all tells me it’s a small amount. If it weren’t, my lungs would have chemical burns and I’d be out cold or dead now.

As it is, there’s just a bad smell. I can handle a bad smell.

I climb out the back of the suit while the cylinder floats in the middle of the console room. Now that it’s not a surprise anymore, I can handle the ammonia. It’s no worse than using a lot of Windex in a small room. Unpleasant but not dangerous.

I grab the cylinder—and it’s super hot!

I yelp and pull my hands away. I blow on them for a moment and check for burns. It wasn’t too bad. Not stovetop hot. But hot.

Grabbing it with my bare hands was dumb. Bad thinking. I figured that since I’d held it before, it was safe to do now. But earlier I had thick spacesuit gloves on.

“You’ve been a bad alien cylinder,” I tell it. “You need a time-out.”

I pull my arm back into my sleeve and wrap my hand in the cuff. I use my protected knuckles to push the cylinder into the airlock. Once it’s in, I shut the door.

I’ll leave it alone for now. It’ll cool down to the room temperature eventually. And while it does, I don’t want it floating around my ship. I don’t think there’s anything in the airlock that can be damaged by some heat.

How hot was it?

Well, I had both hands on it (like an idiot) for a split second. My own reaction time was enough to keep me from getting burned. So it’s probably less than 100 degrees Celsius.

I open and close my hands a few times. They don’t hurt anymore, but I still remember the pain.

“Where’d the heat come from?” I mumbled.

The cylinder was out in space for a good forty minutes. It should have lost heat through radiation. It should be cold , not hot. I’m about 1 AU from Tau Ceti, and Tau Ceti has half the brightness of the sun. So I don’t think the Taulight could have heated the cylinder up much. Definitely not more than radiation would cool it down.

So either it has a heater inside or it was very hot when it started. I guess I’ll find out soon. It’s not very heavy, so it’s probably thin. If there’s no internal heat, it’ll cool off very fast in the air here.

The room still smells like ammonia. Ugh.

I float down to the lab. I don’t know where to start. So many things I want to do. Maybe I should begin by figuring out what the cylinder is made of? Something safe for the Blip-A ’s crew might be very poisonous to me, and neither of us would know.

Maybe I should check for radiation.

I drift to the lab table and reach out to steady myself. I’m getting better at dealing with zero-g. I think I remember an astronaut documentary saying some people are fine with it, while others really struggle. Looks like I’m one of the fortunate ones.

I’m using “fortunate” loosely here. I’m on a suicide mission. So…yeah.

The lab is a puzzle. It has been for a while. It’s clearly designed as if there will be gravity. It has tables, chairs, test-tube trays, and so on. There’s none of the usual stuff you’d expect in a weightless place. No Velcro on the walls, no computer screens at all angles. No smart use of space. Everything assumes there will be a “floor.”

The ship can speed up just fine. For a long time, too. It kept me at 1.5 g’s for probably a few years. But they can’t expect me to just keep the engines on and fly in circles to keep gravity in the lab, right?

I look around at each piece of lab equipment and try to clear my mind. There has to be a reason for this. And it’s in my memory somewhere. The trick is to think about what I want to know, but not worry about it too much. It’s like falling asleep. You can’t really do it if you focus on it too hard.

So many high-quality pieces of equipment. I let my mind wander as I look across them all….
