Project Hail Mary

Chapter 6

Okay, if I’m going to die, it has to matter. I need to figure out how to stop Astrophage. Then I’ll send my findings to Earth. And then…I’ll die. There are many ways to die without pain here—from taking too many pills to lowering the oxygen until I just fall asleep and die.

Nice thought.

I eat a tasty tube of “Day 4—Meal 2.” I think it tastes like beef. The food is getting thicker now. There are actually some solid pieces in it. I think I’m chewing on a small piece of carrot. It’s nice to feel some texture in the food for a change.

“More water!” I say.

The NannyBot (as I now call it) quickly takes my plastic cup and gives me a full one. It’s funny. Three days ago, those arms on the ceiling were a scary mechanical monster. Now they’re just…there. Part of life.

I like to think in the dormitory. Now that the dead bodies are gone, anyway. The lab doesn’t have anywhere comfortable to relax. The control room has a nice chair, but it’s small and has lights flashing everywhere. But the dormitory has my nice, comfortable bed where I can lie back and think about what to do next. Also, the bedroom is where all the food comes from.

I’ve remembered a lot over the past few days. It seems Project Hail Mary worked, because I’m here, in another star system. I guess it’s Tau Ceti. It makes sense that I thought it was the sun. Tau Ceti is a lot like the sun as stars go. Same type, color, and so on.

And I know why I’m here! Not just in general terms like “Hey, the world’s ending. Stop that.” But very specifically: Find out why Astrophage didn’t affect Tau Ceti.

Easy to say. Hard to do. I hope I remember more details later.

A million questions run through my mind. Some of the most important are:

  1. How do I search an entire solar system for information about Astrophage?
  2. What am I supposed to do? Throw some of my Astrophage fuel at Tau Ceti to see what happens?
  3. How do I steer this ship anyway?
  4. If I do find useful information, how do I tell Earth about it? I think that’s what the beetles are for, but how do I upload data to them? How do I aim them? How do I launch them?
  5. Why would I, of all people, be part of this mission? Yes, I figured out a bunch of stuff about Astrophage, but so what? I’m a lab guy, not an astronaut. It’s not like they sent Wernher von Braun into space. Surely there were people better suited for this.

I decide to start small. First I have to figure out what this ship can do and how to control it. They put the crew in comas. They must have known it might mess with our minds. There has to be an instruction manual somewhere.

“Flight manual,” I say out loud.

“Ship information can be found in the control room,” says the NannyBot.

“Where?”

“Ship information can be found in the control room.”

“No. Where in the control room can ship information be found?”

“Ship information can be found in the control room.”

“You kind of suck,” I say.

I head up to the control room and really examine every screen. I spend an hour noting what each area seems to show and guessing what the functions are. I’m really hoping to find something like “Information” or “Here to save humanity? Press this button to learn more!”

No such luck. After hours of messing with screens, I’ve found nothing. I guess they figured if the crew is so brain-dead that they don’t remember how to use the ship, they’re probably not useful as scientists anyway.

I did find out that any screen can show any instrument panel. They’re pretty much the same. Just tap the upper-left corner, and a menu appears. Pick whatever panel you want.

That’s nice. You can change what you’re looking at. And the screen right in front of the pilot’s seat is the biggest.

I decide to try a more hands-on approach: I’m gonna start pushing buttons!

Hopefully there’s no “Blow Up the Ship” button. I think Stratt would have stopped that from happening.

Stratt. I wonder what she’s doing right now. Probably in a control room somewhere with the pope making her a cup of coffee. She was (is?) a really bossy person. But gosh darn it, I’m glad she was in charge of making this ship happen. Now that I’m aboard it and all. Her attention to detail and insistence on perfection are nice to have all around me.

Anyway, I bring up the “Scientific Instrumentation” panel on the main screen. It’s the same panel I spent a lot of time with earlier—the one that currently shows an image of Tau Ceti. It has the word “Helioscope” in the upper-left corner. I hadn’t noticed that before. The left side of the screen has a bunch of icons. Other equipment, I assume. I press one at random.

Tau Ceti disappears. The top-left corner changes to read “External Collection Unit.” The screen shows a diagram of a plain rectangle. There are some controls here and there to change the angle and to “open bow side” and “open stern side.” Okay. Noted. Not sure what to do with that information. I press another icon at random.

This time it changes to “Petrovascope.” Beyond that, there’s just a black screen with an error message: PETROVASCOPE CANNOT BE USED WHILE SPIN DRIVE IS ACTIVE.

“Hmph,” I say.

Okay, what’s a Petrovascope? Best guess: a telescope and/or camera that looks specifically for the IR light that Astrophage emit. It looks for the Petrova line via the Petrova wavelength so it’s a Petrovascope and we really need to stop putting “Petrova” in front of everything.

Why can’t I use it when the spin drive is active?

I don’t how a spin drive works, or why it’s called a spin drive, but I do know I have one in the back of the ship and it’s consuming Astrophage as fuel. So it’s my engine. It probably activates enriched Astrophage to use them as thrust.

Ah…that would mean there’s a ridiculous amount of IR light coming out the back of the ship right now. Like…enough to vaporize a battleship or something. I’d have to do the math to know for sure but—I can’t help it, I want to do the math right now.

The engines consume 6 grams of Astrophage per second. Astrophage stores energy as mass. So basically, the spin drive converts 6 grams of mass into pure energy every second and spits it out the back. Well, it’s the Astrophage doing the work, but whatever.

I open the “Utility” panel on a smaller screen to my right. It has a bunch of familiar apps, all ready to go. One of them is a calculator. I use it to calculate the energy from converting that 6 grams…good Lord. It’s 540 trillion Joules. And the ship is sending out that much energy every second. So it’s 540 trillion watts. I can’t even imagine that much energy. It’s way more than the surface of the sun. Seriously. Like…you’d get less energy if you were on the surface of the sun than if you were standing behind the Hail Mary at full power.

I’m slowing down now. I have to be. The plan is to stop in the Tau Ceti system. So I’m probably pointed away from the star and slowing down—after spending a really long time at almost light speed during the trip.

Okay, so all that light energy will hit dust particles, ions, and anything else between me and Tau Ceti as I move along. Those poor little particles will be completely vaporized. And that’ll send some IR light back at the ship. Not much compared to the engine's power, but it would blind the Petrovascope, which is carefully designed to look for tiny amounts of that exact frequency.

So I can’t use the Petrovascope with the engine on.

But man. I would love to know if Tau Ceti has a Petrova line.

In theory, any star infected with Astrophage should have one, right? The little pests need carbon dioxide to breed. They can’t get that from the star (unless you go way into the core, and I don’t know if even Astrophage could survive those temperatures).

If I see a Petrova line, it means that Tau Ceti has an active Astrophage population that, for some reason, hasn’t grown out of control like it has everywhere else. And that line will lead to a planet that has carbon dioxide. Maybe there’s some other chemical in that atmosphere that stops the Astrophage? Maybe the planet has a strange magnetic field that messes with their ability to navigate? Maybe the planet has a bunch of moons that the Astrophage physically crashes into?

Maybe Tau Ceti just doesn’t have any planets with carbon dioxide in their atmospheres. That would be awful. It would mean this whole trip was pointless and Earth is doomed.

I could guess all day. Without data, it’s just a shot in the dark. And without the Petrovascope, I don’t have data. At least, not the data I want.

I turn my attention to the Navigation screen. Should I mess with it? I mean—I don’t know how to fly this ship. The ship does, but I don’t. If I push the wrong button, I’ll be dead in space.

Actually, it would be worse. I’d be speeding toward Tau Ceti at—I check the screen—7,595 kilometers per second. Wow! A few days ago, it was over 11,000. That’s what constantly speeding up at 1.5 g’s does. Or "slowing down," I guess. It’s the same in physics. The point is, I’m slowing down compared to the star.

There’s a button on the screen that says “Course.” Seems okay to tap, right? Famous last words. I should just wait for the computer to think the trip is done. But I can’t help it.

I tap the button. The screen shows the Tau Ceti solar system. Tau Ceti is in the center, shown with the Greek letter tau.

Ohhhh…that’s what the lowercase t is on the Hail Mary symbol. It’s a tau, for “Tau Ceti.” Okay.

Anyway, four planet paths are shown as thin white lines around the star. The planets are shown as circles with error bars. We don’t have perfect info on exoplanets. If I could get the science tools working, I could get better info on where the planets are. I’m twelve light-years closer to them than astronomers on Earth.

A yellow line goes almost straight into the system from off-screen. It curves toward the star between the third and fourth planets and into a circle. There’s a yellow triangle on the line, far from the four planets. That’s me, I think. And the yellow line is my path. Above the map is the text:

TIME TO ENGINE CUTOFF: 0005:20:39:06

The last number counts down every second. Okay, I learned a few things. First, I have about five days left (almost six) before the engine stops. Second, the timer shows days with four numbers. That means this trip took at least one thousand days. Over three years. Well, it takes light twelve years to get here, so it should take me a long time too.

Oh, right. Relativity.

I don’t know how long it took. Or, I don’t know how long it felt for me. When you go close to the speed of light, time changes for you. More time will have passed on Earth than I felt since I left Earth.

Relativity is strange.

Time is crucial. And sadly, while I slept, Earth aged at least thirteen years. Even if I solve the Astrophage problem now, it will take at least thirteen years for that news to reach Earth. So, Earth will suffer at least twenty-six years of Astrophage damage. I hope they are finding ways to cope or at least lessen the harm. They wouldn't have sent the Hail Mary if they didn't think they could last at least twenty-six years, right?

Anyway, the trip took at least three years (from my view). Is that why we were put in comas? Was it a problem for us to be awake the whole time?

I only feel the tears when one falls off my face. The decision to put us in comas killed two close friends. They're gone. I don't recall a single moment with them, but the loss is huge. I'll join them soon. There's no way home. I'll die out here too. But unlike them, I'll die alone.

I wipe my eyes and try to think of other things. My whole species is at stake.

Based on the map, the ship will automatically put me in a stable orbit around Tau Ceti, between the third and fourth planets. I'd guess that's about 1 AU, the same distance Earth is from the sun. A nice, safe distance from the star. A slow orbit that takes about a year. Maybe longer, because Tau Ceti is smaller than the sun, so it has less mass. Less mass means less gravity and a slower orbit at the same distance.

Okay, I have five days to wait until the engine stops. I'll just wait it out instead of messing around. Once the engines are off, I’ll use the Petrovascope to see what's out there. Until then, I’ll learn as much about the ship as I can.

I’ll do anything to avoid thinking about Yáo and Ilyukhina.

Officially, the ship was called the People’s Liberation Army Navy Gansu. Why their navy includes "Army" in its name is beyond me. But people stopped using that name and started calling it Stratt’s Vat. Despite the sailors' protests, the name remained. We sailed around the South China Sea, always keeping a distance from land.

I had spent a wonderful week focused solely on science.

No meetings. No interruptions. Just experiments and building. I had forgotten how enjoyable it was to become completely absorbed in a project.

My first breeder prototype had successfully completed another test. It wasn’t much to look at—mostly a 30-foot metal pipe with various pieces of control equipment attached randomly. But it worked. It could only produce a few micrograms of Astrophage per hour, but the idea was sound.

I had a team of twelve people—engineers from different countries. Two Mongolian brothers were my best engineers. When Stratt called me to meet her in the conference room, I left them in charge.

I found her alone in the meeting room. The table was covered with papers and charts, as usual. Graphs and diagrams were displayed on all the walls—some new, some old.

Stratt was sitting at one end of the long table, with a bottle of Dutch gin and a short glass. I had never seen her drink before.

“You wanted to see me?” I asked.

She looked up. Her eyes were puffy. She hadn’t slept. “Yes. Sit down.”

I sat in the chair next to her. “You look awful. What’s happening?”

“I have to make a decision. And it’s not easy.”

“How can I help?”

She offered me the gin. I shook my head. She filled her own glass. “The Hail Mary will have a very small crew area—about 125 cubic meters.”

I tilted my head. “That’s actually pretty big for spaceships, right?”

She waved her hand back and forth. “Big for a capsule like Soyuz or Orion. But tiny for a space station. It’s about one-tenth the size of the International Space Station’s crew area.”

“Okay,” I said. “What’s the problem?”

“The problem”—she grabbed a manila folder and dropped it in front of me—“is that the crew will kill each other.”

“Huh?” I opened the folder. Inside were many typed pages. Actually, they were scans of typed pages. Some were in English, some in Russian. “What is all this?”

“During the Space Race, the Soviets briefly wanted to go to Mars. They thought if they put people on Mars, the U.S. moon landing would seem unimportant.”

I closed the folder. The Cyrillic writing meant nothing to me. But I guessed Stratt could read it. She always seemed to know whatever language was being spoken.

She rested her chin on her hands. “Getting to Mars with 1970s tech means using a Hohmann transfer trajectory, so the crew would spend over eight months on a ship. The Soviets tested what happens when people are in a small, isolated space for months.”

“And?”

“After seventy-one days, the men were fighting daily. They stopped the test on day ninety-four because one person tried to stab another with broken glass.”

“How big will the crew be?”

“The current plan is three,” she said.

“Okay,” I said. “So you’re worried about what happens when we send three astronauts on a four-year trip in a 125-cubic-meter space?”

“It’s not just if they get along. Each person will know they’re going to die in a few years. And that the ship is the only world they’ll know for their short lives. The psychiatrists I spoke to think crushing depression is likely. And suicide is a real risk.”

“Yeah, that’s heavy,” I said. “But what else can we do?”

She picked up a stack of papers and slid it to me. I picked it up and read the title: “A Study of Long-Term Primate and Human Coma Patients and Detrimental Aftereffects—Srisuk et al.”

“Okay. What am I looking at?”

“That’s a study by a failed company in Thailand.” She swirled the gin in her glass. “Their idea was to put cancer patients in comas for their chemotherapy. The patient gets the chemo, but doesn’t have to be awake to suffer through it. Wake them up when the cancer goes away. Or when it can’t be treated anymore and it’s time for hospice. Either way, they avoid a lot of suffering.”

“That…sounds like a great idea,” I said.

She nodded. “It would be, if it wasn’t so deadly. It turns out the human body isn’t meant to be in a coma for a long time. Chemo lasts months, and often needs more rounds after that. They tried different ways to induce comas in primates, and the primates either died during the coma or woke up with brain damage.”

“So why are we talking about it?”

“Because they did more studies—this time on old data from human coma patients. They looked at humans who had come out of long comas okay and tried to see what they had in common. They found something.”

Old Russian space-agency documents were a mystery to me, but scientific papers were my thing for a long time. I flipped through the paper and skimmed to the findings. “Gene markers?” I said.

“Yes,” she said. “They found a group of genes that give a human ‘coma resistance.’ That’s what they’re calling it. The sequences are in what scientists used to think was useless DNA. But apparently it’s something we developed a long time ago for some unknown reason and still exists in some people’s DNA.”

“Are they sure these genes cause coma resistance?” I said. “They’re related, but do they cause it?”

“Yes, they’re sure. The genes are also found in lower primates. Whatever it is, it’s very old. Some think it might go all the way back to our water ancestors that used to hibernate. Anyway, they ran tests on primates with those genes and they survived long comas with no side effects. Every single one of them.”

“Okay. I see what you’re getting at.” I put the paper down. “Do DNA tests on all applicants, and use only the people who have those coma-resistance genes. During the trip, put the crew in comas. They don’t have to experience four years of getting on each other’s nerves or thinking about their deaths.”

She raised her glass to me. “It gets better. Having the crew in comas makes getting food much easier. We just pump powdered, balanced nutrients into their stomachs. No need for tons of different meals. Just powder and a water system that cleans itself.”

I smiled. “This sounds amazing. Like being frozen in sci-fi stories. Why are you drinking and stressed?”

“There are a few problems,” she said. “First, we'd need a fully automatic system to watch and care for the coma patients. If it failed, everyone would die. It’s not just about checking vitals and giving drugs through an IV. It would have to move and clean them, handle bedsores, and treat problems like infections around the IVs. Things like that.”

“Okay, but it seems like the world's doctors could figure that out,” I said. “Use your Stratt power to make them do it.”

She took another sip. “That’s not the big issue. The big issue is this: Only one in seven thousand people has the right genes for it.”

I sat back. “Wow.”

“Yeah. We couldn’t send the best people. We’d be sending the seven-thousandth best.”

“More like the three-thousand-five-hundredth best, on average,” I said.

She rolled her eyes.

“Still,” I said. “One in seven thousand is a million people worldwide. Think of it that way. You’d have a million people to choose from. You only need three.”

“Six,” she said. “We need a main crew and a backup crew. Can’t have the mission fail because someone gets hit by a car crossing the street the day before launch.”

“Okay, then six.”

“Yeah. Six people who are astronaut material, who have the scientific skills to figure out what’s going on with Astrophage at Tau Ceti, and who are willing to go on a suicide mission.”

“Out of a population of a million,” I said. “A million.”

She fell silent and took another sip of gin.

I cleared my throat. “So you either take your chances with picking the best people and maybe they kill each other, or you take your chances on medical technology that we still have to develop to automatically care for less talented people.”

“More or less. Either way, it’s a terrible risk. It’s the hardest decision I’ve ever had to make.”

“Good thing you already made up your mind, then,” I said.

She raised an eyebrow. “Huh?”

“Sure,” I said. “You just wanted someone to tell you what you already know. If you leave the crew awake, there’s nothing you can do about the psychosis risk. But we’ve got years to perfect the automated-coma-bed technology.”

She frowned a little but didn’t speak.

I softened my voice. “Besides, we’re already asking these people to die. We shouldn’t ask them to suffer emotionally for four years, too. Science and morality both agree, and you know it.”

She nodded, barely noticeably. Then, she finished her gin. “All right, you can go.” She slid her laptop over and began typing.

I left without another word. She had her own problems, and I had mine.

—

The memories are coming back easier now. I still can’t remember everything, but it’s not a surprise when they happen. It’s just sort of…“Oh hey, I know that. Always knew it, really.”

I guess I’m one of those people who can resist comas. That explains why I’m here instead of the much better people who should have been sent.

But Yáo and Ilyukhina probably had those genes, too, and they didn’t make it. I think the medical robot wasn’t perfect. They must have had some medical problem it couldn’t solve.

I shake off their memory.

The next few days test my patience. I distract myself by learning more about the ship.

I take inventory of the entire lab. One of the first things I find is a touchscreen computer in a pull-out drawer in the center table. It's a great find, because it has many research-related programs, unlike the panels in the control room, which focus on the ship or its instruments.

I see many math and science apps—mostly common ones I recognize. But the real treasure is the library!

As far as I can tell, this panel can access almost any scientific textbook ever written, every scientific paper ever published, and much more. One directory is simply called "Library of Congress," and it seems to be the complete digital record of everything copyrighted in the United States. Sadly, no books about the Hail Mary.

And the reference manuals. So many reference manuals. Data piled on data. I guess they figured solid-state hard drives are light, so they didn't need to limit information. They may have even burned the data into ROMs.

They included reference material on things that are probably useless. But it's good to know that if I ever need the average rectal temperature of a healthy goat, I can find it! (It's 103.4°F / 39.7°C.)

Playing with the panel leads to my next discovery: I know how I'll send information back to Earth using the beetles.

I knew they'd be involved, but now I know the details. Besides the ship's huge data storage, the panel also has four smaller external drives: John, Paul, George, and Ringo. Each shows 5 terabytes free. It's easy to guess that's the beetle's data.

So how do I launch them when the time comes? To find out, I go to the control room.

I have to search through a few menus on the Beetles panel to find the launch command, but I find it. It seems to be just a button labeled "Launch." I guess they use stars to orient themselves and fly toward Earth on their own. The Hail Mary did the same thing to get here, so they know how. No need to add human error when choosing their course.

While I’m here, I check out the Scientific Instrumentation screen. The first few sections are for the helioscope, the Petrovascope, and a telescope that can see visible light, infrared light, and other types of light.

I play with the visible-light telescope. It’s kind of fun. I can look at the stars. I mean, there’s not much else out there. Even Tau Ceti’s planets would just be tiny dots from here. But it’s still nice to see the outside from my small, enclosed space.

I also found a screen for spacewalks (EVAs). It has what I expected. There are controls for the spacesuit, so someone in the control room can handle any suit problems during a spacewalk. That way, the person in the suit doesn’t have to. Also, the ship has a complicated tether system on the outside. It’s basically tracks that the tether hook can slide along. They really thought spacewalks would be important. Probably to gather local Astrophage.

If there is any.

If Tau Ceti has a Petrova line, then there’s Astrophage to collect. Getting some would be the first step: bringing it to the lab and seeing if it’s different from the Astrophage on Earth. Maybe it’s a weaker type?

The next two days are mostly me worrying about what will happen. I know what’s going to happen, but I still worry.

I fidget in the control room and watch the seconds go by.

“You’re going to be in zero gravity,” I say. “You’re not going to fall. You won’t be in danger. The ship's acceleration will stop. But that’s okay.”

I don’t like roller coasters or water slides. That falling feeling terrifies me. And soon I’m going to feel that exact feeling because the “gravity” I’ve been feeling will stop completely.

The seconds tick by. “Four…three…two…”

“Here we go,” I said.

“One…zero.”

Right on time, the engines stopped. The 1.5 g’s I’ve been feeling are gone. There’s no gravity.

I panic. No amount of thinking about it could have helped. I just panic.

I scream and wave my arms. I make myself curl up like a baby—it feels good and keeps me from bumping into anything.

I shiver and shake as I float in the control room. I should have strapped myself in, but I didn’t think of it. Idiot.

“I’m not falling!” I yell. “I’m not falling! This is just space! Everything is fine!”

It’s not fine. I feel sick to my stomach. I’m going to throw up. Throwing up in zero g is bad. I don’t have a bag. I really wasn’t ready for this. I was dumb to think I could just talk myself out of a basic fear.

I pull open my jumpsuit collar and lean my head down. Just in time. I throw up all of “Day 9—Meal 3” into my shirt. I hold the collar tight to my chest after. It’s gross, but it’s contained. Better than it floating around and choking me.

“Oh gosh…” I whine. “Gosh…this is…”

Can I do this? Will I be completely useless from now on? Will humanity die because I can’t handle zero g?

No.

I clench my teeth. I clench my fists. I clench my butt. I clench every part of me that I know how to clench. It makes me feel in control. I’m doing something by aggressively doing nothing.

After forever, the panic starts to fade. Human brains are amazing. We can get used to almost anything. I’m getting used to it.

The slight lessening of fear helps me even more. I know I will be less afraid now. And knowing that makes the fear go away even faster. Soon, the panic turns into fear, which fades into general worry.

I look around the control room and nothing feels right. Nothing changed, but now there’s no down. I still feel sick. I grab my collar in case I need to throw up again but I don’t. I hold it in.

The feeling of warm vomit squishing between my chest and jumpsuit is disgusting. I need to change.

I aim myself at the hatchway to the lab and kick off the wall behind me. I float down and into the lab. The whole room is full of floating stuff. I left things on the table when I listed them. Now all that stuff is floating around freely, pushed by air from the vents.

“Dummy,” I say to myself. I should have known that would happen.

I go on to the bedroom. It’s also got junk floating everywhere. I opened most of the bins in the storage area to see what was inside. Now the bins and everything in them drift around.

“Clean me!” I say to the arms.

The arms don’t do anything.

I strip and use the jumpsuit to wipe gunk off my body. I found the sponge-bath area a few days ago—just a sink with sponges that pop out of the wall. No room for a shower, I guess. Anyway, I clean up with that stuff.

I’m not sure what to do with the dirty gunk.

“Laundry?” I say.

The arms reach down and take the dirty jumpsuit. A panel in the ceiling opens and they put it in there. What happens when it’s full? No idea.

I find another jumpsuit in the junk and put it on. Getting dressed in zero g is interesting. Not harder, but different. I get the new jumpsuit on. It’s a little tight. I check the name tag. It says 姚. It’s one of Yáo’s jumpsuits. Well, it’s not too tight. And I don’t want to float around the bedroom all day looking for one of mine. I’ll sort it out later.

For now, I’m too excited to see what’s out there. I mean, come on! I’m the first human to explore another star system! And I’m here!

I push off the floor toward the hatch…and miss. I crash into the ceiling. At least I get my arms up to protect my face. I bounce off the ceiling and back to the floor.

“Ow,” I mumble. I try again, a little more slowly, and I make it. I float up through the lab, and into the control room. Getting around is easier with no gravity. I still feel sick but I have to admit: This is pretty fun.

I pull myself into the pilot’s chair and strap myself in to keep from floating away.

The Navigation screen reads PRIMARY TRANSIT COMPLETE. The Spin Drive screen says THRUST: 0. But most important, the Petrovascope screen says READY.

I rub my hands together, then reach for the screen. The interface is simple enough. The corner has an icon that is a toggle switch with two states: “Visible” and “Petrova.” It’s currently set to “Visible.” The rest of the screen shows a visible-light view from the ship. Seems like an ordinary camera. I poke at the screen and quickly realize I can pan, zoom in or out, rotate, and so on.

All I see is stars in the distance. I guess I should pan around until I find Tau Ceti. I swipe my finger left, left, left…just generally trying to see where the star is. I don’t have a point of reference to work with. Every few left swipes I throw in a down swipe. Just to cover all angles over time. I do finally find Tau Ceti, but it doesn’t look like it should.

A few days ago, when I looked at it with the helioscope, it looked like any other star. But now it’s a solid black circle with a hazy ring of light around it. I realize why immediately.

The Petrovascope is a pretty sensitive piece of equipment. It’s fine-tuned to spot even the smallest amounts of the Petrova wavelength. A star will give off absolutely obscene amounts of light at all wavelengths. It’d be like staring at the sun with binoculars. The equipment has to protect itself from the star. It probably has a physical metal plate that it keeps between its sensors and the star at all times. So I’m looking at the back of that plate.

Good design.

I reach up to the toggle switch. This is it. If there’s no Petrova line here, I don’t know what to do. I mean, I’ll try to figure out something. But I’ll be kind of lost.

I flip the toggle.

The stars disappear. The hazy ring surrounding Tau Ceti remains. That’s to be expected. It’s the star’s corona, which will be emitting plenty of light, so some of it’s bound to be the Petrova wavelength.

I search the image desperately. At first, nothing, but then I see it. A beautiful dark-red arch coming out of the bottom-left of Tau Ceti.

I clap my hands. “Yes!”

The shape is clear. It’s a Petrova line! Tau Ceti has a Petrova line! I do a little wiggly dance in my chair. It’s not easy in zero g but I try my best. Now we’re getting somewhere!

There are so many experiments I need to do, I don’t even know where to start. I should see where the line goes, to begin with. One of the planets, of course, but which one and what’s interesting about it? And I should get a sample of local Astrophage to see if it’s the same as what we have back on Earth. I could do that by flying into the Petrova line and then scraping the dust off the hull during a spacewalk.

I could spend a week just writing a list of experiments I want to do!

I see a flash on the screen. Just a quick blink of light.

“What’s that?” I say. “Another clue?”

The flash happens again. I move and zoom in on that part of space. It’s nowhere near the Petrova line or Tau Ceti. Maybe light reflecting off a planet or asteroid?

I can see how that could happen. A very shiny asteroid could be bouncing enough light from Tau Ceti around that I see it on the Petrovascope, but it’s not constant, so maybe it’s an odd shape that’s rotating and—

The flash becomes a steady light. It’s just…“on” now. All the time.

I stare at the screen. “What…what’s going on here?…”

The light gets brighter. Not suddenly. Just slowly over time. I watch for a minute. It seems to be getting brighter faster now.

Is it something coming toward me?

A quick idea comes to mind: Maybe Astrophage are drawn to other Astrophage? Maybe some of them saw the flare from my engines, which would be the wavelength they use, and they came toward me. Maybe this is how they find the main group? So this could be a bunch of Astrophage headed my way, thinking I can lead them to the planet with the carbon dioxide?

Interesting idea. But I have no proof.

The steady light grows brighter, brighter, brighter, and then vanishes.

“Huh,” I say. I wait a few minutes, but the light doesn’t come back.

“Hmm….” I remember the strange event. But there’s nothing I can do about it right now. Whatever it was, it’s gone.

Back to the Petrova line. First, I want to find out which planet the line goes to. I guess I’ll have to figure out how to navigate the ship, but that’s another problem.

I look back at the Petrova line. Something’s wrong. Half of it is just…gone.

It’s coming from Tau Ceti, just like before, but then it stops suddenly at a random spot.

“What is going on?”

Did I mess up their path, maybe? If it’s that easy, wouldn’t we have figured it out when the Hail Mary was wandering around our solar system?

I zoom in on where it stops. It’s just a straight line. Like someone cut the Petrova line with a knife and tossed the scraps.

A huge line of moving Astrophage doesn’t just vanish. I have an easier idea: Something’s on the camera lens. Some junk. Maybe a clump of excited Astrophage. That would be great. I’d have a sample to check out right away!

Maybe a regular view will help me see what’s happening. I hit the button.

And that’s when I see it.

Something is blocking my view of the Petrova line. It’s right by my ship. Maybe a few hundred meters away. It’s sort of triangle-shaped and has pointy bits along its side.

Yes. I said side. It’s not an asteroid—the lines are too smooth; too straight. This thing was made. Built. Put together. Shapes like that don’t happen naturally.

It’s a ship.

Another ship.

There’s another ship in this system with me. Those flashes of light—those were its engines. It’s powered by Astrophage. Just like the Hail Mary. But the design, the shape—it’s unlike any spacecraft I’ve ever seen. It's made of huge, flat surfaces—the worst way to make a container that holds pressure. No one would make a ship like that.

No one on Earth would, anyway.

I blink a few times at what I’m seeing. I gulp.

This…this is an alien spacecraft. Made by aliens. Aliens smart enough to make a spacecraft.

Humanity isn’t alone in the universe. And I’ve just met our neighbors.

“Holy fucking shit!”
