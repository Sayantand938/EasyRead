I check the Centrifuge control screen. We are at full speed and extension. The crew area has turned 180 degrees correctly. The diagram shows the two halves of the ship fully apart. We are spinning smoothly in space. The “Lab Gravity” reads “1.00 g.”

“Nine point eight meters per second per second.”

“That’s a lot of gravity,” I say. But that’s expected. He’d told me all about Erid before, including its size and mass. I knew their gravity had to be about double Earth’s. Good to have my math confirmed, though.

And wow. Rocky’s mass is 168 kilograms. That means on his home planet he weighs almost 800 pounds. And that’s normal for him, so I guess he can move around just fine.

Eight hundred pounds and can skitter around easily. Remember: Don’t arm-wrestle an Eridian.

Like epoxy. But really, really strong epoxy.

Seventy-two years? Man, I wish Earth had that kind of time. But seventy-two years from now Earth will be a frozen wasteland and 99 percent of the human population will be dead.

Why isn’t Erid as badly affected? I furrow my brow. I only have to think for a moment before I have my answer: It’s all about thermal energy storage.

“Erid is much hotter than Earth,” I say. “And Erid is much larger with a much thicker atmosphere. So Erid has a lot more heat stored in its air. Earth is getting cold fast. Very fast. In fourteen more years, most humans will be dead.”

His voice becomes monotone. It’s a very serious tone. “I understand. Stress. Concern.”

“Yes.”

I sigh. I’ll have to explain this sooner or later. Might as well be now. “I’m not going back. I’m going to die here.”

His shell shudders. “Why, question?”

“My ship only had enough fuel to get here. I don’t have enough to go home. I have tiny probes that will return to Earth with my findings. But I will stay here.”

“Why is mission like this, question?”

“This was all the fuel my planet could make in time.”

“You knew this when you left Earth, question?”

“Yes.”

“You are good human.”

“Thanks.” I try not to think about what’s coming. “So, let’s collect Astrophage. I have ideas for how we can get some samples. My equipment is very good at detecting small amounts—”

“Wait.” He holds up a claw. “How much Astrophage does your ship need to return to Earth, question?”

“Uh…just over two million kilograms,” I say.

“I can give it,” he says.

I sit up in my chair. “What?!”

“I can give it. I have extra. I can give that much and still have plenty to return to Erid. You can have it.”

My heart skips a beat. “Seriously?! That’s a lot of fuel! Let me say it again: two million kilograms. Two times ten to the sixth power!”

“Yes. I have much Astrophage. My ship used less than planned on the trip here. You can have two million kilograms.”

I fall back into my seat. I pant. I almost can’t breathe. My eyes fill with tears. “Oh my God…”

“Don’t understand.”

I wipe away tears.

“You okay, right?”

“Yes!” I sob. “Yes, I’m okay. Thank you! Thank you thank you!”

“I am happy. You not die. Let’s save planets!”

I break down, crying tears of joy. I’m going to live!

Half the Chinese crew stood on the flight deck. Some were actually doing their jobs, but most were there to see humanity’s saviors. The whole science team was there as well. The same people we had at our weekly meetings. Stratt, me, Dimitri, Lokken, and our newest scientist, Dr. Lamai. The helicopter came in low and slow, then landed perfectly on the helipad. A ground crew rushed up to secure it. The rotors kept spinning and the cargo door opened.

Three people walked out, each in blue jumpsuits, each with their country’s flag on the shoulder. A Chinese man, a Russian woman, and an American man.

The ground crew led them away, and the chopper lifted off again. Shortly after, another helicopter came down. Like before, this helicopter had three astronauts on board. This time, a Russian man, a Russian woman, and an American woman.

These six were the main and reserve crews for the Hail Mary. Either of the helicopters could have easily fit all six astronauts, but Stratt had a firm rule: No crewmember and their backup could share a plane, helicopter, or car, no matter what. Each role was unique and took years of training. We couldn’t let one car accident wipe out humanity's hope.

There weren't many choices. Not many people could resist comas, had "the right stuff," and would agree to a suicide mission.

Even so, picking the team was long, hard, and full of fighting between the governments involved. Stratt stood her ground and demanded only the best, but she had to give in sometimes.

"Women," I said.

"Yes," Stratt said, annoyed.

"Even though you didn't want them."

"Yes."

"Good."

"No, it's not." She frowned. "The Americans and Russians made me do it."

I folded my arms. “I never thought a woman would be so sexist towards women.”

“It’s not sexism. It’s realism.” She brushed a strand of hair from her face. “My rules were that all candidates must be straight men.”

“Why not all straight women?”

“Most scientists and astronaut candidates are men. That’s just how it is. Don’t like it? Tell your female students to study science and math. I’m not here to make things equal. I’m here to do what it takes to save humanity.”

“Still seems sexist.”

“Call it what you want. There’s no room on this mission for sexual tension. What if someone falls in love? Or there’s a fight? People kill for less.”

I looked across the deck at the candidates. Captain Yang welcomed them aboard. He seemed especially interested in his countryman—they were smiling and shaking hands.

“You didn’t want a Chinese guy either. You thought their space program was too new. But I hear you chose him to lead the crew.”

“He’s the best person for the job. So he’s the commander.”

“Maybe the Russians and Americans are good enough too. Maybe the people saving the world will act like adults. Maybe ignoring half the people who could help because you’re afraid astronauts can’t control themselves isn’t smart.”

“We’ll have to hope so. The Russian woman—Ilyukhina—is on the main crew as well. She’s a materials expert and the best person for the job. The science expert is Martin DuBois—the American man. Two men and one woman. Sounds like trouble.”

I put my hand to my chest, pretending to be shocked. “Oh my! DuBois is black! I can’t believe you let that happen! Are you worried he’ll mess up the mission by talking about rap music and basketball?”

“Oh, be quiet,” she said.

We watched the astronauts get swarmed by the deck crew. They were completely in awe—especially of Yáo.

“DuBois has three PhDs—physics, chemistry, and biology.” Stratt pointed to the American woman. “That’s Annie Shapiro. She created a new way to splice DNA that’s now called the Shapiro method.”

“Yes, yes. She’s very smart.”

“She did it for her PhD project. Her project. Do you know how many people might win a Nobel Prize for research they did in grad school? Not many, I can tell you. And she’s your second choice for the science expert?”

“She’s the best DNA splicing expert alive. But DuBois knows a lot about many different things, and that’s more important. We don’t know what they’ll find out there. We need someone who knows a little about everything.”

“I’m glad you’re impressed. Because you’ll be training DuBois and Shapiro.”

“Are you kidding? They’re way smarter than me. What would I teach them?”

“Don’t underestimate yourself,” said Stratt. “You’re the world’s leading expert on Astrophage biology. You’re going to share everything you know about it with both of them. Here comes the main crew.”

I leaned to Dimitri. “Are all Russians crazy?”

I and the other science heads all shook hands with the three astronauts. It was messy, more like a casual party than a serious meeting.

In the middle of it all, DuBois turned to me. “You must be Ryland Grace?”

“Yeah,” I said. “It’s an honor to meet you. What you’re doing is just… I can’t even imagine the sacrifice you’re making. Or should I not mention it? I don’t know. Maybe we shouldn’t talk about it?”

DuBois smoothly changed the subject. “Anyway, I’m looking forward to learning all about the Astrophage life-cycle. Dr. Shapiro—my partner on the backup crew—is also very excited. It’ll be a class of two, I guess. Do you have any experience teaching?”

“Excellent.”

I’m all smiles. It’s been three days since I found out I won’t die and I’m still all smiles.

Well, actually, I could still easily die. The trip home is long and dangerous. Just because I survived my coma on the way here, that doesn’t mean I’ll survive it on the way home. Maybe I can stay awake and just eat the feeding-tube slurry when my normal food runs out? I can do four years all alone, right? We were in comas to keep from killing one another. But being alone is a whole different kind of mental problem. I should read up on it.

But not now. Right now I have to save Earth. My own survival is a problem for later. But it’s a problem , not a hopeless guarantee of death.

The light on the Centrifuge screen blinks green.

“Gravity at full,” I say with a smile.

We were back in zero g for a short time, but now I have the centrifuge going again. I had to stop it because I needed to use the engines. We can’t have fake gravity and the engines on at the same time. Just imagine firing up the spin drives while the ship is in two pieces connected by a hundred meters of cable. It’s not a pleasant thought.

During the decades (gasp!) that Rocky’s been here, he studied the system very well. He gave me all the information he’d gathered. He listed six planets, noting their size, weight, positions, orbits, and general atmosphere. He didn’t have to travel around to do it. He just did astronomical observations from the Blip-A. Turns out Eridians are as curious as humans are.

And it’s a good thing too. This isn’t Star Trek. I can’t just turn on a scanner and get all that information about a star system. It took Rocky months of watching to get things this detailed.

And more important, Rocky knows all about the local Petrova line. As expected, it leads to one specific planet—probably the one with the most carbon dioxide. In this case, it’s the third planet from the star, “Tau Ceti e.” At least, that’s what Earth calls it.

So that’ll be our first stop.

Sure, we could fly the Hail Mary through any part of the Petrova line and get some Astrophage that way. But we’d only cross the line for a few seconds. A solar system isn’t still. We have to keep moving fast enough to orbit the star.

But Tau Ceti e is a nice, big planet in the widest part of the Petrova line. We can park the Hail Mary in orbit and be surrounded by local Astrophage for half of every orbit. And we can stay there as long as we want, getting all the data we need about the Astrophage here and how the Petrova line works.

So we’re on our way to the mysterious planet.

I can’t just ask Mr. Sulu to plot a course. I spent two days doing math, checking my work, and checking it again before I figured out the exact angle and power needed.

Sure, I have 20,000 kilograms of Astrophage left. And yes, that’s a lot of fuel considering I can get 1.5 g’s by using 6 grams per second. And yes, Rocky’s ship seems to have tons of Astrophage (I still don’t understand how he has so much extra fuel). But I’m saving fuel anyway.

I got us going fast and we’re on course for Tau Ceti e. I’ll slow down to orbit in about eleven days. While we wait, we may as well have gravity. So we’re back to using the centrifuge.

Eleven days. Amazing. The total distance we’ll travel is over 150 million kilometers. That’s about the same as the distance from Earth to the sun. And we’re doing it in eleven days. How? By going incredibly fast.

I used three hours of thrust to get us going, and I’ll use another three when we get to Tau Ceti e to slow down. Right now, we’re moving at 162 kilometers per second. It’s crazy. If you left Earth at that speed, you’d get to the moon in forty minutes.

This whole trip, including the slowdown burn at the end, will use up 130 kilograms of fuel.

Astrophage. Wild stuff.

He thinks about it.

Xenonite is amazing. Just a centimeter of this clear stuff keeps my thin air with oxygen separate from Rocky’s thick air with ammonia. And my 20 degrees Celsius separate from Rocky’s 210 degrees Celsius.

He’s taken over more of some rooms than others. The dormitory is mostly his now. I made him move all his junk into his room, so we agreed he could have most of the space in there.

He also put a big airlock in the dormitory. He made it the size of the Hail Mary’s airlock, thinking anything important would fit through it. I can’t go into his area. My spacesuit wouldn’t work there. I’d be crushed. The airlock is so we can pass things back and forth.

The lab is mostly mine. He has a tunnel up the side and another going along the ceiling and into the control room. He can watch my science stuff. But Earth equipment won’t work in his air, so it has to be in mine.

The control room is small. Rocky put the xenonite bulb in the floor by the hatch. He tried to not take up much space. He says the holes he added won’t hurt the ship.

I don’t need the frequency analyzer now. It was an A-below-middle-C major fifth, then an E-flat octave, and then a G-minor seventh. I put it in my spreadsheet, but I don’t know why. I haven’t looked at it in days. “What does it mean?”

We’d talked about some basic biology during our travels. I explained how humans reproduce, and he told me where baby Eridians come from. They’re hermaphrodites and reproduce by laying eggs next to each other. Things happen between the eggs and one of them absorbs the other, leaving one good egg that will hatch in one Eridian year—forty-two Earth days.

Laying eggs together is, basically, the Eridian version of sex. And they mate for life. But this is the first I’ve heard of Rocky doing it.

“You have a mate?”

"Unknown," Rocky says. "Mate possibly has new mate. I gone a long time."

"Yes, sad. But necessary. Must save Erid. You pick human word for ♫♩♪♫ ."

Proper nouns are annoying. If you’re learning German from a guy named Hans, you just call him Hans. But I literally can’t make the sounds Rocky makes and vice versa. So when one of us tells the other about a name, the other one has to pick or make up a word to represent that name in their own language. Rocky’s actual name is a sequence of notes—he told it to me once but it has no meaning in his language, so I stuck with “Rocky.”

But my name is actually an English word. So Rocky just calls me the Eridian word for "grace."

Anyway, now I need to think of an English word for “Rocky’s partner.”

“Adrian,” I say. Why not? “Human word is ‘Adrian.’ ”

He ate in his ship when we were connected. And I think he grabbed a few meals now and then while I slept.

To be fair, he doesn’t have much space either. He takes up most of the room, but it’s filled with all his stuff. Plus, he made a makeshift workshop in there and a system to keep him alive using parts from his ship.

I guess eating for them is something gross that is to be done in private.

He tears the rough chunks off the food and reveals meat underneath. It’s definitely meat—it looks just like Earth meat. Since we almost certainly come from the same basic building blocks of life, I bet we use the same proteins and have the same general solutions to evolutionary problems.

Once again, I feel sad. I want to spend the rest of my life studying Eridian biology! But I have to save humanity first. Stupid humanity. Getting in the way of my hobbies.

He pulls all of the rough chunks off the meat and sets them aside. Then he tears the meat into small pieces. He always keeps the food on the packaging it came in. It never touches the floor. I wouldn’t want my food touching the floor either.

After a while, he has shredded the edible parts of his meal as much as he can with his hands. Much more than any human would with their food.

Then he steps to the other side of his compartment, leaving his food where it was. He pulls a flat, round container from a sealed box and places it under his thorax.

Then things…get gross. He did warn me. I can’t complain.

The rough armor on his abdomen splits, and I see something fleshy rip open underneath. A few drops of shiny silver liquid dribble out. Blood?

Then a gray blob falls out of his body into the pan. It lands with a wet splat.

He closes the pan and puts it back in its box.

He goes back to the food and rolls onto his back. The big hole in his stomach is still open. I can see inside. There’s soft flesh in there.

He reaches over with some of his hands and grabs some good pieces of food. He brings them to his opening and drops them in. He does this again and again, slowly and carefully, until all the food is in his…mouth? Stomach?

There is no chewing. There are no teeth. As far as I can tell, nothing is moving inside.

He finishes the last of his food, then lets his arms drop. He lies flat on the floor, not moving.

I try not to ask if he’s okay. I mean, he looks dead. But this is probably just how Eridians eat. And poop. Yeah. I’m guessing that blob that came out earlier was what’s left of his last meal. He’s a monostome—that is, waste comes out the same opening that food goes in.

The opening in his stomach closes slowly. Something like a scab forms where the skin was broken. But I don’t see it for long. The rocky stomach covering folds back into place soon after.

“I…sleep…” he mumbled. “You…watch…question?”

A food coma for Rocky is a big deal. This doesn’t look like he's doing it on purpose at all. This is a nap his body forces him to take after eating.

“Yes, I watch. Sleep.”

“Sle…ee…p…” he mumbles. Then he conks out, still belly-up on the floor.

His breathing speeds up. It always does when he first falls asleep. His body has to get rid of all the heat in his hot blood.

After a few minutes, he stops panting. Now I know he’s really asleep. Once he stops panting, I’ve never seen him wake up again in less than two hours. I can sneak off to do my own thing. In this case, I’ll write down everything I just saw about his digestion.

Step 1: Subject poops from mouth.

“Yup,” I say to myself. “That was pretty freaking gross.”
