Project Hail Mary

Chapter 9

I let the model float in the middle of the room. The xenonite is almost unbreakable, so I don’t need to worry about it hitting anything.

Is this a good idea? I have a planet to save. As amazing as it is to meet smart aliens, is this risk worth it?

The Eridians clearly understand Astrophage. At least enough to make engines from it. And—I think—they’re trying to tell me they’re here for the same reason I am. They might have information I don’t. They might even have the solution I’m looking for. And they seem nice enough.

But this is like a stranger offering me candy in space. I want the candy (information), but I don’t know the stranger.

What else can I do? Ignore them?

I could keep going as if I never saw them. They’re probably as surprised to see me as I am to see them. They might keep trying to talk, but I don’t think they’d get mean.

Or would they? I can’t know.

No, this is obvious. I have to talk to them. If they have any information about Astrophage, even a little bit, I have to talk to them. It’s a risk, yes, but this whole mission is a risk.

Okay. So what would I do if I were them?

I’m an Eridian. I want to build a tunnel to connect to the strange human ship. But I don’t know what the human ship is made of. How can I ensure they'll connect or seal? My xenonite knowledge is unmatched, but how do I join it to “humanium” or whatever that ship is made of? I’ve sent the human my xenonite designs, so he knows what I have. But I still don’t know what he has.

They’ll need a piece of my hull. And they’ll need to know it’s a piece of my hull.

“Right,” I say to no one.

I don’t know if this is a good idea or a terrible one. But I’m going to break off a piece of my hull.

I grab some EVA tools from Drawer 17E in the lab. I found them a while ago. They’re on a tool belt that clips onto the EVA suit. Stratt and the others made sure we had all the tools we'd need for hull repairs. Ilyukhina would normally fix things, but she’s gone.

Huh. Random thought. Ilyukhina was our engineer—the one who fixed everything. Okay. Well, now it’s me.

I get back in the EVA suit, and back outside. Again. Going in and out is getting annoying. I hope this tunnel works.

I move along the hull, adjusting the tether each time. And I start thinking…

What good is a tunnel, really? Our environments probably aren't compatible. We can’t just connect the ships with a tunnel and shake hands. I think there’s a lot of ammonia over there.

And the temperature. Those cylinders are hot when I get them.

Some quick calculations tell me that first cylinder they sent should have lost 100 degrees Celsius or more during that forty-minute trip (depending on its starting temperature). And it was still hot when I got it. So it was really hot when it left their ship. Like…way hotter than the boiling point of water.

I try not to guess too much, but come on. I’m a scientist and these are aliens. I’m going to guess.

Do Eridians live in a place hotter than boiling water? If so, it proves I was right! The goldilocks zone is nonsense! You don’t need liquid water for life!

I should be more focused on the “first contact with smart aliens” thing or the “save all of humanity” thing, but darn it, I can spend a moment being happy about being right when everyone said I was wrong!

I finally reach a spot on the ship that seems right for the job. I’m behind the entire pressurized part of the ship, past the part where it gets wider. If I’m right, I’m standing on a big empty tank that used to be full of Astrophage. If I break the ship here, it shouldn’t matter.

I pull out a hammer and chisel. Not the best way to do this, but I can’t think of anything better. I start by putting one corner of the chisel on the ship and tapping it lightly. It makes a noticeable dent. It doesn’t take much to get through this outer layer.

I use the hammer and chisel to cut out a 6-inch circle of ship material. There’s a layer of something underneath. I can feel it with the chisel. Probably insulation.

I have to pry the circle out with the chisel. The layer underneath holds strong, but then gives way suddenly. The ship sample flies off into space.

“Shoot!”

I jump off the ship. I grab the circle right before my tether pulls tight. I breathe for a second, thinking about how dumb I am, then pull myself back to the ship. Looking at the circle, it seems like there’s a light, foamy stuff attached to the underside. Styrofoam, maybe. Probably something more complex than that.

“I hope you guys watched all that,” I say. “Because I’m not doing it again.”

I throw the hull chunk at the Blip-A.

Doing this right in front of them will make them sure I’m sending them a sample of the hull. I hope it’s enough for what they want to do. I don’t even know if they wanted it or needed it. They might be looking at their screens right now and saying, “What is this idiot doing? Is he poking a hole in his own ship? Why?”

I stay on the hull and watch as the chunk tumbles in the Taulight. The multi-armed robot on the Blip-A’s hull slides along its rails to catch it. Once it’s in position, it waits for the hull chunk to arrive and catches it perfectly.

And then, I swear to God, it waves at me! One of its little arms waves at me!

I wave back.

It waves again.

Okay, this could go on all day. I head back toward the airlock.

Your move, guys.

Their move is taking forever, and I’m getting bored.

Wow. Here I am in a spaceship in the Tau Ceti system, waiting for the smart aliens I just met to continue our talk…and I’m bored. Humans sure can get used to weird stuff and make it seem normal.

I look through the Radar panel controls to see what else it can do. After poking around in the settings, I find what I want: the close-range warning settings. Right now, it’s set to 100 kilometers. Seems okay. You’d think things would be millions of kilometers away, or at least tens of thousands. So, a rock within 100 kilometers would be a big deal.

I change the setting to 0.26 kilometers. I hope it doesn’t say it’s too low, but it works.

I stretch and float out of the pilot seat. The Blip-A is 271 meters away. If they get closer than 260 meters, or send another gift that gets that close, the warning will sound. I don’t have to keep staring at the screen. The control room will yell a warning when the Blip-A does something interesting.

I float down to the sleeping area.

“Food,” I say.

The arms grab a box from their little stash in the ceiling and stick it to my bunk. I should check out what’s in there sometime. For now, I push off the ceiling and float down to the food. The box, marked DAY 10—MEAL 1, has a Velcro-like strip on the bottom to keep it on the bed. I open it up and see a burrito.

Not sure what I expected, but okay. A burrito it is.

Turns out it’s a room-temperature burrito. Beans, cheese, some red sauce…all pretty tasty, really. But room temperature. Either the crew doesn’t get hot meals here or the machine doesn’t trust a recent coma patient not to burn himself on hot food. Probably the latter.

I float up to the lab and put the burrito in the sample furnace. I leave it there for a few minutes before pulling it out with tongs. The cheese bubbles and a cloud of steam slowly floats out in all directions.

I leave the burrito to float in the air and cool.

I snicker. If I really wanted a hot burrito, I’d turn on the spin drives, go outside, and hold the burrito in the light from it. That’d get it hot really quick. As in: It would get vaporized along with my arm and whatever else was in the blast range, because—

—

“Welcome to Little Russia!” said Dimitri. He waved dramatically at the aircraft carrier’s lower hangar deck. The whole space had been changed into labs full of high-tech equipment. Dozens of scientists in lab coats worked at their tasks, sometimes speaking Russian to each other. Dimitri’s Denizens, we called them.

We probably spent too much time naming stuff.

I held my little sample container like Scrooge with a bag of coins. “I’m not happy about this.”

“Oh, hush,” said Stratt.

“I’ve only made eight grams of Astrophage so far, and I’m supposed to just give away two grams of it? Two grams may not seem like much, but it’s ninety-five billion Astrophage cells.”

“It is for a good cause, my friend!” said Dimitri. “I promise you will like it. Come, come!”

He led Stratt and me through to the main lab. The center was taken up by a huge cylindrical vacuum chamber. The chamber was open and three technicians were attaching something to a table inside.

Dimitri said something in Russian to them. They answered him. He said something else and pointed to me. They smiled and made happy Russian sounds.

Then Stratt said something stern in Russian.

“Sorry,” said Dimitri. “English only for now, my friends! For the American!”

“Hello, American!” said one of the technicians. “I speak English for you! You have fuel?”

I gripped my sample container tighter. “I have some fuel….”

Stratt looked at me the way I look at stubborn students in my class. “Hand it over, Dr. Grace.”

“You know, my breeder doubles Astrophage population over time, right? Taking away two grams now is like taking away four grams next month.”

She took the container from me and gave it to Dimitri.

He held up the small metal vial and looked at it with pleasure. “This is a good day. I have been waiting for this day. Dr. Grace, let me show you my spin drive!”

He motioned for me to follow and hurried up the stairs into the vacuum chamber. The technicians left, one by one, to give us space.

“Everything is connected,” one of them said. “Checklist complete. Ready for test.”

“Good, good,” Dimitri said. “Dr. Grace, Ms. Stratt. Come!”

He led Stratt and me into the vacuum chamber. A thick, shiny metal plate was against one wall. In the middle of the chamber was a round table with a device on it.

“This is spin drive.” Dimitri grinned.

It wasn’t impressive to look at. It was about two feet wide, mostly round, but with one side cut flat. Sensors and wires stuck out from openings all over it.

Dimitri lifted the top cover to show the inside. It was more complex. Inside was a clear triangle on a rotor. Dimitri spun it a little. “See? Spin. Spin drive.”

“How does it work?” I asked.

He pointed to the triangle. “This is the revolver—made of strong, clear plastic. And this”—he pointed to a space between the revolver and the outer shell—“is where fuel goes in. A light inside that part of the revolver shines a little light with wavelengths of 4.26 and 18.31 microns—wavelengths that attract Astrophage. The Astrophage move to that revolver face. But not too fast. The Astrophage's push is based on the strength of the light. A dim light makes a weak push, but it’s enough to make the Astrophage stick to the surface.”

He turned the triangle and lined up an edge with the flat side of the shell. “Rotate 120 degrees, and this face of the revolver with Astrophage stuck to it now faces the back of the ship. Increase the light inside. The Astrophage are now very excited and push very hard toward the light! Their thrust—Petrova-frequency light—leaves the back of the ship. This pushes the ship forward. Millions of little Astrophages pushing on the back of the ship make it go, yes?”

I bent down to look. “I see…this way no part of the ship has to be in the path of the light.”

“Yes, yes!” said Dimitri. “Astrophage force only limited by the brightness of light attracting it. I did much math and decided best is to make Astrophage use all energy in four seconds. Any faster and force will break revolver.”

He turned the revolver another 120 degrees and pointed to the remaining third of the shell. “This is cleaning area. A squeegee wipes dead Astrophage off the revolver.”

He pointed to the cleaning area, then the fueling area, and then the open face. “All three areas active at same time. So while this area cleans dead Astrophage off this face, the fueling area adds Astrophage to that face, and the other face is pointed out back of ship, providing thrust. This pipelining means the part of triangle pointed out back of ship is always thrusting.”

Dimitri opened my vial of Astrophage and put it in the fueling chamber. I guess since the Astrophage will find their way to the triangle face, no special handling was needed. He could just…let the fuel see the light.

“Come, come,” he said. “Experiment time!”

We left the vacuum chamber, and Dimitri sealed it off. He yelled something in Russian, and all the Russians started repeating it. Everyone made their way to the far side of the hangar deck, including us.

They’d set up a folding table. It had a laptop on it with Russian writing on the screen.

“Ms. Stratt. How far is the carrier from the nearest land?” Dimitri asked.

“About three hundred kilometers,” she said.

“This is good.”

“Wait, why?” I said. “Why is that good?”

Dimitri pursed his lips. “It is…good. Time for science!”

He pushed a button. There was a muffled whump from the far side of the bay, followed by a hum, and then nothing.

“Experiment done.” He leaned forward to read the screen. “Sixty thousand Newtons of force!”

He turned to the other Russians. “60,000 ньютонов!”

They all cheered.

Stratt turned to me. “That’s a lot, right?”

I was too busy staring at Dimitri to answer her. “Did you say sixty thousand Newtons?”

He pumped his fist. “Yes! Sixty thousand Newtons! Held for one hundred microseconds!”

“Oh my God. From that little thing?!” I started to walk forward. I had to see it myself.

Dimitri grabbed my arm. “No. You stay here, friend. We all stay here. One point eight billion Joules of light energy came out. That’s why we needed the vacuum chamber and one thousand kilograms of silicon. No air to turn into plasma. Light goes straight to silicon block. Energy is absorbed by melting the metal. See?”

He turned the laptop toward me. A camera inside the vacuum chamber showed the glowing blob that used to be a thick metal plate.

“Whoa…” I said.

“Yes, yes,” Dimitri said. “That Mr. Einstein with his E = mc 2. Very powerful stuff. We let the cooling system work on it for a few hours. It uses seawater. It will be fine.”

I just shook my head in amazement. In just 100 microseconds—that’s one ten-thousandth of a second—Dimitri’s spin drive melted a metric ton of metal. All that energy had been stored in my little Astrophages. Slowly collected from the carrier’s nuclear reactor heat over time by my breeder. The math made sense, but seeing it happen was different.

“Wait…how much Astrophage did you use?”

Dimitri smiled. “I can only guess based on thrust. But it was close to twenty micrograms.”

“I gave you two whole grams! Can I have the rest back, please?”

“Don’t be greedy,” Stratt said. “Dimitri needs it for more tests.”

She turned to him. “Good work. How big will the real drive be?”

Dimitri pointed to the video feed. “That big. That is real drive.”

“No, I mean the one on the ship.”

“That,” he said, pointing again. “You want backup, safety, and it to work all the time, yes? So we don’t make just one big engine. We make a thousand little ones. One thousand and nine, actually. Enough for all the thrust we need, with plenty extra. If some break during the trip? Not a problem. More thrust from the others makes up for it.”

“Ah.” Stratt nodded. “Lots of little spin drives. I like it. Keep up the good work.”

She headed to the stairwell.

I stared at Dimitri. “If you’d set off all two grams of that sample at once…”

He shrugged. “Fwoosh! We turn to vapor. All of us. The ship too. The explosion would make a small tsunami. But we're three hundred kilometers away from land, so it is okay.”

He slapped me on the back. “And I’d owe you a drink even after death, yes?! Ha-ha-ha-ha!”

—

“Huh,” I say to myself. “So that’s how the spin drive works.”

I munch on my burrito.

So I guess I have a thousand of those (“A thousand and nine!” I hear Dimitri’s voice in my head). At least—that’s how many I started with. Some probably broke down during the trip. There’s probably a panel on the Spin Drive console that’ll tell me how each one is doing.

The proximity alert interrupts my thoughts.

“Finally!”

I “drop” the burrito (it floats where I leave it) and push myself up to the control room. The hatch from the dormitory to the lab doesn’t line up with the hatch from the lab to the control room, but there’s a diagonal path that will send me through both if I do it just right.

I don’t get it right this time. I have to push off a lab wall on the way. Still, I’m getting better at it.

I check the Radar panel and, sure enough, the Blip-A is coming! Not just a piece of it this time. The whole ship is headed my way. Nice and slow. Maybe they’re trying to seem friendly? Either way, it’s almost here.

It looks like its hull has something new. On that diamond part that’s as big as the Hail Mary itself, there’s a tube sticking straight up. The hull robot is sitting next to it, looking proud. Maybe I’m imagining things a little.

The tube looks like it’s made of xenonite. It’s patchy gray and tan with lines like wood grain. Hard to see from here, but it also looks like it’s hollow.

I think I know what’s going to happen. If they do what they showed me with the model, they’ll put the other end of it against my airlock.

How will they connect their tunnel? My airlock can connect to other ships—probably the ship that brought me and my crew to the Hail Mary—but I don’t expect Eridians to know all the details of a universal airlock.

The Blip-A gets closer and closer. What if they mess up? What if they don’t get the measurements right? What if they accidentally poke a hole in my ship? I’m the only thing standing between humanity and extinction. Could a mistake in alien math destroy my entire species?

I rush to the airlock and put on the EVA suit. I’m in it faster than ever. Better to be safe than sorry.

The Blip-A is so close now that the Telescope screen just shows a blurry patch of hull. I switch to the external cameras. There are cameras all over my ship. They’re all controlled from a screen on the EVA panel. I guess it’s good to know where your astronaut is when you’re telling them what to do out there.

The tunnel is about 20 feet long. Or 7 meters. Man, being an American scientist is annoying sometimes. You think in random units depending on the situation.

The hull robot reaches out with some seriously long arms. I didn’t know it could do that. It stretches way past the tunnel toward my airlock. Not creepy at all. Five alien robot arms reaching for my front door. Nothing to worry about.

Each arm’s three-fingered “hand” is holding…something. A curved bar with a flat plate on each end. Like a coffee-mug handle. Three of the arms reach the Hail Mary and stick the flat parts of their devices to the hull. Soon after, the other two arms do the same. Then, all five pull back, dragging the Hail Mary toward the tunnel.

Okay. So those flat things are handles. How are they stuck on? Good question! My hull is smooth and made of aluminum that isn't magnetic (why do I remember that now?). The handles definitely aren’t connected with screws or bolts. Must be glue.

And it starts to click.

Of course they aren’t going to figure out how the docking thing works. They’re going to glue one end of the tunnel to my ship. Why not? Much easier.

My ship groans. It’s a 100,000-kilogram machine that was never meant to be pulled by its airlock. Can the hull handle this?

I check the seals on my spacesuit again.

The control room moves around me. It’s not fast—just a few centimeters per second. Hey, for small spaceship speeds I think in metric! Much better than “cubits per fortnight” or whatever.

I let the wall come to me. Without really thinking about it, I like being a bit farther from the airlock. Something scary is happening over there.

Clunk.

The Eridian tunnel has bumped the hull. Clicks and scrapes come next. I watch the hull camera views.

The tunnel mouth, now stuck to the airlock opening, is bigger than the whole airlock door. I guess this is it. If the glue holds the pressure. They don't even know my air pressure. What's the glue made of? So many questions.

I can't use the control-room panels with my EVA suit gloves. I wish I could zoom in somehow. I squint at a video feed showing the tunnel. It looks tight against the hull to me. The hull curves around that spot. It's a hard shape to make, but the Eridians copied it perfectly.

After another minute, the robot arms let go of the handles, leaving them on the hull.

A soft sound comes from the airlock. A whooshing sound. Is that air flowing? They're filling the tunnel with air!

My heart races. Can my ship handle this? What if their air eats aluminum? What if aluminum kills Eridians instantly? This is a terrible idea!

The whooshing stops.

I gulp.

They're done. Nothing's dissolved yet. I float to the airlock to take a look.

I had both airlock doors sealed, of course. More protection in case of a leak. I open the inner door and float inside. I look out the small window.

The blackness of space is gone, replaced by the blackness of a dark tunnel. I turn on the helmet lights and angle my head to shine light through the window.

The end of the tunnel is very close. I don’t mean I’m worried about it. I mean the end is not 20 feet away. It’s more like 10 feet. And while the rest of the tunnel is made of gray and tan spotted xenonite, the wall at the end has a hexagonal pattern of random colors.

They didn’t just put in a tunnel. They connected my airlock to theirs, with a wall in between.

Smart.

I close the inner airlock door with me inside and remove the air. I turn the outer door’s handle and push. It opens easily. The tunnel has no air—at least, on my side of the divider.

I think I understand. This is a test. They had the same worries I had. Connect it, let me fill my half with air, and see what happens. Either it works or it doesn’t. If it works, great! If not, they’ll try something else. Or maybe ask me to try something.

Okay. Let’s see.

I tell the airlock to add air. It won’t—the outer door is open. Good to know that safety feature is there, but I’ll have to get around it.

It’s not hard—there’s a manual valve that will let air from the ship into the airlock. It ignores all computer controls. You don’t want someone to die because of a software problem, right?

I open the valve. Air rushes in from the Hail Mary and, with the airlock wide open, into the tunnel. After three minutes, the airflow slows and then stops. My suit readings say there’s 400 hectopascals of pressure outside. The Hail Mary has the same pressure as my part of the tunnel.

I close the valve and wait. I watch the pressure gauge on my suit. The pressure stays at 400 hectopascals. We have a good seal.

Eridians know how to stick xenonite to aluminum. Of course they do. Aluminum's an element, and any species that could invent xenonite probably knows the periodic table a thousand times better than we do.

Time for a leap of faith. I open the EVA suit and climb out. The strong smell of ammonia fills the air but it’s otherwise breathable. It’s my own air, after all. I push the suit back toward the airlock. The helmet lamps are my only light, so I position the suit so the lights point down the tunnel.

I float to the mystery wall and reach out, but stop. I can feel the heat even from a few inches away. Eridians like it hot.

In fact, I’m starting to sweat. The tunnel walls are heating my air. It’s uncomfortable, but not too bad. I can open the Hail Mary ’s inner airlock door if I want the climate control to take over. Then our systems can fight it out. They’ll keep the hot side hot and I’ll keep the cold side cold.

Even with sweat on my brow and the ammonia making my eyes water, I keep going. I’m too curious not to. Who could blame me?

There are at least twenty hexagons on this wall. They’re all different colors and textures and some might be see-through. I should list each one and see if I can figure out what they're made of. Looking closer, I see a seam along the edges of the hexes.

That’s when I hear a sound from the other side:

Knock, knock, knock.
