Project Hail Mary

Chapter 20

Rocky’s body warms the whole room.

I can barely move because the centrifuge is so strong.

“Nnnn!” I groan, pushing myself up from the broken monitor. I pull myself across the shards to the next monitor. I try not to lift too much of my body at once—I need to save my strength.

I slide my finger onto the monitor from the edge and tap the screen-select buttons. This is my only chance.

I remember the navigation controls. The manual-control section has a button to stop all rotation. That’s very tempting, but I can’t risk it. The fuel bay is wide open, I’ve dropped a couple of pods, and I don’t know what other damage there is. I don’t want to start any spin drives—even the small ones that control the ship's position.

I bring up the Centrifuge screen. It flashes red and white, still upset about the ship's spinning. I dismiss the warning and go into manual mode. There are many “don’t do this” messages, but I dismiss them all. Soon I can directly control the cable spools. I set them spinning at top speed.

The room spins and tilts oddly. My inner ears and my eyes don’t like the difference. I know it’s because the two halves of the ship are separating, which affects what I feel in the control room. But logic doesn’t help. I turn my head and throw up on the wall.

After a few seconds, the force drops a lot. It’s much easier to handle now. Less than 1 g, actually. All thanks to the magic of centrifuge math.

The force in a centrifuge is the opposite of the square of the radius. By spooling out the cables, I made the radius go from 20 meters (half the ship's length) to 75 meters (distance from the control room to the center with full cable extension). I don’t know how much force there was before, but now it’s one-fourteenth of what it was.

I’m still stuck against the monitor, but not as hard. I guess it’s about half a g. I can breathe again.

Everything feels backwards. I used the centrifuge in manual, so it did exactly what I told it: It extended the cables. It did not turn the crew area to face inward. The centrifuge pushes everything toward the front of the crew area. The lab is “up” from me now, and the dormitory is even farther “up.”

I don’t even know where the manual controls to turn the crew area are, and I don’t have time to find them. For now, I’ll have to work in upside-down land.

I float to the airlock and open it. Everything is a mess inside, but I don’t care. I untangle the crumpled spacesuit and take off the gloves. I put them on.

Back in the control room, I stand on the consoles (the control panels are “down” now). I hope I’m not hurting things too much. I get over Rocky’s body, grab both sides of his shell with my gloved hands, and lift.

Oh. God.

I put him back down. If I try to move him like that, I’ll hurt my back. But I did lift him, for a second. It felt like 200 pounds. Thank god we’re in one-half gravity. He’d weigh 400 pounds at full gravity.

I need more than my hands to lift him.

I take off the gloves, float back to the airlock, and throw things aside until I find the safety ropes. I wrap two ropes under Rocky’s shell and loop them over my shoulders. I burn my arms in a few places doing this, but I’ll deal with that later.

I clip each rope to itself under my armpits. This won’t be comfortable, and it won’t look cool, but my hands will be free, and I’ll be lifting with my legs.

I reach through the hatch to the lab with both hands and grab the closest rung of the ladder. It’s slow going at first. There’s no ladder in the control room. Why would there be? No one thought it would be upside down.

My shoulders scream in pain. This is not a well-designed backpack with a properly balanced load. It’s 200 pounds of alien held up by two thin straps digging into my collarbones. And I just have to hope the nylon straps don't melt from Rocky’s body heat.

I grunt and grimace, one rung at a time, until I get my feet into the lab. I use the edge of the hatch to brace my feet and pull Rocky up with the straps.

The lab is a mess. Everything is in piles all over the ceiling. Only the table and chairs are still on the floor above me—they’re bolted down. And, thankfully, most of the delicate equipment is bolted to them. However, that delicate, store-bought lab equipment wasn’t designed to be shaken like popcorn and subjected to 6 or 7 g’s. I wonder how much is completely broken.

The gravity is less up here. I’m closer to the center of the centrifuge. The higher I get the easier things will be.

I kick lab supplies and equipment out of my way and drag Rocky to the dormitory hatch. I repeat the painful process I just did a moment ago. The force is less, but it still hurts. Again, I use the hatch as a bracing point to pull Rocky into the room.

My little section of the dormitory barely fits us both. Rocky’s section is a mess, just like the lab. His workbench wasn’t bolted in place, so it’s on the ceiling now.

I drag him across the ceiling and I get up on my bunk. It has turned completely around, thanks to its rocking pivot mounting. It’s a handy platform for reaching the airlock between my zone and Rocky’s.

The airlock door sits open on my side. He used it to come save me.

“Man, why did you do that?!” I complain.

He could have let me die. He really should have. He could handle the pull of the spin easily. He could have taken his time, made some gadget, and used it to regain control of the ship. Yeah, I know, he’s a good guy and he saved my life, but this isn’t about us. He has a planet to save. Why risk his life and his whole mission for me?

The airlock door doesn’t go all the way to the ceiling, so I’ll have to play “The Floor Is Lava” to get in.

I jump into the airlock from my bunk, then use the straps to pull Rocky in with me. I start to climb back out, and that’s when I see the airlock control panel.

Or, rather, I see the smashed box that used to be the airlock control panel.

“Oh, come on!” I yell.

Both sides of the airlock had control panels, so either Rocky or I could use it as needed. But now mine are ruined—probably hit by some debris flying around during the chaos.

I have to get him back into his environment, but how? I have an idea. It’s not a good idea. There’s an emergency valve in the airlock itself that can let air in from Rocky’s side.

It’s there for a very specific situation. There’s no way I can ever go into Rocky’s area of the ship. I definitely can’t handle his environment, and my spacesuit would be crushed like a grape. But Rocky can come into my area with his homemade ball-spacesuit thing. So, just to be extra safe—in case there was an emergency while Rocky was in his ball in the airlock—there’s a valve that will let the air from his side vent in. It’s a large iron lever, so it can be moved with the magnets Rocky carries with him while in the ball.

I look at the lever in the airlock. I glance at the airlock’s door to my compartment and its spinning-wheel lock. I look back to the lever, then back to the door.

I tense my muscles and mentally count to three.

I pull the lever and jump toward my room.

Burning-hot ammonia fills the airlock and dormitory. I slam the airlock door behind me and spin the wheel lock. I hear hissing on the other side but I don’t see anything. I might never see anything again.

My eyes burn like they’re on fire. My lungs feel like a hundred knives are dancing. My skin is numb all along my left side. And my nose—forget it. The smell is so strong my sense of smell just quits.

My throat completely closes. My body wants nothing to do with the ammonia.

“Com…” I gasp. “Com…pu…ter…”

I want to die. Pain is everywhere. I climb into my bunk.

“Help!” I gasp.

“Multiple injuries,” says the computer. “Too much eye mucus. Blood around the mouth, second-degree burns. Trouble breathing. Triage result: insert breathing tube.”

The mechanical arms, which thankfully don’t seem to mind being upside down, grab me and something is shoved hard down my throat. I feel a poke on my good arm.

“IV fluids and sedation,” the computer reports.

And then I'm out cold.

—

I wake up covered in medical stuff and pain.

There's an oxygen mask on my face. My right arm has an IV, and my left arm is bandaged from wrist to shoulder. It hurts a lot.

Everything else hurts too. Especially my eyes.

But at least I can see. That's good.

"Computer," I say with a rough voice. "How long have I been asleep?"

"You were unconscious for six hours, seventeen minutes."

I take a deep breath. My lungs feel like they're coated in tar. Probably phlegm or some other gunk. I look over to Rocky's area. He's right where I left him in the airlock.

How can I tell if an Eridian is dead? When Rocky sleeps, he doesn't move at all. But that's probably also what happens when an Eridian dies.

I see a pulse-ox monitor on my right index finger.

“Compu—” I cough. “Computer: What is my blood oxygen level?”

“Ninety-one percent.”

“That’s good enough.” I take off the mask and sit up in bed. My bandaged arm hurts with every move. I pull the various things off my body.

I open and close my left hand. It’s working. The muscles are only a little sore.

I got hit with a quick burst of very hot, very high-pressure ammonia. I probably have chemical burns in my lungs and on my eyes. And probably a physical burn on my arm. My left side took the worst of the blast.

Twenty-nine atmospheres of pressure at 210 degrees Celsius (over 400 degrees Fahrenheit!). That must be what a grenade feels like. On another note: It’s lucky we didn’t crash into the planet with no one steering.

The ship is either in a stable orbit or we escaped Adrian’s gravity completely. I shake my head. It’s ridiculous how much power I have in the fuel bay. To not even know if I’m still near a planet …wow.

I’m lucky to be alive. There’s no other way to say it. Anything I do after this is a gift from the universe. I step off the bed and stand in front of the airlock. Gravity is still at one-half g and everything is still upside down.

What can I do for Rocky?

I sit on the floor facing him. I touch the airlock wall. That seems too dramatic, so I pull my hand back. Okay, I know some basic Eridian biology. But that doesn’t make me a doctor.

I grab a tablet and look through my notes. I don’t remember everything he said, but at least I wrote a lot down.

When badly hurt, an Eridian body will shut down to try to heal itself. I hope Rocky’s cells are doing their job in there. And I hope they can fix damage from: (1) dropping the air pressure way down, (2) suddenly being exposed to oxygen, and (3) being almost 200 degrees colder than normal.

I stop worrying and go back to my notes.

“Ah, here!” I say.

Here’s what I need: The capillaries in his shell radiator are made of metal alloys without oxygen. His mercury blood flows through these tubes, and air passes over them. In Erid’s air, which has no oxygen, this makes sense. In our air, it’s a fire hazard.

Oxygen just went over very hot metal pipes as thin as hair. They burned. That’s the smoke I saw from Rocky’s vents. His radiator was on fire.

Jesus.

The whole thing must be full of soot and ash. And the capillaries will be covered in oxides, which stop heat from flowing. Oxides don’t conduct electricity. The worst thing that could happen.

Okay. If he’s dead, he’s dead. I can’t hurt him anymore. But if he’s alive, I have to help. There’s no reason not to try.

But what do I do?

—

So many pressures. So many temperatures. So many air mixtures. I have to keep track of them all. My own space, Rocky’s space, and now the Adrian Astrophage breeding space too.

But first: gravity. I’m tired of living in The Poseidon Adventure. Time to fix this ship.

I go back “down” to the control room. The center panel is wrecked, but the others work fine. And they can be swapped out anyway. I’ll put a new one in the middle when I have time.

I pull up the Centrifuge screen and look around at the controls. I finally find the manual controls for the crew-compartment rotation. They were hidden pretty deep in the options; I’m glad I didn’t try to find them during the crisis.

I tell the crew compartment to rotate. Very, very slowly. I set the speed at 1 degree per second. It takes three minutes to turn around. And I hear a lot of thumps, clangs, and crashes from the lab. I don’t care about any of that. I just want to make sure Rocky doesn’t get hurt more. This slow speed should make his body slide along the airlock ceiling, then along the wall, and finally to the floor. That’s the plan, anyway.

Once the rotation is done, things feel normal again, but at half a g. I go back down to the dormitory to check on Rocky. He’s now on the airlock floor, and still right-side up. Good. He slid instead of tumbled.

I really want to work on Rocky, but I need to be sure the adventure that might have killed him wasn’t pointless. I grab the sample container from the ship’s airlock. I’m almost glad I left it there. The EVA suit it was packed with cushioned it from the crazy, sudden speed changes.

Rocky was smart enough to put readouts on the sampler to tell us the temperature and pressure inside. They’re analog dials with numbers in Eridian base-six. But I’ve seen enough of those to be able to understand them. The inside of the ball is minus 51 degrees Celsius with a pressure of 0.02 atmospheres. And I know from my earlier spectrometry test what the air is made of.

Okay, that’s the environment I need to recreate.

I sort through what’s left of the lab. It’s slow because I can barely use my left arm. But I can use it to help move things, at least. Just no heavy lifting for now.

I find a vacuum container that’s only a little broken. It’s a drum-shaped glass cylinder about a foot across. I patch up the crack with epoxy and test it. It can pump the air out and keep a vacuum. If it can keep a vacuum, it can keep 0.02 atmospheres.

I put the sample container inside.

The chemical cabinet is still firmly attached to the wall. I open it. Everything is jumbled inside, of course, but most containers look undamaged. I grab the small vial of Earth Astrophage.

There’s about a gram in there, part of the supplies for testing. I can always get more if I need it. All I have to do is cut any of the Astrophage coolant lines in the hull. But there’s no need for that now.

The sample is an oily sludge at the bottom of the vial. I open the vial and scoop it up with a cotton swab. (That gram of Astrophage has 100 trillion Joules of energy. Best not to think about it.)

I smear the Astrophage along the inside of the vacuum chamber and drop the cotton swab in next to the sample probe.

I remove all the air from the vacuum chamber.

The chemistry supplies include several small tanks of gases. Luckily, steel tanks are strong, so they survived the rough ride we just had. I add gases into the vacuum chamber, one at a time, through the input valve. I want to copy Adrian’s atmosphere. I add carbon dioxide, methane, and even argon. I don’t think the argon will matter—it’s a noble gas, so it shouldn’t react with anything. But that’s what I used to think about xenon, and that turned out to be wrong.

I can’t chill the air in there to minus 50 degrees, so I’ll just have to hope the life inside can handle Earth room temperature.

I hear a click just as I finish adding the argon. It’s the sampler. Just like Rocky designed them, the small valves opened when the outside pressure matched the pressure where Astrophage breed on Adrian. Good old Rocky. Best engineer I’ve ever known.

Okay. I’ve made the sample as safe as possible. The air is as close to its natural environment as I could make it, and there’s plenty of Astrophage to eat. If there are any tiny predators in there, they should be doing well.

I wipe my forehead with my bandaged arm, and immediately regret it. I wince in pain.

“How hard is it, Ryland?!” I snap to myself. “Stop using your burned arm!”

I climb back down the ladder to the dormitory.

“Computer: painkillers.”

The arms reach up and hand me a paper cup with two pills in it and a cup of water. I take the pills without even checking what they are.

I look back at my friend and try to think of a plan….

—

It’s been over a day since I pushed Rocky into that airlock, and he still hasn’t moved. But I haven’t been doing nothing. I’ve been busy making inventions in the lab. Making gadgets like this is usually Rocky’s thing, but I’m trying my best.

I thought about a lot of different ways to help. But I think I should let Rocky’s body heal itself as much as it can. I don’t want to operate on a human, especially not an Eridian. His body should know what to do. I just have to let it.

That doesn’t mean I’m going to do nothing, though. I have an idea about what’s happening. And if I’m wrong, what I want to do won’t hurt him.

Right now, there’s a lot of soot and other stuff from the explosion in his radiator organ. So it probably isn’t working well. If he’s alive, it’ll take his body a long time to clean it out. Maybe too long.

So maybe I can help?

I hold the box in my hand. It’s closed on five sides, with one side open. The walls are 4-inch-thick steel. It took me all day to fix the mill and get it working, but once I did, making this box was easy.

Inside is a strong air pump. That’s all. It can shoot air out really hard. I tested it in the lab, and it blew a hole in a thin sheet of aluminum from a foot away. It really works. I wish I could say I made it all myself, but I only made the box. The pump is from a high-pressure tank.

Also in the box are a battery, a camera, some stepper motors, and a drill. I need all these things for my plan to work.

I've cleaned up the lab, a bit. Most of the equipment is broken, but some might be repairable. I go to the other side of the table, where I have another experiment.

I have a small piece of xenonite—some scraps from when we made two hundred thousand chain links. I used a lot of epoxy to glue it to the end of a rough drill bit. It's been drying for over an hour. It should be ready.

I pick up the bit, and the xenonite stays attached. I try as hard as I can to pull them apart. I can't.

I nod and smile. This might work.

I do a few more tests with the box. My remote control for the motors works okay. It's not really remote control. It's a set of switches attached to a plastic container lid. Wires from the switches go through a small hole in the steel, which is then filled with resin. I can turn the power on or off to any of the parts inside. That's my "remote control." I just hope the motors can handle high heat or ammonia.

I take everything to the dormitory and prepare the epoxy. I mix it and put a lot of it on the edges of the open side of the steel box. I press the box against the airlock wall and hold it there. Then I just stand there for ten minutes, holding the box in place. I could have taped it to the wall while the epoxy dried, but I need a really good seal and don't want to risk it. My hands are better clamps than any tool I have in the lab.

I carefully let go of the box and wait for it to fall. It doesn't. I tap it a few times, and it seems pretty secure.

It's five-minute epoxy, but I'll wait an hour for it to fully dry.

I go back to the lab. Why not, right? Let's see what's happening in my little alien terrarium.

Not much, really. I don’t know what I thought would happen. Maybe little flying saucers zipping around in the room?

But the cylinder looks just like it did before. The sampler is where I left it. The Astrophage smear hasn't changed. The cotton swab is…

Wait…

I crouch down and sit. I peer into the chamber. The cotton swab is different. Just a bit. It’s…fuzzier.

Cool! Maybe there’s something on it I can check out. I just need to put it under a microscope to—

Oh.

It hits me. I have no way to take samples out. I totally forgot about that part.

“Idiot!” I hit my forehead.

I rub my eyes. Between the burn pain and the druggy feeling from the painkillers, it’s hard to focus. And I’m tired. I learned this in grad school: When you’re super tired, know you’re super tired. Don’t try to figure things out then. I have a sealed container that I need to open at some point. I’ll figure out how later.

I get out my tablet and take pictures of the container. Science rule number 1: If something is changing on its own, record it.

To be more scientific, I point a webcam at the experiment and set the computer to take a time-lapse at one frame per second. If anything is happening slowly, I want to know.

I go back to the control room. Where are we, anyway?

After working with the Nav console, I learn we’re still in orbit. It’s somewhat stable. The orbit will likely decay over time. But there's no immediate worry.

I check all the ship’s systems and run as many diagnostics as I can. The ship did well, even though it wasn't designed for this.

The two fuel bays I got rid of are gone, but the other seven seem fine. The diagnostics test shows some cracks in the hull here and there. But they all seem to be inside. None are on the outside, which is good. I don’t want my Astrophage to see Adrian again.

One of the tiny leaks is marked in red. I look closer. The computer is worried about where the leak is. It’s in the wall between the fuel area and the edge of the pressure vessel. I understand why it's a concern.

The wall is between the storage bay below the dormitory and Fuel Bay 4. I go to take a look.

Rocky still hasn’t moved. Not surprising. My steel box is still where I left it. I could use it now, but I'm going to wait the full hour.

I open the storage panels and take out a bunch of boxes. I climb into the storage area with a flashlight and toolkit. It’s cramped—less than 3 feet tall. I have to crawl around in there for about twenty minutes before I find the leak. I only see it because there’s a little frost around the edges. Air escaping into a vacuum gets very cold quickly. The ice probably helped slow the leak.

But it didn't really matter. The leak is so small it would take weeks to cause a problem. And the ship probably has extra air in tanks anyway. Still, there’s no reason to let it leak. I put a lot of epoxy on a small metal patch and seal the leak. I have to hold it for more than five minutes before it hardens. Epoxy takes a long time to harden when it’s cold, and the wall is below freezing there because of the leak. I thought about getting a heat gun from the lab but…that’s too much effort. I just hold the patch longer. It takes about fifteen minutes.

I climb back down and wince the whole time. My arm hurts constantly now. It’s a steady sting. It’s been less than an hour, but the painkillers aren’t working anymore.

“Computer! Painkillers!”

“Another dose available in three hours and four minutes.”

I frown. “Computer: What time is it?”

“Seven-fifteen p.m., Moscow Standard Time.”

“Computer: Set time to eleven p.m. Moscow Standard Time.”

“Clock set complete.”

“Computer: Painkillers.”

The arms hand me a package of pills and a bag of water. I swallow them down. What a dumb system. Astronauts trusted to save the world but not to manage their own painkiller doses? Dumb.

Okay. It’s been long enough. I turn back to the box.

First, I need to drill a hole in the xenonite. If something goes wrong, that's when things will get really bad. The plan is for the drill inside the box to make a hole in the xenonite, and for the box to hold in the pressure that bursts out. But you never know. The box might not be attached well enough.

I put on a medical breathing mask and eye protection. If superheated, high-pressure ammonia shoots into this room, I need to survive.

Earlier, I filed down a metal rod to make a spike. Its size is a bit bigger than the drill bit I have ready in the steel box. I hold the spike and hammer, ready to use them. If the pressure blows the box off, I'll hammer the spike into the hole, hoping it seals it.

Of course, the pressure might not blow the box off completely. It might just leak out around the edges of the glue. If that happens, I'll have to hit the box with the hammer until it comes off, then push in the spike.

Yes, it’s incredibly dangerous. But I don’t know if Rocky will survive without help. Maybe I’m being emotional instead of thinking clearly. But who cares?

I grip the hammer and spike. Then I turn on the drill.

It takes so long for the drill to get through the xenonite that I actually calm down from being bored. It’s only 1 centimeter, but it’s like trying to grind down diamond. I’m lucky the drill bit is strong enough to do anything at all. The camera inside shows slow but steady progress. Instead of drilling like wood or metal, this is more like glass. It breaks off in small pieces.

Finally, the drill bit breaks through to the other side. The pressure immediately shoots it back into the box, bending it sideways. There’s a whump as Eridian air rushes into the small box. I squint. After a few seconds, I open my eyes again.

If the box was going to blow off, it would have happened then. The seal held. For now, at least. I sigh in relief.

But I don’t take off the mask or goggles. You never know when the seal might break.

I check the camera screen. This will need careful aim, so I was very smart to make sure a camera could—

The camera feed is dead.

My wrist hurts, and I pull it away.

Oh. Right. Webcams aren’t made to work at 210 degrees Celsius and 29 atmospheres. And my solid steel box is, well, solid steel. Steel is a great heat conductor. I can’t even touch it now, it’s so hot.

I’m still stupid. First the Adrian sample container, and now this. I want to sleep, but Rocky is more important. At least being stupid isn’t forever. I’ll keep going. I know I shouldn’t, but I’m too stupid to care.

Okay, the camera is dead. I can’t see into the box. But I can still see Rocky in the airlock because the xenonite is clear. I’ll have to work with what I have.

I start the high-pressure pump. It still works—at least, it’s making noise. It should be shooting a very high-pressure jet of air toward Rocky. At 29 atmospheres, air acts almost like water. You can really push things around with it. But ammonia is clear. So I have no idea where it’s going.

I change the angle of the jet with the servo controls. Are they working? I don’t know. The pump is too loud for me to hear if the servos are doing anything. I sweep left and right, moving down and up a little bit at a time.

Finally, I see something. One of the levers in the airlock wiggles a bit. I focus on it. It gets pushed back several inches.

“Gotcha!” I say.

Now I know where it’s aimed. I guess a bit and aim for Rocky’s shell vents. Nothing happens, so I search in a grid pattern, back and forth, up and down, until I get one.

And wow, what a result!

I hit the right spot. Suddenly, Rocky’s shell vents puff out black smoke. The gross dust and bits that gathered when he was on fire. It feels really good. Like when you blow an air duster into an old computer.

I sweep back and forth, trying to hit each vent. The later ones don’t make as much of a show as the first one. I think they all go to the same place—like a person’s mouth and nose. Several openings for backup and safety.

After a few minutes, no more dirty dust comes out. I turn off the pump.

“Well, buddy,” I say. “I’ve done what I can. I just hope you can do the rest.”

I spend the rest of the day working on a second and third box to hold everything. I glue them over my device. The Eridian air will have to get through three seals to get into my space now. That’s all I can do.

I hope Rocky wakes up.
