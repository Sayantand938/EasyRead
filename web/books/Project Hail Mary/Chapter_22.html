Project Hail Mary

Chapter 22

Total darkness.

No lights. No monitor glow. Not even the LEDs on the lab equipment.

“Okay, stay calm,” I say. “Stay calm.”

“Why not be calm, question?” Rocky asks.

Well, of course he didn’t notice the lights go out. He doesn’t have eyes. “The ship just shut down. Everything stopped working.”

Rocky scuttles a bit in his tunnel. “Your equipment quiet now. My equipment still working.”

“Your equipment gets electricity from your generator. Mine’s powered by my ship. All the lights are off. Nothing is working at all!”

“This is bad, question?”

“Yes, it’s bad! For one thing, I can’t see!”

“Why ship turn off, question?”

“I don’t know,” I say. “Do you have a light? Something you can shine through the xenonite to see me?”

“No. Why would I have light, question?”

I fumble in the darkness, feeling around the lab. “Where’s the ladder to the control room?”

“Left. More left. Keep going…yes…reach forward…”

I grab a rung. “Thanks.”

“Amaze. Humans helpless without light.”

“Yes,” I say. “Come to the control room.”

“Yes.” I hear him scurry through his tunnel.

I climb up and it’s just as dark. The whole control room is dead. The monitors are off. Even the airlock window doesn't help—that part of the ship is facing away from Tau Ceti right now.

“Control room also have no light, question?” Rocky’s voice says—probably from his bulb in the ceiling.

“Nothing—wait…I see something….”

In one corner of one panel, there’s a small red LED. It’s glowing, but not very brightly. I sit in the pilot’s seat and squint at the control. The seat wobbles a bit. I didn’t do a great job repairing it, but it’s anchored to the floor again, at least.

Instead of the usual flat-panel displays all over the control room, this one little section has real buttons and an LCD display nearby. The light is coming from a button.

Of course, I push it. What else would I do?

The LCD display lights up. Some very pixelated text appears, saying: PRIMARY GENERATOR: OFFLINE. SECONDARY GENERATOR: OFFLINE. EMERGENCY BATTERIES: 100%.

“Okay, how do I use the batteries…?” I mumble.

“Progress, question?”

“Hang on.” I look all around the LCD panel until I finally see it. A little switch, covered by a plastic safety shield. It’s labeled “Batt.” It’ll have to do. I lift the shield and flip it.

Dim LEDs light up the control room—not nearly as well as the normal lights do. The smallest control screen—and only that screen—lights up. The Hail Mary mission patch is on the center of the screen and the words “Loading Operating System…” appear at the bottom.

“It’s working a bit,” I say. “My backup battery is on. But the generators are off.”

“Why not work, question?”

“I don’t know.”

“Your air okay, question? No power, no air to breathe. Humans breathe out carbon dioxide. You use all air and get hurt, question?”

“It’s okay,” I say. “The ship is big. It will take a long time for the air to go bad. I need to find out why this happened first.”

“Machines break. Show me. I fix.”

That’s a good idea. Rocky can do almost anything. Maybe he’s special, or all Eridians are like that. Either way, I’m very lucky. But can he fix human tech?

“Maybe. But first I need to know why both generators stopped at the same time.”

“Good question. More important: Can you fly ship without power, question?”

“No. I need power to do anything.”

“Then, most important: How long until orbit falls apart, question?”

I blink a couple of times. “I…don’t know.”

“Work fast.”

“Yeah.” I point at the screen. “First I have to wait for my computer to start up.”

“Hurry.”

“Okay, I’ll wait faster.”

“Sarcasm.”

The computer finishes starting up and shows a screen I’ve never seen. I can tell it’s bad news, because the word “TROUBLE” is in big letters at the top.

The nice buttons and tools from before the blackout are gone. This screen is just three rows of white text on a black background. The left is all Chinese, the middle is Russian, and the right is English.

I guess when things are normal, the ship changes the language based on who’s reading the screen. And this “safe boot” screen doesn’t know who will read it, so it’s in all our languages.

“What's going on?”

“This screen showed up with some information.”

“What’s wrong?”

“Let me read!”

Rocky can be a real pain when he’s worried. I read the status report.

EMERGENCY POWER: ONLINE

BATTERY: 100%

ESTIMATED TIME REMAINING: 04D, 16H, 17M

SABATIER LIFE SUPPORT: OFFLINE

CHEMICAL ABSORPTION LIFE SUPPORT: ONLINE. !!!LIMITED DURATION, NON-RENEWABLE!!!

TEMPERATURE CONTROL: OFFLINE

TEMPERATURE: 22°C

PRESSURE: 40,071 PA

“The ship’s keeping me alive, but not doing anything else.”

“Give me generator. I fix.”

“First I need to find it,” I say.

Rocky slumps. “You don't know where ship parts are?!”

“The computer has all that! I can’t remember everything!”

“Human brain useless!”

“Oh, shut up!”

I climb down the ladder to the lab. The emergency lights are on in here too. Rocky follows along in his tunnel.

I reach down, grab my tool bag, and keep going to the next ladder. He keeps following me.

“Where you go, question?”

“The storage area. It’s the only place I haven’t completely searched. And it’s the very bottom of the crew area. If the generator is easy for the crew to get to, that’s where it’ll be.”

Once in the dormitory, I crawl into the storage space. My arm hurts. I climb around to check the wall with the fuel bay. My arm hurts more.

By now, my arm just always hurts, so I try to ignore it. But no more painkillers. They just make me too dumb. I lie back in the storage area and let the pain go down a bit. There must be access panels in here, right? I can’t remember exactly how the ship is laid out, but important equipment is probably inside the pressurized area. For this very reason. Right?

How do I find it, though? I’d need x-ray vision to know where—oh, hey!

“Rocky! Are there any doors in here?”

He is silent for a moment. He taps on the wall a few times. “Six small doors.”

“Six?! Ugh. Tell me where the first one is.” I put my hand on the compartment ceiling.

“Move hand toward your feet and left…”

I follow his directions to the first door. Man, they’re hard to see. The emergency lights in the dormitory are weak to begin with, and very little light gets into the compartment. It's dim.

The panel is held by a simple flat-head screw that controls a latch. I turn it with a short screwdriver from my toolkit. The panel opens to show a pipe with a valve on it. The label reads PRIMARY OXYGEN SHUTOFF. Definitely don’t want to touch that. I close the cabinet.

“Next door.”

One by one, he leads me to each door and I check what’s behind it. I know he can sense the shapes behind the doors with sonar, but that’s no good. I’d rather just see what’s there than have him describe what he senses in our limited way of talking.

Behind the fourth door, I find it.

It’s a lot smaller than I expect. The whole space is about 1 cubic foot. The generator is in a black case with an odd shape, and I only know it’s a generator because of the label. I see two thick pipes with shutoff valves, and some normal-looking electrical wires.

“Found it,” I say.

“Good,” Rocky’s voice comes from the dormitory. “Take it out and give it to me.”

“I want to look at it first.”

“You not good at this. I fix.”

“The generator might not work in your environment!”

“Mmm,” he grumbles.

“If I can’t fix it, you can tell me how.”

“Mmm.”

The two pipes with shutoff valves must be the Astrophage supply lines. I look closer in the cubby and see labels. One says “fuel” and the other says “waste.” Easy enough.

I use a wrench to unscrew the hose on the “waste” line. Right when it comes loose, a dark liquid drips out. Not much—just what was between the shutoff valve and my end of the hose. It must be the fluid we use to carry away dead Astrophage. I got some on my hand—it feels slimy. Maybe it’s oil. That’s smart, actually. Any liquid will work, oil is lighter than water, and it won’t rust the pipes.

Next I unscrew the “fuel” line. It also sloshes out brown liquid. But this time, it smells terrible.

I wince and hide my face in my arm. “Ugh! God!”

“What is problem?” Rocky asks from below.

“The fuel smells bad,” I say. Eridians can't smell. But while explaining sight to Rocky took forever, smell was easy. Eridians can taste. Smell is just tasting from far away.

“Is it a natural or chemical smell?”

I sniff again, slowly. “It smells like rotten food. Astrophage usually doesn’t smell bad. It usually doesn't smell at all.”

“Astrophage is alive. Maybe Astrophage can rot.”

“Astrophage can’t rot,” I say. “How could it rot—OH NO! OH GOD NO!”

I wipe my hand across the stinky goo and climb out of the compartment. Then, keeping my gooey hand in the air and not touching anything, I climb the ladder to the lab.

Rocky clatters through his tunnel. “What’s wrong?”

“No, no, no, no…” I say, my voice cracking. My heart is pounding. I think I’m going to throw up.

I smear some goo on a glass slide and shove the slide into the microscope. There's no power for the light, so I grab a flashlight from the drawer and shine it on the plate. It’ll have to work.

I look through the lenses and my worst fears come true. “Oh God.”

“What is problem, question?!” Rocky’s voice is much higher than usual.

I grab my head, getting gross stuff on myself, but I don’t care. “Taumoeba. There are Taumoeba in the generator.”

“They hurt generator, question?” Rocky says. “Give me generator. I fix.”

“The generator works,” I say. “If there are Taumoeba in the generator, they’re also in the fuel. Taumoeba ate all the Astrophage. We have no power because we have no fuel.”

Rocky lifts his shell so fast it hits the tunnel roof. “How Taumoeba get into fuel, question?!”

“There are Taumoeba in my lab. I didn’t keep them closed off. I didn’t think about it. Some probably got out. The ship has cracks and holes since we almost died at Adrian. A small hole in a fuel line must have let Taumoeba in. It only takes one.”

“Bad! Very bad!”

I start breathing too fast. “We’re dead in space. We’re stuck here forever.”

“Not forever,” Rocky says.

I look up. “Really?”

“No. Orbit failing soon. Then we die.”

—

I spend the whole next day checking the fuel lines I can reach. It’s the same everywhere. Instead of Astrophage in oil, it’s Taumoeba and (let’s be honest) a lot of Taumoeba waste. Mostly methane with some other stuff mixed in. I guess that’s why there’s methane in Adrian’s air. Life goes on and all that.

There’s a little live Astrophage here and there, but with so much Taumoeba in the fuel, it won’t last. It’s no use trying to save it. It’d be like trying to get good meat from something rotten with botulism.

“Hopeless,” I say, dropping the latest fuel sample hard on the lab table. “The Taumoeba is everywhere.”

“I have Astrophage on my side of wall,” Rocky says. “About two hundred sixteen grams left.”

“That wouldn’t power my spin drive for long. Maybe thirty seconds. And it probably wouldn’t last. There’s Taumoeba everywhere on my side of the wall. Keep your Astrophage safe on your side.”

“I build new engine,” Rocky says. “Taumoeba change Astrophage to methane. Burn with oxygen. Make fire. Make push. Go to my ship. Lots of Astrophage there.”

“That’s…not a bad idea.” I rub my chin. “Use Taumoeba farts to move through space.”

“No understand word after Taumoeba.”

“It’s not important. Hang on, let me do the math….”

I pull up a tablet—the computer screen in the lab is still offline. I don’t remember how strong methane’s push is, but I do know that a hydrogen-oxygen reaction is about 450 seconds. Let’s say that’s the best it could be. I had 20,000 kilograms of Astrophage, so let’s pretend it’s all methane now. The ship weighs about 100,000 kilograms without fuel. I don’t even know if I have enough oxygen for this, but let’s ignore that for now….

It’s hard to concentrate. I’m tired and I know it.

I type away on the calculator app, then shake my head. “It’s no good. The ship would only get less than 800 meters per second of speed. We can’t escape Adrian’s gravity with that, much less cross 150 million kilometers of the Tau Ceti system.”

“Bad.”

I drop the tablet on the table and rub my eyes. “Yes. Bad.”

He clicks along his tunnel to hover above me. “Give me generator.”

I slump my shoulders. “Why? What good would it do?”

“I clean and sterilize. Remove all Taumoeba. I make tiny fuel tank with my Astrophage. Seal generator airtight. Give back to you. You hook up to ship. Power restored.”

I rub my aching arm. “Yeah. It’s a good idea. If the generator doesn’t melt in your air.”

“If melt, I fix.”

A few hundred grams of Astrophage isn’t enough to fly around the galaxy, but it’s more than enough to power the ship’s electrical system for…I don’t know…at least the rest of my life.

“Okay. Yeah. That’s a good idea. At least we’ll have the ship back online.”

“Yes.”

I trudge to the hatch. “I’ll get the generator.”

I really shouldn’t be using tools feeling like this, but I keep going. I go back to the dormitory, get into the crawlspace, and detach the generator. Or maybe it’s the backup generator. I don’t know. Either way, it turns Astrophage into electricity and that’s what matters.

I get back into the dormitory and put the generator in our airlock there. Rocky cycles the airlock and brings the generator to his workbench. Two claws get to work on it right away. A third points to my bunk. “I work on this now. You sleep.”

“Make sure you don’t get Taumoeba in your Astrophage over there!”

“My Astrophage in sealed xenonite container. Is safe. You sleep now.”

Everything aches, especially my bandaged arm. “I can’t sleep.”

He points more firmly. “You tell me humans need to sleep eight hours every sixteen hours. You have not slept for thirty-one hours. You sleep now.”

I sit on my bunk and sigh. “You’re right. I should at least try. It’s been a hard day. Night. Whatever. A hard day’s night.” I lie back in the bunk and pull the blanket over me.

“That sentence makes no sense.”

“It’s an Earth saying. From a song.” I close my eyes and mumble. “…and I’ve been working like a dog…”

A moment passes while I drift off…

“Whoa!” I shoot straight up. “The beetles!”

Rocky is surprised enough to drop the generator. “What is the problem?”

“Not a problem! A solution!” I leap to my feet. “The beetles! My ship has four smaller ships aboard called beetles! They’re made to take information back to Earth!”

“You told me this before,” Rocky says. “But they use the same fuel, correct? All the astrophage are dead now.”

I shake my head. “They use Astrophage, yeah, but each beetle is its own sealed unit. They don’t share air, fuel, or anything with Hail Mary. And each beetle has 120 kilograms of fuel! We have plenty of Astrophage!”

Rocky waves his arms in the air. “Enough to get us to my ship! Good news! Good good good!”

I wave my arms in the air too. “Maybe we won’t die here after all! I need to go outside to get beetles. I’ll be right back.” I hop off the bunk and head to the ladder.

“No!” Rocky says. He skitters over to the partition and taps the divider. “You sleep. Humans don't work well when tired. Going outside is dangerous. Sleep first. Outside later.”

I roll my eyes. “All right, all right.”

He points back to my bunk. “Sleep.”

“Yes, Mom.”

“Sarcasm. You sleep. I watch.”

“This doesn’t seem like a good idea anymore,” I say into my radio.

“Do task,” Rocky replies mercilessly.

I slept well and woke up ready to face the day. I had a nice breakfast. I stretched. Rocky gave me a sealed, fully functional generator that will last basically forever. I installed it and got the ship’s power back on without a problem.

Rocky and I talked about the best way to use the beetles to get back to the Blip-A. Everything seemed like a good idea until just now.

I stand in the airlock, all suited up for a spacewalk, looking out onto the vast emptiness of space. Planet Adrian reflects its pale-green light at me, lighting up the ship. Then it drifts out of view. I’m in darkness. But not for long. Because the planet shows up again at the top of my vision twelve seconds later.

The Hail Mary is still spinning. That’s a problem.

The ship has little Astrophage-powered thrusters on the sides to spin up and spin down for the artificial gravity. They don’t work, of course. They’re full of Taumoeba poop just like everything else. So here I am on another spacewalk that has to deal with gravity. But instead of Adrian’s gravity, it’s centripetal force threatening to throw me off into space.

One death is as good as another. So why is this worse than my little Adrian sample adventure? Because this time I have to balance on the nose of the ship. One wrong move could lead to death.

When I got the sampler, I stayed close to the hull, kept tethered, and had lots of handholds all around just in case I slipped.

But the beetles are stored in the nose of the ship.

The nose points toward the other side of the ship because of how the centrifuge works. This puts the beetles at the “top” of the crew area, as far as the artificial gravity is concerned. I need to go up there, open the nose, and get the small ships out. All while hoping I don’t slip. There are no places to attach a safety line at the nose. So, I’ll have to clip onto a spot lower down. This means if I fall, I’ll pick up speed before the line stops me. Will it hold? If not, the centrifuge will throw me into space, and I’ll become Adrian’s newest moon.

I check the safety lines four times. I used two of them, just to be safe. They’re firmly attached to a strong point in the airlock and to my suit. They should be able to handle the force if I fall.

“Should.”

I step out, grab the top of the airlock, and pull myself up. I could never do this with all my equipment on at full gravity.

The angle of the nose cone isn’t too steep, so I don’t slide off. I check the lines again, then crawl up the nose toward the top. The centrifuge pushes me to the side as I move. I have to stop every few feet and let friction with the ship’s surface stop my sideways movement.

“Status, question?”

“Making progress,” I say.

“Good.”

I reach the nose. The artificial gravity is weakest here, since it’s closest to the center of the spinning. That’s a nice little bonus.

The universe slowly spins around me every twenty-five seconds. For half the time, Adrian fills my entire view below. Then I see Tau Ceti’s bright light for a few seconds. Then nothing. It’s a little unsettling but not too bad. Just a bit annoying.

The beetle hatch is right where it should be. I’ll have to be careful here. I don’t want to break anything.

This was meant to be a suicide mission. They didn’t care if the Hail Mary made it back. The mechanism inside has explosives to blow off this hatch. Then the beetles can launch and find their way back to Earth. Good system, but I need this hatch to be undamaged for when I go home. It’s all about the aerodynamics.

Yes, aerodynamics.

The Hail Mary has always looked like something from a Heinlein novel. Shiny silver, smooth hull, sharp nose cone. Why bother with that for a ship that will never be in an atmosphere?

Because of the stuff between stars. There’s a very small amount of hydrogen and helium floating around in space. It’s about one atom per cubic centimeter, but when you’re traveling close to the speed of light, that adds up. Not only because you’re hitting many atoms, but also because those atoms, from your point of view, weigh more than normal. Relativistic physics is strange.

In short: I need the nose undamaged.

The whole panel and explosive device is attached to the hull with six hex bolts. I get a socket wrench from my tool belt and start working.

As soon as I unscrew the first bolt, it slides down the nose cone and falls into the endless distance.

“Um…” I say. “Rocky, you can make screws, right?”

“Yes. Easy. Why ask?”

“I dropped one.”

“Hold screws better.”

“How?”

“Use hand.”

“My hand’s busy with the wrench.”

“Use second hand.”

“My other hand’s on the hull to keep me steady.”

“Use third han—hmm. Get beetles. I’ll make new screws.”

“Okay.”

I start on the second bolt. This time I’m very careful. I stop using the wrench halfway and finish by hand. The thick fingers of the spacesuit are terrible for this. It takes ten minutes just for this one bolt. But I finish it and, most important, I don’t drop it.

I put it in a pouch on my suit. Now Rocky will know what I need him to copy.

I unscrew the next four bolts with the wrench and let them drop. I guess they’ll orbit Adrian for a while, but not forever. The slight drag up here will gradually slow them until they fall into Adrian’s atmosphere and burn up.

One bolt is left. But first, I lift the opposite corner of the assembly enough to create a small gap. I slip a tether through an empty bolt hole and clip it to itself. Then I clip the other end of the tether to my belt. Now I have four tethers attached to me. And I like it that way. I may look like space Spider-Man, but who cares?

I still have two more tethers coiled on my tool belt ready if needed. You can't have too many tethers.

I unscrew the last bolt and the assembly slides down the nose. I let it go past me and it stops at the end of the tether. It bounces a few times and hits the hull, then sways.

I look into the compartment. The beetles are where they should be, each in its own slot. The four little ships are identical except for a small name on each rounded fuel bay. They’re labeled “John,” “Paul,” “George,” and “Ringo,” of course.

“Status, question?”

“Recovering beetles.”

I start with John. A small clamp holds it in place, but I easily force it open. Behind the probe is a compressed air cylinder with a nozzle pointing out. That’s how they're launched. They need to be far from the ship before they start their spin drives. Even a cute little spin drive will vaporize anything behind it.

John comes out easily. The probe is bigger than I remember—almost the size of a suitcase. Of course, everything seems bigger when you’re doing an EVA with clumsy gloves.

Ol’ John is really heavy, too. I don’t know if I could even lift it in Earth’s gravity. I tie it to the backup tether, then reach in to get Paul.

—

Rocky can work fast when he needs to. And he needs to now.

We’re in a shaky orbit around Adrian. Now that the computers and guidance systems are back on, I can see the orbit. It’s not good. Our orbit is still very oval-shaped, and the closest part of it is way too close to the planet.

Every ninety minutes, we touch the very top of the atmosphere. It’s barely an atmosphere at that height. Just a few stray air molecules floating around. But it’s enough to slow us down just a little bit. That slowdown makes us go a little deeper into the atmosphere the next time around. You can see what’s going to happen.

We brush the atmosphere every ninety minutes. And I really don’t know how many times we can do that without problems. For some reason, the computer doesn’t have information for “oddly oval orbits around the planet Adrian.”

So yeah. Rocky is in a hurry.

It takes him just two hours to take Paul apart and understand most of how he works. This wasn't easy—before we moved Paul into Rocky’s area of the ship, we had to make a special “cooling box.” The beetles have plastic parts inside that would melt in Rocky’s air. A big piece of Astrophage took care of that. Astrophage may be too hot for humans to touch, but it’s cool enough that plastic won’t melt, and of course it easily absorbs the extra heat and keeps things at 96 degrees Celsius.

Paul has a lot of electronics and wires inside. Rocky doesn’t understand that too well—Eridian electronics aren’t nearly as advanced as Earth’s. They haven’t invented the transistor yet, much less IC chips. Working with Rocky is like having the world’s best engineer from 1950 on the ship with me.

It seems strange that a species could invent interstellar travel before the transistor, but Earth invented nuclear power, television, and even launched things into space several times before the transistor.

An hour later, he's gotten around all the computer controls. He doesn't have to understand them to do it—he just needs to know which wires to put power to directly. He rigged the spin drive to be turned on by a sound-based remote control. Where humans use radio for short-range digital communication, Eridians use sound.

He does the same for Ringo and John. This time it's much faster, because there's no research needed. That leaves George unchanged. The little beetles don't have much power, so the more we use the better, but I have to stop somewhere. I want to keep one safe, unchanged, ready to do what it was originally meant to do.

Because of Rocky, I might survive this mission, but it's not certain. The Hail Mary is in bad shape. Several fuel tanks are gone, and there's damage and leaks everywhere. There are Taumoeba waiting to eat any new fuel Rocky gives me. I can think of at least a hundred ways the trip home could fail. So, before I leave, I'm going to send George off with all my data and some Taumoeba. I'd rather have kept two, but we need three beetles to aim the thrust so we can angle the ship where we need it.

Rocky passes the three changed beetles through the dormitory airlock to my side.

“You put on hull,” he says. “Aim forty-five degrees out away from center of ship.”

“Okay.” I sigh. Another EVA on a spinning ship. Great.

But what else can I do? We can't stop the rotation without thrust.

I do the EVA. The only hard part is getting to the right place. The airlock is near the front, and I need to put the beetles on the back. And the ship is now in two halves connected by only five cables. But the designers of the Hail Mary thought of this. There are loops all along the cables to attach a tether to.

I'm getting better at the strange skill of doing EVAs in non-zero gravity. And unlike my risky move on the front of the ship, the back has lots of handles. Putting the beetles on is easy. I attach them to handles on the hull to hold them still while Rocky's xenonite glue dries and holds them in place.

In the end, I have John, Paul, and Ringo spaced equally around the hull, each angled so their engine points 45 degrees away from the ship's length.

“Beetles set,” I say into my radio. “Checking damaged area.”

“Good,” Rocky replies.

I head to where the fuel tank broke. There isn’t much to see—I got rid of the bad tanks right away. A missing rectangle of hull shows the space where the tanks used to be. The area around the hole shows damage. Black scorch marks stain the shiny hull. Two panels next to it are clearly bent.

“Some panels are bent. Some have burn marks. Not too bad.”

“Good news.”

“The burn marks are strange, don’t you think? Why burn marks?”

“Lots of heat.”

“Yeah, but no oxygen. This is space. How did it burn?”

“Idea: Many Astrophage in tanks. Some probably dead. Dead Astrophage have water. Dead Astrophage not protected from heat. Water and lots of heat become hydrogen and oxygen. Oxygen, heat, and hull become burn marks.”

“Yeah,” I say. “Good idea.”

“Thank.”

I get back across the cable bridge and into the airlock without any trouble. Rocky waits for me in his ceiling bulb in the control room.

“All is well, question?”

“Yes,” I say. “Controls for John, Paul, and Ringo are good?”

He holds identical control boxes in three of his hands. Each has a wire going to a speaker/microphone on the wall attached to the hull. He taps a readout box with a fourth hand. “Communication established. All beetles work and are ready.”

I strap myself into the command chair. This next part is going to be uncomfortable.

We put the beetles at 45-degree angles from the ship's middle line so we can angle the ship as needed. It also lets us control the ship’s spin. But we can only use the beetles when the ship is in one piece. So first I have to pull the halves together.

Because rotational inertia stays the same, that means the ship is going to spin really fast. In fact, it’ll spin exactly as fast as it was when Rocky had to save me last time. We haven’t gained or lost any inertia in that time.

I bring up the Centrifuge panel on the main control screen. Well, it’s just above the original main screen. That main screen got wrecked in the Adrian adventure. But this one’s good enough.

“You ready?”

“Yes.”

“The g-forces will be strong,” I say. “Easy for you, but hard for me. I might pass out.”

“Unhealthy for human, question?” There’s a slight tremble at the end.

“A little unhealthy. If I pass out, don’t worry. Just keep the ship stable. I’ll wake up when we stop spinning.”

“Understand.” Rocky holds the three controls, ready to go.

“Okay, here goes.” I put the centrifuge into manual mode and skip three warning messages. First, I turn the crew compartment 180 degrees. Just like last time, I go slow. But unlike last time, I have everything tied down. So as the world turns around and gravity changes directions, the lab and dormitory aren’t thrown into chaos.

Now I feel half a g pushing me toward the control panels. The nose is facing away from the rest of the ship again. I tell all four spools to pull in, no matter how fast the ship is spinning. The icons on the ship show them pulling in as ordered, and the force pushing me into the restraints gets stronger.

After just ten seconds, the forces are at 6 g’s and I can barely breathe. I gasp and struggle.

“You are not healthy!” Rocky squeaks. “Undo this. We make new plan.”

I can’t speak, so I shake my head. I feel the skin of my face pull tight. I must look like a monster right now. The edges of what I see fade to black. This must be the tunnel vision I’ve heard about. It’s a good name.

The tunnel gets darker and darker until it’s all black.

I wake up a few moments later. At least, I think it’s a few moments. My arms float freely, and only my straps keep me from floating out of my chair.

“Grace! You okay?”

“Uh.” I rub my eyes. My vision is blurry, and I’m still groggy. “Yeah. What’s happening?”

“Rotation stopped,” he says. “Beetles hard to control. Correction: Beetles easy to control. Ship powered by beetles hard to control.”

“You got it done, though. Good job.”

“Thank.”

I release my straps and stretch. Nothing seems broken or hurt except my burned arm from before. It feels great to be back in zero g. I’m always achy. Lots of hard work, and I’m still healing from injuries. Getting rid of gravity puts less stress on my body.

I check the screens on the monitor. “All systems are okay. At least, nothing’s damaged more than before.”

“Good. What's the next step, question?”

“Now I do math. A lot of math. I have to figure out how long and at what angle we need to fire the engines to get back to your ship, using the beetles as engines.”

“Good.”
