Project Hail Mary

Chapter 28

It’s been three days since the Great Taumoeba Escape. I’m taking no chances.

I manually shut off all the fuel bays—completely separating each one from the fuel system. Then, one tank at a time, I opened it, took an Astrophage sample from the line, and checked it in the microscope for Taumoeba.

Thankfully, all nine tanks were clean. I turned the spin drives back on and I’m cruising along at 1.5 g’s again.

I put together a “Taumoeba alarm” to warn me if this happens again. I should have done that in the first place, but it’s easy to say that now.

It’s a slide of Astrophage—the same as I used in the Taumoeba farms—with a light on one side and a light sensor on the other. The whole system is open to the air in the lab. If Taumoeba get to that Astrophage, they’ll eat it, the slide will turn clear, and the light sensor will start beeping. So far, no beeping. The slide is still jet-black.

Now that things have calmed down and the problem is taken care of, I can start asking the big question: How did the Taumoeba get out?

I put my hands on my hips and stare at the quarantine zone.

“Which one of you did this?” I say.

None of it makes sense. The farms worked for months without any sign of a leak. The mini-farms are sealed steel capsules.

Maybe some Taumoeba had been hiding on the ship since the last time—back at Adrian. How did they not find any Astrophage until now?

No. Rocky and I learned from our experiments that Taumoeba can only live about a week without food before they starve. And they don’t do things in moderation. They either breed like crazy and eat all the Astrophage, or they aren’t there at all.

One of these containers must be leaking. I can’t just throw everything out—I need these Taumoeba to save Earth—so what do I do? I have to find out which one is the problem.

I check each farm as best I can. Since they’re in bins, I can’t use any of the controls, but I don’t need to. They’re fully automated. It’s a simple system—Rocky usually finds easy solutions to hard problems. The farm watches the air temperature inside. If it drops below 96.415 degrees Celsius, it means there’s no more Astrophage because the Taumoeba ate it. So it adds a little more Astrophage. Simple as that. And the system keeps track of how often it has to feed them. From that, it guesses how many Taumoeba are inside. It changes the Astrophage feed rate to control the population and, of course, has a display to show us the current state.

I check each farm’s displays. Each one shows 96.415 degrees Celsius with about 10 million Taumoeba. Exactly what they should show.

“Hm,” I say.

The air pressure inside those farms is much lower than the nitrogen pressure around them. If any of them had a leak, the nitrogen would get in, and the Taumoeba would all die pretty quickly. But they haven’t. And it’s been three days.

The breeder farms aren’t leaking. It must be the mini-farms. But how could a microbe get through half a centimeter of Eridian steel? Rocky knows what he’s doing, and he knows all about Eridian steel. He’d know if it wasn’t good for keeping microbes in. They don’t have Taumoeba on Erid, but they definitely have other microbes. This isn’t new to them.

All of this makes me think something that I would normally think is impossible: Rocky made an engineering mistake.

He never makes mistakes. Not when it comes to building things. He’s one of the best engineers on his whole planet! He couldn’t have messed up.

Could he?

I need solid proof.

I make more Astrophage test slides. They're really useful for finding Taumoeba and easy to make.

I start with the bin holding the two mini-farms—the ones for George and Ringo. They look sealed. They're just capsule-shaped pieces of metal. Lots of stuff happening inside, but smooth Eridian steel on the outside.

I peel the duct tape off a corner of the box, lift the lid, and drop an Astrophage slide in, then seal everything up again. Experiment number one: Make sure I didn't accidentally create some Super-Taumoeba that can live in pure nitrogen.

Another thing I've learned: Once Taumoeba get to an Astrophage slide, it'll be clear in a few hours. So I wait a few hours and the slide is still black. Okay, good. No Super-Taumoeba.

I unseal the bin, open the lid, and let it air out for a minute. Then I reseal it. The nitrogen level in there will be normal now. Much less than Taumoeba-82.5 needs to worry about. If those mini-farms are leaking, the slide will show it.

One hour, nothing happens. Two hours, nothing happens.

I take a sample of the air inside the bin just to be sure. The nitrogen level is almost zero. So that's not a problem.

I seal it up again and wait another hour. Nothing.

The mini-farms aren’t leaking. At least, not the ones for George and Ringo. Maybe the leak was in a mini-farm I already put in place.

They’re just stuck to the outside of John and Paul. They aren't protected by the beetle's shell or anything. I do the Taumoeba test again with John and Paul's bins.

I get the same result: no Taumoeba at all.

“Hm.”

Okay, time for the real test. I take John, Paul, and the two uninstalled mini-farms out of quarantine. I put them on the lab table near my Taumoeba alarm. I’m pretty sure they’re clean. But if they aren’t, I want to know fast.

I start looking at the even less likely cause: the breeder farms.

If Taumoeba can’t get out of Eridian steel, they definitely can’t get through xenonite. One centimeter of that stuff can easily hold in Rocky’s 29 atmospheres of pressure! It’s harder than diamond but not brittle.

But I need to be careful. I do the Astrophage slide test again with all ten breeder farm bins. No point in doing them one at a time. I do them all at once. Now all ten farms are in sealed bins full of normal air with an Astrophage slide inside.

It’s been a long day. Good time for a break and some sleep. I’ll leave them overnight to see what happens. I bring bedding from the dormitory to the lab. If my Taumoeba alarm goes off, I want to be sure it wakes me up. I’m too tired to find a louder way. So I’ll just put my ears closer to the lab table and go to sleep.

I drift off. It feels wrong to sleep without someone watching.

I wake up about six hours later. “Coffee.”

But the nanny robots are downstairs in the dormitory. So of course I get no response.

“Oh, right…” I sit up and stretch.

I get up and shuffle over to the quarantine zone. Let’s see how those Taumoeba farm tests are going.

I check the first farm’s Astrophage slide. It’s completely clear. So I move on to the next—

Wait. It’s clear?

“Uh…”

I’m still not fully awake. I wipe my eyes and take another look.

It’s still clear.

Taumoeba reached the slide. It escaped the breeder farm!

I rush to the Taumoeba alarm on the lab table. It isn’t beeping, but I check it anyway. The Astrophage slide inside is still black.

I take a deep breath and let it out.

“Okay…” I say.

I go back to the quarantine zone and check the other farms. Every slide is clear. The farms are leaking. They’re all leaking. The mini-farms are fine. They’re on the lab table near the Taumoeba alarm.

I rub my neck.

I’ve found the problem, but I don’t get it. Taumoeba are escaping the farms. But how? If the xenonite was cracked, the nitrogen pressure would have gotten in and killed everything. All ten farms have healthy Taumoeba. So what’s going on?

I go down to the dormitory for breakfast. I stare at the xenonite wall that used to be Rocky’s workshop. The wall is still there, but with a hole I asked for. I mainly use the area for storage.

I eat my breakfast burrito, trying not to think about getting closer to coma slurry. I stare at the hole. I pretend I’m a Taumoeba, millions of times bigger than a nitrogen atom. But I can get through a hole the nitrogen atom can’t. How? And where did the hole come from?

I’m starting to get a bad feeling. A suspicion, really.

What if Taumoeba can, for lack of a better way to put it, get around the molecules of xenonite? What if there’s no hole at all?

We often think of solid materials as magical barriers. But they’re not at the molecular level. They’re strands of molecules or atom structures, or both. When you get down to the really, really small level, solid objects are more like thick jungles than brick walls.

I can get through a jungle, no problem. I may have to climb over bushes, go around trees, and duck under branches, but I can do it.

Imagine a thousand tennis-ball launchers at the edge of that jungle aimed in random directions. How far into the jungle will the tennis balls get? Most of them won’t get past the first few trees. Some may get lucky bounces and go a little deeper in. Even fewer may get multiple lucky bounces. But soon, even the luckiest tennis ball runs out of energy.

You’d be hard-pressed to find any tennis balls 50 feet into that jungle. Now, let’s say it’s a mile wide. I can make it to the other side, but there’s just no way a tennis ball can.

That’s the difference between Taumoeba and nitrogen. The nitrogen is just moving in a line and bouncing off things like a tennis ball. It’s inactive. But Taumoeba is like me. It can react to things. It senses its environment and takes action based on what it senses. We already know it can find Astrophage and move toward it. It definitely has senses. But nitrogen atoms are controlled by randomness. They won’t “try” to do anything. I can walk uphill. But a tennis ball can only roll so far before it rolls back down.

That all seems really weird. How could Taumoeba, from the planet Adrian, know how to carefully move through xenonite, a technological invention from the planet Erid? It doesn’t make sense.

Life-forms don’t develop traits for no reason. Taumoeba lives in the upper atmosphere. Why would it develop the ability to move through dense molecular structures? What reason could there be to—

I drop my burrito.

I know the answer. I don’t want to admit it to myself. But I know the answer.

—

I go back to the lab and do a stressful experiment. The experiment itself isn’t stressful. I’m just worried that the results will be what I expect.

I still have Rocky’s AstroTorch. It’s the only thing on the ship that can get hot enough to break down xenonite. There’s plenty of xenonite around the ship, thanks to Rocky’s tunnel system. I cut into the dormitory wall. I can only cut a little bit at a time, then I have to wait for life support to cool things down. The AstroTorch makes a lot of heat.

In the end, I have four rough circles, each a couple of inches across.

Yes, inches. When I’m stressed, I go back to using imperial units. It’s hard to be an American, okay?

I take them up to the lab and set up an experiment.

I smear some Astrophage on one of the circles and put another circle on top of it. Astrophage sandwich. Delicious, but only if you can get through the xenonite “bread.” I glue the two halves together. I make another identical sandwich.

And then I make another two similar sandwiches, but instead of xenonite, I use ordinary plastic discs that I cut from some stock material.

Okay. Four Astrophage samples, sealed so nothing can get in—two with xenonite discs, two with plastic discs, all sealed with epoxy.

I get two clear, containers that can be sealed and put them on the lab table. I put one xenonite and one plastic sample in each container.

In the sample cabinet, I have some metal vials filled with natural Taumoeba. The original stuff from Adrian, not the Taumoeba-82.5 version. I put a vial in one of the containers, open it, and quickly seal the experiment. This is risky, but I know how to stop a Taumoeba outbreak if it happens. As long as I have nitrogen, I’m okay.

I go to Breeder Tank One in the quarantine zone. I use a syringe to take the Taumoeba-infected air from the bin, then immediately fill the bin with nitrogen. I tape over the hole from the syringe.

Back to the lab table, I close the other container and use the syringe to inject the Taumoeba-82.5. Again, I seal that hole with tape.

I rest my chin in my hands and look into the two boxes. “Okay, you little creeps. Let’s see what you can do…”

It takes a few hours, but I finally see results. They’re exactly what I expected and the opposite of what I wanted.

I shake my head. “Damn it.”

The xenonite-covered Astrophage in the Taumoeba-82.5 experiment is gone. The plastic-covered Astrophage hasn’t changed. In the other experiment, both Astrophage samples are fine.

What that means: The “control” samples (the plastic discs) show Taumoeba can’t get through epoxy or plastic. But the xenonite samples show something different. Taumoeba-82.5 can get through xenonite, but natural Taumoeba can’t.

“I’m so stupid!” I hit myself on the head.

I thought I was so smart. All that time in the breeder tanks. Generation after generation of Taumoeba. I used evolution to help me, right? I made Taumoeba that could resist nitrogen! I’m so great! Let me know when I can pick up my Nobel Prize!

Ugh.

Yes, I made a type of Taumoeba that could live through nitrogen. But evolution doesn’t care what I want. And it doesn’t just do one thing at a time. I bred a bunch of Taumoeba that evolved to live…in xenonite breeder tanks.

Sure, it resists nitrogen. But evolution has a clever way of working on a problem from every direction. So they not only resisted nitrogen, they figured out how to hide from nitrogen by sneaking into the xenonite itself! Why wouldn’t they?

Xenonite is a complex mix of proteins and chemicals I can’t even begin to understand. But I guess Taumoeba can find a way to get in. There’s a nitrogen disaster happening in the breeder farm. If you can get deep enough into the xenonite walls where the nitrogen can’t reach, you get to live!

Taumoeba can’t get through normal plastic. It can’t get through epoxy resin. It can’t get through glass. It can’t get through metal. I don’t even know if it could get through a ziplock bag. But thanks to me, Taumoeba-82.5 can get through xenonite.

I took a life-form I knew nothing about and used technology I didn’t understand to change it. Of course there would be unexpected results. It was incredibly arrogant of me to think I could predict everything.

I take a deep breath and let it out.

Okay, this isn’t the end of the world. Actually, it’s the opposite. This Taumoeba can get into xenonite. No problem. I’ll store it in something else. It still resists nitrogen. It doesn’t need xenonite to live. I tested it carefully in my glass lab equipment when we first found the strain. It’ll still do its job on Venus and Threeworld. Everything’s fine.

I glance back at the breeder farms.

Yeah. Fine. I’ll make a big farm out of metal. It’s not hard. I have a mill and all the materials I need. And I have plenty of time. I’ll take the working parts from a farm Rocky made. Only the outside is xenonite. Everything else is metal. I don’t need to start from scratch. I just need to put it on a different base.

“Yeah,” I tell myself. “Yeah, this is okay.”

I just need to make a box that can hold a Venus-like atmosphere. All of the hard work is already done, thanks to Rocky.

Rocky!

I feel sick all of a sudden. I have to sit down and put my head down. Rocky has the same kind of Taumoeba on his ship. It’s kept in xenonite farms like mine.

All important walls of his ship, even the fuel tanks, are made of xenonite. There’s nothing between his Taumoeba and his fuel.

“Oh…God…”
