Project Hail Mary

Chapter 27

I jump off the stool, but I don’t have a plan.

“Okay, don’t panic,” I tell myself. “Think clearly. Then act.”

The BOCOA is still hot. That means there’s a lot of live Astrophage still in it. I caught it early. That’s good. Not because of the BOCOA—it’s ruined. I’ll never be able to separate the Taumoeba from the Astrophage in there. But it means that however the Taumoeba got in there, it’s very recent and hopefully hasn’t reached the ship’s fuel.

Yes. That’s the top priority. Don’t let Taumoeba into the fuel bays. Last time they got in was because of tiny leaks in the system. But it had to have gotten there from the crew areas where I had brought it aboard. There isn’t much connection between the fuel system and the crew area. There’s just one likely place for transfer.

Life support.

If the ship is too cold, it runs air across coils filled with Astrophage to warm it up. A leak in one of those coils would do it. Luckily for me, I’ve had a big pile of 96°C Astrophage in the lab keeping the crew area so warm the ship has to use the air-conditioning system.

Okay, now I have a plan.

I hurry up the ladder to the control room. I pull up the Life Support panel and look at the logs. As I thought, the heater hasn’t been turned on in over a month. I turn off the heater completely. It shows as disabled, but I don’t trust it.

I go to the main breaker box. It’s under the pilot’s seat. I find the breaker for the heating system and switch it off.

“Okay,” I say.

I get back in the seat and check the Fuel panel. The fuel tanks all seem fine. The temperature is right. Taumoeba could quickly spread and eat everything in a fuel tank—I know that for sure. If they were affected, they’d be colder.

I pull up the spin-drive controls and shut off the engines. I feel weightless again. I probably don’t need to shut them off, but I don’t want the fuel doing anything right now. If there’s Taumoeba in a fuel line, I want it to stay there, not get pumped all over the ship.

“Okay…” I say again. “Okay…”

More thinking.

How did it get loose? I cleaned every part of this ship with nitrogen before getting any Astrophage from Rocky. The only Taumoeba on board are in the sealed mini-farms on the beetles and the sealed, xenonite breeder tanks.

No. No time for science questions. I can guess about the cause later. Right now I have an engineering problem. I wish Rocky were here.

I always wish Rocky were here.

“Nitrogen,” I say.

I don’t know how the Taumoeba got out, but I need them dead. Taumoeba-82.5 can handle 8.25 percent nitrogen at 0.02 atmospheres. Maybe a little higher. But it definitely can’t handle 100 percent nitrogen at the crew compartment’s 0.33 atmospheres. That's two hundred times the amount that would kill it.

I float to the breaker box and shut off everything for life support. Right away, the emergency alarm sounds and red lights turn on. I kick across the control room to the emergency system’s breaker box and shut those off too.

The master alarm is annoying, so I silence it on the main control panel.

I fly down to the lab and throw open my gas cylinder supply cupboard. I have about 10 kilograms of nitrogen gas in a single canister. Again, I owe my life to DuBois’s preferred way of suicide.

I don’t remember all the details about the life-support system, but I know it has manual overpressure valves. The ship simply won’t allow more than 0.33 atmospheres. If everything else fails (which it will, because I shut the emergency systems down), it’ll vent extra pressure into space.

I can’t just release the nitrogen and hope it works. I want to get rid of the oxygen first. I’m done messing around with this. I want 100 percent nitrogen in here. I want to make this ship so toxic to Taumoeba that it has no chance of living. Even if it’s hiding under some goop somewhere—I want the nitrogen to soak through it. Nitrogen everywhere. Everywhere!

I grab the nitrogen cylinder, kick off the floor, and float back up to the control room. I throw open the airlock inner door and get into the Orlan suit faster than ever before. I start everything up and don’t even bother with the self-check. No time.

I leave the inner airlock door open and turn the manual emergency valve on the outer door. The ship’s air hisses away into space. The main and emergency life-support systems are powered down. They can’t replace the lost gas.

Now I wait.

It takes a surprisingly long time for a ship to lose all its air. In movies, a small leak means instant death for everyone. Or the strong hero blocks the hole with his arm or something. But in reality, air just doesn’t move that fast.

The emergency valve on the airlock is 4 centimeters wide. That seems like a pretty big hole in your spaceship, right?

It took twenty minutes for the ship’s air pressure to drop to 10 percent of what it was. And it’s dropping very slowly now. I think it’s a logarithmic thing. So in the middle of this emergency, I just have to stand here with my tank in my hand.

“Okay. Ten percent is good enough,” I say. I close the airlock emergency valve to seal the ship again. Then I open the nitrogen tank.

So now, instead of hearing a hiss from the airlock, I hear a hiss from the nitrogen tank.

Not much different.

Again. It’s a bit of a wait. But not as long this time. Probably because the pressure inside that nitrogen tank was much higher than the pressure in the ship. Whatever. The point is, soon the ship is back to 0.33 atmospheres of pressure. But it’s almost all nitrogen.

Funny thing—I’d be perfectly fine if I took this space suit off. I’d breathe without any problems. Right up until I died. There’s nowhere near enough oxygen for me to survive.

I want that nitrogen to spread everywhere it can. I want it to get into every crack. Wherever Taumoeba are hiding, I want them found and killed. Go forth, my N2 helpers, and destroy them!

I go down to the lab and check out the BOCOA. I left in such a hurry I forgot to seal the container. Luckily, Astrophage is sticky stuff. Surface tension and inertia kept it inside. I close the lid and take it up to the airlock. I throw the whole thing out.

I probably could have saved the Astrophage that lived in the vat. I could have used nitrogen to get rid of all the tiny Taumoeba hiding inside. But why risk it? I have over 2 million kilograms of Astrophage. There’s no reason to risk the whole mission to save a few hundred.

I wait three hours. Then I turn the power back on. After a short panic, the life-support system fixes the air thanks to the ship’s large oxygen supply.

I have to find every source of Taumoeba on this ship. Ideally before the life-support system finishes pumping out the nitrogen. Why not do it before the air is back to normal? Because it’ll be easier and faster without the EVA suit. I need to use my bare hands, not my hands inside bulky gloves.

I get out of the Orlan and fly down to the lab, with the nitrogen cylinder.

First: the breeder farms.

I put each of the ten farms in big plastic bins. I add a small valve to each bin (epoxy can do anything), and pump in nitrogen. If any farms leak, the nitrogen will get in and kill everything. Any farm that’s working right—staying airtight—won’t have any issues.

The bins are already airtight, but I seal them with duct tape anyway, and add a little extra pressure. The sides bulge out. Now if any of the farms leak, I’ll see it because the bulging will disappear.

Next: the beetles and their mini-farms.

John and Paul already have their mini-farms set up. I put them in bins like the breeder farms. I was working on Ringo when things went bad, so that mini-farm and the one for George aren’t set up yet. I put them together in another bin.

I tape everything to the walls. I don’t want any bins floating around. They might hit something sharp.

The lab is a mess. I was halfway through taking Ringo apart when I turned off the spin drives. Tools, beetle pieces, and all sorts of junk float around the room. I'll have to clean all that up without gravity's help before I can even take a break.

"Well, this sucks," I mutter.
