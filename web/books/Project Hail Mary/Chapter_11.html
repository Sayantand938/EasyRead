Project Hail Mary

Chapter 11

My timer beeps. I set it for two hours. It just hit zero. I blink a few times. I’m floating in a fetal position in the control room. I didn’t even make it to the dormitory.

I am not rested at all. Every part of me yells at me to go back to sleep, but I told Rocky I’d be back in two hours and I don’t want him to think humans are untrustworthy.

I mean…we’re pretty untrustworthy, but I don’t want him to know that.

I trudge (can you trudge in zero g? I say yes) through the airlock. Rocky is there waiting for me in the tunnel. He’s been busy while I was gone. There’s all sorts of stuff in there now.

The Eridian clock is still ticking—now attached to one of the lattice poles. But more interesting to me is the box that’s been added to the dividing wall. It’s a 1-foot cube and it sticks out into my side of the tunnel. It’s made of the same clear xenonite as the rest of the wall.

On Rocky’s side, the box has a flat panel door with a solid xenonite border. Also, there’s a square hole with a perfectly fitted square pipe leading away.

There are some…controls?…on the pipe near the box. Buttons, maybe? A wire from the control box runs along the pipe, disappearing into the hull where the pipe does.

Meanwhile, on my side of the cube is a crank, about the same shape as my own airlock door’s crank. And that’s attached to a square panel like the one on Rocky’s side and—

“It’s an airlock!” I said. “You made an airlock in our airlock tunnel!”

Brilliant. Just brilliant. Rocky and I can both use it. He can control the air in that little room with the mystery pipe, which I guess goes to some pumps or something in the Blip-A. And those buttons are the controls. Just like that, we can move stuff back and forth.

I do jazz hands. He does them back.

Hmm. More square, flat panels. Who makes a square airlock? Especially for Eridian air pressure. Even the pipe for the mini-airlock is square. I know they can make round xenonite—the cylinders he sent me when we met were round. This tunnel is round.

Maybe I’m thinking too much about it. Xenonite is so strong you don’t need to shape it carefully for pressure. Flat panels are probably easier to make.

This is awesome. I hold up a finger—he does the same. I fly to the lab and grab a tape measure. He showed me a time unit, so I’ll show him a length unit. The tape measure is metric, thank God. Using base-6 Eridian seconds will be confusing enough. I don’t want to add imperial units—even if they’re what I'm used to.

Back in the tunnel, I hold up the tape measure. I pull it out a little, then let it retract. I do this a few times. He does jazz hands. I point to the “squarelock” (what else would I call it?) and he does jazz hands again.

I hope that means it doesn’t have 29 atmospheres of ammonia in it right now. I guess we’ll see….

I turn the crank and open my door. It opens toward me easily.

Nothing explodes. I don’t even smell ammonia. And it wasn’t a vacuum in there. I couldn’t have opened the door if it was. Rocky made it exactly like my atmosphere. That's nice of him.

I put the tape measure in the middle of the box and let it float. I close the door and turn the crank.

Rocky presses a button and I hear a soft fwump then a steady hiss. A foggy gas pours in from the pipe. Probably ammonia. The tape measure bounces inside—pushed around like a leaf. Soon, the hiss slows to a trickle.

And then I realize my mistake.

The tape measure is a strong, metal kind used on construction sites, with tough rubber grips. But Eridians like it hot. How hot? I don’t know exactly, but it’s hotter than the temperature at which the rubber on the tape measure melts.

The blob of liquid rubber moves on the tape measure, held to it by surface tension. Rocky opens his door and carefully picks up my bad gift by the metal. At least that’s still solid. I think it’s aluminum. It’s good to know Eridian air isn’t hot enough to melt that too.

As Rocky pulls the tape measure toward him, the rubber blob breaks off and floats to his side of the tube.

He pokes the rubber blob and it sticks to his claw. He shakes it off easily. The temperature clearly doesn’t bother him. It’s like a human shaking water off his hand.

In my air, rubber that hot would burn. It would give off nasty, toxic gases. But there’s no oxygen on Rocky’s side of the wall. So the rubber just…stays a liquid. It floats to the tunnel wall and sticks there.

I shrug at him. Maybe he’ll know that means “I’m sorry.”

He shrugs back a little. But he does it with all five shoulders. It looks strange, and I don’t know if he understood me.

He pulls the tape out a little, then lets it snap back. He’s surprised, even though he must have known it would happen. He lets it go completely and lets it spin. He grabs it and does it again. Then again.

And again.

“Yeah, it’s fun,” I say. “But look at the markings. Those are centimeters. CEN-TI-ME-TERS.”

The next time he pulls the tape out, I point to the tape. “Look!”

He just keeps pulling it out and back again. I don’t see any sign that he cares about what’s written there.

“Ugh!” I hold up a finger. I go back to the lab and get another tape measure. It’s a well-stocked lab, and every space mission needs backups. I come back to the tunnel.

Rocky is still playing with the tape measure. Now he’s really enjoying himself. He pulls the tape out as far as he can, about a meter, then lets go of both the tape and the tape measure. The snap back makes the tape measure spin wildly in front of him.

“♩♪♫♪!!!” he says. I’m pretty sure that was a happy squeal.

“Look. Look,” I say. “Rocky. Rocky! Hey!”

He finally stops playing with the makeshift toy.

I pull some tape out on my tape measure, then point to the markings. “Look! Here! See these?”

He pulls his own out to about the same length. I can see the markings on his are still there—they didn’t get burned off in the intense Eridian heat or anything. What’s the problem?

I point at the 1-centimeter line. “Look. One centimeter. This line. Here.” I tap the line again and again.

He holds the tape out with two hands and taps it with a third. He matches my speed, but he’s nowhere near the 1-centimeter mark.

“Here!” I tap the mark harder. “Are you blind?!”

I pause.

“Wait. Are you blind?”

Rocky taps the tape some more.

I’ve always thought he had eyes somewhere that I didn’t recognize. But what if he doesn’t have eyes at all?

The airlock of the Blip-A was dark, and Rocky didn’t have any problem with it. So I thought he saw in light frequencies I can’t see. But the tape measure has white tape with black markings on it. Any vision in any spectrum should be able to see black on white. Black is the absence of light, and white is all frequencies reflected equally.

Wait—this doesn’t make sense. He knows what I’m doing. He copies my gestures. If he doesn’t have vision, how can he read my clock? How can he read his own clock?

Hmm…his clock has thick numbers. About an eighth of an inch. And, thinking back, he did have some trouble with my clock. He needed me to tape it to the wall. When it floated an inch away he got upset. Just being near the wall wasn’t enough. The clock had to be touching it.

“Sound?” I say. “Do you ‘see’ with sound?”

It would make sense. Humans use electromagnetic waves to understand our three-dimensional world. So why couldn’t another species use sound waves? Same idea—and we even have it on Earth. Bats and dolphins use echolocation to “see” with sound. Maybe Eridians have that ability, but amplified. Unlike bats and dolphins, Eridians have passive sonar. They use surrounding sound waves to figure out their environment instead of making a specific noise to track prey.

Just a theory. But it makes sense with what I know.

That’s why his clock numbers are thick. Because his sonar can’t perceive things that are too thin. My clock was hard for him. He can’t “see” the ink, but the hands are solid objects. So he knew about them. But the whole thing is covered in plastic….

I slapped my forehead. “That’s why you needed the clock pressed against the wall. You needed the sound waves bouncing around inside it to get to you more easily. And the tape measure I just handed you is useless. You can’t see the ink at all!”

He plays with the tape measure some more.

I hold up a finger. He’s more focused on the tape measure toy, but he absently returns the gesture with one of his spare hands.

I fly back into the ship, through the control room, and into the lab. I grab a screwdriver and head farther down to the dormitory. I detach a storage panel from the floor. Simple aluminum sheet. Maybe one-sixteenth of an inch thick, with the edges rounded so we don’t cut ourselves. Strong, durable, and light. Perfect for space travel. I fly back to the tunnel.

Rocky has wrapped one end of the tape around one of his tunnel’s grab-handles and tied it in a somewhat clumsy knot. He hangs on to the dispenser with one hand and uses the other four to climb backward along the bars.

“Hey,” I say. I hold up my hand. “Hey!”

He stops playing with the tape measure for a moment. “♩♪♩?”

I hold up two fingers.

Rocky holds up two fingers.

“Yeah. Okay. We’re copying each other again.” I hold up one finger, then two, then back to one, and then three.

Rocky repeats the same order, just as I hoped.

Now I put the aluminum panel between my hand and Rocky. Behind the panel, I hold up two fingers, then one, then three, then five.

Rocky holds up two fingers, then one, then three. He uses a second hand to hold up two more fingers for a total of five.

“Wow!” I say.

One-sixteenth-inch aluminum will block almost all light. Some very high frequencies can get through, but those frequencies would also pass right through me. So he wouldn’t see my hands. But sound travels through metals easily.

That’s proof. He’s not using light to see. It has to be sound. To Rocky, that metal plate is like a glass window. Maybe it blurs the image a little, but not much. He probably knows what the Hail Mary ’s control room looks like. Why not? The hull is just more aluminum.

How did he see me in space? No air in space. So no sound.

Wait. No. That’s a dumb question. He’s not a caveman in space. He’s an advanced space traveler. He has technology. He probably has cameras and radar that turn data into something he can understand. Like my Petrovascope. I can’t see IR light, but it can and shows it to me on a monitor with light I can see.

The Blip-A control room probably has awesome Braille-like readouts. Well, I’m sure it’s way more advanced than that.

“Wow…” I stare at him. “Humans spent thousands of years looking at the stars and wondering what was out there. You guys never saw stars but still worked out space travel. You Eridians must be amazing. Scientific geniuses.”

The knot in the tape comes loose, snaps back, and hits Rocky’s hand. He shakes his hand in pain for a moment, then keeps messing with the tape measure.

“Yeah. You’re definitely a scientist.”

“All rise,” said the bailiff, “the United States District Court for the Western District of Washington is now in session. Judge Meredith Spencer is presiding.”

The entire courtroom stood as the judge sat down.

“Be seated,” the bailiff said. He gave the justice a folder. “Your Honor, today’s case is Intellectual Property Alliance v. Project Hail Mary.”

The judge nodded. “Plaintiff, are you ready for trial?”

The plaintiff’s table was full of well-dressed men and women. The oldest of them, a man in his sixties, stood to answer. “We are, Your Honor.”

“Defense, are you ready for trial?”

Stratt sat alone at the defense table, typing on her tablet.

The justice cleared her throat. “Defense?”

Stratt finished typing and stood. “I’m ready.”

Justice Spencer pointed to Stratt’s table. “Counselor, where is the rest of your team?”

“Just me,” she said. “And I’m not a counselor—I’m the defendant.”

“Ms. Stratt.” Spencer took off her glasses and glared. “The defendant in this case is a rather famous group of international scientists.”

“Led by me,” said Stratt. “I ask that you dismiss the case.”

“You can’t ask that yet, Ms. Stratt,” said Spencer. “Just tell me if you’re ready to start.”

“I’m ready,”

“All right. Plaintiff, you may begin your opening statement.”

The man stood. “Thank you, your Honor, ladies and gentlemen of the jury, my name is Theodore Canton, lawyer for the Intellectual Property Alliance in this case.

“During this trial, we will show that Project Hail Mary has gone too far in how it gets and uses digital data. They have a huge storage device with copies of every single piece of copyrighted software, as well as every single book and written work available digitally. They did all this without paying or getting permission from the correct copyright holders or intellectual property owners. Also, many of their technical designs break patents held by—”

“Your Honor,” Stratt interrupted. “Can I ask that you dismiss the case now?”

“Technically,” said the justice, “but it’s not standard—”

“I ask that you dismiss the case.”

“Your Honor!” Canton argued.

“On what basis, Ms. Stratt?” said the justice.

“Because I don’t have time for this nonsense,” she said. “We are building a ship to literally save our species. And we have very little time to finish it. It will have three astronauts—just three—to do experiments we can’t even imagine now. We need them to be ready for any possible study they think is necessary. So we are giving them everything. All of human knowledge, along with all software. Some of it is silly. They probably won’t need Minesweeper for Windows 3.1, and they probably don’t need a complete Sanskrit-to-English dictionary, but they’re going to have them.”

Canton shook his head. “Your Honor, my clients don’t deny the importance of the Hail Mary Project. The issue is the illegal use of copyrighted material and patented technology.”

Stratt shook her head. “It would take a crazy amount of time and effort to get permission from every company. So we’re not doing it.”

“I assure you, Ms. Stratt, you will follow the law,” said the justice.

“Only when I want to.” Stratt held up a sheet of paper. “According to this international agreement, I am personally protected from being charged with any crime anywhere on Earth. The United States Senate approved that agreement two months ago.”

She held up a second piece of paper. “And to make situations like this easier, I also have a pardon in advance from the president of the United States for any and all crimes I am accused of within U.S. territories.”

The bailiff took the papers and handed them to the justice.

“This…” said the justice, “this is exactly what you say it is.”

“I’m only here as a favor,” said Stratt. “I didn’t have to come at all. But since the software business, patent abusers, and everyone tied to creative rights joined together in one lawsuit, I thought it would be quickest to settle this right away.”

She grabbed her bag and put the tablet inside. “I’m leaving now.”

“Wait, Ms. Stratt,” said Justice Spencer. “This is still a court, and you will stay until this is over!”

“No, I won’t,” said Stratt.

The officer walked forward. “Ma’am. I’ll have to stop you if you don’t listen.”

“Who’s going to make me?” Stratt asked.

Five armed men in military uniforms came into the courtroom and stood around her. “Because I have the U.S. Army,” she said. “And it’s a really good army.”

I look through my software while eating a peanut-butter tortilla. I know that doesn’t sound good, but it is.

I’ve learned how to grip the lab chair with my legs so I don’t float off while using the laptop. Turns out I have a lot of laptops. At least six that I’ve found in the storage area so far. And they’re all connected to a shipwide Wi-Fi network. Useful.

If I remember right, I should have almost all the software somewhere on the ship. The hard part is finding the one I need. I wouldn’t even know its name. Luckily, one of the books in the digital library is a list of software. That helped.

Finally I find something that will work: “Tympanum Labs Waveform Analyzer.” There are many waveform-analysis programs in my library. This one just has the best reviews according to a 2017 computer magazine that reviewed waveform analyzers.

I install the software on one of the laptops. It’s easy to use and has many features. But I’m most interested in the Fourier transform. It’s the most basic tool in sound-wave analysis and probably the most important. The math behind it is complicated, but the result is this: if you run a sound wave through a Fourier transform, it will give you a list of the notes being played at the same time. So if I played a C-major chord and let this app listen, it would tell me there’s a C, an E, and a G. It’s incredibly useful.

No more acting. It’s time to learn Eridianese. Yes, I just made up that word. No, I don’t regret it. I’m doing many things for the first time in human history out here and a lot needs naming. Just be glad I don’t name things after myself.

I launch Microsoft Excel on another laptop and tape the two laptops together back to back. Yes, I could just run both programs on one laptop, but I don’t want to switch back and forth.

I fly up through the ship and back into the tunnel. Rocky isn’t there.

Hmph.

Rocky can’t spend all day waiting for me, but why isn’t there someone in the tunnel all the time? If my crewmates were still here, we would take turns watching. Ilyukhina would probably be camped out here nonstop and only leave to sleep.

What if they are having different people in the tunnel? How do I know Rocky is just one person? I don’t know how to tell Eridians apart. Maybe I’ve been talking to six different people. That’s a disturbing thought.

No…that’s not it. I’m pretty sure Rocky is just Rocky. The ridges on his shell and rocky bumps on his hands are unique. I remember there’s a jagged bit sticking up out of one of his fingers…yeah. It’s the same guy.

If you looked at a rock for hours, and someone replaced it with a very similar, but slightly different rock, you would know.

Okay, so where is the rest of the crew? I’m alone because my crewmates didn’t make it. But Eridians have better space technology. Bigger ship, almost indestructible hull material. There has to be a crew in there.

Ah! I bet Rocky’s the captain! He puts himself at risk by talking to the scary alien. Everyone else stays back on the ship. That’s what Captain Kirk would do. So why not Captain Rocky?

Anyway, I have cool stuff I want to do and I’m impatient.

“Yo! Rocky!” I yell. “Come here!”

I listen for any sounds of movement. “Come on, man! Your entire way of sensing things is sound—I bet you can hear a pin drop a mile away! You know I’m calling you! Move your…whatever you sit on! I want to talk!”

I wait and wait, but no Rocky.

My guess is I’m pretty high priority to him. So whatever he’s doing must be really important. After all, he’s got a ship to deal with. He probably needs to eat and sleep. Well, he has to eat, anyway—all living things need energy somehow. I don’t know if Eridians sleep.

Come to think of it…sleep might not be such a bad idea. Out of the past forty-eight hours I’ve had a two-hour nap and nothing else. Rocky’s clock is still there, stuck between a grab bar and the wall. It’s ticking away as normal. It’s interesting that his clock only has five digits. By my math, it’ll roll over back to ℓℓℓℓℓ every five hours or so. Maybe that’s the length of an Eridian day?

Think about it later. Sleep first. I made a simple chart on my laptop to change Rocky's time to mine and back. I want to sleep eight hours. I type in the time on Rocky’s clock, which is IℓIVλ, and the chart tells me what the clock will say eight hours from now. The answer: Iλ+VVλ.

I rush back to the lab for Popsicle sticks and tape. Rocky can’t see ink, so I have to make do.

I tape the sticks to the wall to tell Rocky when I’ll be back: Iλ+VVλ. Luckily, the symbols are mostly straight lines, so my little project should be good enough for him.

It's strange that my return time has six digits, one more than Rocky’s clock. But he’ll figure it out. If Rocky said “I’ll be back at thirty-seven o’clock,” I’d know what he meant.

Before bed, I grab a small camera from the lab’s vacuum chamber. It’s a wireless camera that connects to a screen attached to the chamber. I tape the camera in the tunnel, aimed at the wall. I take the screen to my bunk.

Now I have a baby monitor in the tunnel. It has no sound, since the camera is for experiments, not talking. But it’s better than nothing.

I tuck the sheets and blankets tightly around the oval mattress. I squeeze in between the bedding, so I won’t float around while I sleep.

My big plans for talking to Rocky will have to wait. I’m a little annoyed, but not for long. I fall asleep fast.
