Project Hail Mary

Chapter 23

I arrived at the meeting on time, or so I thought. The email said 12:30, but when I got there, everyone was already seated, silent, and staring at me.

We were keeping the accident quiet for now. The world was watching this project—their only hope. The last thing we needed was for people to know our main and backup scientists were dead. Say what you will about the Russians, they know how to keep a secret. All of Baikonur was locked down.

The meeting room, a simple trailer provided by the Russians, had a great view of the launch pad. I could see the Soyuz through the window. It was old technology, but the most reliable launch system ever made.

Stratt and I hadn’t spoken since the explosion. She suddenly had to lead a quick investigation into the disaster. It couldn’t wait—if the accident was caused by something that would be used on the mission, we needed to know. I wanted to help, but she wouldn’t let me. Someone had to deal with the small, desperate issues reported by the ESA team.

Stratt stared right at me. Dimitri played with some papers—probably a design to improve the spin-drive. Dr. Lokken, the passionate Norwegian who designed the centrifuge, drummed her fingers on the table. Dr. Lamai wore her lab coat as always. Her team had perfected a fully automated medical robot, and she’d probably win a Nobel Prize someday, if Earth survived that long. Even Steve Hatch, the eccentric Canadian who invented the beetle probes, was there. He, at least, didn’t look uncomfortable. He just typed on a calculator. He had no papers, just the calculator.

Commander Yáo and Engineer Ilyukhina were also present. Yáo looked gloomy as usual, and Ilyukhina had no drink in her hand.

“Am I late?” I asked.

“No, you’re just in time,” Stratt said. “Have a seat.”

I sat in the only empty chair.

“We think we know what happened at the research center,” Stratt began. “The building is gone, but their records were all digital and saved on a server used by all of Baikonur. Luckily, that server is in the Ground Control Building. Also, DuBois—being DuBois—kept very careful notes.”

She held up a paper. “According to his online journal, yesterday he planned to test a very unusual problem that could happen in a generator powered by Astrophage.”

Ilyukhina shook her head. “I should have been the one testing this. I take care of ship maintenance. DuBois should have asked me.”

“What was he testing?” I asked.

Lokken cleared her throat. “A month ago, JAXA found a possible problem with the generator. It uses Astrophage to create heat, which powers a small turbine using a material that changes state. Old, reliable technology. It uses very little Astrophage—only twenty cells at a time.”

“That seems safe,” I said.

“It is. But if the system that controls the generator's pump fails, and there’s a very thick clump of Astrophage in the fuel line at the same time, up to one nanogram of Astrophage could enter the reaction chamber.”

“What would that do?”

“Nothing. The generator also controls how much IR light shines on the Astrophage. If the chamber gets too hot, the IR lights turn off to let the Astrophage cool down. Safe backup system. But there's a possible, very unlikely situation where a short in this system could make the IR lights turn on at full power and ignore the temperature safety. DuBois wanted to test this very, very unlikely situation.”

“So what did he do?”

Lokken paused and her lip trembled a bit. She braced herself and continued. “He got a fake generator—one of the ones we use for testing on the ground. He changed the pump and lights to make that weird thing happen on purpose. He wanted to set off a whole nanogram of Astrophage at once and see how it wrecked the generator.”

“Hold on,” I said. “One nanogram isn’t enough to destroy a building. At worst it could melt a little metal.”

“Yeah,” said Lokken. She breathed in deeply and then let it out. “You know how we keep tiny amounts of Astrophage, right?”

“Sure,” I said. “In little plastic containers floating in propylene glycol.”

She nodded. “When DuBois asked for one nanogram of Astrophage from the supply center, they gave him one milligram by mistake. And since the containers look the same and the amounts are so small, he and Shapiro couldn’t tell.”

“Oh God.” I rubbed my eyes. “That’s a million times more heat than they thought. It blew up the building and everyone inside. God.”

Stratt shuffled her papers. “The truth is: We don’t know how to handle Astrophage safely. If you asked for a firecracker and someone gave you a truckload of explosives, you’d know something was wrong. But the difference between a nanogram and a milligram? People just can’t tell.”

We were all quiet for a moment. She was right. We’d been playing with energy like an atomic bomb like it was nothing. In any other situation it would have been crazy. But we had no choice.

“So are we going to push back the launch?” I asked.

“No, we’ve discussed it and we all agree: We can’t delay the Hail Mary ’s launch. It’s put together, tested, fueled, and ready.”

“It's the orbit,” Dimitri said. “It's in a close orbit at a 51.6-degree angle, so Cape Canaveral and Baikonur can reach it easily. But it's also in a low orbit, which is getting lower. If it doesn't leave in the next three weeks, we'll have to send an entire mission just to raise it to a higher orbit.”

“The Hail Mary will leave on time,” said Stratt. “Five days from now. The crew will have two days of preflight checks, so the Soyuz has to launch in three days.”

“Okay,” I said. “What about the science expert? I'm sure we have hundreds of volunteers all over the world. We can give the chosen person a quick course in the science they need to know—”

“The decision's been made,” Stratt said. “Actually, it made itself. There's no time to train someone in everything they need to know. There's just too much information and research to learn. Even the smartest scientists couldn't learn it all in just three days. And remember, only about one in seven thousand people have the right genes to resist comas.”

Right then, I got a bad feeling. “I think I see where this is going.”

“As you probably know, your tests were positive. You're that one in seven thousand.”

“Welcome to the crew!” Ilyukhina said.

“Wait, wait. No.” I shook my head. “This is crazy. I know about Astrophage, but I don't know anything about being an astronaut.”

“We'll train you as we go.” Yáo spoke quietly, but confidently. “And we'll do the hard work. You'll only be used for science.”

“I just mean…come on! There has to be someone else!” I looked to Stratt. “What about Yáo's backup? Or Ilyukhina's?”

“They’re not biologists,” said Stratt. “They’re incredibly skilled at knowing the Hail Mary inside and out, how it works, and how to fix it. But we don’t have time to teach someone all the biology they need to know. It’s like asking a great building engineer to do brain surgery. It’s just not what they do.”

“What about other people on the list? The ones who weren’t first choices?”

“No one is as good as you. Honestly, we’re incredibly lucky you can resist comas. Do you think I kept you on the project this long because I needed a middle school teacher?”

“Oh…” I said.

“You know how the ship works,” Stratt continued. “You know the science behind Astrophage. You know how to use a spacesuit and all the special equipment. You’ve been at every important science or strategy meeting about the ship and its mission—I made sure of it. You have the right genes, so I made damn sure you had the right skills. I didn’t want this to happen, but here we are. You’ve been the backup science expert all along.”

“N-No, that can’t be true,” I said. “There must be other people. Scientists who are much better. And, you know, people who actually want to go. You must have made a list, right? Who’s the next person after me?”

Stratt picked up a paper in front of her. “Andrea Cáceres, who works at a distillery in Paraguay. She can resist comas and has a chemistry degree with some biology. And she offered to go on the mission when we first asked for astronauts.”

“Sounds great,” I said. “Let’s call her.”

“But you’ve had years of training. You know the ship and the mission completely. And you’re one of the world’s top experts on Astrophage. We’d only have a few days to train Cáceres. You know how I work, Dr. Grace. Better than anyone. I want to give Hail Mary every chance to succeed. And right now, that’s you.”

I looked down at the table. “But I…I don’t want to die….”

“Nobody does,” said Stratt.

“It must be your decision,” said Yáo. “I won’t have someone on my crew who doesn’t want to be here. You must come willingly. If you refuse, we’ll bring in Ms. Cáceres and try to train her. But I urge you to say yes. Billions of lives are at stake. Our lives don’t matter much compared to such a tragedy.”

I put my head in my hands. The tears started to flow. Why did this have to happen to me? “Can I think about it?”

“Yes,” Stratt said. “But not for long. If you say no, we have to get Cáceres here quickly. I want your answer by five p.m. tonight.”

I stood and shuffled out of the room. I don’t think I even said goodbye. It’s a dark and depressing feeling to have all your closest colleagues get together and decide you should die.

I checked my watch—12:38 p.m. I had four and a half hours to decide.

—

The Hail Mary’s spin drives are much too strong for its current weight. When we left Earth, the ship weighed 2.1 million kilograms—mostly fuel. Now the ship only weighs 120,000 kilograms, about one-twentieth of its starting weight.

Because the Hail Mary is so light now, the little beetles can give me 1.5 g’s of thrust together. But the ship wasn’t made to have thrust coming in at a 45-degree angle, pushing on random EVA handles on the hull. If we use the beetles at full power, they’ll just rip free of the handles and fly off into the Tauset.

Rocky remembered that when he stopped our spin. Now we have it under control, and I can do spacewalks in zero gravity as easily as breathing. I 3-D print a model of the Hail Mary’s insides and give it to Rocky to study. In less than an hour, he not only has a solution but has made the xenonite supports to put it in place.

So, I do another spacewalk. I add the xenonite supports to the beetles. For once, everything goes as planned. Rocky assures me that the ship can now handle full power from the beetles, and I believe him completely. The guy knows engineering.

I type a bunch of calculations into a complicated Excel spreadsheet that probably has errors in it somewhere. It takes me six hours to put together. I finally come up with what I think is the right answer. At least, it should put us close enough that we can see the Blip-A. Then we can adjust our course from there.

“Ready?” I say from the pilot seat.

“Ready,” Rocky says in his bulb. He holds the three control boxes in his hands.

“Okay…John and Paul to 4.5 percent.”

“John and Paul, 4.5 percent, confirmed,” he says.

Sure, Rocky could have made controls for me to use, but this is better. I have to watch the screen closely and pay attention to our course. Best to have someone give their full attention to the beetles. Besides, Rocky’s a ship engineer. Who better to run our makeshift engines?

“John and Paul to zero. Ringo to 1.1 percent,” I say.

“John and Paul zero. Ringo 1.1.”

We nudge the ship's direction a little at a time until it points roughly where I want. Finally, it seems right.

“Here goes nothing,” I say. “All ahead full!”

“John, Paul, Ringo 100 percent.”

I'm pushed back as the ship jumps forward, 1.5 g's of gravity pressing down as we speed straight (I hope) toward Blip-A.

“Keep this thrust for three hours,” I say.

“Three hours. I watch engines. You relax.”

“Thanks, but I can’t rest. I want to use gravity while I can.”

“I stay here. Tell me about the experiments.”

“Will do.”

I’m aiming for another eleven-day trip. It takes 130 kilograms of fuel to do that—about a quarter of what the beetles have (including George, who is on the lab table full of Astrophage). That should leave enough to fix any dumb errors in my calculations.

We’ll get up to full speed in three hours, then we’ll drift for almost eleven days. I don’t want to mess with starting or stopping the centrifuge. Yes, it can be done—Rocky showed it when he turned it off before. But it was tricky with lots of guessing and chances to lose control. Or worse—getting the cables tangled.

So, for the next three hours I have 1.5 times the normal force of gravity to deal with. After that it’ll be zero gravity for a while. Time to go to the lab.

I climb down the ladder. My arm hurts, but not as much as before. I’ve been changing the bandages every day—or rather, Dr. Lamai’s amazing medical machine has been doing it. There’s definitely scarring all over the skin. I’m going to have an ugly arm and shoulder forever. But I think the deeper layers of skin must have survived. If they hadn’t, I probably would have died of gangrene by now. Or Lamai’s machine would have cut off my arm when I wasn’t looking.

It’s been a while since I had to deal with 1.5 g’s. My legs don’t like it. But I’m used to this by now.

I walk to the main lab table, where the Taumoeba experiments are still going on. Every part of them is securely attached to the table, just in case we have more unexpected changes in speed. Of course, it’s not like I’m short on Taumoeba. I have a lot of them where my fuel used to be.

I check the Venus experiment first. The cooling system hums quietly, keeping the inside temperature right for Venus’s upper atmosphere. I originally planned to let the Taumoeba incubate in there for only an hour, but then the lights went out and we had other things to worry about. So now it’s been four days. If nothing else, they’ve had plenty of time to do their thing.

I gulp. This is important. The small glass slide inside had a thin layer of Astrophage, only one cell thick. If the Taumoeba are alive and eating Astrophage, light will be able to pass through. The more light I see through that slide, the fewer Astrophage are still alive on it.

I brace myself, take a deep breath, and look inside.

Pitch black.

My breathing quickens. I take a flashlight out of my pocket and shine it from behind. No light gets through at all. My heart drops.

I step over to the Threeworld Taumoeba experiment. I look at the slide and see the same thing. Completely black.

Taumoeba can’t live on Venus or Threeworld. Or, at least, they aren’t eating. My stomach feels like it’s going to melt.

So close! We were so close! We have the answer right here! Taumoeba! A natural enemy to the thing that’s ruining our worlds! And it’s strong too. It can live and grow in my fuel tanks, obviously. But not in Venus or Threeworld’s air. Why not?!

“What you see?” Rocky asks.

“Failure,” I say. “Both experiments. The Taumoeba are all dead.”

I hear Rocky punch the wall. “Anger!”

“All this work! All of it for nothing. Nothing!” I slam my fist on the table. “I gave up so much for this! I sacrificed so much!”

I hear Rocky’s shell fall to the ground in his bulb. A sign of deep sadness.

We’re both quiet for a while; Rocky slumped in his bulb and me with my face buried in my hands.

Finally, I hear a scrape. It’s Rocky picking his shell off the floor. “We work more,” he says. “We no give up. We work hard. We are brave.”

“Yeah, I guess so.”

I’m not the right person for this job. I’m a last-minute fill-in because the people who were really qualified messed up. But I’m here. I might not have all the answers, but I’m here. I must have signed up, thinking it was a hopeless task. It doesn’t help Earth much, but it’s something.

—

Stratt’s trailer was twice as big as mine. Perks of being in charge, I guess. Though to be fair, she needed the space. She sat at a large table full of papers. I could see at least six different languages in four different writing systems on the papers in front of her, but she seemed fine with all of them.

A Russian soldier stood in one corner of the room. Not exactly standing straight, but not relaxed either. There was a chair next to him, but he had chosen to stand.

“Hello, Dr. Grace,” Stratt said without looking up. She pointed to the soldier. “That’s Private Meknikov. Even though we know the explosion was an accident, the Russians aren’t taking any risks.”

I looked at the soldier. “So he’s here to make sure made-up terrorists don’t kill you?”

“Something like that.” She looked up. “So, it’s five o’clock. Have you decided? Are you going to be the Hail Mary ’s science expert?”

I sat across from her. I couldn’t look her in the eye. “No.”

She scowled at me. “I see.”

“It’s…you know…the kids. I should stay here for the kids.” I shifted in my seat. “Even if the Hail Mary finds the answer, we’re going to have almost thirty years of misery.”

“Uh-huh,” she said.

“And, um, well, I’m a teacher. I should teach. We need to raise a strong, solid generation of survivors. Right now we’re soft. You, me, the whole Western world. We’re the result of growing up in comfort and stability like never before. It’s the kids of today that’ll have to make the world of tomorrow work. And they’re going to inherit a mess. I can really do a lot more by preparing kids for the world that’s coming. I should stay here on Earth where I’m needed.”

“On Earth,” she repeated. “Where you’re needed.”

“Y-Yeah.”

“As opposed to on the Hail Mary, where you could be key to solving the whole problem because you’re completely trained for it.”

“It’s not like that,” I said. “I mean. It’s a little like that. But look, I’m no good on a crew. I’m not some fearless explorer.”

“Oh, I know,” she said. She clenched her fist and looked away for a moment. Then back to me with a burning look I’d never seen before. “Dr. Grace. You’re a coward and you’re full of it.”

I winced.

“If you really cared so much about the children, you’d get on that ship without hesitation. You could save billions of them from the apocalypse instead of preparing hundreds of them for it.”

I shook my head. “It’s not about that—”

“Do you think I don’t know you, Dr. Grace?!” she yelled. “You’re a coward and you always have been. You gave up a great science career because people didn’t like a paper you wrote. You ran to the safety of children who think you’re the best teacher ever. You don’t have a romantic partner because that would mean you might get hurt. You avoid risk like the plague.”

I stood up. “Okay, it’s true! I’m afraid! I don’t want to die! I worked so hard on this project and I deserve to live! I’m not going, and that’s final! Get the next person on the list—that chemist from Paraguay. She wants to go!”

She slammed her fist on the table. “I don’t care who wants to go. I care who’s most qualified! Dr. Grace, I’m sorry, but you are going on that mission. I know you’re afraid. I know you don’t want to die. But you’re going.”

“You’re crazy. I’m leaving now.” I turned to the door.

“Meknikov!” she shouted.

The soldier quickly stepped between me and the door.

I turned back to her. “You have got to be kidding.”

“It would have been easier if you’d just said yes.”

“What’s your plan?” I pointed at the soldier. “Hold me at gunpoint for four years?”

“You’ll be in a coma during the trip.”

I tried to run past Meknikov, but he stopped me easily. He wasn’t rough. He was just much stronger than me. He held me by the shoulders and turned me to face Stratt.

“This is crazy!” I yelled. “Yáo will never agree to this! He said he doesn’t want anyone on his ship who doesn't want to be there!”

“Yeah, that was unexpected. He’s annoyingly honorable,” Stratt said.

She picked up a checklist written in Dutch. “First, you’ll be held in a cell for a few days until the launch. You can’t talk to anyone. Right before launch, you’ll get a strong sedative to knock you out, and we’ll put you in the Soyuz.”

“Don’t you think Yáo will suspect something?”

“I’ll tell Commander Yáo and Specialist Ilyukhina that because you don't have much astronaut training, you were worried you’d panic during the launch, so you chose to be unconscious. Once on the Hail Mary , Yáo and Ilyukhina will put you in your medical bed and start your coma. They’ll do all the pre-launch prep from there. You’ll wake up at Tau Ceti.”

I started to panic. This crazy plan might work. “No! You can’t do that! I won’t do it! This is insane!”

She rubbed her eyes. “Believe it or not, Dr. Grace, I kind of like you. I don’t respect you very much, but I think you’re a good person.”

“Easy for you to say when you’re not the one being murdered! You’re murdering me!” Tears rolled down my face. “I don’t want to die! Don’t send me off to die! Please!”

She looked sad. “I don’t like this any more than you do, Dr. Grace. If it’s any comfort, you’ll be seen as a hero. If Earth survives this, there’ll be statues of you everywhere.”

“I won’t do it!” I choked, tasting bile. “I’ll ruin the mission! You kill me?! Fine! I’ll kill your mission! I’ll destroy the ship!”

She shook her head. “No, you won’t. You’re just saying that. Like I said, you’re a good man at heart. When you wake up, you’ll be angry. I’m sure Yáo and Ilyukhina will be pretty mad about what I did to you too. But in the end, you three will be out there and you’ll do your job. Because humanity needs you to. I’m almost certain you’ll do the right thing.”

“Try me!” I screamed. “Go on! Try me! See what happens!”

“But I can’t just hope for the best, can I?” She glanced at her paper again. “I always thought the American CIA would have the best interrogation drugs. But did you know it’s actually the French? It’s true. Their DGSE has perfected a drug that makes you forget things for a long time. Not just hours or days, but weeks. They used it during anti-terror operations. It can be helpful for a suspect to forget he was ever questioned.”

I stared at her in horror. My throat hurt from yelling.

“Your med bed will give you a good dose of it before you wake up. You and your crewmates will just think it’s a side effect of the coma. Yáo and Ilyukhina will explain the mission to you and you’ll get right to work. The French say the drug doesn’t erase skills, language, or anything like that. By the time you remember everything, you guys might have already sent the beetles back. And if not, I bet you’ll be too involved in the project to quit.”

She nodded to Meknikov. He dragged me out the door and pulled me down the path.

I twisted my head back toward the door and screamed, “You can’t do this!”

"Just think about the kids, Grace," she said from the doorway. "All those kids you'll be helping. Think about them."
