Project Hail Mary

Chapter 25

We did it!

We actually did it!

I have Earth’s salvation in a little tank on the floor.

“Happy!” Rocky says. “Happy happy happy!”

I’m so giddy I might throw up. “Yes! But we’re not done yet.”

I strap myself into my bunk. A pillow tries to float away, but I grab it and wedge it under my head. I’m all wired up, but if I don’t go to sleep soon, Rocky will start hassling me. Sheesh—you almost mess up a mission one time and suddenly you have an alien-enforced bedtime.

“Taumoeba-35!” Rocky says. “Took many many generations but finally success!”

Scientific breakthroughs are a weird feeling. There’s no sudden flash of insight. Just a slow, steady progress toward a goal. But man, when you reach that goal it feels good.

We linked the ships back together weeks ago. Rocky was pretty excited to have access to his much larger ship again. First thing he did was set up a tunnel directly from his part of the Hail Mary to the Blip-A. It meant another hole in my hull, but at this point I trust Rocky to do any engineering task. Heck, if he wanted to do open-heart surgery on me, I’d probably let him. The guy is amazing at this stuff.

With the ships connected, I can’t run the Hail Mary’s centrifuge, so we’re back to zero g. But now that we’re just growing Taumoeba in tanks, I can do without my gravity-based lab equipment for now.

Over the weeks, we saw each generation of Taumoeba become more and more resistant to nitrogen. And now, today, we finally have Taumoeba-35: a type of Taumoeba that can survive with 3.5 percent nitrogen and a total air pressure of 0.02 atmospheres—like on Venus.

“You. Be happy now,” Rocky says from his workbench.

“I am, I am,” I say. “But we need to get to 8 percent so it can live on Threeworld. We’re not done yet.”

“Yes yes yes. But this is moment. Important moment.”

“Yeah.” I smile.

He’s playing with some new device. He’s always working on something. “Now you make exact Venus air in one tank and do careful tests on Taumoeba-35, question?”

“No,” I say. “We’ll keep going until we get to Taumoeba-80. It should work on Venus and Threeworld. I’ll test everything then.”

“Understand.”

I turn to look at his side of the room. The whole “watching me sleep” thing doesn’t bother me anymore. If anything, it’s nice. “What are you working on?”

The device is clamped to his workbench so it doesn't move. He works on it from many angles, using many tools. "This is Earth electricity thing."

"You're making a power converter?"

"Yes. Change Eridian electricity to Earth electricity, which is not as good."

"Prime sequence?"

"It would take too long to explain."

I remember to ask about it later. "Okay. What will you use it for?"

He puts down two tools and picks up three more. "If everything works, we make good Taumoeba. I give you fuel. You go to Earth, and I go to Erid. We say goodbye."

"Yeah, I guess," I mumble. I should be happier about surviving a suicide mission, coming home a hero, and saving my whole species. But saying goodbye to Rocky forever will be hard. I try not to think about it.

"You have many computers. I ask a favor: Can you give one to me as a gift?"

"A laptop? You want a laptop? Sure, I have a lot of them."

“Good good. And thinking machine has information, question? Science information from Earth, question?”

Ah, of course. I’m an advanced alien race with knowledge far beyond Eridian science. I think the laptops have terabyte drives. I could copy the entire contents of Wikipedia over to him.

“Yes. I can do that. But I don’t think a laptop will work in Eridian air. Too hot.”

He points to the device. “This is just one part of the thinking-machine life-support system. System will give power, keep Earth temperature, Earth air inside. Many backups. Make sure thinking machine doesn’t break. If it breaks, no Eridian can fix.”

“Ah, I see. How will you read the output?”

“Camera inside converts from Earth light readout to Eridian texture readout. Like camera in control room. Before we leave, you explain written language to me.”

He certainly knows enough English to look up any words he doesn’t know. “Yeah, sure. Our written language is easy. Kind of easy. There are only twenty-six letters, but many strange ways to say them. Well, I guess there are actually fifty-two symbols because capital letters look different even though they’re pronounced the same. Oh, and then there’s punctuation…”

“Our scholars will figure it out. You just get me started.”

“Yes. I’ll do that,” I say. “I want a gift from you too: xenonite. Solid form and liquid pre-xenonite form. Earth scientists will want that.”

“Yes, I give.”

I yawn. “I’m going to sleep soon.”

“I watch.”

“Good night, Rocky.”

“Good night, Grace.”

I fall asleep easier than I have in weeks. I have Taumoeba that can save Earth.

Changing an alien life-form. What could possibly go wrong?

—

When I was a kid, like most kids, I imagined being an astronaut. I imagined flying through space in a rocket ship, meeting aliens, and being awesome. I didn’t imagine cleaning out sewage tanks.

But that’s pretty much what I’m doing today. To be clear, it’s not my poop I’m cleaning. It’s Taumoeba poop. Thousands of kilograms of Taumoeba poop. Each of my seven remaining fuel bays has to be cleared of all that gunk before I can put new fuel in.

So, on one hand, I’m shoveling poop. On the other, at least I’m in an EVA suit while I do it. I’ve smelled this stuff before. It’s not great.

The gunky methane and rotting cells aren’t a problem. If that were all I had to deal with, I’d just ignore it. Twenty thousand kilograms of gunk in a two-million-kilogram tank? Barely worth paying attention to.

The problem is there’s probably surviving Taumoeba in there. The contamination ate all the available fuel several weeks ago, so they’ve mostly starved by now. At least, according to recent samples I checked. But some of the little bastards will probably still be alive. And the last thing I want to do is feed them 2 million kilograms of fresh Astrophage.

“Progress, question?” Rocky radios.

“Almost done with Fuel Bay Three.”

Fully inside the tank, I scrape black gunk off the walls with a homemade spatula and fling it out through a one-meter-wide hole in the side. Where’d the one-meter-wide hole come from? I made it.

The fuel tanks have no human-sized entry hatches. Why would they? Valves and piping lead in and out, but the largest of them is only a few inches wide. I don’t have anything to flush the tanks with—I left my “ten thousand gallons of water” collection back home. So for each tank I have to cut a hole, clean the gunk out, and then reseal it.

I have to say, though, the cutting torch Rocky made for me works like a charm. A little Astrophage, an IR light, some lenses, and I have a freakin’ death ray in my hands. The trick is keeping the output low. But Rocky put extra safeties in. He made sure the lenses had some impurities and they aren’t made of transparent xenonite. They’re IR-permeable glass. If the light output from the Astrophage inside gets too high, the lenses will melt. Then the beam will defocus and the cutter will be useless. I’d have to sheepishly ask Rocky to make me another one, but at least I wouldn’t cut my leg off.

So far, that hasn’t happened. But I wouldn’t put it past me.

I scrape a particularly stubborn crust of gunk off the wall. It floats away and I use the scraper to bat it out the hole. “Status on breeder tanks?” I ask.

“Tank Four still has live Taumoeba. Tank Five and higher all dead.”

I shuffle forward in the tank. It’s narrow enough that I can stay in place by putting both boots on one side of the cylinder and a hand on the other. This leaves my other hand free to scrape sludge. “Tank Four was 5.25 percent, right?”

“Not right. Five point two zero percent.”

“Okay. So we’re up to Taumoeba-52. Doin’ good.”

“How is progress, question?”

“Slow and steady,” I say.

I flick a clump of gunk off into space. I wish I could just flush the tanks with nitrogen and be done with it. After all, this Taumoeba can’t resist nitrogen at all. But it wouldn’t work. The gunk is several centimeters thick. No matter how much nitrogen I pumped in, there would be some Taumoeba it doesn’t reach—protected by a centimeter-thick wall of their kind.

All it takes is one survivor to start an infestation when I refill the tanks with Rocky’s spare Astrophage. So I have to clean the tanks out as best I can before doing the nitrogen cleanse.

“Your fuel tanks are big. Do you have enough nitrogen, question? I can give ammonia from Blip-A life support if you need.”

“Ammonia wouldn’t work,” I say. “Taumoeba doesn’t have a problem with nitrogen compounds. Just with pure N2. But don’t worry, I’m fine. I don’t need as much nitrogen as you think. We know 3.5 percent at 0.02 atmospheres will kill natural Taumoeba. That’s a partial pressure of less than 1 Pascal. These fuel bays are only 37 cubic meters each. All I need to do is squirt a few grams of nitrogen gas in here and it’ll kill everything. It’s amazingly deadly to Taumoeba.”

I put my hands on my hips. It was an awkward pose in the EVA suit, making me float away from the wall, but it felt right. “Okay. Fuel Bay Three is done.”

“Want to patch hole with xenonite now?”

I float out of the fuel bay and into space. I tug on my tether to pull me back to the hull. “No, I’ll clean everything first, then patch them all in one go.”

I use the handholds to get to Fuel Bay Four, secure myself, and turn on the Eridian AstroTorch.

—

Xenonite makes great containers for pressurized gas.

My fuel bays are all freshly cleaned and resealed. I filled them with about a hundred times more nitrogen than needed to kill any local Taumoeba. Then I just left it like that for a while. I’m taking no chances.

After a few days of sterilizing, it’s time for a test. Rocky gives me a few kilograms of Astrophage to use. I remember when “a few kilograms of Astrophage” would have been a blessing for everyone on Stratt’s Vat. But now it’s just, “Here’s a few quadrillion Joules of energy. Tell me if you need more.”

I divide the Astrophage into seven roughly equal blobs, release the nitrogen, and squirt one blob into each fuel bay. Then I wait a day.

During this time, Rocky is on his ship, working hard on a pump system to move Astrophage from his fuel tanks to mine. I offer to help, but he nicely says no. What could I even do on the Blip-A? My spacesuit can’t handle the conditions in there, so Rocky would have to build me a whole tunnel system…it’s not worth it.

I really want it to be worth it. It’s an alien spaceship! I want to see inside! But yeah, saving humanity is more important. That’s the priority.

I check the fuel bays. Any live Taumoeba will have found and eaten the Astrophage. So, if the Astrophage is still there, the bay is clean.

To make a long story short: Two of the seven bays weren’t clean.

“Hey, Rocky!” I yell from the control room.

He’s somewhere on the Blip-A, but I know he can hear me. He always hears me.

After a few seconds, the radio turns on. “What, question?”

“Two fuel bays still have Taumoeba.”

“Understood. Not good, but not bad. Other five are clean, question?”

I hold onto a handhold in the control room to stay in place. It’s easy to float away when you’re focused on talking. “Yeah, the other five seem good.”

“How did Taumoeba survive in the bad two bays, question?”

“I probably didn’t clean them well enough. Some gunk remained and shielded live Taumoeba from the nitrogen. That’s my guess.”

“Plan, question?”

“I’m going back into those two, scraping them down some more, and I’ll sterilize them again. I’ll leave the other five sealed for now.”

“Good plan. Do not forget to purge fuel lines.”

With all the tanks infected, it’s safe to assume the fuel lines (currently sealed off) will also be infected. “Yes. They’ll be easier than the tanks. I just need to blow high-pressure nitrogen through them. It’ll clean out the chunks and sterilize the rest. Then I’ll test them the same as the fuel bays.”

“Good good.” He says. “What is status of breeder tanks, question?”

“Still making good progress. We’re up to Taumoeba-62 now.”

“Someday we find out why nitrogen was problem.”

“Yeah, but that’s for other scientists. We just need Taumoeba-80.”

“Yes. Taumoeba-80. Maybe Taumoeba-86. Safety.”

When you think in base six, adding six to things randomly is normal.

“Agreed,” I say.

I enter the airlock and climb into the Orlan EVA suit. I grab the AstroTorch and attach it to my tool belt. I turn on the helmet radio and say, “Beginning EVA.”

“Understood. Radio if problem. My ship hull robot can help if you need.”

“I shouldn’t need it, but I’ll let you know.”

I seal the door behind me and start the airlock cycle.

—

“Screw it,” I say. I press the final confirmation button to drop Fuel Bay Five.

The explosives pop, and the empty tank drifts off into the void.

No amount of scrubbing, cleaning, nitrogen-flushing, or anything else could get the Taumoeba out of Fuel Bay Five. No matter what I did, they lived and devoured the test Astrophage I put in afterward.

At some point, you just have to give up.

I cross my arms and sink into my pilot’s seat. There’s no gravity to really sink with, so I have to consciously push myself into the seat. I’m pouting, damn it, and I intend to do it right. I’m missing three of my original nine fuel bays. Two from our adventure over Adrian, and another just now. That’s about 666,000 kilograms of fuel storage I no longer have.

Do I have enough fuel to get home? Sure. Any amount of fuel that can get me away from Tau Ceti’s gravity is enough to eventually get home. I could get home with just a few kilograms of Astrophage if I didn’t mind waiting a million years.

It’s not about getting there. It’s about how long it will take.

I do a lot of math, and I get answers I don’t like.

The trip from Earth to Tau Ceti took three years and nine months. And it was done by constantly accelerating at 1.5 g’s the entire time—which is what Dr. Lamai decided was the maximum g-force a human should be exposed to for almost four years. Earth experienced something like thirteen years during that time, but time dilation helped us out.

If I do the long trip home with just 1.33 million kilograms of fuel (which is all my remaining tanks can hold), the best way is a constant acceleration of 0.9 g’s. I’d be going slower, which means less time dilation, which means I experience more time. In total, I’ll experience five and a half years on that trip.

So what? It’s only an extra year and a half. What’s the big deal?

I don’t have much food.

This was a suicide mission. They gave us food to last several months, and that’s all. I’ve been using the food at a steady pace, but then I’ll have to use coma slurry. It won’t taste good, but it’s balanced, at least.

But again, this was a suicide mission. They didn’t give us enough coma slurry to get home either. The only reason I have any is because Commander Yáo and Specialist Ilyukhina died on the way.

In total, I have three months of real food left and about forty months of coma slurry. That’s just enough food to survive the trip home with full fuel and a little extra. But not enough to last the five and a half years of a slower trip.

Rocky’s food is useless. I’ve tested it many times. It’s full of heavy metals, some toxic and some highly toxic. There are good proteins and sugars that my body could use, but there’s no way to separate the poison from the food.

And there’s nothing here for me to grow. All my food is freeze-dried or dried. No seeds or plants or anything. I can eat what I have, and that’s it.

Rocky clicks along his tunnel to the control-room bulb. He goes in and out of the Blip-A so often that I don’t know what ship he’s on.

“You sound angry. Why?”

“I’m missing a third of my fuel bays. The trip home will take longer than I have food for.”

“How long since you last slept?”

“Huh? I’m talking about fuel here! Stay focused!”

“Grumpy. Angry. Stupid. How long since last sleep, question?”

I shrug. “I don’t know. I’ve been working on the breeder tanks and fuel bays…I forget when I last slept.”

“You sleep. I watch.”

I gesture wildly at the console. “I have a serious problem here! I don’t have enough fuel storage to survive the trip home! It’s 600,000 kilograms of fuel. It would take 135 cubic meters of storage! I don’t have that much space!”

“I make storage tank.”

“You don’t have enough xenonite for that!”

“Don’t need xenonite. Any strong material will do. Have much metal aboard my ship. Melt, shape, make tank for you.”

I blink a couple of times. “You can do that?”

“Obvious I can do that! You are stupid right now. You sleep. I watch and also design replacement tank. Agree, question?” He starts down the tube toward the dormitory.

“Huh…”

“Agree, question?!” he says, louder.

“Yeah…” I mumble. “Yeah, okay…”

—

I’ve done many EVAs by now. But none were as tiring as this one.

I’ve been out here for six hours. The Orlan is a tough, old suit and can handle it. I can’t say the same about myself.

“Installing final fuel bay now,” I pant. Almost there. Stay focused.

Rocky’s quickly made fuel bays are perfect, of course. All I had to do was take off one of my bays and give it to him to study. Well, I gave it to his robot. However he uses that robot to measure things, it does a good job. Every valve connection is in the right place and the right size. Every screw is perfectly spaced.

In total, he made three perfect copies of the fuel bay I gave him. The only difference is what they're made of. My original bays were made of aluminum. Someone on Stratt’s team had suggested a carbon-fiber hull, but she turned that down. Only well-tested technology. Humanity had over sixty years of testing aluminum spacecraft.

The new bays are made of…an alloy. What alloy? No idea. Rocky doesn’t even know. It’s a mix of metals from less important systems on the Blip-A. Mostly iron, he says. But there are at least twenty different elements all melted together. It’s basically “metal soup.”

But that’s okay. The fuel bays don’t need to hold pressure. They only have to keep the Astrophage on the ship—nothing else. They do need to be strong enough to not break apart from the weight of the fuel inside when the ship speeds up. But that’s not hard. They could literally be made out of wood and work just as well.

“You are slow,” he says.

“You are mean.” I tighten the large cylinder into place with straps.

“Sorry. I am excited. Breeder Tanks Nine and Ten!”

“Yeah!” I say. “Fingers crossed!”

We’re up to Taumoeba-78 as of the latest generation. That strain is breeding away in the tanks while I work on these fuel bays. The spacing is 0.25 percent, which means for the first time ever, some breeder tanks actually have 8 percent or more nitrogen inside.

As for installing the tanks…geez. I’ve learned that the first bolt is the hardest. The fuel bay has a lot of inertia and it’s hard to keep lined up with the hole. Also, the original mounting system for the bay is gone. The pyros took care of that. They never thought I’d be adding in new bays after getting rid of old ones. The pyros don’t just open a clamp. They shear the bolts clean off. And they don’t care about the damage to where it mounts.

I spend a lot of time un-dooming this suicide mission.

While the threaded mounting holes are in okay shape, every one of them has a sheared-off bolt to deal with. With no bolt head, they’re a real pain to unscrew. I’ve found that the best way is to bring extra steel rods and the AstroTorch. Melt the bolt a little, melt the rod a little, and weld them together. The result is ugly but it gives me something to grab onto with enough force to remove the bolt. Usually.

When I can’t get the bolt off, I just start melting things. Can’t be stuck if it’s liquid.

Three hours later, I finally have all the new fuel bays installed…sort of.

I cycle the airlock, climb out of the Orlan, and enter the control room. Rocky is in his bulb waiting for me.

“It went well, question?”

I wiggle my hand back and forth—a gesture both humans and Eridians do and with the same meaning. “Maybe. I’m not sure. A lot of the bolt holes were broken. So the bays aren’t connected as well as they should be.”

“Danger, question? Your ship accelerates at 15 meters per second per second. Will tanks hold, question?”

“I’m not sure. Earth engineers often build in extra safety. I hope they did this time. But I will test to be sure.”

“Good good. Enough talk. Check breeder tanks, please.”

“Yeah, yeah. Let me get some water first.”

He bounces and skitters down his tube to the lab. “Why humans need so much water, question? Inefficient life-forms!”

I gulp down a full liter of water I left in the control room before the EVA. It makes you thirsty. I wipe my mouth and let the bag float away. I push off the wall to float down the tunnel to the lab.

“Eridians need water, too, you know.”

“We stay inside. Closed system. Some waste inside, but we get all the water we need from food. Humans leak! Disgusting.”

I laugh as I float into the lab where Rocky is waiting. “On Earth, we have a scary spider that can kill you. You look like one of those. Just so you know.”

“Good. I’m happy. I am scary space monster. You are leaky space blob.” He points to the breeder tanks. “Check tanks!”

I kick off the wall and float over to the breeders. This is the crucial moment. I should check them one by one starting with Tank One, but forget that. I go straight to Tank Nine.

I shine a penlight into the tank and get a good look at the glass slide that was earlier covered in Astrophage. I check the tank numbers, then check the slide again.

I grin at Rocky. “Tank Nine’s slide is clear. We have Taumoeba-80!”

He makes a huge racket! His arms wave around, his hands hit the tunnel walls. It’s just random sounds with no pattern. After a few seconds he calms down. “Yes! Good! Good good good!”

“Ha-ha, wow. Okay. Calm down.” I check Tank Ten. “Hey, Tank Ten is also clear. We have Taumoeba-82.5!”

“Good good good!”

“Good good good, indeed!” I say.

“Now you test a lot. Venus air. Threeworld air.”

“Yes. Absolutely—”

He moves back and forth from one tunnel wall to the other. “Same gases in each test. Same pressure. Same temperature. Same death ‘radiation’ from space. Same light from nearby star. Same same same.”

“Yes. I’ll do that. I’ll do all of that.”

“Do now.”

“I need rest! I just did an eight-hour EVA!”

“Do now!”

“Ugh! No!” I float over to his tunnel and face him through the xenonite. “First I’m going to breed a lot more Taumoeba-82.5. Just to make sure we have enough for testing. And I’ll make several stable groups of it in sealed containers.”

“Yes! And some on my ship too!”

“Yes. More backups are better.”

He bounces back and forth some more. “ Erid will live! Earth will live! Everyone live! ” He curls the claws of one hand into a ball and presses it against the xenonite. “ Fist me! ”

I push my knuckles against the xenonite. “It’s ‘fist-bump,’ but yeah.”

—

There has to be alcohol somewhere. I can’t imagine Ilyukhina going on a suicide mission without having some booze. I can’t imagine her going across the street without some booze, honestly. And after looking through every box in the storage compartment, I finally find it—the personal kits.

The box has three zipped duffel bags. Each one is labeled with a crewman’s name. “Yáo,” “Ilyukhina,” and “DuBois.” I guess they never replaced DuBois’s personal kit, because I never got a chance to make mine.

Still a little mad about how that happened. But maybe I’ll get a chance to tell Stratt how I feel about it.

I pull the kits into the dormitory with me and Velcro them to the wall. Personal belongings of three people who are now dead. Friends who are now dead.

I may have a sad moment later and spend some time looking at all these bags. But for now, this is a time to celebrate. I want booze.

I open Ilyukhina’s bag. There are all sorts of random little things inside. A pendant with some Russian writing on it, a worn old teddy bear she probably had as a kid, a kilogram of heroin, some of her favorite books, and there we are! Five 1-liter bags of clear liquid labeled водка.

It’s Russian for “vodka.” How do I know that? Because I spent months on an aircraft carrier with a bunch of crazy Russian scientists. I saw that word a lot.

I zip up her bag and leave it Velcroed to the wall. I fly through to the lab where Rocky waits in his tunnel.

“Found it!” I say.

“ Good good! ” His usual jumpsuit and tool-belt is nowhere to be seen. He has an outfit on I’ve never seen before.

“Well, well, well! What have we here?” I say.

He sticks out his shell with pride. It’s covered with a smooth cloth that supports matching hard shapes here and there. Almost like armor, but not fully covering, and I don’t think they’re metal.

The top hole, where his vents are, is ringed with rough gems. Definitely jewelry of some kind. They’re cut, similar to how Earth jewelry might be cut, but the quality is horrible. They’re blotchy and discolored. But they’re really big and I bet they sound great to sonar.

The sleeves leading off the shirt stop about halfway down his arms and are also decorated at the cuffs. Each shoulder is connected to its neighbors by loose braided cords. And for the first time I’ve ever seen, he has gloves on. All five hands are covered in rough material like burlap.

This outfit would really stop Rocky from moving around, but hey, fashion isn’t about being comfy or easy.

“You look great!” I say.

“Thank! This is special clothing for celebration.”

I hold up a liter of vodka. “This is special liquid for celebration.”

“Humans…eat to celebrate?”

“Yeah. I know Eridians eat in private. I know you think it’s gross to watch. But this is how humans celebrate.”

“Is okay. Eat! We celebrate!”

I float over to the two experiments on the lab table. One has a copy of Venus’s air. The other has the air of Threeworld. I made them as exact as I could. I used the best info I have, which is a lot because I have every human reference book ever and Rocky knows his own system.

In both, Taumoeba have not only lived but done great. They breed fast, and even a tiny bit of Astrophage put into either one gets eaten right away.

I hold the bag of vodka up. “To Taumoeba-82.5! Savior of two worlds!”

“You will give that liquid to the Taumoeba, right?”

I unclip the straw. “No, it’s just something humans say. I am honoring Taumoeba-82.5.” I take a sip. It’s like fire in my mouth. Ilyukhina liked her vodka strong and rough.

“Yes. Much honoring!” he says. “Human and Eridian work together, save everyone!”

“Ah!” I say. “That reminds me: I need a life-support system for Taumoeba—something that feeds them just enough Astrophage to keep the colony alive. It has to be completely automatic, work on its own for several years, and weigh less than a kilogram. I need four of them.”

“Why so small?”

“I’m going to put one on each beetle. Just in case something happens to the Hail Mary on the way home.”

“Good plan! You are smart! I can make these for you. Also, today I finish fuel-transfer device. Can give you Astrophage now. Then we both go home!”

“Yeah.” My smile fades.

“This is happy! Your face looks sad. Why?”

“Going to be a long trip and I’ll be all alone.” I haven’t decided if I want to risk a coma on the way home. I may have to for my own sanity. Total solitude and nothing to eat but chalky, nasty coma slurry might just be too much. For the first part of the trip, at least, I definitely plan to stay awake.

“You’ll miss me, right? I’ll miss you. You are friend.”

“Yeah. I’m going to miss you.” I take another sip of vodka. “You’re my friend. Heck, you’re my best friend. And pretty soon we’re going to say goodbye forever.”

He tapped two gloved claws together. They made a muffled sound instead of the usual click that comes with the dismissive gesture. “Not forever. We save planets. Then we have Astrophage technology. Visit each other.”

I give a small smile. “Can we do all that within fifty Earth years?”

“Probably not. Why so soon, right?”

“I only have fifty years or so left to live. Humans don’t”—I hiccup—“don’t live long, remember?”

“Oh.” He’s quiet for a moment. “So we enjoy the time we have left, then go save planets. Then we are heroes!”

“Yeah!” I straighten up. I’m a little dizzy now. I’ve never been much of a drinker, and I’m drinking this vodka more than I should. “We’re the most important people in the galaxy! We’re awesome!”

He grabs a nearby wrench and raises it in one of his hands. “To us!”

I raise the vodka. “To ush!”

“Well. This is it,” I say from my side of the connector.

“Yes,” says Rocky on his side. His voice is low, even though he tries to keep it high.

The Hail Mary is fully fueled: 2.2 million kilograms of Astrophage. That's 200,000 kilograms more than when it left Earth. Rocky’s new tanks were, of course, better and bigger than my old ones.

I rub the back of my neck. “I think our people will meet again. I know humans will want to learn all about Erid.”

“Yes,” he says. “Thank you for laptop. Our scientists can learn about centuries of human technology. You have given greatest gift in history of my people.”

“You tested it in the life-support system you built for it, right?”

“Yes. That is a stupid question.” He grabs a handle on his side to stay in place.

Rocky had taken away his connecting tunnel and resealed the Hail Mary’s hull. He put the connector in place to finish packing.

At my request, he left the alien walls and tunnels in the Hail Mary as they were, but with a few meter-wide holes here and there so I can use the space. I think the more alien material Earth scientists have to study, the better.

The ship still smells a little like ammonia. I guess even alien material isn’t completely airtight. It’ll probably smell that way for a while.

“And your farms?” I say. “You double-checked them all?”

“Yes. Six backup Taumoeba-82.5 colonies, each in separate tanks with separate life-support systems. Each with a fake Earth atmosphere. Your farms are working, question?”

“Yeah,” I say. “Well, it’s just my ten breeder tanks. But now I have them all set up with Venus’s atmosphere. Oh, and thanks for the mini-farms, by the way. I’ll install them in the beetles during my trip. I won’t have much else to do.”

He glances at a notepad. “These numbers you gave me. Are you sure these are the times for me to turn around and the times for me to reach Erid, question? They are so soon. So fast.”

“Yeah, that’s time moving differently for you. Weird stuff. But those are the correct values. I checked them four times. You’ll reach Erid in under three Earth years.”

“But Earth is almost the same distance from Tau Ceti, and you will take four years, question?”

“I’ll feel four years, yes. Three years and nine months. Because time won’t be as compressed for me as it is for you.”

“You have explained before, but again…why, question?”

“Your ship speeds up quicker than mine. You’ll be getting closer to the speed of light.”

He wiggles his shell. “So complicated.”

I point to his ship. “All the info about relativity is on the laptop. Have your scientists check it out.”

“Yes. They will be very happy.”

“Not when they learn about quantum physics. Then they’ll be really annoyed.”

“Don’t get it.”

I laugh. “Don’t sweat it.”

We’re both quiet for a bit.

“Guess this is it,” I say.

“It is time,” he says. “We go save our homes now.”

“Yeah.”

“Your face is leaking.”

I wipe my eyes. “Human thing. Don’t worry about it.”

“I understand.” He pushes himself to his airlock door. He opens it and pauses there. “Goodbye, friend Grace.”

I wave weakly. “Goodbye, friend Rocky.”

He disappears into his ship and closes the airlock door. I return to the Hail Mary. After a few minutes, the Blip-A’s hull robot removes the tunnel.

We fly our ships almost side by side but with slightly different paths. This makes sure neither of us burns up the other with the exhaust from our Astrophage engines. Once we are a few thousand kilometers apart, we can aim in any direction we want.

Hours later, I sit in the cockpit with my spin drives off. I just want one last look. I watch the dot of IR light with the Petrovascope. That’s Rocky, going back to Erid.

“Godspeed, buddy,” I say.

I set course for Earth and start the spin drives.
