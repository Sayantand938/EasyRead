Project Hail Mary

Chapter 5

I glared at the Astrophage. “Why do you go to Venus?”

The microscope view was on the big wall monitor. Each of the three cells was a foot wide at this size. I watched for any clues to why they did it, but Larry, Curly, and Moe didn’t answer.

I’d named them, of course. It’s what teachers do.

“What’s so special about Venus? And how do you even find it?” I crossed my arms. If Astrophage understood body language, they’d know I was serious. “It takes a room full of really smart people at NASA to figure out how to get to Venus. And you do it as a single-celled thing with no brain.”

It had been two days since Stratt left me alone with the lab. The army guys were still at the doors. One was named Steve. Nice guy. The other never talked to me.

I ran my hands through my greasy hair (I hadn’t showered that morning). At least I didn’t have to wear the hazmat suit anymore. Scientists in Nairobi had risked exposing one of their Astrophage to Earth air to see what would happen. It didn’t change. So, thanks to them, labs everywhere could relax and stop working in rooms filled with argon.

I glanced at the pile of papers on a desk. The science world had sped up in a very unscientific way. Careful reviews and published papers were gone. Astrophage research was a free-for-all where researchers posted what they found right away and without proof. It caused confusion and mistakes, but we just didn’t have time to do things the right way.

Stratt kept me updated on most things. Not everything, I was sure. Who knows what other strange things she was doing. She seemed to have power everywhere.

A Belgian research team proved that Astrophage reacts to magnetic fields, but only sometimes. Other times, it seems to ignore them completely, no matter how strong they are. Still, the Belgians could (very rarely) steer Astrophage by putting it in a magnetic field and changing the field’s direction. Was that helpful? No idea. Right now, the world was just gathering information.

A researcher in Paraguay found that ants get confused when they're close to Astrophage. Was that helpful? Okay, that probably wasn't. But it was interesting.

Most importantly, a group in Perth took apart an Astrophage and looked closely at everything inside. They found DNA and mitochondria. Normally, this would have been the biggest discovery ever. Alien life—definitely alien—had DNA and mitochondria!

And…ugh…lots of water…

The point is: The inside of an Astrophage was a lot like the inside of any simple cell on Earth. It used ATP, RNA, and many other very familiar things. Some researchers thought it started on Earth. Others guessed that these molecules were the only way life could happen and Astrophage developed them on its own. And a smaller, louder group suggested life might not have started on Earth at all, and that Astrophage and Earth life have a shared ancestor.

“You know,” I told the Astrophage, “if you weren’t a threat to everything on my planet, you’d be amazing. You’re full of secrets.”

I leaned on a table. “You have mitochondria. So you use ATP for energy, like us. But the light you use to move takes way more energy than your ATP can hold. So you have another way to store energy. One we don’t get.”

One of the Astrophage on-screen moved a bit to the left. It happened often. Now and then, for no reason, they’d just twitch.

“What makes you move? Why move? And how does this random twitching get you from the sun to Venus? And why go to Venus at all?!”

Many people were studying the insides of Astrophage. Trying to figure out how it worked. Looking at its DNA. Good for them. I wanted to know its basic life cycle. That was my goal.

Simple cells don’t just store tons of energy and fly through space for no reason. Astrophage must need something from Venus or it would stay on the sun. And it needs something from the sun, too, or it would stay on Venus.

The sun part was easy: It was there for the energy. Like plants growing leaves. You need that energy to live. It makes sense. But what about Venus?

I picked up a pen and played with it while I thought.

“The Indian Space Research Organization says you go up to 0.92 times the speed of light.” I pointed at them. “Didn’t know we could do that, did you? Figure out how fast you’re going? They used how the light you send out changes to figure it out. And they also know you’re going both ways: to and from Venus.”

I frowned. “But if you hit an atmosphere that fast, you should die. So why don’t you?”

I tapped my forehead. “Because you can handle any heat. Right. So you fly into the atmosphere, but you don’t get hotter. Okay, but you have to slow down. So you’d just be in the upper atmosphere of Venus. Then you…what? Turn around and go back to the sun? Why?”

I stared at the screen for ten minutes, thinking hard.

“Okay, enough. I want to know how you find Venus.”

I went to the hardware store and bought wood, plywood, power tools, and other things I needed. Steve helped me carry a lot of it in. Jerk did nothing.

Over the next six hours, I built a dark closet with a shelf. It was big enough for me to get in and out. I put the microscope on the shelf. The “door” was a piece of plywood I could remove with screws.

I ran power and video lines into the closet through a small hole that I filled with putty to keep light out. I set up my IR camera on the microscope and sealed the closet.

Out in the lab, the monitor showed the infrared light the camera picked up. It was basically a change in frequency. Very low IR bands showed up as red. Higher-energy bands were orange, yellow, and so on. I could see the Astrophage cells as little red blobs, which was expected. At their constant temperature of 96.415 degrees Celsius they would naturally emit an IR wavelength of around 7.8 microns—the low end of what I’d set the camera to look for. It confirmed that the setup was working.

But I didn’t care about that dark-red color. I wanted to see a bright-yellow flash. That would be the Petrova frequency that Astrophage used to move around. If any of my Astrophages moved even a tiny bit, I’d see a very obvious yellow flash.

But it never came. Nothing happened. Nothing at all. Usually, I’d see a jerky motion from at least one of them every few seconds. But now there was nothing.

“So,” I said. “You little brats have calmed down, eh?”

Light. Whatever their navigation system was, it was based on light. I thought that would be the case. What else could you use in space? There’s no sound. No smell. It would have to be light, gravity, or electromagnetism. And light’s the easiest of those three to detect. At least, as far as evolution is concerned.

For my next experiment, I taped a little white LED and a watch battery together. Of course, I wired it backward at first and the LED didn’t light up. That’s pretty much a rule in electronics: You never get diodes right on the first try. Anyway, I rewired it correctly and the LED lit up. I taped the whole thing to the inside wall of the closet. I made sure to position it so the Astrophage on the sample slide would have a direct view of it. Then I sealed everything up again.

Now, from the Astrophage’s point of view, there was a lot of black nothingness and one shining spot of white. That’s kind of what Venus might look like if you were out in space and looking directly away from the sun.

They didn’t move. No hint of motion at all.

“Hmph,” I said.

To be fair, it wasn’t likely to work. If you were at the sun, looking away from it for the brightest spot of light you could see, you’d probably focus on Mercury, not Venus. Mercury is smaller than Venus, but it’s a lot closer so you’d see more light.

“Why Venus?” I wondered. But then a better question popped into my head. “How do you guys find Venus?”

Why did they move randomly? My guess: By simple luck, every few seconds, an Astrophage thought it saw Venus. So it pushed that way. But then the moment was gone, so it stopped pushing.

The answer had to be light waves. My boys didn’t move at all in the dark. But it wasn’t just about how much light there was, or they would have gone for the LED. It had to be something about the wave of the light.

Planets don’t just bounce light. They also give it off. Everything gives off light. How hot something is decides the light wave it gives off. Planets are the same. So maybe Astrophage looked for Venus’s heat signal. It wouldn’t be as bright as Mercury’s, but it would be different—a different “color.”

A quick search told me Venus’s average temperature was 462 degrees Celsius.

I had a whole drawer of extra bulbs for microscopes and other lab tools. I grabbed one and hooked it up to a power supply that I could change. Incandescent bulbs work by making the wire inside so hot it gives off light. That happens around 2,500 degrees Celsius. I didn’t need anything that strong. I just needed a small 462 degrees. I changed the power going to the bulb, watching with a heat camera, until I got just the light wave I wanted.

I moved the whole setup into my test closet, watched the screen with my boys on it, and turned on the fake Venus.

Nothing. The little jerks didn’t move at all.

“What do you want from me?!” I shouted.

I took off my goggles and threw them down. I tapped my fingers on the table. “If I was an astronomer, and someone showed me a light, how would I know if it’s Venus?”

I answered myself. “I’d look for that IR signature! But that’s not what Astrophage does. Okay, someone shows me a blob of light and says I’m not allowed to use emitted IR to figure out the body's temperature. How else could I find out if it’s Venus?”

Spectroscopy. Look for carbon dioxide.

I raised an eyebrow as the idea came to me.

When light hits gas molecules, the electrons get excited. Then they calm down and re-emit the energy as light. But the frequency of the photons they emit is very specific to the molecules involved. Astronomers have used this for decades to know what gases are out there far, far away. That’s what spectroscopy is all about.

Venus’s atmosphere is ninety times Earth’s pressure and almost entirely carbon dioxide. Its CO2 spectroscopy signature would be incredibly strong. Mercury had no carbon dioxide at all, so the closest comparison would be Earth. But we had a tiny CO2 signature compared to Venus. Maybe Astrophage used emission spectra to find Venus?

New plan!

The lab had what seemed like endless light filters. Pick a frequency, and there’s a filter for it. I looked up the spectral signature of carbon dioxide—the peak wavelengths were 4.26 microns and 18.31 microns.

I found the right filters and built a little box for them. Inside, I put a small white lightbulb. Now I had a box that would emit the spectral signature of carbon dioxide.

I put it in the test closet and went out to watch the monitor. Larry, Curly, and Moe hung out on their slide, just like they had all day long.

I flicked on the light box and watched for any reaction.

The Astrophage left. They didn’t just drift toward the light. They were gone. Completely gone.

“Um…”

I had been recording the camera, of course. I rewound it to watch frame by frame. Between two frames they simply vanished.

“Um!”

Good news: Astrophage were drawn to the spectral signature of carbon dioxide!

Bad news: My three irreplaceable, 10-micron-wide Astrophage had shot off somewhere—maybe at speeds close to the speed of light—and I had no idea where they went.

“Craaaaaap.”

—

Midnight. Total darkness. The army guys changed shift to two guys I didn’t know. I missed Steve.

I had aluminum foil and duct tape covering every window of the lab. I sealed the gaps around the doors with electrical tape. I shut off every machine that had a screen or light on it. I put my watch in a drawer because the hands glowed in the dark.

I waited for my eyes to get used to the complete darkness. If I saw even one shape that wasn’t just in my head, I found the light and covered it with tape. Finally, I got the room so dark I couldn’t see anything at all. Opening or closing my eyes made no difference.

The next thing I needed was my new IR goggles.

The lab had a lot of stuff, but infrared goggles weren’t one of them. I thought about asking Steve, the army guy, if he could get some. I probably could have called Stratt, and she would have had them delivered by the president of Peru or something. But this was quicker.

The “goggles” were just the LCD screen from my IR camera with a lot of tape around it. I pressed them to my face and added more tape. Then more and more and more. I’m sure I looked silly. But whatever.

I turned on the camera and looked around the lab. Lots of heat showing up. The walls were still warm from the sun, everything electrical was glowing, and my body was like a spotlight. I changed the settings to look for much hotter things. Specifically, things over 90 degrees Celsius.

I crawled into my small microscope closet and looked at the light box I’d used for the CO2 test.

Astrophage are only 10 microns wide. No way I’d see something that tiny with the camera (or with my eyes, either). But my little aliens are very hot, and they stay hot. So, if they’re not moving, they will have been slowly heating up what’s around them for the last six hours or so. That was the plan.

It worked. I immediately saw a circle of light on one of the plastic light filters.

“Oh thank God,” I gasped.

It was very faint, but it was there. The spot was about 3 millimeters across and faded as it spread from the center. The little guy had been heating up the plastic for hours. I scanned back and forth across the two plastic squares. I quickly found a second spot.

My experiment worked much better than I expected. They saw what they thought was Venus and went straight for it. When they hit the light filters, they couldn’t go any farther. They probably kept pushing until I turned off the light.

Anyway, if I could just make sure that all three Astrophage were there, I could bag the filters, then spend as long as I needed to find and collect them with a microscope and pipette.

And there it was. The third Astrophage.

“The gang’s all here!” I said. I reached into my pocket for a sample bag and got ready to very carefully pull the filter off the light box. That’s when I saw the fourth Astrophage.

Just…doing its own thing. A fourth cell. It was right in the same general area as the first three, on the filters.

“Holy…”

I’d been staring at these guys for a week. There’s no way I would have missed one. There could only be one explanation: One of the Astrophage split. I’d accidentally made the Astrophage reproduce.

I stared at that fourth spot of light for a full minute, taking in how big a deal this was. Breeding Astrophage meant we would have an unlimited supply to study. Kill them, poke them, take them apart, do whatever we wanted. This changed everything.

“Hello, Shemp,” I said.

—

I spent the next two days constantly studying this new behavior. I didn’t even go home—I just slept in the lab.

Steve the army guy brought me breakfast. Great guy.

I should have shared all my findings with the rest of the science community, but I wanted to be sure. Peer review might have been ignored, but at least I could review it myself. Better than nothing.

The first thing that bothered me: CO2 spectral emissions are 4.26 and 18.31 microns. But Astrophage are only 10 microns across, so they couldn’t really interact with light that had a larger wavelength. How could they even see the 18.31 micron band?

I repeated my earlier spectral experiment with just the 18.31 micron filter and got a result I didn’t expect. Strange things happened.

First off, two of the Astrophage zipped over to the filter. They saw the light and went right for it. But how? It should be impossible for Astrophage to interact with a wavelength that big. I mean…literally impossible!

Light is a funny thing. Its wavelength decides what it can and can’t interact with. Anything smaller than the wavelength is basically nonexistent to that photon. That’s why there’s a mesh over the window of a microwave. The holes in the mesh are too small for microwaves to pass through. But visible light, with a much shorter wavelength, can go through easily. So you get to watch your food cook without melting your face off.

Astrophage is smaller than 18.31 microns but somehow still absorbs light at that frequency. How?

But that’s not even the weirdest part. Yes, two of them left for the filter, but the other two stayed there. They didn’t seem to care. They just stayed on the slide. Maybe they didn’t react to the longer wavelength?

So I tried another test. I shined the 4.26 micron light at them again. And I got the same result. The same two went straight for the filter like before, and the other two just didn’t care.

And that was it. I couldn’t be totally sure, but I was pretty sure I’d just figured out the whole Astrophage life-cycle. It clicked in my head like a puzzle coming together.

The two that stayed behind didn’t want to go to Venus anymore. They wanted to go back to the sun. Why? Because one of them had just split and made the other.

Astrophage stay on the sun's surface, collecting energy from the heat. They store it inside somehow, in a way no one understands. Then, when they have enough, they travel to Venus to reproduce, using that stored energy to fly through space with infrared light as fuel. Lots of species travel to reproduce. Why would Astrophage be any different?

The Aussies already figured out that the inside of Astrophage wasn’t that different from life on Earth. It needed carbon and oxygen to make the complex proteins for DNA, mitochondria, and all the other cell stuff. There’s lots of hydrogen on the sun. But the other elements aren’t there. So Astrophage travels to the closest source of carbon dioxide: Venus.

First, it follows magnetic field lines and goes straight from the sun’s North Pole. It has to do that, or the sunlight would be too bright to find Venus. And going straight up from the pole means the Astrophage will have a clear view of Venus’s entire path—with no part of it blocked by the sun.

Ah, and that’s why Astrophage reacts to magnetic fields so inconsistently. It only cares about them at the start of its trip and at no other time.

Then it looks for Venus’s huge carbon dioxide signal. Well, not really “looks for.” It’s probably more like a simple reaction triggered by the 4.26 and 18.31 micron light bands. Anyway, once it “sees” Venus, it goes straight to it. The path it takes—straight from the solar pole, then turning sharply toward Venus—that’s the Petrova line.

Our heroic Astrophage gets to Venus’s upper atmosphere, gets the CO2 it needs, and can finally reproduce. After that, both parent and child go back to the sun, and the cycle starts again.

It’s simple, really. Get energy, get resources, and make copies. It’s what all life on Earth does.

And that was why two of my little Stooges didn’t walk toward the light.

So how does Astrophage find the sun? I guess: Look for the really bright thing and go that way.

I separated Moe and Shemp (the sun-seekers) from Larry and Curly (the Venus-seekers). I put Larry and Curly on a different slide and put it in a sealed container. Then I set up an experiment in the dark closet for Moe and Shemp. This time, I put a bright lightbulb in there and turned it on. I thought they’d go right toward it, but no luck. They didn’t move. Probably not bright enough.

I went to a photography store downtown (San Francisco has a lot of people who love photography) and bought the biggest, brightest, most powerful flash I could find. I replaced the lightbulb with the flash and did the experiment again.

Moe and Shemp went for it!

I had to sit down and take a breath. I should have taken a nap—I hadn’t slept in thirty-six hours. But this was too exciting. I pulled out my cell phone and called Stratt’s number. She answered right away.

“Dr. Grace,” she said. “Find anything?”

“Yeah,” I said. “I figured out how Astrophage reproduce and made it happen.”

Silence for a second. “You bred Astrophage?”

“Yes.”

“Without hurting them?” she asked.

“I had three cells. Now I have four. They’re all alive and fine.”

Silence for another second. “Stay there.”

She hung up.

“Huh,” I said. I put the phone back in my lab coat. “Guess she’s coming.”

Steve the army guy burst into the lab. “Dr. Grace?!”

“Wha…uh, yes?”

“Please come with me.”

“Okay,” I said. “Let me just put my Astrophage samples away—”

“There are lab techs on the way to handle all that. You have to come with me now.”

“O-Okay…”

—

The next twelve hours were… strange.

Steve, the army guy, drove me to a high school football field where a U.S. Marine Corps helicopter had already landed. Without a word, they rushed me into the chopper, and we took off. I tried not to look down.

The chopper took me to Travis Air Force Base, about 60 miles north of the city. Did the marines often land at air force bases? I don’t know much about the military, but that seemed strange. It also seemed a bit much to send in the marines just to keep me from sitting in traffic for a couple of hours, but okay.

There was a jeep waiting for me on the tarmac where the helicopter landed, with an air force guy standing next to it. He introduced himself, I’m sure he did, but I don’t remember his name.

He drove me across the tarmac to a waiting jet. Not a regular passenger jet. And not a Learjet or anything like that. This was a fighter jet. I don’t know what kind. Like I said, I don’t know military stuff.

My guide rushed me up a ladder and into the seat behind the pilot. He gave me a pill and a small paper cup of water. “Take this.”

“What is it?”

“It’ll keep you from throwing up on our clean cockpit.”

“Okay.”

I swallowed the pill.

“And it’ll help you sleep.”

“What?”

He left, and the ground crew took the ladder away. The pilot didn’t say anything to me. Ten minutes later, we took off really fast. I’d never felt acceleration like that before. The pill worked. I definitely would have thrown up.

“Where are we going?” I asked through the headset.

“I’m sorry, sir. I’m not allowed to talk to you.”

“This is going to be a boring trip, then.”

“They usually are,” he said.

I don’t know exactly when I fell asleep, but it was just minutes after we took off. Thirty-six hours of crazy science plus whatever was in that pill knocked me right out, even with the loud jet engines.

I woke up in the dark from a jolt. We had landed.

“Welcome to Hawaii, sir,” said the pilot.

“Hawaii? Why am I in Hawaii?”

“I wasn’t told that information.”

The jet moved to a side runway, and a ground crew brought a ladder. I was only halfway down when I heard, “Dr. Grace? This way, please!”

It was a man in a U.S. Navy uniform.

“Where am I?!” I demanded.

“Naval Station Pearl Harbor,” said the officer. “But not for long. Please follow me.”

“Sure. Why not?”

They put me on another jet with another quiet pilot. The only change was that this time it was a navy jet, not an air force one.

We flew for a long time. I stopped counting the hours. It didn’t matter anyway. I didn’t know how long we’d be flying. Finally, believe it or not, we landed on a real aircraft carrier.

Next, I was on the flight deck feeling like a fool. They gave me earmuffs and a coat and moved me to a helipad. A navy chopper waited for me.

“Will this trip…ever end?!” I asked.

They ignored me and strapped me in. The chopper took off right away. This time, the flight wasn’t as long, just an hour or so.

“This should be interesting,” the pilot said. It was all he’d said the whole flight.

We went down, and the landing gear came out. Below us was another aircraft carrier. I looked closely. Something was different. What was it? Oh, yeah. It had a big Chinese flag on it.

“Is that a Chinese aircraft carrier?!” I asked.

“Yes, sir.”

“Are we, a U.S. Navy helicopter, going to land on that Chinese aircraft carrier?”

“Yes, sir.”

“I see.”

We landed on the carrier’s helipad, and a group of Chinese Navy men watched us with interest. There would be no servicing of the helicopter after the flight. My pilot grinned at them through the windows, and they grinned back.

As soon as I stepped out, he took off again. I was in China’s hands now.

A navy man came forward and motioned for me to follow him. I don’t think anyone spoke English, but I understood what he wanted. He led me to a door in the tower and we went inside. We walked through hallways, up stairwells, and through rooms I didn’t even know what they were for. All the while, Chinese sailors watched me with curiosity.

Finally, he stopped at a door with Chinese writing on it. He opened the door and pointed inside. I walked in, and he slammed the door behind me. So much for my guide.

I think it was an officer’s meeting room. At least, that’s what I thought based on the large table with fifteen people sitting around it. They all turned to look at me. Some were white, some were Black, some were Asian. Some wore lab coats. Others wore suits.

Stratt, of course, sat at the head of the table. “Dr. Grace. How was your trip?”

“How was my trip?” I said. “I got dragged across the whole world without any warning—”

She held up her hand. “It was just a greeting, Dr. Grace. I don’t really care how your trip was.” She stood and spoke to the room. “Ladies and gentlemen, this is Dr. Ryland Grace from the United States. He found out how to breed Astrophage.”

Gasps came from around the table. One man jumped up and spoke with a strong German accent. “Are you serious? Stratt, warum haben sie—? ”

“ Nur Englisch, ” Stratt interrupted.

“Why are we just hearing about this now?” the German asked.

“I wanted to be sure first. While Dr. Grace was traveling, I had technicians pack up his lab. They got four live Astrophage from his lab. I only left him three.”

An older man in a lab coat spoke Japanese in a calm, gentle voice. Next to him, a younger Japanese man in a gray suit translated. “Dr. Matsuka would like to politely ask for a detailed description of the process.”

Stratt stepped aside and pointed to her chair. “Doctor, sit down and explain it to us.”

“Wait,” I said. “Who are these people? Why am I on a Chinese aircraft carrier? And have you ever heard of Skype?!”

“This is an international group of top scientists and political people that I’ve brought together to lead Project Hail Mary.”

“What’s that?”

“That would take a while to explain. Everyone here is eager to hear about your Astrophage findings. Let’s start with that.”

I shuffled to the front of the room and sat awkwardly at the head of the table. All eyes turned to me.

So I told them. I told them all about the wooden closet experiments. I explained all my tests, what I did for each one, and how I did them. Then I explained my conclusions: I told them my idea about the Astrophage life-cycle, how it works, and why. There were a few questions from the scientists and politicians, but mostly they just listened and took notes. Several had translators whispering in their ear.

“So…yeah,” I said. “That’s pretty much everything. I mean—it’s not fully tested yet but it seems pretty simple.”

German Guy raised his hand. “Could we grow Astrophage on a large scale?”

Everyone leaned forward a little. Apparently this was a pretty important question that everyone was thinking about. I was surprised by the sudden intensity of the room.

Even Stratt seemed unusually interested. “Well?” she said. “Please answer Minister Voigt.”

“Sure,” I said. “I mean…why not?”

“How would you do it?” asked Stratt.

“I guess I’d make a big elbow-shaped ceramic pipe and fill it with carbon dioxide. Make one end of it as hot as possible and have a bright light there. Wrap a magnetic coil around it to act like the sun's magnetic field. Put an IR light emitter at the other end of the elbow and have it emit light at 4.26 and 18.31 microns. Make the inside of the pipe as black as possible. That should do it.”

“How does that work?” she said.

I shrugged. “The Astrophage will collect energy on the ‘sun’ side, and when they’re ready to reproduce, they’ll follow that magnetic field to the pipe’s bend. They’ll see the IR light at the other end and go toward it. Seeing that light and being exposed to carbon dioxide makes them reproduce. Then the parent and daughter cells will go back to the sun side. Simple enough.”

A man who looked like a politician raised his hand and spoke with an African accent. “How much Astrophage could be made this way? How fast is the process?”

“It would double in a certain time,” I said. “Like algae or bacteria. I don’t know how long it is, but since the sun is getting dim, it must be pretty quick.”

A woman in a lab coat had been on her phone. She put it down and spoke with a strong Chinese accent. “Our scientists have copied your results.”

Minister Voigt frowned at her. “How did you even know his process? He just told us!”

“Spies, I guess,” said Stratt.

The German scoffed. “How dare you go around us with—”

“Quiet,” said Stratt. “We’re done with all that. Ms. Xi, do you have any other information to share?”

“Yes,” she said. “We think the doubling time is a little over eight days, if everything is perfect.”

“What does that mean?” the African diplomat said. “How much can we make?”

“Well.” I opened the calculator on my phone and tapped some numbers. “If you started with the one hundred and fifty Astrophage we have, and grew them for a year, you’d end up with…about 173,000 kilograms of Astrophage.”

“And would this Astrophage have the most energy possible? Would it all be ready to reproduce?”

“So you want…I guess you’d call it ‘boosted’ Astrophage?”

“Yes,” he said. “That’s a perfect word for it. We want Astrophage holding as much energy as it can.”

“Uh…I guess that could be done,” I said. “First, grow the amount of Astrophage you want, then give them lots of heat but don’t let them see any carbon dioxide. They’ll gather energy and just wait until they can see somewhere to get CO2.”

“What if we needed two million kilograms of boosted Astrophage?” said the diplomat.

“It doubles every eight days,” I said. “Two million kilos would be about four more doublings. So, one month more.”

A woman leaned forward on the table, her fingers together. “We might just have a chance.” She had an American accent.

“A slim chance,” said Voigt.

“There is hope,” said the Japanese translator—probably speaking for Dr. Matsuka.

“We need to talk privately,” said Stratt. “Go get some rest. The sailor outside will show you to a bed.”

“But I want to know about Project Hail Mary!”

“Oh, you will. Trust me.”

—

I slept for fourteen hours.

Aircraft carriers are great in many ways, but they aren’t fancy hotels. The Chinese had given me a clean, comfortable bed in an officer’s room. I had no complaints. I was so tired I could have slept on the flight deck.

When I woke up, I felt something strange on my forehead. I reached up and it was a Post-it note. Someone put a Post-it on my head while I slept. I pulled it off and read it:

Clean clothes and toiletries in the duffel under your bunk. Show this note to any sailor when you’ve cleaned up: 请带我去甲板 7 的官员会议室

—Stratt

“She is such a pain in my butt…” I mumbled.

I got out of my cot. A few officers glanced at me but ignored me. I found the duffel bag with clothes, dental stuff, and soap, just like she said. I looked around the bunkroom and saw a locker room through a doorway.

I used the bathroom (or “head,” I guess, since I was on a ship). Then I showered with three other guys. I dried off and put on the yellow jumpsuit Stratt had left. It had Chinese writing on the back and a big red stripe down the left leg. I figured it was so everyone would know I was a foreign civilian and not allowed everywhere.

I stopped a sailor and showed him the note. He nodded and told me to follow him. He led me through a maze of twisty little passages until we got back to the room I’d been in the day before.

I walked in to see Stratt and some of her…teammates? Some of the same people from yesterday. Minister Voigt, the Chinese scientist—I think her name was Xi—and a guy in a Russian military uniform. The Russian had been there yesterday but hadn’t said anything. They all looked focused, and the table was covered in paper. They mumbled to each other now and then. I didn’t know exactly what was going on, but Stratt was clearly in charge.

She looked up when I came in.

“Ah. Dr. Grace. You look refreshed.” She pointed to her left. “There’s food on the credenza.”

And there was! Rice, steamed buns, fried dough sticks, and a coffee pot. I hurried over and grabbed some. I was really hungry.

I sat at the table with a full plate and cup of coffee.

“So,” I said with a mouth full of rice. “You gonna tell me why we’re on a Chinese aircraft carrier?”

“I needed an aircraft carrier. The Chinese gave me one. Well, they lent it to me.”

I slurped my coffee. “There was a time when something like that would surprise me. But…you know…not anymore.”

“Commercial air travel takes too long and gets delayed,” she said. “Military aircraft work on their own schedule and travel at top speed. I need to get experts from anywhere on Earth in the same room quickly without delays.”

“Ms. Stratt can be very convincing,” said Minister Voigt.

I shoveled more food into my mouth. “Blame whoever gave her all that power,” I said.

Voigt chuckled. “I helped make that decision, actually. I am Germany’s minister of foreign affairs. Like your country’s secretary of state.”

I paused my chewing. “Wow,” I managed to say. I gulped down the mouthful. “You’re the highest-ranking person I’ve ever met.”

“No, I’m not.” He pointed to Stratt.

She put a piece of paper in front of me. “This is what started the Hail Mary Project.”

“You’re showing him?” Voigt said. “Now? Without getting him clearance?”

Stratt put her hand on my shoulder. “Dr. Ryland Grace, I now give you top-secret access to all information about Project Hail Mary.”

“That’s not what I meant,” Voigt said. “There are rules and checks to follow.”

“No time,” Stratt said. “No time for any of that. That’s why you made me the boss. Speed.”

She turned to me and tapped the paper: “These are readings from amateur astronomers worldwide. They show something very important.”

The page had columns of numbers. I saw the column titles: “Alpha Centauri,” “Sirius,” “Luyten 726-8,” and so on.

“Stars?” I said. “These are all stars near us. And wait—did you say amateur astronomers? If you can tell the German foreign minister what to do, why not have professional astronomers working for you?”

“I do,” Stratt said. “But this is old data from the past few years. Professional astronomers don’t study stars near us. They look at distant things. Amateurs record data on local stars. Like train spotters. Hobbyists in their backyards. Some with equipment worth tens of thousands of dollars.”

I picked up the paper. “Okay, what am I looking at?”

“Brightness readings. Adjusted using thousands of amateur data sets and corrected for weather and visibility. Supercomputers helped. The point is: Our sun isn't the only star getting dimmer.”

“Really?” I said. “Ohhh! That makes perfect sense! Astrophage can travel at 0.92 times the speed of light. If it can go dormant and stay alive long enough, it could infect stars near us. It spores! Just like mold! It spreads from star to star.”

“That’s our theory, yes,” said Stratt. “This data is old, going back decades. It’s not super reliable, but the trends are there. The NSA figured out that—”

“Wait. NSA? The U.S. National Security Agency?”

“They have some of the best supercomputers in the world. I needed their supercomputers and engineers to try all sorts of scenarios and spreading patterns for how Astrophage could get around in the galaxy. Back to the point: These nearby stars have been dimming for decades. And the rate of dimming increases quickly—just like we’re seeing with the sun.”

She handed me another piece of paper. It had a bunch of dots connected by lines. Above each dot was a star name. “Because of the speed of light, we had to adjust our observations of the dimming for the distances of the stars and such, but there’s a clear pattern of ‘infection’ from star to star. We know when each star was infected and by which infected star. Our sun was infected by a star called WISE 0855–0714. That star was infected by Sirius, which was infected by Epsilon Eridani. From there, the trail stops.”

I looked closely at the chart. “Huh. WISE 0855–0714 also infected Wolf 359, Lalande 21185, and Ross 128.”

“Yes, every star eventually infects all of its neighbors. Based on our data, we think Astrophage has a maximum range of just under eight light-years. Any star within that range of an infected star will eventually be infected.”

I looked at the data. “Why eight light-years? Why not more? Or less?”

“Our best guess is the Astrophage can only survive so long without a star, and it can travel about eight light-years in that time.”

“That makes sense, from an evolution point of view,” I said. “Most stars have another star within eight light-years, so that’s as far as Astrophage had to evolve to travel while sporing.”

“Probably,” Stratt said.

“Nobody noticed those stars getting dimmer?” I said.

“They only get about ten percent dimmer before stopping. We don’t know why. It’s not easy to see without help, but—”

“But if our sun dims by ten percent, we’re all dead,” I said.

“Pretty much.”

Xi leaned forward on the table, sitting very straight. “Ms. Stratt hasn’t told you the most important thing yet.”

The Russian nodded, his first movement I’d seen.

Xi continued. “Do you know what Tau Ceti is?”

“Do I know?” I said. “I mean, I know it’s a star, about twelve light-years away, I think.”

“Eleven point nine,” said Xi. “Very good. Most people wouldn't know that.”

“I teach junior high school science,” I said. “These things happen.”

Xi and the Russian exchanged surprised glances. Then they both looked at Stratt.

Stratt stared them down. “He’s more than just that.”

Xi gathered herself (though she hadn't really lost it). “Ahem. Anyway, Tau Ceti is definitely inside the group of infected stars. It’s actually close to the middle.”

“Okay,” I said. “I’m guessing it’s special somehow?”

“It’s not infected,” Xi said. “Every star around it is. Two very infected stars are well within eight light-years of Tau Ceti, but it’s still not affected.”

“Why?”

Stratt flipped through her papers. “That’s what we want to learn. So we’re going to build a ship and send it there.”

I scoffed. “You can’t just ‘build’ an interstellar ship. We don’t have the technology. We don’t have anything close to the technology.”

The Russian spoke for the first time. “Actually, my friend, we do.”

Stratt pointed to the Russian. “Dr. Komorov is—”

“Please call me Dimitri,” he said.

“Dimitri leads the Russian Federation’s research into Astrophage,” she said.

“It is a pleasure to meet you,” he said. “I am happy to say that we can actually make interstellar voyages.”

“No, we can’t,” I said. “Unless you’ve got an alien spaceship you never told anyone about.”

“In a way, we do,” he said. “We have many alien spaceships. We call them Astrophage. You see? My group has studied how Astrophage manages energy. It is very interesting.”

I suddenly forgot everything else going on in the room. “Oh God, please tell me you understand where the heat goes. I can’t figure out what it’s doing with the heat energy!”

“We have figured this out, yes,” said Dimitri. “With lasers. It was a very illuminating experiment.”

“Was that a pun?”

“It was!”

“Good one!”

We both laughed. Stratt glared at us.

Dimitri cleared his throat. “Um…yes. We aimed a strong, focused laser at one Astrophage cell. As usual, it didn't get hotter. But after twenty-five minutes, light starts to reflect off it. Our little Astrophage is full. Good meal. It used 1.5 megajoules of light energy. Doesn't want more. But this is a lot of energy! Where does it put all this energy?”

I’m leaning too far forward over the table, but I can’t stop myself. “Where?!”

“We measure Astrophage cell before and after experiment, of course.”

“Of course.”

“Astrophage cell is now seventeen nanograms heavier. You see what this means, right?”

“No, it can’t be. It must have gained that weight from reacting with the air or something.”

“No, it was in a vacuum for the test, of course.”

“Oh my God.” I was thrilled. “Seventeen nanograms…times nine times ten to the sixteenth…1.5 megajoules!”

I flopped back into my chair. “Holy…I mean just…wow!”

“This was how I felt, yes.”

Mass conversion. As Albert Einstein famously said: E = mc 2 _. _ There’s a crazy amount of energy in mass. A modern nuclear plant can power a whole city for a year with the energy in just one kilogram of Uranium. Yes. That’s it. A nuclear reactor's entire output for a year comes from a single kilogram of mass.

Astrophage can, it seems, do this both ways. It takes heat energy and somehow turns it into mass. Then when it wants the energy back, it turns that mass back into energy—as Petrova-frequency light. And it uses that to move in space. So it’s not just perfect for energy storage, it’s a perfect spaceship engine.

Evolution can be incredibly effective when you let it run for billions of years.

I rub my head. “This is just crazy. In a good way, though. Do you think it’s making antimatter inside? Something like that?”

“We don’t know. But it definitely gains mass. And then, after using light as thrust, it loses mass equal to the energy released.”

“That’s…! Dimitri, I want to hang out with you. Like—can we hang out? I’ll buy you a beer. Or vodka. Or anything. I bet there’s an officers’ club on this boat, right?”

“It would be my pleasure.”

“Glad you’re making friends,” said Stratt. “But you’ve got a lot of work to do before you start going to bars.”

“Me? What do I have to do?”

“You need to design and build a place to grow Astrophage.”

I blinked. Then I jumped up. “You’re going to make a ship powered by Astrophage!”

They all nodded.

“Wow! It’s the best rocket fuel ever! How much would we need to—oh. Two million kilograms, right? That’s why you wanted to know how long it would take to make that much?”

“Yes,” said Xi. “For a one hundred thousand kilogram ship, we would need two million kilograms of Astrophage to get it to Tau Ceti. And, thanks to you, we now know how to turn on the Astrophage and make it create thrust when we want.”

I sat back down, pulled out my phone, and opened the calculator. “This would take, like…a lot of energy. Like, more energy than the world has. It would be about ten to the twenty-third Joules. The biggest nuclear reactor on Earth makes about eight gigawatts. It would take that reactor two million years to make that much energy.”

“We have ideas for getting the energy,” said Stratt. “Your job is to make the breeder. Start small and get a test version working.”

“Okay, sure,” I said. “But I didn’t really enjoy the ‘tour of the world’s militaries’ on the way here. Can I take a regular plane home? Economy is fine.”

“You are home,” said Stratt. “The flight hangar is empty. Just tell me what you need—including people to help—and I’ll make it happen.”

I looked at the others in the conference room. Xi, Voigt, and Dimitri all nodded. Yes, this was real. No, Stratt wasn’t kidding.

“Why?!” I demanded. “Why the heck can’t you just be normal, Stratt?! If you want fast military transport, fine, but why not just work at an air base or something sane people would do?!”

“Because we’ll be testing a lot of Astrophage once we grow it. And if we accidentally activate even a couple of kilograms of that stuff, the explosion will be bigger than the biggest nuclear bomb ever made.”

“Tsar Bomba,” said Dimitri. “Made by my country. Fifty megatons. Boom.”

Stratt continued. “So we’d rather be in the middle of the ocean where we won’t wipe out any cities.”

“Oh,” I said.

“And as we get more Astrophage, we’ll go further and further out to sea. Anyway. Head down to the hangar deck. I have carpenters building rooms and offices right now. Pick some you like and take them.”

“This is our life now,” said Dimitri. “Welcome.”
