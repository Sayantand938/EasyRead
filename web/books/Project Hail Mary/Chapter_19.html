Project Hail Mary

Chapter 19

Rocky was stunned by relativity. At first, he just didn't believe me. But as I explained more about how it made sense of his trip, he started to accept it. He doesn't like it, but he understands that the universe follows rules that are more complex than we realize.

And since then, we’ve spent forever making chain.

I made molds as fast as I could, and Rocky made links as fast as the xenonite hardened. It was a good system—one where the results grew quickly. Each new mold I made added one to the number of links Rocky could make at a time.

Chain, chain, chain.

If I never see another chain, it will be too soon. Ten kilometers of chain—each link only 5 centimeters long. That’s two hundred thousand links. Each one connected by hand or claw. We each worked eight hours a day for two weeks just connecting links.

I saw chain whenever I closed my eyes. I dreamed about chain every night. One time I had spaghetti for dinner, and all I could see were smooth, white chains instead of noodles.

But we finished it.

Once we had all the links, we put them together side by side. We each made ten-meter lengths that we linked into twenties, and so on. At least we could be efficient that way. The hard part was finding a place to put it all. Ten kilometers is a lot of chain.

The lab became a storage area. But even then, it wasn’t big enough. Rocky—always a talented engineer—made large spools that barely fit through the airlock. After a lot of spacewalks, I attached them to the hull. Then I stored the chain on them in 500-meter sections. But to do spacewalks, I had to stop the centrifuge. So everything from then on was in zero g.

Ever put together a chain in zero gravity? It’s not easy.

The final assembly of those 500-meter pieces was tough, to say the least. I had to connect all twenty of them while in my spacesuit. Luckily, I had the manipulator tool from the IVME. NASA didn’t plan for it to make chains, but that’s what I used it for.

Now Rocky and I float in the control room. He’s in his pod and I’m in my pilot’s seat.

“Probe status?” I say.

Rocky checks his readouts. “Device is working.”

Rocky did a good job on the sampler probe. At least, I think he did. Engineering isn’t my strong suit.

The sampler is a steel ball, 20 centimeters across. It has a good, thick ring on top that connects to the chain. Small holes go through the ball around its middle. They lead to an empty space inside. There’s a pressure sensor in there and a few switches. The pressure sensor knows when the probe is at the right height, and will tell the switch to seal off the space. It’s easy to do: just turn the inner shell a little to move the holes out of line with the holes in the outer ball. That, with some good seals, will trap the local air in the space.

He also added a thermometer and heater in there. Once the sampler seals, the heater will keep the air inside at the same temperature it started at. Simple stuff, really, but I hadn’t thought of it. Life can be pretty sensitive to temperature changes.

The only thing left is a small radio transmitter that sends out a strange signal I couldn’t read or figure out with my gear. Apparently it’s a very common Eridian data connection. But he has the receiver for it and that’s what matters.

Just like that, with very little trouble, Rocky had made a life-support system for Adrian life-forms—a system that didn’t need to know what conditions to provide ahead of time. It just keeps things as they are.

He really is a genius. I wonder if all Eridians are like that, or if he’s special.

“I guess…we’re ready?” I say. I’m not exactly full of confidence.

“Yes,” he quavers.

I strap myself into the pilot’s seat. He uses three of his hands to grip handholds in his bulb.

I bring up the Attitude Control panel and start a roll. Once I have the ship pointed backward to where we're going and parallel to the ground below, I stop the rotation. Now we’re hurtling along, butt-first, at 12 kilometers per second. I need that to be almost zero.

“Orientation is good,” I say. “Starting thrust.”

“Yes,” says Rocky. He watches his readout screen intently. It shows him the textured version of my own screen, thanks to that camera he set up earlier.

“Here goes…” I fire up the spin drives. We go from zero g to 1.5 g’s in under a second. I am pressed back in my chair and Rocky grabs a support with a fourth hand to stay steady.

As the Hail Mary slows down, our speed can no longer keep us in orbit. I glance at the Radar panel and it confirms that we are losing altitude. I adjust the ship’s angle so we are pointing very slightly upward from horizontal. Just a fraction of a degree.

Even that small amount is too much! The radar shows us gaining altitude rapidly. I bring the angle back down. This is a sloppy, nasty, horrible way to fly a spacecraft, but it’s all I have. There was no point to calculating this maneuver in advance. There are so many variables and ways to mess up the math I’d be flying on manual almost immediately anyway.

After a few more tries, I get the hang of it. I increase the angle little by little as the ship slows down compared to the planet.

“You say when to drop probe,” Rocky says. His claw hangs over the button that will shoot out the spools and let the chain drop. We just have to hope it doesn’t get stuck.

“Not yet,” I say.

The angle screen shows we’re at 9 degrees from the flat. I need to get us to 60. Something catches my eye to the right. It’s the outside camera view. The planet below is…shining.

No. Not the whole planet. Just the part right behind us. It’s the air reacting to the IR blast from the engines. The Hail Mary is putting out hundreds of thousands of times more energy into that spot than Tau Ceti does.

The IR heats the air so much it breaks apart and it’s glowing red. The brightness gets stronger as our angle gets sharper. Then the glowing area starts to grow. I knew it would be a lot, but I didn't know it would be this much. We’re leaving a red line across the sky, destroying everything in the air. The carbon dioxide is probably being broken apart by the heat into carbon bits and free oxygen. The oxygen might not even be forming O2. That’s a lot of heat.

“The engines are heating up Adrian’s air a lot,” I say.

“How you know, question?”

“Sometimes I can see heat.”

“What, question?! Why you no tell me this, question?”

“It’s about sight…there’s no time to explain. Just trust me: We are making the air very hot.”

“Danger, question?”

“I don’t know.”

“I don’t like that answer.”

We go up and up and up. The light behind us gets brighter and brighter. Finally, we reach the right angle.

“Angle achieved,” I say.

“Happy! Release, question?”

“Stand by. Speed…”—I check the navigation screen—“127.5 meters per second! Just what I figured! Wow, it worked!”

I feel Adrian pull me back into my seat.

This is something I often have to explain to my students. Gravity doesn’t just “disappear” when you’re in orbit. Actually, the gravity you feel in orbit is almost the same as on the ground. The weightlessness astronauts feel in orbit is from constantly falling. But the curve of the Earth makes the ground move away at the same rate you fall. So you just fall forever.

The Hail Mary isn’t failing. The engines keep us in the sky, and our angle makes us move forward at 127 meters per second—about 285 miles per hour. Fast for a car, but surprisingly slow for a spaceship.

The air behind us glows so brightly that the outside camera shuts down to protect itself.

The Life Support panel appears on my main screen by itself. EXTERNAL TEMPERATURE EXTREME, it warns.

“The air is hot,” I say. “The ship is hot.”

“Ship no touch air,” Rocky says. “Why ship hot, question?”

“It’s reflecting our IR back at us. And it’s so hot now it’s creating its own IR. We’re getting baked.”

“Your ship Astrophage-cooled, question?”

“Yes. Astrophage cools the ship.”

Astrophage pipes run all along the hull for times like this. Well, not for “blasting a planet’s atmosphere with so much IR light it can melt steel,” but for the usual situations where heat builds up. Mostly from the sun or Tau Ceti heating the ship, and the heat having nowhere to go.

“Astrophage absorb heat. We safe.”

“Okay. We’re safe. And ready. Drop probe!”

“Drop probe!” He slams his claw on the Drop button.

I hear the scrape and clink of the spools sliding off the hull one by one and falling toward the planet. Twenty spools in total, each one drops and unwinds before the next is released. Our best shot at keeping the chain from tangling.

“Spool Six away…” Rocky says.

The Life Support panel blinks its warning again. I mute it again. Astrophage lives on stars. I’m sure a little reflected IR light won’t be too hot for it.

“Spool Twelve away…” Rocky says. “Sampler signal good. Sampler detecting air now.”

“Good!” I say.

“Good good,” he says. “Spool Eighteen away…air density increasing…”

With the external cameras offline, I can’t see what’s happening. But Rocky’s readings match our plan. Right now, the chain is unfurling as it falls. Our angled engines keep us in the sky, but nothing stops the chain from falling straight down.

“Spool Twenty away. All spools released. Air density of sampler is almost like an Astrophage breeding ground…”

I watch Rocky with bated breath.

“Sampler has closed! Seal is airtight, heater is on! Success success success!”

“Success!” I yell.

It’s working! It’s actually working! We have a sample of Adrian air from where the Astrophage breed! If there are any predators, they have to be there, right? I hope so.

“Step two now.” I sigh. This is not going to be fun.

I unhitch my restraints and climb out of the chair. Adrian’s gravity, 1.4 times Earth's, pulls me down at an angle. The whole room feels tilted because it is tilted. This isn’t engine thrust I’m feeling. It’s gravity.

One point four g’s isn’t too bad. Everything’s a bit harder, but not too much. I climb into the Orlan EVA suit. This is going to be difficult, to say the least. I have to go outside and do an EVA while completely under the effects of gravity.

Of course, no part of the EVA suit, the airlock, or my training was designed for this. Who would have thought I would have to walk around on the ship in full gravity? More than full, in fact?

Yet however much gravity there may be, there’s still no air. The worst possible situation. But there’s no other way. I have to get the sample.

Right now, the sampler hangs at the end of a 10-kilometer chain, just dangling in the air. There’s no easy way for us to get it back to the ship.

When planning this all out, my first thought was to blast off from the planet, then grab the sampler when we’re back to zero g. Problem is, there’s just no way to do that without destroying the sampler. Any way I try to get the ship out of Adrian’s gravity—or even into a stable orbit—will mean using the spin drives. They’d push the ship along, which would make the chain and sample fall behind us into the IR blast from the ship. And then the sampler, everything in it, and the chain all become individual, very hot atoms.

The next idea I had was to make a huge spool that could winch up the chain. But Rocky told me he’d never be able to make a spool big enough and strong enough to bring up the entire 10-kilometer length.

Rocky had a pretty smart idea: The sampler could climb the chain when it was done. But after some testing he dropped the idea. He said the risks just weren’t worth it.

So we have…this other plan.

I grab a special winch Rocky designed and attach it to my suit’s tool belt.

“Be careful,” says Rocky. “You are friend now.”

“Thanks,” I say. “You are friend also.”

“Thank.”

I cycle the airlock and look outside.

This is a strange experience. Space is black. The planet below me is grand. Everything looks right for being in orbit. But there's gravity.

A red glow from the planet shows around the edges of the Hail Mary. I'm not dumb—I turned the ship so it would protect me from the deadly heat coming off the atmosphere.

The airlock door is "up." I have to pull myself—and a hundred pounds of gear—up and through that opening. And I have to do it in 1.4 g.

It takes me five whole minutes. I grunt. I say some things that aren't quite swear words, but I get it done. Soon I’m standing on top of my ship. One wrong step and I’ll fall to my death. It wouldn't take long, either. If I fell below the ship, the engines would end me fast.

I attach a tether to the handrail at my feet. Will a zero-g tether save me if I fall? It’s not climbing gear. It wasn't made for this. But it’s better than nothing, I guess.

I walk along the hull toward the chain anchor. It’s a big xenonite square that Rocky made. He explained in detail how to stick it to the hull. Looks like it worked. The chain is still attached.

I reach it and get on my hands and knees. The gravity is really hard in this EVA suit. None of this is how it should be.

I hook my (maybe useless) tether to the closest handrail and pull the winch from my tool belt.

The chain hangs at a 30-degree angle and goes into the planet below. It goes so far that it's too thin to see after a kilometer or so. But I know from Rocky’s readings it’s the full 10 kilometers down, with a sample container full of what could save two whole planets of people.

I fit the winch between the chain and the anchor plate. The chain doesn’t move—not even a millimeter. But that was expected. There’s just no way human strength could move something that heavy.

I hook the winch to the anchor plate. The winch casing is xenonite, so the xenonite connection should be strong enough for what comes next.

I tap the winch a couple of times just to make sure it’s in place. It is.

Then I press the start button.

A gear comes out from the center of the winch, one tooth catching a chain link in the middle. The gear turns and pulls the chain into the winch. Inside, it turns the link around and slides it across its neighbor to release it.

When we made the chain, we used “trap” links that connect without us having to seal each one. It’s very unlikely the links would come apart on their own. But the winch is made to do just that.

Once the link is free, the winch pushes it out the side and does the same for the next link.

“The winch works,” I say into my radio.

“Happy,” Rocky says.

It’s simple, clear, neat, and solves all the problems. The winch is strong enough to lift the chain. It separates the links and lets them fall to the planet below. Having a long chain hanging down next to the one we’re pulling up would be a disaster. Imagine tangled earbud wires, but 10 kilometers long.

No, each link will fall away on its own, and the rising chain will keep going.

“When the winch reaches link two hundred sixteen, speed up.”

“Yes.”

I don’t know how many links have gone through. But it’s moving steadily. Maybe two links every second. A careful, slow start. I watch for two minutes. That seems right. “It’s working. At least two hundred sixteen links are done.”

“Speed up.”

Two links a second might sound fast, but it would take about thirty hours to raise the chain at that rate. I don’t want to be out here that long, and we don’t want to stay in this risky, constant-thrust position for that long. I push the control lever forward. The winch speeds up. Everything looks okay, so I put it in the final spot.

Now the links come out of the winch too fast to count, and the chain rises quickly.

“The winch is at top speed. Everything’s fine.”

“Good.”

I keep my hand on the control lever and watch the chain. If that sampler reaches the winch, everything will go wrong. The sample container will break, all the samples will die, and we’d have to make another chain.

I don’t want to do that. God, I can't say how much I don’t want to do that.

I squint into the distance, always watching. Boredom is a real problem here. I know it will take a while to pull up this whole chain, but I have to be ready for the sampler.

“Sampler device radio signal strong,” Rocky says. “Getting closer. Be ready.”

“I’m ready.”

“Be very ready.”

“I am very ready. Calm down.”

“Am calm. You calm down.”

“No, you calm dow—wait. I see the sampler!”

The end of the chain, with the sampler attached, rushes toward me from the planet below. I grab the control lever and slow the winch. The sampler climbs slower and slower until it’s barely moving. All but the last few links of the chain fall and the sampler is finally within reach. I stop the winch.

Instead of risking dropping the big orb, I grab the top link of the chain and unhook it from the winch. Now I have a ball and chain. I hold on to the chain tightly and clip it to my belt. I still don’t let go. I’m not taking any chances with this.

“Status, question?”

“I have the sample. Returning.”

“Great! Happy happy happy!”

“Don’t celebrate until I’m inside!”

“Understood.”

I take two steps and the ship shakes. I fall to the floor and grab two handrails.

“What the heck was that?!”

“I don't know. Ship moved. Suddenly.”

The ship shakes again, this time it’s a steady pull. “We’re going the wrong way!”

“Get inside fast fast fast!”

The horizon comes into view. The Hail Mary isn’t holding its position anymore. It’s tipping forward. That's definitely not right.

I scramble from grip to grip. I don’t have time to clip the tether each time. I just have to hope I don’t fall.

Another sudden jolt and the hull slides sideways under me. I fall on my back but keep holding tight to the sampler chain. What is happening?! No time to think. I have to get inside before the ship flips and kills me.

I hold onto the grips for dear life and crawl to the airlock. Thank God it’s still mostly facing up. I hold the sampler to my chest and fall inside. I land headfirst. Lucky for me the Orlan helmet is so strong.

I struggle to my feet as best I can in the bulky spacesuit. I reach up, grab the outer hatch, and slam it shut. I cycle the airlock and get out of the suit as fast as I can. I’ll leave the sampler in the airlock for now. I need to know what is wrong with the ship.

I half climb, half fall into the control room. Rocky is in his bulb.

“Screens flash many colors!” he yells over the noise. He points his camera around, watching the feed on his textured screen.

A metallic groan screams from somewhere below. Something is bending and doesn’t want to. I think it’s the hull.

I get in the control seat. No time to strap in. “Where’s that noise coming from?”

“All around,” he says. “But loudest at starboard dormitory wall. It bending inward.”

“Something’s tearing the ship apart! It has to be the gravity.”

“Agree.”

But that bothers me. This ship was made for speeding up. It lasted four years at 1.5 g’s. It should handle this same force, right? Something’s wrong.

Rocky grabs his handholds to stay put. “We have sample. We leave now.”

“Yeah, let’s get out of here!” I crank the spin-drive controls all the way up. The ship can hit 2 g’s if it has to. And it definitely has to now.

The ship jumps forward. This isn't a smooth, planned takeoff. This is just running away in a panic.

The smart way to escape a gravity well is sideways, using the Oberth effect. I try to keep us mostly level with the ground. I'm not trying to get away from Adrian. I just want a stable orbit that doesn't need engines to stay up. I need speed, not distance.

I need to keep the drives at full power for ten minutes. That should give us the 12 kilometers per second we need to stay in orbit. I just need to point a little above the horizon and go.

That’s what I want, anyway. But it’s not working. The ship keeps turning forward and drifting sideways. What’s happening?!

“Something wrong,” I say. “It’s fighting me.”

Rocky holds on easily. He's way stronger than me. "Engine problem? Adrian causing lots of heat."

"Maybe." I look at the Nav console. We're speeding up. That's good, at least.

"Ship bending in large space under sleeping area," Rocky says.

"What? There's no space below—oh." He can feel the whole ship with his echolocation. Not just where we live. So, "large space under the sleeping area" means the fuel tanks.

Oh no.

"Turn off engines?"

"We're too slow. We'll fall into the atmosphere."

"Okay. Hope."

"Hope." Yes, hope. That's all we have now. Hope that the ship doesn't break apart before we get into a stable orbit.

The next few minutes are the most nerve-wracking of my life. And, I've had some pretty nerve-wracking moments lately. The ship keeps making awful noises, but we're still alive, so I guess it didn't break. Finally, after what feels like way more than ten minutes, we're fast enough to stay in orbit.

“Speed good. Engines off.” I slide the spin-drive power sliders to zero. I let my head fall back to the headrest in relief. Now we can take our time and figure out what went wrong. No need to use the engines to…

Wait.

My head fell back into the headrest. It fell back into the headrest.

I hold my arms out in front of me, then relax them. They fall down and to the left.

“Uh…”

“Gravity still here,” says Rocky, echoing my own observations.

I check the Nav console. Our speed is good. We’re in a stable orbit around Adrian. Well, actually it’s ugly as heck—the highest point is 2,000 kilometers farther from the planet than the lowest point. But it’s an orbit, darn it. And it’s stable.

I check the Spin Drive panel again. All three drives are at zero. No thrust at all. I go into the diagnostics screen and confirm that each of the 1,009 revolver triangles spread throughout the three drives is stationary. They are.

I let my arm fall again. It does the same strange movement. Down and to the left.

Rocky does a similar motion with one of his arms. “Adrian gravity, maybe?”

“No. We’re in orbit.” I scratch my head.

“Spin drive, question?”

“No. It’s off. There’s no thrust.”

I let my arm fall again. This time it hits the seat's armrest.

“Ow!” I shake my hand. That really hurt.

I let it fall again as a test. It fell faster this time. That’s why it hurt.

Rocky pulls several tools from his jumpsuit and drops them one by one. “Gravity increasing.”

“This doesn’t make any sense!” I say.

I check the Nav panel again. Our speed has increased a lot since I last looked. “Our speed is increasing!”

“Engines on. Only explanation.”

“No way. The spin drives are off. We shouldn't be speeding up!”

“Force increasing,” he says.

“Yes,” I say. I’m struggling to breathe. Whatever force we're experiencing, it’s way more than a g or two. This is getting out of control.

Using all my strength, I reach for the screen and flip through the displays. Navigation, Petrovascope, External View, Life Support…everything looks fine. Until I get to “Structure.”

I'd never really looked at the Structure panel. It's just a gray outline of the ship. But now, for the first time, it shows something.

There’s an odd red spot on the left fuel tank. Is that a hole? It could be. The fuel tanks are outside the part that holds air. They could have a huge hole and we wouldn’t lose air.

“There’s a hole in the ship…” I say. I try to switch back to the external cameras.

Rocky watches my screen with his camera and texture pad. He’s doing great—no problems from the huge forces.

I move the cameras to show the damaged part of the ship.

And there it is. A huge hole in the left side of the ship. It must be 20 meters long and half as wide. The edges of the hole show what happened—the hull melted.

It was the backlash from Adrian’s atmosphere. Not a real explosion, but just infrared light bouncing off the air. The ship tried to warn me the hull was too hot. I should have listened.

I thought the hull couldn’t melt. Astrophage cooled it! But it can melt. Even if Astrophage perfectly absorbs heat (and maybe it does), the heat must pass through the metal to be absorbed. If the hull's outer layer melts before the heat can pass through it, the Astrophage can’t help.

“Confirmed. Hull breach. Port fuel tank.”

“Why thrust, question?”

It clicks. “Oh crap! The Astrophage in the fuel bay! It’s exposed to space! It can see Adrian! My fuel is going to Adrian to breed!”

“Bad bad bad!”

That’s the thrust source. Trillions of horny Astrophages, ready to breed. They see Adrian all at once. Not just carbon dioxide, but their homeland. The planet they evolved to find over billions of years.

As each new Astrophage layer rushes toward Adrian, the next layer gets exposed. The ship is pushed by the departing Astrophage's IR thrust. Luckily, the Astrophage behind them absorb the energy. But they also absorb the momentum.

It’s not perfect. It’s a chaotic, sputtering explosion. Any second, this could become a larger, less controlled IR plume, and we’ll be vaporized. I must stop this.

I can jettison fuel bays! I saw that the first day in the control room! Where was it…?

It takes all my strength to lift my arm to the screen, but I manage to pull up the Astrophage panel. It shows a map of the ship, and the fuel-bay area is divided into nine rectangles. I don’t have time to compare these rectangles to the damaged part of the hull. I grunt, force my arm forward, and tap one that I think is in the right spot.

“Getting…rid…of…bad…fuel bay…” I say through gritted teeth.

“Yes yes yes!” Rocky says, cheering me on.

The Fuel Pod screen appears: ASTROPHAGE 112.079 KG. Next to that, a button labeled “Jettison.” I hit it. A confirmation window appears. I confirm.

A sudden jerk of acceleration throws me to the side. Even Rocky can’t stay still. He slams into the side of his bulb but quickly recovers and grabs his handholds with all five hands.

The hull groans louder than before. The acceleration hasn’t stopped, and my vision gets blurry. The pilot’s seat starts to bend. I’m about to pass out, so we’re probably at 6 g or more.

“Thrust continues,” Rocky says, trembling.

I can’t reply. I can’t make any sound at all.

I know the fuel bay I got rid of was in the damaged area. There must be more than one broken bay. No time to be careful. In a few seconds, the force will be too strong for me to reach the screen at all. If there’s a second broken bay, it’ll be next to the one I just ditched. But there are two bays next to it. I pick one at random. Fifty-fifty chance. With a huge effort, I tap its icon, the Jettison button, and confirm.

A jolt shakes the ship, and I’m thrown around like a rag doll. In my darkening vision, I see Rocky curled up in a ball, bouncing against the walls, leaving silver blood splatters wherever he hits.

If anything, the force is worse than before. But wait…now it’s the other direction.

Instead of being pulled back into my seat, I’m now being pulled away from it, my body pressing into the restraints.

The Centrifuge screen, of all things, comes to the foreground. EXCESSIVE CENTRIFUGAL FORCE WARNING, it blinks.

“Nnnng,” I say. I meant to say Oh God, but I can’t breathe anymore.

All that fuel blasting out into space…it didn’t just leave along the ship’s long side. It blew out at an angle, spinning us like a top. And the exploding fuel bays probably made things even worse.

Well, I stopped the fuel leak, at least. There are no new forces pushing the ship. Now I just have to deal with the spin. I manage to get a breath in. The centrifugal force is less than the uncontrolled thrust force, but it’s still huge. But hey, at least it pulls my arms toward the screen instead of away from it.

If I can get the spin drives back online, maybe I can stop the—

My seat finally gives out. I hear the pops as the anchor points break. I fall forward, into the screen, still strapped to the metal seat, which crushes me from behind.

The chair probably doesn’t weigh much in normal gravity. Maybe 20 kilograms. But with this much centripetal force, it’s like having a cement block on my back. I can’t breathe.

This is it. The weight of the chair is so much I can’t fill my lungs. I get dizzy.

It’s called mechanical suffocation. That’s how boa constrictors kill. Funny thing to think about as I die.

Sorry, Earth, I think. Better last thought.

My lungs, now full of carbon dioxide, are freaking out. But the adrenaline doesn’t give me the strength to get away. It just keeps me awake to feel death better.

Thanks, adrenal glands.

The ship isn’t groaning anymore. I guess everything that could break has broken, and what’s left can handle it.

My eyes water. They sting. Why? Am I crying? I screwed up for everyone, and we’re all going to die because of it. Good reason to cry. But this isn’t feelings. It’s pain. My nose hurts too. Not from pressure. Something burns inside my nose.

Something probably broke in the lab. Some bad chemical. Good thing I can’t breathe. I probably wouldn’t like the smell.

Then, suddenly, I can breathe again! I don’t know how, but I gasp for air. Then I cough like crazy. Ammonia. It’s everywhere. Too strong. My lungs scream, and my eyes water more. Then there’s a new smell.

Fire.

I roll over and see Rocky floating above me. Not in his pod. He’s in the control room!

He has cut my restraints and pulled the chair free. He shoves it aside.

He stands over me, unsteady. I can feel the heat coming from his body just inches away. Smoke pours out of the radiator slits on his shell.

His knees give way, and he falls onto the screen next to me, breaking it. The LCD screen goes black, and the plastic frame melts.

I see a trail of smoke leading up the tunnel to the lab and beyond.

“Rocky! What have you done!”

The crazy guy must have used the big airlock in the dormitory! He came into my space to save me. And he’ll die because of it!

He shivers and pulls his legs under himself.

“Save…Earth…Save…Erid…” he trembles. Then he slumps down.

“Rocky!” I grab his shell without thinking. It’s like putting my hands on a hot stove. I pull back quickly. “Rocky…no…”

But he doesn't move.
