Project Hail Mary

Chapter 3

“All right,” I said, looking at the clock. “We have one minute until the bell. You know what that means!”

“Lightning round!” yelled my students.

Life hadn't changed much since the Petrova line announcement, surprisingly.

The situation was terrible and deadly, but it was also normal. Londoners during the Blitz in World War II went about their day as usual, knowing that sometimes buildings get blown up. No matter how bad things were, someone still had to deliver milk. And if Mrs. McCreedy’s house was bombed at night, you just took it off the delivery list.

So, with the apocalypse coming—possibly caused by an alien—I stood in front of a bunch of kids and taught them basic science. Because what's the point of having a world if you're not going to pass it on to the next generation?

The kids sat in neat rows of desks, facing the front. Pretty standard stuff. But the rest of the room was like a mad scientist's lab. I’d spent years making it look that way. I had a Jacob’s ladder in one corner (I kept it unplugged so the kids didn’t kill themselves). Along another wall was a bookshelf full of jars with animal parts in formaldehyde. One of the jars was just spaghetti and a boiled egg. The kids guessed about that one a lot.

And in the center of the ceiling was my pride and joy—a huge mobile that was a model of the solar system. Jupiter was the size of a basketball, while tiny Mercury was as small as a marble.

It had taken me years to get a reputation as the “cool” teacher. Kids are smarter than most people think. And they can tell when a teacher actually cares about them, not just going through the motions. Anyway, it was time for the lightning round!

I grabbed a handful of beanbags off my desk. “What is the real name of the North Star?”

“Polaris!” said Jeff.

“Correct!” I threw a beanbag to him. Before he even caught it, I asked the next question. “What are the three basic kinds of rocks?”

“Igneous, sedentary, and metamorphic!” yelled Larry. He was easily excited, to say the least.

“So close!” I said.

“Igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic,” said Abby with a sneer. She was a pain, that one, but really smart.

“Yes!” I threw her a beanbag. “What wave do you feel first during an earthquake?”

“The P-wave,” Abby said.

“You again?” I threw her a beanbag. “What’s the speed of light?”

“Three times ten to—” Abby began.

“C!” yelled Regina from the back. She rarely spoke. It was good to see her opening up.

“Tricky, but right!” I threw her a beanbag.

“I was answering first!” Abby complained.

“But she finished her answer first,” I said. “What’s the closest star to Earth?”

“Alpha Centauri!” Abby said quickly.

“Wrong!” I said.

“No, I’m not!”

“Yes, you are. Anyone else?”

“Oh!” Larry said. “It’s the sun!”

“Right!” I said. “Larry gets the beanbag! Watch out for what you assume, Abby.”

She folded her arms in a huff.

“Who knows the radius of Earth?”

Trang raised his hand. “Three thousand, nine hundre—”

“Trang!” Abby said. “The answer is Trang.”

Trang froze, confused.

“What?” I asked.

Abby smirked. “You asked who could tell you the radius of Earth. Trang can. I answered correctly.”

Outsmarted by a thirteen-year-old. Not the first time. I dropped a beanbag on her desk as the bell rang.

The kids jumped up and grabbed their books and backpacks. Abby, thrilled with her win, took her time.

“Remember to trade in your beanbags at the end of the week for toys and prizes!” I called to them as they left.

Soon, the classroom was empty. Only the sounds of kids in the hallway showed anyone had been there. I picked up their homework from my desk and put it in my bag. Sixth period was over.

Time to hit the teachers’ lounge for a cup of coffee. Maybe I’d grade some papers before I headed home. Anything to avoid the parking lot. A bunch of helicopter moms would be swarming the school to pick up their children. And if one of them saw me, they always had some complaint or suggestion. I can’t blame someone for loving their kids, and God knows we could use more parents involved in their kids’ educations, but there’s a limit.

“Ryland Grace?” said a woman’s voice.

I looked up, surprised. I hadn’t heard her come in.

She looked to be in her mid-forties, wearing a nice business suit. She carried a briefcase.

“Uh, yeah,” I said. “Can I help you?”

“I think you can,” she said. She had a slight accent. Something European—I couldn’t quite place it. “My name is Eva Stratt. I’m with the Petrova Taskforce.”

“The what?”

“The Petrova Taskforce. It’s an international group formed to handle the Petrova-line situation. I’ve been given the job of finding a solution. They’ve given me some power to get things done.”

“They? Who's they?”

“Every country in the UN.”

“Wait, what? How did—”

“Unanimous secret vote. It’s complicated. I want to talk to you about a scientific paper you wrote.”

“Secret vote? Never mind.” I shook my head. “I'm done writing papers. Academia didn’t suit me.”

“You’re a teacher. You’re still in academia.”

“Well, yeah,” I said. “But I mean, you know, academia. With scientists and peer review and—”

“And jerks who got you kicked out of your university?” She raised an eyebrow. “And who cut off all your funding and made sure you never got published again?”

“Yeah. That.”

She pulled a binder out of her briefcase.

She opened it and read the first page. “ ‘An Analysis of Water-Based Assumptions and Recalibration of Expectations for Evolutionary Models.’ ” She looked up at me. “You wrote this paper, right?”

“I’m sorry, how did you get—”

“A boring title, but the content is really exciting, I must say.”

I set my bag on my desk. “Look, I wasn't in a good place when I wrote that, okay? I was tired of the research world, and it was my way of saying goodbye. I’m much happier now as a teacher.”

She flipped through a few pages. “You spent years fighting the idea that life needs liquid water. You have a whole section here called ‘The Goldilocks Zone Is for Dummies.’ You call out many famous scientists by name and scold them for thinking a certain temperature range is necessary.”

“Yeah, but—”

“Your doctorate is in molecular biology, right? Don’t most scientists agree that life needs liquid water to develop?”

“They’re wrong!” I crossed my arms. “There’s nothing special about hydrogen and oxygen! They're needed for life on Earth, sure. But another planet could have completely different conditions. All life needs is a chemical reaction that makes copies of the original thing that started it. And you don’t need water for that!”

I closed my eyes, took a deep breath, and let it out. “Anyway, I got mad, and I wrote that paper. Then I got a teaching license, a new career, and started enjoying my life. So I’m glad no one believed me. I’m better off.”

“I believe you,” she said.

“Thanks,” I said. “But I have papers to grade. Can you tell me why you’re here?”

She put the binder back in her briefcase. “I assume you know about the ArcLight probe and the Petrova line.”

“I wouldn’t be a very good science teacher if I didn’t.”

“Do you think those dots are alive?” she asked.

“I don’t know—they might just be dust moving around in magnetic fields. I guess we’ll find out when ArcLight gets back to Earth. That’s soon, right? Just a few weeks?”

“It comes back on the twenty-third,” she said. “Roscosmos will get it from low-Earth orbit with a special Soyuz mission.”

I nodded. “Then we’ll know pretty soon. The smartest people in the world will look at them and figure out what they are. Who’s going to do that? Do you know?”

“You,” she said. “You’re going to do it.”

I stared blankly.

She waved her hand in front of my face. “Hello?”

“You want me to look at the dots?” I said.

“Yes.”

“The whole world asked you to solve this, and you came to a middle school science teacher?”

“Yes.”

I turned and walked out. “You’re lying, crazy, or both. I have to go now.”

“This isn’t a choice,” she said to my back.

“Looks like one to me!” I waved goodbye.

Yeah. It wasn’t a choice.

When I got back to my place, before I reached my door, four men in suits surrounded me. They showed me their FBI badges and rushed me into one of three black SUVs in the parking lot. After a twenty-minute drive where they wouldn't answer any of my questions or even talk to me, they parked and took me into a plain-looking building in a business park.

My feet barely hit the ground as they led me down an empty hall with doors every 30 feet. Finally, they opened double doors at the end of the hall and nudged me inside.

Unlike the rest of the empty building, this room was full of furniture and shiny, high-tech gadgets. It was the best biology lab I’d ever seen. And right in the middle was Eva Stratt.

“Hello, Dr. Grace,” she said. “This is your new lab.”

The FBI agents closed the doors behind me, leaving Stratt and me alone in the lab. I rubbed my shoulder where they had handled me a little too roughly.

I looked at the door behind me. “So…when you say ‘a certain amount of authority’…”

“I have all the authority.”

“You have an accent. Are you even from America?”

“I’m Dutch. I used to manage things at ESA. But that doesn’t matter. Now I’m in charge here. There is no time for slow, international groups. The sun is dying. We need a solution. It’s my job to find it.”

She pulled up a lab stool and sat down. “These ‘dots’ are probably alive. The sun dimming faster and faster matches how a living thing's population grows.”

“You think they’re… eating the sun?”

“They’re at least eating its energy,” she said.

“Okay, that’s—well, scary. But anyway: What do you want from me?”

“The ArcLight probe is bringing the samples back to Earth. Some of them might still be alive. I want you to study them and find out what you can.”

“Yeah, you said that before,” I replied. “But I still think there are people who can do this better than me.”

“Scientists everywhere will study them, but I want you to see them first.”

“Why?”

“It lives on or close to the sun. Does that sound like a life-form that needs water?”

She was right. Water can't exist at those temperatures. After about 3,000 degrees Celsius, hydrogen and oxygen break apart. The sun's surface was 5,500 degrees Celsius.

She went on. “The field of guessing about life on other planets is small—only about five hundred people in the world. And everyone I speak to—from Oxford professors to Tokyo University researchers—seems to think you could have been a leader if you hadn’t suddenly quit.”

“Wow,” I said. “I didn’t leave well. I’m surprised they said such good things about me.”

“Everyone knows how serious this is. There’s no time to hold grudges. But you can prove you were right. You don’t need water for life. That must be something you want.”

“Yeah,” I said. “I mean…yes. But not like this.”

She jumped off her stool and walked to the door. “It is what it is. Be here on the twenty-third at seven p.m. I’ll have the sample for you.”

“Wha—” I said. “It’ll be in Russia, won’t it?”

“I told Roscosmos to land their Soyuz in Saskatchewan. The Royal Canadian Air Force will get the sample and bring it straight here to San Francisco by fighter jet. The U.S. will let the Canadians use the airspace.”

“Saskatchewan?”

“Soyuz capsules launch from Baikonur Cosmodrome, which is at a high latitude. The safest landing spots are at that same latitude. Saskatchewan is the closest big, flat area to San Francisco that works.”

I held up my hand. “Wait. Do the Russians, Canadians, and Americans all just do what you say?”

“Yes. No questions asked.”

“Are you kidding me with all this?!”

“Get settled in your new lab, Dr. Grace. I have other things to do.”

She walked out the door without another word.

“Yes!” I pump my fist.

I jump up and climb the ladder to the lab. Then I climb that ladder and grab the Mystery Hatch.

Like before, when I touch the handle, the computer says, “To unlock hatch, state your name.”

“Ryland Grace,” I say, smiling. “ Dr. Ryland Grace.”

A small click from the hatch is the only answer. After all the thinking I did to remember my name, I hoped for something more exciting. Confetti, maybe.

I grab the handle and twist. It turns. My area is about to get bigger by at least one room. I push the hatch up. Unlike the door between the bedroom and the lab, this one slides to the side. This next room is pretty small, so I guess there wasn’t space for a swinging door. And that next room is…um…?

LED lights turn on. The room is round, like the other two, but the walls slant inward toward the ceiling. It’s like the top of a cone.

I haven’t had much information lately. Now it’s hitting me from everywhere. Computer monitors and touchscreens cover every surface. The number of blinking lights and colors is amazing. Some screens show numbers, others have diagrams, and others are just black.

On the edge of the slanted walls is another hatch. This one is less mysterious. It says AIRLOCK across the top, and it has a round window. Through the window, I see a small chamber—just big enough for one person—with a spacesuit inside. The far wall has another hatch. Yup. It’s an airlock.

And in the center of everything is a chair. It's placed just right so I can easily reach all the screens and touch panels.

I climb into the room and sit in the chair. It’s comfortable, like a bucket seat.

“Pilot detected,” the computer says. “Angular anomaly.”

Pilot. Okay.

“Where is the anomaly?” I ask.

“Angular anomaly.”

This computer is no HAL 9000. I look around at the many screens for a clue. The chair turns easily, which is good in this 360-degree computer pit. I see one screen with a blinking red border. I lean in to get a better look.

ANGULAR ANOMALY: RELATIVE MOTION ERROR

PREDICTED VELOCITY: 11,423 KPS

MEASURED VELOCITY: 11,872 KPS

STATUS: AUTO-CORRECTING TRAJECTORY. NO ACTION REQUIRED.

Well. That doesn’t mean anything to me. Except “kps.” That might mean “kilometers per second.”

Above the text is a picture of the sun. It’s jiggling slightly. Maybe it’s a video? Like a live feed? Or am I just imagining it? On a whim, I touch the screen with two fingers and spread them apart.

The image zooms in, just like on a smartphone. There are a couple of sunspots on the left side of the image. I zoom in on those until they fill the screen. The image stays amazingly clear. It’s either a very high-resolution photo or a very high-resolution solar telescope.

I guess the group of sunspots is about 1 percent the width of the circle. Pretty normal for sunspots. That means I’m now looking at half a degree of the sun’s edge (very rough math here). The sun rotates about once every twenty-five days (science teachers know this stuff). So it should take an hour for the spots to move off the screen. I’ll check back later and see if they have. If so, it’s a live image. If not, it’s a picture.

Hmm…11,872 kilometers per second.

Speed is relative. It only makes sense if you compare two things. A car on the freeway might be going 70 miles per hour compared to the ground , but compared to the car next to it, it’s moving almost 0. So what is that “measured velocity” measuring the speed of? I think I know.

I’m in a spaceship, right? I must be. So that value is probably my speed. But compared to what? Judging by the big picture of the sun above the text, I’m guessing it’s the sun. So I’m going 11,872 kilometers per second compared to the sun.

I see a flicker from the text below. Did something change?

ANGULAR ANOMALY: RELATIVE MOTION ERROR

PREDICTED VELOCITY: 11,422 KPS

MEASURED VELOCITY: 11,871 KPS

STATUS: AUTO-CORRECTING TRAJECTORY. NO ACTION REQUIRED.

Those numbers are different! They both went down by one. Oh wow. Wait a second. I pull the stopwatch from my toga (the best ancient Greek philosophers always carried stopwatches in their togas). Then I stare at the screen for what feels like forever. Just before I’m about to quit, the numbers both drop by one again. I start the timer.

This time, I’m ready for the wait. Again, it feels endless, but I stand still. Finally, the numbers both drop again and I stop the timer.

Sixty-six seconds.

“Measured velocity” is going down by one every sixty-six seconds. Some quick math tells me that’s an acceleration of…15 meters per second per second. That’s the same “gravity” acceleration I figured out earlier.

The force I’m feeling isn’t gravity. And it’s not a centrifuge. I’m in a spaceship that is constantly speeding up in a line. Well, actually it’s slowing down—the values are going down.

And that velocity…it’s a lot of velocity. Yes, it’s going down, but wow! To reach Earth orbit you only need to go 8 kps. I’m going over 11,000. That’s faster than anything in the solar system. Anything that fast will escape the sun’s gravity and fly off into interstellar space.

The readout doesn’t have anything to show what direction I’m going. Just a relative velocity. So now my question is: Am I heading toward the sun, or away from it?

It’s almost a given. I’m either going to crash into the sun or head out into deep space with no chance of coming back. Or, I might be going generally toward the sun, but not directly at it. If that’s the case, I’ll miss the sun…and then fly off into deep space with no hope of returning.

Well, if the sun image is live, the sunspot will get bigger or smaller on-screen as I move. So I just have to wait to see if it’s live. That’ll take about an hour. I start the stopwatch.

I look at the million other screens in the small room. Most of them are active, but one just shows a picture of a round emblem. I think it’s probably just a screen saver or something. If I touch it, that computer will turn on. But that screen saver might be the most important thing in here.

It’s a mission emblem. I’ve seen enough NASA documentaries to recognize it. The round emblem has a blue outer ring with white letters. The letters say HAIL MARY at the top and EARTH at the bottom. The name and “home port” for this ship.

I didn’t think the ship came from somewhere besides Earth, but okay. Anyway, I guess I finally know the name of the ship I’m on.

I’m on the Hail Mary.

Not sure what to do with that information.

But that’s not all the emblem tells me. Inside the blue ring, there’s a black circle with strange symbols inside: a yellow circle with a dot in the middle, a blue circle with a white cross, and a smaller yellow circle with a lowercase t. No idea what any of that is supposed to mean. Around the edge of the black area it says: “姚,” “ИЛЮХИНА,” and “GRACE.”

The crew.

I’m “Grace,” so the other two must be the names of the mummies in the bunks downstairs. A Chinese person and a Russian person. The memory of them is almost there, but I can’t quite grasp it. I think some internal protection is blocking it. When I remember them, it’s going to hurt, so my brain won’t let me remember them. Maybe. I don’t know—I’m a science teacher, not a trauma expert.

I wipe my eyes. Maybe I won’t try so hard to remember that just yet.

I have an hour to spare. I let my mind drift to see what else I can recall. It’s getting easier and easier.

—

“I’m not completely comfortable with all this,” I said. My voice was muffled by the hazmat suit. My breath fogged up the clear plastic face shield.

“You’ll be fine,” Stratt’s voice said over the intercom. She watched from behind thick, double-layered glass.

They’d improved the lab a bit. The equipment was the same, but now the whole room was sealed off from the air. The walls were covered in thick plastic sheets, held together with special tape. I saw CDC logos everywhere. Quarantine rules. Not very comforting.

The only way in was through a big plastic airlock. And they made me put on the hazmat suit before entering. An air hose connected my suit to a spool in the ceiling.

All the best equipment was ready for anything I wanted to do. I’d never seen a lab so well supplied. And in the middle was a cart with wheels holding a cylinder. The word образец was stenciled on the cylinder. Not very helpful.

Stratt wasn’t alone in the observation room. About twenty people in military uniforms stood with her, all watching with interest. There were Americans, Russians, a few Chinese officers, and many more uniforms I didn’t recognize. A large international group. None of them spoke, and they all stayed a few feet behind Stratt, without a word.

I grabbed the air hose with my gloved hand and pointed it at Stratt. “Is this really necessary?”

She pressed the intercom button. “There’s a good chance the sample in that cylinder is an alien life-form. We’re not taking any risks.”

“Wait… you’re not taking any risks. But I am!”

“It’s not like that.”

“How is it not like that?”

She paused. “Okay, it’s exactly like that.”

I walked to the cylinder. “Did everyone else have to go through all this?”

She looked at the military people and they shrugged at her. “What do you mean by ‘everyone else’?”

“You know,” I said. “The people who moved it to this container.”

“That’s the sample container from the capsule. It’s three centimeters of lead around a shell of steel that’s a centimeter thick. It’s been sealed since it left Venus. It has fourteen latches you’ll need to open to get to the sample itself.”

I looked at the cylinder, back to her, back to the cylinder, and back to her. “This is nonsense.”

“Look at the bright side,” she said. “You’ll be forever known as the man who made first contact with extraterrestrial life.”

“If it even is life,” I mumbled.

I got the fourteen latches open with some effort. Those things were tight. I vaguely wondered how the ArcLight probe closed them in the first place. Must have been some kind of cool automated system.

The inside wasn’t impressive. I didn’t expect it to be. Just a small, clear, plastic ball that appeared to be empty. The mysterious dots were microscopic and there weren’t very many of them.

“No radiation detected,” Stratt said through the intercom.

I glanced over at her. She watched her tablet intently.

I took a good long look at the ball. “Is this a vacuum?”

“No,” she said. “It’s full of argon gas at normal pressure. The dots have been moving around the whole time the probe was returning from Venus. So it looks like the argon doesn’t affect them.”

I looked all around the lab. “There’s no glove box here. I can’t just expose unknown samples to normal air.”

“The whole room is full of argon,” she said. “Don’t kink your air line or tear your suit. If you breathe argon—”

“I’ll suffocate without even knowing. Yeah, okay.”

I took the ball to a tray and carefully twisted it open. I put one half in a sealed plastic container and wiped the other half with a dry cotton swab. I scraped the swab on a slide and took it to a microscope.

I thought they’d be harder to find, but there they were. Dozens of little black dots, all wriggling.

“You recording all this?”

“From thirty-six different angles,” she said.

“Sample has many round objects,” I said. “Almost the same size—each about ten microns across…”

I adjusted the focus and tried different backlighting. “Samples are solid…I can’t see inside, even with the brightest light….”

“Are they alive?” Stratt asked.

I glared at her. “I can’t tell that instantly. What do you think will happen?”

“I want you to find out if they’re alive. And if they are, find out how they work.”

“That’s a big ask.”

“Why? Biologists figured out how bacteria work. Just do what they did.”

“That took thousands of scientists two centuries!”

“Well…do it faster than that.”

“Here’s the thing”—I pointed back to the microscope—“I’m going back to work. I’ll tell you what I find out when I find it out. Until then, you can all enjoy some quiet time to study.”

I spent the next six hours doing small tests. During that time, the military people left, until only Stratt was left. I had to admire her patience. She sat in the back of the room and worked on her tablet, sometimes looking up to see what I was doing.

She perked up when I went through the airlock and into the room. “Got something?” she asked.

I unzipped the suit and stepped out. “Yeah, I need to pee.”

She typed on her tablet. “I didn’t think of that. I’ll have a bathroom put in the quarantine area tonight. It’ll have to be a portable toilet. We can’t have pipes going in and out.”

“Fine, whatever,” I said. I hurried off to the restroom to take care of things.

When I came back, Stratt had moved a small table and two chairs to the middle of the observation room. She was sitting in one of the chairs and pointed to the other. “Have a seat.”

“I’m in the middle of—”

“Have a seat.”

I sat down. She definitely had a powerful presence. Maybe it was her tone of voice or just how confident she seemed? Either way, when she spoke, you just felt like you should do what she said.

“What have you found so far?” she asked.

“It’s only been one afternoon,” I said.

“I didn’t ask how long it’s been. I asked what you’ve found out so far.”

I scratched my head. After being in that suit for hours, I was sweaty and probably smelled bad. “It’s…weird. I don’t know what those dots are made of. And I really want to know.”

“Is there some equipment you need that you don’t have?” she asked.

“No, no. It has everything a guy could want. It just…doesn’t work on these dots.” I sat back in the chair. I’d been standing most of the day and it was nice to rest for a moment. “The first thing I tried was the x-ray spectrometer. It shoots x-rays into a sample, which makes it send out photons. You can then tell what elements are there from the photons' wavelengths.”

“And what did that tell you?”

“Nothing. As far as I can tell, these dots just soak up x-rays. The x-rays go in, but they never come out. Nothing comes out. That’s very strange. I can’t think of anything that does that.”

“Okay.” She wrote some notes on her tablet. “What else can you tell me?”

“Next, I tried gas chromatography. That’s where you turn the sample into a gas and then figure out what elements or compounds are in the gas. That didn’t work either.”

“Why not?”

I threw my hands up. “Because the darn things just won’t turn into gas. That made me try all sorts of burners, ovens, and crucible furnaces, but nothing happened. The dots don’t change even at two thousand degrees Celsius. Nothing.”

“And that’s odd?”

“It’s really odd,” I said. “But these things live on the sun. At least sometimes. So, I guess being very heat-resistant makes sense.”

“They live on the sun?” she said. “So they’re alive?”

“I’m pretty sure they are, yeah.”

“Explain more.”

“Well, they move around. You can easily see it through the microscope. That alone doesn’t prove they’re alive—lifeless things move all the time because of static electricity or magnetic fields or whatever. But I noticed something else. Something weird. And it made everything make sense.”

“Okay.”

“I put a few dots in a vacuum and ran a spectrograph. Just a simple test to see if they give off light. And they do, of course. They give off infrared light at the 25.984 micron wavelength. That’s the Petrova frequency—the light that makes the Petrova line. I expected that. But then I noticed they only give off light when they’re moving. And they really give off a lot of it. I mean, not a lot from our point of view, but for a tiny single-celled organism it’s a ton.”

“And why is that important?”

“I did some quick calculations. And I’m pretty sure that light is how they move around.”

Stratt raised an eyebrow. “I don’t understand.”

“Believe it or not, light has momentum,” I said. “It creates a force. If you were out in space and you turned on a flashlight, it would give you a tiny bit of thrust.”

“I didn’t know that.”

“Now you do. And even a tiny push on a tiny object can move it. I figured out the dots’ average mass was about twenty picograms. That took a while, but the lab equipment is great. Anyway, the movement I see matches the force of the light they send out.”

She set her tablet down. I had, it seemed, actually gotten her full attention. “Does that happen naturally?”

I shook my head. “No. Nothing in nature stores that much energy. You don’t get how much energy these dots are giving off. It’s like…reaching the point where mass turns into energy. E = mc 2 stuff. These tiny dots have way more energy stored inside them than they should.”

“Well,” she said. “They did just come from the sun. And the sun is losing energy.”

“Yeah. That’s why I think it’s alive,” I said. “It takes in energy, stores it somehow, then uses it to move. That’s not just physics or chemistry. That’s complicated and on purpose. It must have evolved.”

“So the Petrova line is…tiny rocket flames?”

“Probably. And I bet we’re only seeing a bit of the light from that area. They use it to push themselves to Venus or the sun. Or both. I don’t know. The light will go away from where they’re going. Earth isn’t in that line, so we only see light bouncing off space dust nearby.”

“Why go to Venus?” she asked. “And how do they make more of themselves?”

“Good questions. I don’t know. But if they’re simple things that just react to stuff, they probably split in half.” I paused. “That’s when a cell splits to make two new ones—”

“Yes, I know that.” She looked up. “People always thought if we found aliens, they’d be little green guys in UFOs. We never thought it’d be something simple and dumb.”

“Yeah,” I said. “This isn’t Vulcans dropping by to say hi. This is…space algae.”

“An invasive species. Like cane toads in Australia.”

“Good comparison.” I nodded. “And there are more and more of them. Fast. The more there are, the more solar energy they use.”

She pinched her chin. “What would you call something that eats stars?”

I tried to remember my Greek and Latin. “I think you’d call it ‘Astrophage.’ ”

“Astrophage,” she said. She typed it into her tablet. “Okay. Get back to work. Find out how they breed.”

—

Astrophage!

Just the word makes all my muscles tense up. A chilling terror that hits hard.

That’s the name. The thing that threatens all life on Earth. Astrophage.

I glance at the monitor with my zoomed-in image of the sun. The sunspots have moved a lot. Okay, it’s a real-time image. Good to know.

Waaaaait…I don’t think they’re moving fast enough. I check the stopwatch. I was only daydreaming for about ten minutes. The sunspots should have moved a little bit. But they’re halfway off the screen. Way more than they should have.

I pull the tape measure from my toga. I zoom out the image and actually measure the widths of the sun and sunspot cluster on the screen. No more guesses. I want real math here.

The solar disc is 27 centimeters on-screen and the sunspots are 3 millimeters. And they moved half their width (1.5 millimeters) in ten minutes. Actually, it was 517 seconds, according to my stopwatch. I scribble some math on my arm.

At this resolution, they’re moving 1 millimeter every 344.66 seconds. To cross the entire 27 centimeters it would take (scribble, scribble) just over 93,000 seconds. So it’ll take that long for the cluster to cross the near side of the sun. It’ll take twice that long to get all the way around. So 186,000 seconds. That’s a little over two days.

Over ten times faster than it should take to rotate.

This star I’m looking at…it’s not the sun.

I’m in a different solar system.
