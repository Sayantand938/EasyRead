Project Hail Mary

Chapter 29

I built the new Taumoeba farm. I used sheet aluminum and did some simple machining on the CNC mill. It was easy.

Rocky’s ship is the problem.

I’ve been watching his engine fire every day for the past month. Now it’s gone.

I float in the control room. The spin drives are off, and the Petrovascope is at its most sensitive setting. There’s some random Petrova-wavelength light coming from Tau Ceti itself, as always. And even that’s faint. The star, almost as bright as Earth’s sun, now just looks like a slightly larger dot in the night sky.

But aside from that…nothing. I’m too far away to detect the Tau Ceti–Adrian Petrova line, and the Blip-A is nowhere to be seen.

And I know exactly where it should be. Down to the milli-arc-second. And from here, its engines should be lighting up my scope….

I checked the numbers again and again, even though I’d already proven my formulas correct by watching his progress every day. Now there’s nothing. No sign from the Blip-A.

He’s stranded out there. His Taumoeba got out of their container and tunneled into his fuel bays. From there, they ate everything. Millions of kilograms of Astrophage gone in just days.

He’s smart, so he must have the fuel separated into compartments. But those compartments are made of xenonite, right? Yeah.

Three days.

If the ship was broken, he'd fix it. Rocky can fix anything. And he works fast. Five arms moving quickly, often doing different things at once. He might be fighting a huge Taumoeba infection, but how long would that take? He has lots of nitrogen. He can get as much as he wants from his ammonia air. Let's say he did that as soon as he saw the infection.

How long would it take him to get everything working again?

Not this long.

Whatever happened, if the Blip-A could be fixed, he would have fixed it by now. The only reason it's still dead in space is that it's out of fuel. He couldn't stop the Taumoeba in time.

I put my head in my hands.

I can go home. I really can. I can go back and be a hero for the rest of my life. Statues, parades, and all that. And I'll be in a new world where all energy problems are solved. Cheap, easy, endless energy everywhere because of Astrophage. I can find Stratt and tell her where to go.

But then Rocky dies. And more important, Rocky's people die. Billions of them.

I'm so close. I just need to live for four years. Yeah, I'll be eating gross coma goo, but I'll be alive.

My annoying logical mind tells me about the other choice: Launch the beetles—all four of them. Each with its own Taumoeba mini-farm and a USB stick full of data and results. Earth scientists will take it from there.

Then turn the Hail Mary around, find Rocky, and take him home to Erid.

One problem: It means I die.

I have enough food to survive the trip to Earth. Or I have enough to survive the trip to Erid. But even if the Eridians refuel the Hail Mary right away, there won’t be enough food for me to survive the trip back to Earth from Erid. I’ll only have a few months of food left at that point.

I can’t grow anything. I don’t have any seeds that will grow or any living plants. I can’t eat Eridian food. It has too many heavy metals and other poisons.

So that’s what I’m stuck with. Option 1: Go home a hero and save all of humanity. Option 2: Go to Erid, save an alien species, and starve to death soon after.

I pull on my hair.

I sob into my hands. It feels good but wears me out.

All I see when I close my eyes is Rocky’s dumb shell and his little arms always fiddling with something.

It’s been six weeks since I decided. It wasn’t easy, but I’m not changing my mind.

I turn off the spin drives for my daily routine. I turn on the Petrovascope and look out into space. I see nothing.

“Sorry, Rocky,” I say.

Then I see a tiny bit of Petrova light. I zoom in and look around that area. I see four small dots on the screen, hardly there at all.

“I know you’d like a beetle to take apart, but I couldn’t let one go.”

The beetles, with much smaller spin drives, won’t be seen for much longer. Especially since they’re speeding toward Earth and I’m going almost the other way toward the Blip-A.

The Astrophage inside the mini-farms will keep the Taumoeba safe from radiation, and I tested everything to make sure both the farms and the life inside could handle the strong acceleration that beetles use. They’ll be back at Earth in about two years, from their point of view. About thirteen years, as Earth sees it.

I turn the spin drives back on and keep going.

Finding a spaceship “somewhere just outside the Tau Ceti system” is really hard. It’s like being given a rowboat and told to find a toothpick “somewhere in the ocean.” It’s like that, but much harder.

I know the path he took and that he stayed on it. But I don’t know when his engines stopped working. I only checked on him once a day. Right now, I’m right in the middle of where I think he is and I’m moving at the speed I think he’s moving. But that’s just the start. I have a big search ahead of me.

I wish I had tracked him more often. Because I don’t know the exact time his engines died, my guess could be off by about 20 million kilometers. That’s about one-eighth the distance between the Earth and the sun. It’s a distance so large it takes light a full minute to cross it. That’s the best I can do with the information I have.

Frankly, I’m lucky the error isn't larger. If the Taumoeba had escaped a month later, it would have been much worse. And all this is going on at the edge of the Tau Ceti system. Barely the beginning of the trip. The distance between Tau Ceti and Earth is over four thousand times the size of the entire Tau Ceti system.

Space is big. It’s…so, so big.

So yeah. I’m extremely lucky to have only 20 million kilometers to search.

“Hmm,” I mumble.

This far away from Tau Ceti, his ship won’t be reflecting much Taulight. There’s no chance I’d spot the Blip-A with my telescope.

Side note: I’m going to die.

“Stop,” I say. Whenever I think about my impending death, I think about Rocky instead. He must feel hopeless right now. I’m coming, buddy.

“Wait…”

I’m sure he’s sad, but he’s also not one to stay down for long. He’ll be working on a solution. What would he do? His whole species is on the line and he doesn’t know I’m coming. He wouldn’t just kill himself, right? He’d do anything he could think of, even if it had only a tiny chance of success.

Okay. I’m Rocky. My ship is wrecked. Maybe I saved some Astrophage. The Taumoeba couldn’t have taken all of it, right? So I have some. Can I make my own beetle? Something to send back to Erid?

I shake my head. That would need a guidance system. Computer stuff. Too advanced for Eridian science. That’s why they had twenty-three crew members on a huge ship. Besides, it’s been a month and a half. If he was going to build a small ship, he’d be done by now and I would have seen its engine light up. Rocky is fast.

Okay. No beetle. But he has energy. Life support. Enough food to last a long time (original crew of twenty-three, and it was meant to be a round-trip).

“Radio?” I say.

Maybe he’ll make a radio signal. Something strong enough to be heard on Erid. Just a small chance it will be detected, but it's something. Eridians live a long time. Waiting ten years for rescue wouldn’t be a big deal. Well, not compared to life or death. If you asked me before, I’d say it’s impossible to send a radio signal ten light-years. But this is Rocky, and he might have some rescued Astrophage to power what he makes.

It doesn’t need to have information. It just needs to be noticed.

But…no. It’s just not possible. A quick calculation tells me that even with Earth’s radio technology (which is better than Erid’s), the signal at Erid would be much weaker than background noise.

Rocky will know that too. So there’s no point.

“Hmm.”

I wish I had better radar. Mine is good for a few thousand kilometers. That’s not nearly enough. Rocky could probably make something if he were here. It’s strange, but I wish Rocky were here to help me save Rocky.

“Better radar…” I mutter.

I have plenty of power and a radar system. Maybe I can figure something out.

But I can’t just add power to the emitter and expect it to work. I’ll burn it out for sure. How can I change Astrophage energy into radio waves?

I jump up from my pilot’s seat. “Duh!”

I have everything I need for the best radar ever! Forget my built-in radar system, with its weak emitter and sensors. I have spin drives and a Petrovascope! I can blast 900 terawatts of IR light out the back of my ship and see if any bounces back with the Petrovascope—an instrument made to detect even the smallest amounts of that exact light!

I can’t have the Petrovascope and engines on at the same time. But that’s fine! Rocky is far away!

I make a search grid. It’s pretty simple. I’m right in the middle of where I think Rocky is. So I have to search all directions.

Easy enough. I start the spin drives. I take manual control, which means I have to say “yes,” “yes,” “yes,” and “override” to a bunch of warnings, as usual.

I push the throttle all the way and turn hard to the left with the yaw controls. The force shoves me back and to the side. This is like doing donuts in a parking lot, but with spaceships.

I keep it tight—it takes me thirty seconds to make one full circle. I’m roughly back where I started. Probably a few dozen kilometers off, but whatever. I cut the engines.

Now I watch the Petrovascope. It doesn’t show everything, but it can cover a good 90-degree view at a time. I slowly sweep across space in the same direction I’d pointed the engines, and at the same speed. It’s not perfect; I could mess up the timing. If Rocky is very close or very far away, this won’t work. But this is just my first try.

I finish a full circle with the Petrovascope. Nothing. So I do another lap. Maybe Rocky is farther than I thought.

The second lap turns up nothing.

Well, I’m not giving up yet. Space is three-dimensional. I’ve only searched one flat slice of the area. I tilt the ship forward 5 degrees.

I do the same search pattern again. But this time, the plane of my search is 5 degrees different from the last time. If I don’t see anything this time, I’ll tilt another 5 degrees and try again. And so on until I get to 90 degrees, when I will have searched all directions.

And if that doesn’t work, I’ll start over, but with a faster sweep on the Petrovascope.

I rub my hands together, take a sip of water, and get to work.

—

A flash!

I finally see a flash!

Halfway through my Petrova scan of the 55-degree plane. A flash!

I jerk in surprise, which throws me out of the seat. I bounce around the zero-g control room and quickly get back into position. It’s been slow up to now. I was as bored as I could be. But not anymore!

“Crud! Where was it! Okay! Relax! Calm down. Calm down! ”

I put my finger on the screen where I saw the blip. I check the Petrovascope direction, do some math on the screen, and figure out the angle. It’s 214 degrees’ yaw in my current plane, which is 55 degrees off the Tau Ceti–Adrian orbital path.

“Gotcha!”

Time for a better reading. I strap on my now-old and beat-up stopwatch. Zero g hasn't been easy on the little thing, but it still works.

I take the controls and point the ship directly away from the contact. I start the stopwatch, thrust straight for ten seconds, turn, and shut down the engines. I’m moving about 150 meters per second away from the contact, but that doesn’t matter. I don’t want to cancel out the speed I just added. I want the Petrovascope reading.

I stare at the screen with the stopwatch ticking in my hand. Soon, I see the blip again. Twenty-eight seconds. The spot of light stays for ten seconds, then disappears.

I can’t be sure it’s the Blip-A. But whatever it is, it’s definitely a reflection of my spin drives. And it’s fourteen light-seconds away (fourteen seconds to get there, fourteen seconds to get back equals twenty-eight seconds). That’s about 4 million kilometers.

No point in trying to figure out how fast it’s going by taking lots of measurements. I’m not accurate enough using my “finger on the screen” method. But I know which way it’s headed.

I can travel 4 million kilometers in nine and a half hours.

I fist-pump. “Yes! I’m definitely going to die!”

I don’t know why I said that. I guess…well, if I couldn’t find Rocky, I’d head back to Earth. I’m surprised I tried this hard, actually.

Whatever. I aim for where I saw the blip and start the engines. I don’t even need to worry about relativity this time. Just basic physics. I’ll speed up halfway, then slow down the rest of the way.

—

I spend the next nine hours cleaning. I’m going to have a guest again!

I hope.

Rocky will have to patch all the holes he made in the xenonite walls. But that shouldn’t be hard.

That assumes the contact I got was the Blip-A and not just some random space junk.

I try not to think about it. Keep hoping and all that.

I move all my stuff out of the xenonite areas.

Once I’m done, I fidget a lot. I want to stop and check my heading again, but I fight the urge. Just wait.

I stare at the aluminum Taumoeba farm in my lab. And the Astrophage slide next to it in the Taumoeba alarm. Everything's fine. Maybe I could—

The timer beeps. I’m there!

I hurry up the ladder to the control room and turn off the spin drives. I pull up the Radar screen before I even sit down. I do a full active ping at full power. “Come on…come on….”

Nothing.

I sit down and strap in. I thought this might happen. I’m closer to the contact now, but still not in radar range. I just went 4 million kilometers. Radar range is less than a thousandth of that. So my precision isn’t perfect. No surprise.

Time for another Petrovascope sweep. But this time I don’t have a full light-minute between me and the contact, wherever it is. If I’m, say, 100,000 kilometers away, I’ll have less than a second before the light comes back. And I can’t use the Petrovascope with the spin drives on.

So now what?

I need to make Astrophage light without turning off the Petrovascope. I check the menu but find nothing. I can't have the scope on when the spin drives are running. It must be a safety lock. Somewhere on this ship, a wire connects the spin-drive controls to the Petrovascope. I could spend forever looking for it and fail.

But the main engines aren't my only spin drives.

The small engines on the Hail Mary's sides adjust its position. They let me turn the ship. I wonder if the Petrovascope cares about them?

I leave the scope on and quickly roll to the left. The ship rolls, and the scope stays on!

I love lucky breaks! Someone on the design team probably thought of this. They likely decided the small output from the adjustment drives wouldn't hurt the scope. It makes sense: the engines and adjustment drives point away from the ship and Petrovascope. It shuts down with the main drives because of light reflected off cosmic dust. The light from the weaker adjustment drives was okay.

But those adjustment drives still make enough light to vaporize steel. Maybe they’ll light up the Blip-A.

I aim the Petrovascope at the left yaw thruster. I can even see it in the image. I turn it on.

There’s a glow in the Petrova spectrum. A haze near the thruster, like a flashlight in fog. But it fades after a few seconds. It’s still there, but weaker.

Probably dust and gases from the Hail Mary. Tiny bits drifting away. Once the thruster vaporized the nearby ones, things calmed down.

I keep the thruster on, and let the ship turn as I watch the Petrovascope. Now I have a flashlight. The ship turns faster and faster. I can’t let that happen. So I also turn on the thruster on the right side. The computer makes a fuss. It doesn’t make sense to tell the ship to turn both ways at once. I ignore the warnings.

I turn all the way around and see nothing. Okay. Nothing new. I change the angle by 5 degrees and try again.

On my sixth try—25 degrees from the Adrian ecliptic, I see it. Still too far to see any details. But it’s a flash of light that answers my thruster. I turn the thruster on and off a few times to check how fast it responds. It’s almost right away—less than a quarter second, I’d say. I’m within 75,000 kilometers.

I point toward it and start the engines. This time I won’t rush in without thinking. I’ll stop every 20,000 kilometers or so and take another reading.

I smile. It’s working.

Now I just have to hope I haven’t been chasing an asteroid all day.

—

With careful flying and checking again and again, I finally have it on radar!

It’s right there on the screen. “BLIP-A.”

“Oh, right,” I say. I forgot that's how it got its name.

I’m 4,000 kilometers away—at the very limit of radar. I pull up the telescope view, but I can’t see anything, even when zoomed in all the way. The telescope was made to spot huge objects in space, not a spaceship a few hundred meters long.

I move closer. The object's speed compared to Tau Ceti is about right for Rocky’s ship. Roughly the speed he would have reached when his engines died.

I could take a bunch of measurements and do calculations to figure out its path, but I have an easier plan.

I fire the thrusters for a few minutes here, a few minutes there, slowing down and speeding up until I match the object’s speed. It’s still 4,000 kilometers away, but now it’s barely moving relative to me. Why do this? Because the Hail Mary is very good at telling me its own path.

I open the Nav console and tell it to calculate my current orbit. After looking at the stars and doing some math, the computer tells me exactly what I wanted to hear: The Hail Mary is on a hyperbolic trajectory. That means I’m not in orbit at all. I'm on a path that will take me out of Tau Ceti's gravity completely.

And that means the object I’m tracking is also on that path. You know what objects in a solar system don’t do? They don’t escape the star’s gravity. Anything fast enough to escape did so billions of years ago. Whatever this is, it’s no ordinary asteroid.

“Yes yes yes yes…” I say. I turn on the spin drives and head toward the object. “I’m comin’, buddy. Hold tight.”

When I’m within 500 kilometers, I finally get a better look at the object. All I see is a very blurry triangle. It’s four times as long as it is wide. It’s not much information, but it’s enough. It’s the Blip-A. I know its shape well.

I have a bag of Ilyukhina’s vodka ready for this moment. I take a sip from the zip-straw. I cough and wheeze. Dang, she liked her liquor strong.

Rocky's ship is 50 meters to my right. I approached very carefully—I don't want to travel across a solar system just to accidentally destroy him with my engines. I've matched speeds to within a few centimeters per second.

It's been almost three months since we separated. From the outside, the Blip-A looks the same as always. But something is definitely wrong.

I've tried everything to communicate. Radio. Flashes of spin-drive light. Nothing gets a response.

I have a sinking feeling. What if Rocky's dead? He was all alone in there. What if everything went wrong while he was asleep? Eridians don't wake up until their bodies are ready. What if the life-support system failed while he was asleep and he just…never woke up?

What if he died of radiation sickness? All that Astrophage that was protecting him from radiation turned into methane and Taumoeba. Eridians are very sensitive to radiation. It might have happened so fast he didn't have a chance to react.

I shake my head.

No. He's Rocky. He's smart. He'd have backup plans. A separate life-support system that he sleeps in, I bet. And he'd plan for radiation—it killed his entire crew.

But why no response?

He can’t see. He has no windows. He’d have to use the Blip-A’s sensors to know I’m here. Why would he do that? He thinks he’s lost and forgotten in space.

EVA time.

I climb into the Orlan suit again and go through the airlock. I have a long tether tied to the airlock inside.

I look out at the huge emptiness. I can’t see the Blip-A. Tau Ceti is too far to light things up. I only know where the ship is because it blocks the stars. I’m just…out in space and a big part of it has no stars at all.

There’s no easy way to do this. I’ll just have to guess. I kick off the Hail Mary’s hull as hard as I can, aiming for the Blip-A. It’s a big ship. I just have to hit it somewhere. And if I miss, the tether will pull me back in the galaxy’s first interstellar bungee jump.

I float through space. The blackness ahead gets bigger. More and more stars vanish until I see nothing. I don’t even feel like I’m moving. I know I must be going the same speed as when I kicked off my ship. But there’s nothing to show it.

Then, I see a faint, blurry tan glow ahead. I’m close enough to the Blip-A that my helmet lights are lighting it up. It gets brighter. I can see the hull better now.

It’s time. I have seconds to find something to grab. I know his hull has rails for the robot to move around. I hope I’m close enough to grab one.

I see a rail right in front of me. I reach out.

Slam!

I hit the Blip-A much harder than an EVA suit should. I shouldn’t have pushed off the Hail Mary with so much force. I scramble at the hull, grabbing for anything. My plan to grab a rail failed completely; I got a hand on one but just couldn’t hold on. I bounce and start floating away. The tether gets tangled behind and around me. It’ll be a long climb back to my ship for another try.

Then I spot a strange, jagged piece sticking out of the hull a few meters away. An antenna, maybe? It’s too far to reach with my hands, but maybe I can get it with the tether.

I’m floating away from the hull at a slow but steady speed, and I don’t have a jetpack. It’s now or never.

I tie a quick slipknot in the tether and throw it at the antenna.

And, I’ll be darned, I got it! I just lassoed an alien spaceship. I pull the loop tight. For a second, I worry it might break the antenna off, but then I see the blotchy tan texture. The antenna (if that’s what it is) is made of xenonite. It’s not going anywhere.

I pull myself along the tether to the hull. This time, with the antenna and tether helping me, I manage to grab hold of a nearby robot rail.

“Whew,” I say.

I take a moment to catch my breath. Now to see if Rocky can hear.

I pull the biggest wrench I have from my tool belt. I wind up and smack the hull. Hard.

I smack it over and over. Clank! Clank! Clank! I hear the sound through my own EVA suit. If he’s alive in there, that’ll get his attention.

I push one end of the wrench against the hull and crouch down to touch the other end with my helmet. I stretch my neck inside the helmet and press my chin against the faceplate.

“Rocky!” I shout as loud as I can. “I don’t know if you can hear me! But I’m here, buddy! I’m on your hull!”

I wait a few seconds. “My spacesuit radio is on! Same frequency as always! Say something! Let me know you’re okay!”

I turn up the radio volume. All I hear is static.

“Rocky!”

A crackle. I listen closely.

“Rocky?!”

“Grace, question?”

“Yes!” I’ve never been so happy to hear those musical notes! “Yeah, buddy! It’s me!”

“You are here, question?!” his voice is so high I can barely understand him. But I understand Eridian pretty well now.

“Yes! I’m here!”

“You are…” he squeaks. “You…” he squeaks again. “You are here!”

“Yes! Set up the airlock tunnel!”

“Warning! Taumoeba-82.5 is—”

“I know! I know. It can get through xenonite. That’s why I’m here. I knew you’d be in trouble.”

“You save me!”

“Yes. I caught the Taumoeba in time. I still have fuel. Set up the tunnel. I’m taking you to Erid.”

“You save me and you save Erid!” he squeaks.

“Set up the darn tunnel!”

“Get back in your ship! Unless you want to look at the tunnel from outside!”

“Oh, right!”

—

I wait eagerly by the airlock door, trying to watch through the little window. It’s all happened before—Rocky attaching the airlock-to-airlock tunnel with the hull robot. But this time it was a bit harder. I had to move the Hail Mary into place because the Blip-A can’t move at all. Still, we did it.

A final clank, then a hiss. I know that sound!

I float into the airlock and look through the outer window. The tunnel is in place. He kept it all this time. Why not? It’s a relic from his species’ first contact with alien life. I’d keep it too!

I turn the emergency valve. Air from my ship fills my half of the tunnel. Once it’s equal, I throw open the door and fly in.

Rocky waits for me on the other side. His clothes are a mess, covered in the familiar gunky Taumoeba stuff. And there are burns all along one side of his jumpsuit and two of his arms are in bad shape. Looks like he had a rough time. But his body language is pure joy.

He bounces from handhold to handhold.

“I am very very very happy,” he says, his voice high.

I point to his bad arms. “Are you hurt?!”

“I will get better. I tried many things to stop the Taumoeba, but nothing worked.”

“I did it,” I say. “My ship isn’t made of xenonite.”

“What happened?”

I sigh. “The Taumoeba changed to live with nitrogen. But it also changed to get into xenonite to hide from nitrogen. Now, Taumoeba-82.5 can eat through xenonite over time.”

“Amazing. What now?”

“I still have two million kilograms of Astrophage. Bring your things onto the ship. We’re going to Erid.”

“Happy! Happy happy happy!” He pauses. “I need to make a nitrogen wash. We need to make sure no Taumoeba-82.5 gets into the Hail Mary.”

“Yes. I know you can do it. Make a sterilizer.”

He moves from one set of bars to another. His burned arms are hurting him, I can tell. “What about Earth?”

“I sent the beetles with the mini-farms. Taumoeba-82.5 can’t get through Eridian steel.”

“Good good,” he says. “I’ll make sure my people take good care of you. They will make Astrophage so you can go home!”

“Yeah…” I say. “About that…I’m not going home. The beetles will save Earth. But I won’t ever see it again.”

His happy bouncing stops. “Why, what’s wrong?”

“I don’t have enough food. After I take you to Erid, I will die.”

“You…you can’t die.” His voice gets low. “I won’t let you die. We send you home. Erid will be grateful. You save everyone. We do everything to save you.”

“There’s nothing you can do,” I say. “There’s no food. I have enough to last until we get to Erid and then a few months more. Even if your government gave me the Astrophage to go home, I wouldn’t survive the trip.”

“Eat Erid food. We evolved from the same life. We use the same proteins. Same chemicals. Same sugars. It must work!”

“No, I can’t eat your food, remember?”

“You said it's bad for you. We’ll find out.”

I hold up my hands. “It’s not just bad for me. It will kill me. Your whole environment uses heavy metals everywhere. Most of them are poisonous to me. I’d die right away.”

He trembles. “No. You can’t die. You are friend.”

I float closer to the dividing wall and talk softly. “It’s okay. I’ve made my choice. This is the only way to save both our worlds.”

He backs away. “Then you go home. Go home now. I wait here. Maybe Erid send another ship someday.”

“That’s ridiculous. Do you really want to risk your entire species' survival on that guess?”

He’s silent for a few moments and finally answers. “ No. ”

“Okay. Get that ball thing you use as a spacesuit and come over. Tell me how to fix the xenonite walls. Then you can move your stuff in—”

“Wait,” he says. “You can’t eat Erid life. You don’t have Earth life to eat. What about Adrian life?”

I snort. “Astrophage? I can’t eat that! It’s always ninety-six degrees! It would burn me alive. Plus, I doubt my stomach could even digest its weird cell covering.”

“Not Astrophage. Taumoeba. Eat Taumoeba.”

“I can’t eat—” I pause. “I…what?”

Can I eat Taumoeba?

It’s alive. It has DNA. It has mitochondria—the cell's power source. It stores energy as glucose. It does the Krebs cycle. It’s not Astrophage. It’s not 96 degrees. It’s just an amoeba from another planet. It won’t have heavy metals like Eridian life has adapted to have—they aren’t even in Adrian’s atmosphere.

“I…I don’t know. Maybe I can.”

He points to his ship. “I have twenty-two million kilograms of Taumoeba in fuel bays. How much do you want?”

I widen my eyes. It’s the first time I’ve felt real hope in a long time.

“Settled.” He puts his claw against the divider. “Fist my bump.”

I laugh and put my knuckles against the xenonite. “Fist-bump. It’s just ‘fist-bump.’ ”

“ Understand. ”

Chapter Vl

I finish the last bite of my meburger and gulp down the vitamin-enriched soda. I put my dishes in the sink and check the clock on my kitchen wall. Wow, is it 6:51 already? I better hurry up.

My first few years on Erid were uncertain. Taumoeba kept me alive, but I became severely malnourished. The microbes gave me calories, but they weren’t a balanced diet.

Those were painful days. I had scurvy, beriberi, and many other illnesses. Was it worth it? I still don’t know. I might never know. There’s no way to communicate with Earth. It’s sixteen light-years away.

For all I know, the beetles may have failed or missed their target. I don’t even know if the climatologists like Leclerc were right about what would happen. The Hail Mary might have been hopeless from the start. Earth might already be a frozen wasteland with billions of corpses.

But I try to stay positive. What else can I do?

For what it's worth, the Eridians are fantastic hosts. They don’t have a government, exactly, but all the important groups agreed to do whatever it takes to keep me alive. After all, I played a critical role in saving their planet. And even if I hadn’t, I’m a living, breathing alien. Of course they’re going to keep me alive. I’m of extreme scientific interest.

I live in a big dome in the middle of one of their “cities.” Though “city” isn’t quite the right word. A better description might be a “cluster.”

I have grounds and everything. Thirty Eridians outside the dome maintain my life-support systems, or so I’m told. And my dome is very close to one of the larger science centers. Many of Erid’s greatest minds gather there and thrum. That’s sort of a song and discussion in one. But everyone talks at the same time and it’s not really conscious on their part. Somehow the thrum leads to conclusions and decisions. The thrum itself is much smarter than any Eridian in it. In a way, Eridians can become temporary neurons in a group mind. But they come and go as they please.

I’m particularly interesting, so pretty much every scientist on the planet came together to thrum up ways to keep me alive. I’m told it was the second-largest science-oriented thrum ever. (The largest, of course, was when they had to make a plan for dealing with Astrophage.)

Thanks to my Earth science journals, they know all my nutritional needs and how to make the vitamins I need in labs. Once they figured that out, smaller groups worked on making them taste better. That's mostly up to me, actually. Lots of taste tests. Glucose, common to both Eridian and Human places, comes up a lot.

The best thing, though, is they managed to clone my muscle tissue and grow it in labs. I can thank Earth science for that. They weren't close to that technology when I first showed up. But that was sixteen years ago—they're catching up quickly.

Anyway, it means I can finally eat meat. Yes, that's right, I'm eating human meat. But it's my own meat, and I don't feel bad about it. Spend a decade eating nothing but weird-tasting, kind of sweet vitamin shakes and then see if you'll turn down a burger.

I love meburgers. I eat one every day.

I grab my cane and head out. I'm not a young man anymore, and the high gravity of Erid has only made my bones weaken faster. I think I'm fifty-three years old now, but I'm not sure. I've done a lot of time-affected travel. I can accurately say seventy-one years have gone by on Earth since I was born, if that matters.

I leave through my front door and cross the grounds. There are no plants or anything—I'm the only thing on this planet that can survive my environment. But there are some very nice rocks. It's become a hobby of mine: making the grounds as pretty as possible. The Eridians just see a bunch of rocks, but I see all the colors.

They installed lights at the top of the dome that get brighter and dimmer on a twenty-four-hour cycle. I explained that's important to my mood and they believed me. Though I did have to explain to this species of interstellar travelers how to make lightbulbs.

I make my way along the gravel path to one of the many "meeting" rooms at the dome's wall. Eridians value face-to-face communication as much as humans do, and this is a good compromise. My side is within my protected environment. And on the other side of the 1-centimeter clear xenonite is a room that's out in Erid's natural atmosphere.

I hobble in. It's one of the smaller meeting rooms, only good for a one-on-one conversation. But it's become our go-to spot for meeting up.

Rocky waits for me on the Eridian side. “Finally! I’ve been waiting for ℓ λ minutes! What took you so long?!”

I can understand Eridian fluently now, of course. And Rocky understands English just as well.

“I’m old. Give me a break. It takes me a while to get ready in the morning.”

“Oh, you had to eat, right?” Rocky says, a hint of disgust in his voice.

“You told me not to talk about that when we’re being nice.”

“I’m not nice, my friend!”

I giggle. “So what’s up?”

He wiggles and jiggles. I’ve almost never seen him this excited. “I just heard from the Astronomy group. They have news!”

I hold my breath. “Sol? Is it about Sol?!”

“Yes!” he squeals. “Your star is back to full brightness!”

I gasp. “Are you sure? Like, really, really sure?”

“Yes. The data was analyzed by a group of λ V astronomers. It checks out.”

I can’t move. I can barely breathe. I start to tremble.

It’s over.

We won.

Simple as that.

Sol—Earth’s sun—has returned to how bright it used to be before Astrophage. There’s only one way that can happen: Astrophage is gone. Or at least there are so few of them that it doesn’t matter.

We won.

We did it!

Rocky cocks his carapace. “ Hey, your face is leaking! I haven’t seen that in a long-ass time! Remind me—does that mean you’re happy or sad? ’Cause it can mean either one, right?”

“I’m happy, of course!” I sob.

“Yeah, I thought so. Just checking.” He holds a clenched fist against the xenonite. “Is this a fist-bump kind of thing?”

I press my knuckles to the xenonite too. “This is a hugely epic fist-bump kind of thing.”

“I guess your scientists got right to it,” he says. “If you consider how long it took your beetles to get there and how long it takes light to travel from Sol to Erid…I think it took less than one of your years to finish it.”

I nod. It’s still hard to believe.

“So, will you go home now? Or will you stay?”

The…people…who make big decisions for Erid offered a long time ago to refuel the Hail Mary. It’s still in a good, stable orbit around Erid, where it’s been since Rocky and I got here all those years ago.

The Eridians could fill it with food and supplies, help me check everything, and send me on my way. But I haven’t done it yet. It’s a long, lonely trip, and until a minute ago I didn’t even know if Earth was still okay to live on. Erid might not be where I’m from, but at least I have friends here.

“I…I don’t know. I’m getting old, and the trip is long.”

“Just being selfish here, I hope you stay. But that’s just me.”

“Rocky…that news about Sol…it…it makes my whole life matter. You know? I still can’t…I can’t…” I start crying again.

“Yeah, I know. That’s why I wanted to be the one to tell you.”

I check my watch. (Yes, the Eridians made me a wristwatch. They make anything I ask for. I try not to take advantage of it.) “I have to go. I’m late. But…Rocky…”

“I know,” he says, tilting his shell in what I've come to realize is a smile. “I know. We’ll talk more about it later. I have to get home anyway. Adrian is going to sleep soon, so I have to be there to watch.”

We both head toward our own exits, but he pauses. “Hey, Grace. Do you ever wonder? About other life out there?”

I lean on my cane. “Sure, all the time.”

He walks back in. “I keep thinking about it. The theories are pretty convincing. Some ancestor of Astrophage seeded Earth and Erid with life billions of years ago.”

“Yeah,” I say. “And I know what you're getting at.”

“Do you?”

“Yeah.” I shift my weight from one leg to the other. Arthritis is starting to settle in my joints. High gravity isn’t great for humans. “There are fewer than fifty stars as close to Tau Ceti as we are. But two of them ended up with life. It means life—at least, the life Tau Ceti produces—might be a lot more common in our galaxy than we think.”

“Think we’ll find more of them? Intelligent species?”

“Who knows?” I say. “You and I found each other. That’s something.”

“Yeah,” he says. “It really is something. Go do your job, old man.”

“Later, Rocky.”

“Later!”

I hobble out of the room and walk along the edge of the dome. They made the whole thing out of clear xenonite because they thought that’s what I would want. But it doesn’t matter. It’s always pitch-dark outside. Sure, I can shine a flashlight out there and sometimes see an Eridian doing his thing. But I don’t get wide views of mountains or anything. Just blackness.

My smile fades a little.

How bad did things get back on Earth? Did they work together to survive? Or did millions die in wars and hunger?

They were able to collect the beetles, read my information, and come up with a solution. A solution that would have involved a probe going to Venus. So there’s definitely some advanced technology still there.

I bet they did work together. Maybe it’s just me being childishly optimistic, but humanity can be pretty impressive when we try. After all, everyone worked together to build the Hail Mary. That was no easy task.

I hold my head up high. Maybe I will go home someday. Maybe I’ll find out for sure.

But not right now. Right now, I’ve got work to do.

I continue along the path to the large double doors leading to another meeting space. And I have to say, it’s my favorite one.

I step into the chamber. About one-fifth of the room is my Earth environment. The other side of the divider wall has thirty little Eridians bouncing around like idiots. Each one is no more than thirty Earth years old. The selection process for who gets to attend…well…again, Eridian culture is complicated.

An Earthlike organ keyboard sits in the center of my area, arranged so the player faces the kids. The organ has many more options than a typical keyboard on Earth. I can apply inflection, tone, mood, and all the other little details of spoken language. I settle into the comfortable chair, crack my knuckles, and start the class.

“All right, all right,” I play. “Everyone settle down and get in your seats.”

They scamper to their assigned desks and sit quietly, ready for the lesson to begin.

“Who here can tell me the speed of light?”

Twelve kids raise their claws.
