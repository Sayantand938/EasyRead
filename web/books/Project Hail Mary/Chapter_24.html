Project Hail Mary

Chapter 24

Oh.

Okay.

I get it now.

I’m not some brave explorer who bravely gave his life to save Earth. I’m a scared man who had to be forced onto the mission.

I’m a coward.

It all hit me at once. I sit on the stool and stare at the lab table. I went from almost losing it to…this. This is worse. I’m empty.

I’m a coward.

I’ve known for some time that I’m not the best hope for saving mankind. I’m just a guy with the genes to survive a coma. I accepted that a while ago.

But I didn’t know I was a coward.

I remember the feelings. I remember that panic. I remember it all now. Pure terror. Not for Earth or people or the kids. For me. Total panic.

“God damn you, Stratt,” I mutter.

What bugs me the most is she was right. Her plan worked. My memory’s back, and now I’m so into the mission I’ll still give it my all. And, come on, of course I would. What else would I do? Let 7 billion people die to get back at Stratt?

At some point, Rocky came through his tunnel to the lab. I don’t know how long he’s been there. He didn’t have to—he could "see" everything from the control room with his sonar. But he’s here.

“You are very sad,” he says.

“Yeah.”

“I am sad too. But we not be sad for long. You are scientist. I am engineer. Together we solve.”

I throw my hands up, frustrated. “How?!”

He clicked along the tunnel to the closest spot above me. “Taumoeba eat all your fuel. So Taumoeba survive and breed in fuel-tank environment.”

“So?”

“Most life can’t live without its air. I’d die if I wasn’t in Erid’s air. You’d die if you weren’t in Earth’s air. But Taumoeba survives even when it’s not in Adrian air. Taumoeba is stronger than Erid life—stronger than Earth life.”

I crane my neck to look up at him. “True. And Astrophage are also pretty tough. They can live in a vacuum and on the surface of stars.”

He tapped two claws together. “Yes yes. Astrophage and Taumoeba are from the same biosphere. They probably evolved from a common ancestor. Adrian life is very strong.”

I sit up. “Yeah. Okay.”

“You already have an idea. This isn’t a question. I know you. You already have an idea. Tell me your idea.”

I sigh. “Well…Venus, Threeworld, and Adrian all have a lot of carbon dioxide. The Astrophage breeding zone in all three is where the pressure is 0.02 atmospheres. So maybe I’ll start with a chamber full of pure carbon dioxide at 0.02 atmospheres and see if Taumoeba survives that. Then add in more gases one at a time to see what the problem is.”

“I understand,” says Rocky.

I get to my feet and dust off my jumpsuit. “I need you to make me a test chamber. Clear xenonite with valves so I can let air in and out. Also, I need to be able to set the temperature to minus 100 degrees Celsius, minus 50 degrees Celsius, or minus 82 degrees Celsius.”

I could use my own equipment, but why not take advantage of superior material and craftsmanship?

“Yes yes. I’ll make it now. We’re a team. We’ll fix this. Don’t be sad.” He skitters down the tunnel toward the dormitory.

I check my watch. “The main burn ends in thirty-four minutes. After that, let’s use the beetles to spin the ship.”

Rocky pauses. “Dangerous.”

“Yeah, I know. But we need gravity for the lab and I don’t want to wait eleven days. I want to use our time well.”

“Beetles set up for thrust, not spinning.”

He’s right. Our engine system is very basic. We don’t have motors or swivels to direct our thrust. We’re like a ship from the 1500s, but we’re using beetles for sails. Actually, that’s not quite right. The ship could at least control the angle of its sails. We’re more like a paddlewheel boat with a broken rudder.

It’s not all bad, though. We have some control by choosing how much each engine pushes. That’s how Rocky stopped our spin before. “It’s worth the risk.”

He skitters back up the tunnel to face me. “Ship will spin unevenly. Can’t release centrifuge cables. They’ll tangle.”

“We’ll get the spin right first, then shut off the beetles, then release the cables.”

He draws back. “If ship not released, force is too strong for human.”

That’s a problem. I want 1 g of gravity for the lab when the ship is split in two. To get that much spin with the ship in one piece means spinning it very fast. Last time we did that, I passed out in the control room and Rocky almost died saving me.

“Okay…” I say. “How about this: I’ll lie down in the storage room under the dorm. That’s the closest I can get to the middle of the ship. The force will be weakest there. I’ll be okay.”

“How operate centrifuge from storage room, question?”

“I’ll…umm…I’ll bring the lab’s control screen down there with me. I’ll run data and power cables from the lab to the storage room. Yeah. That should work.”

“What if you unconscious and can’t operate controls, question?”

“Then you stop the rotation and I’ll wake up.”

He shimmies back and forth. “No like. Different plan: Wait eleven days. Get to my ship. Clean out your ship fuel tanks. Sterilize—make sure no Taumoeba. Fill with fuel from my ship. Then can use all of your ship functions again.”

I shake my head. “I don’t want to wait eleven days. I want to work now.”

“Why, question? Why not wait, question?”

He’s completely right, of course. I’m risking my life and maybe the ship itself. But I can’t just sit around for eleven days when there’s so much work to do. How do I explain “impatience” to someone who lives seven hundred years?

“Human thing,” I say.

“Understand. Not really understand, but…understand.”

—

The start-up went as planned. Rocky chose Ringo to do the spinning, leaving John and Paul off. George is still safely on the ship if I need him.

The g-forces during the start-up were tough—I won’t lie. But I stayed awake long enough to manually handle the centrifuge steps. I’m getting good at it now. Since then, it’s been a nice, steady 1 g.

Yeah, it was hasty and a little risky, but because of that, I’ve had seven days of serious science since then.

Rocky delivered the testing equipment as promised. As always, everything worked perfectly. Instead of a small, annoying glass vacuum chamber, I had something like a large fish tank. Xenonite doesn’t care about a lot of air pressure on a large, flat panel. “Bring it on,” says xenonite.

I have, let’s say, a never-ending supply of Taumoeba. The Hail Mary is now the Taumoeba party bus. All I have to do is open the fuel line that used to go to the generator when I want more.

“Hey, Rocky!” I call out from the lab. “Watch me pull a Taumoeba out of a hat!”

Rocky climbs up his tunnel from the control room. “I guess that's an Earth saying.”

“Yeah. Earth has entertainment called ‘television’ and—”

“Don't explain, please. You have results, question?”

Just as well. It would take a long time to explain cartoons to an alien. “I have some results.”

“Good good.” He hunkers down into a comfortable sitting position. “Tell results!” He tries to hide it, but his voice is just a bit higher than normal.

I gesture to the lab setup. “This works perfectly, by the way.”

“Thank. Tell about results.”

“My first experiment was Adrian’s environment. I added Taumoeba and a slide covered in Astrophage. The Taumoeba survived and ate it all. No surprise there.”

“No surprise. Is their normal environment. But proves equipment works.”

“Exactly. I did more tests to learn Taumoeba’s limits. In Adrian air, they can live from minus 180 degrees Celsius to 107 degrees Celsius. They die outside that range.”

“Impressive range.”

“Yes. Also, they can survive in almost a vacuum.”

“Like your fuel tanks.”

“Yeah, but not a total vacuum.” I frown. “They need carbon dioxide. At least a little. I made an Adrian environment but put argon in instead of carbon dioxide. The Taumoeba didn’t eat anything. They stayed still. Eventually they starved.”

“Expected,” he says. “Astrophage need carbon dioxide. Taumoeba from same place. Taumoeba also need carbon dioxide. How they get carbon dioxide in fuel tanks, question?”

“I had the same question!” I say. “So I did a spectrograph of my fuel-bay sludge. There’s a lot of CO2 gas dissolved in the liquid.”

“Astrophage probably have carbon dioxide inside. Or breaking down makes carbon dioxide. Some percentage died in fuel tanks over time. Not all cells are perfect. Flaws. Changes. Some die. Those dead Astrophage put carbon dioxide in tanks.”

“Agreed.”

“Good findings,” he says. He starts climbing back down.

“Wait. I have more. Much more.”

He stops. “More, you ask? Good.”

I lean against my lab table and pat the tank. “I made Venus in the tank. But not exactly Venus. Venus’s air is 96.5 percent carbon dioxide and 3.5 percent nitrogen. I started with just the carbon dioxide. The Taumoeba were fine. Then I added the nitrogen. And the Taumoeba all died.”

He raises his carapace. “All die, you ask? Fast, you ask?”

“Yes,” I say. “In seconds. All dead.”

“Nitrogen…surprising.”

“Yeah, very surprising!” I say. “I repeated the test with Threeworld’s air. Carbon dioxide only: The Taumoeba were fine. I added in the sulfur dioxide: The Taumoeba were fine. I added the nitrogen: Boom! All the Taumoeba died.”

He taps a claw absently on the tunnel wall. “Very very surprising. Nitrogen doesn't hurt Erid life. Many Erid life need nitrogen.”

“Same with Earth,” I say. “Earth’s air is seventy-eight percent nitrogen.”

“Confusing,” he says.

He’s not alone. I’m just as confused as he is. We’re both thinking the same thing: If all life came from one source, how can nitrogen be vital to two planets and deadly to a third?

Nitrogen is completely harmless and barely reactive as a gas. It’s usually happy as N2, which hardly reacts with anything. Our bodies ignore it, even though 78 percent of every breath is nitrogen. As for Erid, their air is mostly ammonia—a nitrogen mix. How could life from space ever seed Earth and Erid—two planets full of nitrogen—if a small bit of nitrogen kills that life?

Well, the answer is simple: Whatever life-form caused the panspermia, it was fine with nitrogen. Taumoeba, which came later, isn't.

Rocky’s shell sinks. “Situation bad. Threeworld air is eight percent nitrogen.”

I sit on the lab stool and cross my arms. “Venus’s air is 3.5 percent nitrogen. Same problem.”

He sinks more and his voice gets lower. “Hopeless. Cannot change Threeworld air. Cannot change Venus air. Cannot change Taumoeba. Hopeless.”

“Well,” I say. “We can’t change the air on Threeworld or Venus. But maybe we can change Taumoeba.”

“How, question?”

I grab my tablet from the workbench and scroll through my notes about Eridian bodies. “Do Eridians get diseases? Sicknesses inside you?”

“Some. Very, very bad.”

“How does your body kill diseases?”

“Eridian body is closed,” he explains. “It only opens when we eat or lay eggs. After it opens, the area inside gets very hot with hot blood for a long time. This kills any disease. Disease can only get into the body through a wound. Then it is very bad. The body shuts down the infected area and uses hot blood to kill the disease. If the disease is fast, the Eridian dies.”

No immune system at all. Just heat. Well, why not? The hot blood of an Eridian boils water to move their muscles. Why not use it to cook and clean food too? And with skin made of heavy rocks, they don’t get many cuts. Even their lungs don’t share material with the outside. If any germs get in, the body seals the area off and boils it. An Eridian body is like a strong fortress.

But a human body is more like a police state without borders.

“Humans are very different,” I say. “We get sick all the time. We have very strong immune systems. Also, we find cures for diseases in nature. We call them ‘antibiotics.’ ”

“No understand,” he says. “Cures for diseases in nature, question? How, question?”

“Other life on Earth created ways to fight the same diseases. They make chemicals that kill the disease without hurting other cells. Humans eat those chemicals, and they kill the disease but not our human cells.”

“Amaze. Erid no have this.”

“It’s not a perfect system, though,” I say. “Antibiotics work very well at first, but then they become less and less effective over time. Eventually, they barely work at all.”

“Why, question?”

Diseases change. Antibiotics kill almost all the disease in the body, but some live through it. By using antibiotics, humans are accidentally teaching diseases how to live through them.

“Ah!” Rocky says. He raises his shell a bit. “Disease learns to block the stuff that kills it.”

“Yes,” I say. I point at the tank. “Now think of Taumoeba as disease. Think of nitrogen as antibiotic.”

He pauses, then lowers his shell back down. “Got it! Make the area just a little deadly. Breed Taumoeba that live through it. Make it more deadly. Breed the ones that live through that. Do it again and again!”

“Yes,” I say. “We don’t need to know why or how nitrogen kills Taumoeba. We just need to breed Taumoeba that can handle nitrogen.”

“Yes!” he says.

“Good!” I slap the top of the tank. “Make me ten of these, but smaller. Also, make a way for me to grab Taumoeba samples without messing up the experiment. Make a really exact gas thing—I need to control the nitrogen in the tank just right.”

“Yes! I’ll make it! I’ll make it now!”

He hurries down to the dormitory.

I check the spectrograph results and shake my head. “No good. A total failure.”

“Too bad,” Rocky says.

I rest my chin in my hands. “Maybe I can filter out the toxins.”

“Maybe focus on the Taumoeba.” Rocky's voice has a special sarcastic tone. It's very clear right now.

“They’re doing well.” I look at the Taumoeba processing tanks along one side of the lab. “All we can do is wait. We’ve had good results. They’re already at 0.01 percent nitrogen and surviving. The next generation might handle 0.015.”

“This is a waste of time, and my food.”

“I need to know if I can eat your food.”

“Eat your own food.”

“I only have a few months of real food left. You have enough on your ship to feed twenty-three Eridians for years. Eridian and Earth life use the same proteins. Maybe I can eat your food.”

“Why you say ‘real food,’ question? What is not real food, question?”

I checked the readout again. Why does Eridian food have so many heavy metals in it? “Real food is food that tastes good. Food that’s fun to eat.”

“You have not-fun food, question?”

“Yeah. Coma slurry. The ship fed it to me during the trip here. I have enough to last me almost four years.”

“Eat that.”

“It tastes bad.”

“Food experience not that important.”

“Hey.” I point at him. “To humans, food experience is very important.”

“Humans strange.”

I point at the spectrometer readout screen. “Why does Eridian food have thallium in it?”

“Healthy.”

“Thallium kills humans!”

“Then eat human food.”

“Ugh.” I walk over to the Taumoeba tanks. Rocky had really done well. I can control the nitrogen content very precisely. And so far, things look promising. Sure, this generation can only handle a tiny bit of nitrogen, but it’s a bit more than the last one could.

The plan is working. Our Taumoeba are becoming resistant to nitrogen.

Will they ever be able to handle the 3.5 percent needed for Venus? Or the 8 percent for Threeworld? Who knows? We’ll just have to wait and see.

I’m using percentages here to track the nitrogen. I can do that because the air pressure where Astrophage breed is always the same. So, since the pressure is always the same in all experiments, I can just track the percent of nitrogen.

The correct way to do it would be to track “partial pressure.” But that’s annoying. I’d just end up dividing by 0.02 atmospheres and multiplying by it again later when using the data.

I pat the top of Tank Three. It’s been my lucky tank. Out of twenty-three generations of Taumoeba, Tank Three made the strongest strain nine times. Pretty good, considering she’s got nine other tanks to compete with.

Yes, Tank Three is a “she.” Don’t judge me.

“How long until we reach the Blip-A?”

“Seventeen hours until reverse-thrust maneuver.”

“Okay, let’s spin down the centrifuge now. Just in case we run into trouble and need more time to fix something.”

“Agree. I go to control room now. You go to storage locker and lie flat. Do not forget control panel with long extension cords.”

I glance around the lab. Everything is firmly secured. “Yeah, okay. Let’s do it.”

“John, Ringo, Paul off,” says Rocky. “Velocity is orbital.”

There is no “stationary” in a solar system. You’re always moving around something. In this case, Rocky reduced our speed to put us in a stable orbit about 1 AU from Tau Ceti. That’s where he left the Blip-A.

Rocky relaxes in his control-room bulb. He clamps the boxes to their wall mounts. Now that the engines are off we’re back to zero g, and the last thing we want is for the “make ship thrust” button to be floating around unattended.

He grabs a couple of handholds and centers his shell over the texture monitor. As always, it shows him my center monitor feed with colors shown as textures.

“You in control now.” He’s done his job. Now it’s my turn.

“How long until the flash?” I ask

Rocky pulls a clock off the wall. “Next flash is three minutes, seven seconds.”

“Okay.”

Rocky’s no fool. He left his ship set up to turn on its engines for a split second every twenty minutes or so, giving us a beacon we really need. It’s easy enough to figure out where the ship should be. But gravity from other planets, not knowing the last speeds exactly, not knowing Tau Ceti’s gravity exactly…they all add up to small errors. And a small error on the location of something orbiting a star is a pretty big distance.

So rather than hoping we can see Taulight reflect off the ship when we get to where it should be, he just set it up to flash the engines now and then. All I have to do is watch with the Petrovascope. It’ll be a very bright flash.

“What is current nitrogen tolerance, question?”

“Tank Three had some survivors at 0.6 percent nitrogen today. I’m breeding them now.”

“What spacing, question?”

It’s a conversation we’ve had many times. But it’s okay for him to be curious. His kind needs it.

The “spacing,” as we call it, is the difference in how much nitrogen each of the ten tanks gets. I don’t do the same thing in every tank. With each new group, I try ten new nitrogen amounts.

“I’m pushing it—0.05 percent more each time.”

“Good good,” he says.

All ten tanks are growing Taumoeba-06 (named for the nitrogen percent it can handle). Tank One is the control, like always. It has 0.6 percent nitrogen in the air. Taumoeba-06 should be fine in there. If it’s not, it means I messed up the last batch and have to go back to an older type.

Tank Two has 0.65 percent nitrogen. Tank Three has 0.7. And so on up to Tank Ten with 1.05 percent. The strongest ones will win and go on to the next round. I wait a few hours to make sure they can breed for at least two generations. Taumoeba doubles really fast. Fast enough to use up all my fuel in days, actually.

If we get to Venus or Threeworld nitrogen levels, I’ll test them more carefully.

“Flash is soon,” Rocky says.

“Copy.”

I put the Petrovascope on the main screen. Usually, it’s to the side, but the center is the only one Rocky can “see.” As expected, there’s just background light in the Petrova frequency from Tau Ceti. I move the camera around. We made sure we were closer to Tau Ceti than the Blip-A should be. So I’m looking almost directly away from the star. That should make the background IR less and give me a good view of the flash.

“Okay. I think I have it aimed about right at your ship.”

Rocky focuses on his screen. “Got it. Thirty-seven seconds until flash.”

“Hey. What’s your ship called, anyway?”

“Blip-A.”

“No, I mean, what do you call it besides that?”

“Ship.”

“Your ship doesn’t have a name?”

“Why would ship need name, question?”

I shrugged. “Ships have names.”

He points to my pilot’s seat. “What’s the name of your chair, question?”

“It doesn’t have a name.”

“Why does ship have name but chair no have name, question?”

“Never mind. Your ship is the Blip-A.”

“That is what I said. Flash in ten seconds.”

“Copy.”

Rocky and I fall silent and stare at our screens. It took me a while to notice the small details, but I can now tell when Rocky is paying attention to something. He angles his shell toward it and moves slightly back and forth. If I follow the line he’s moving on, that’s usually what he’s looking at.

“Three…two…one…now!”

Right on time, a few pixels on-screen blink white.

“Got it,” I say.

“I not notice.”

“It was dim. We must be far away. Hang on…” I switch to the Telescope screen and pan to where the flash came from. I sweep back and forth with small movements until I catch a slight change in the blackness. Taulight reflecting off the Blip-A. “Yeah, we’re pretty far away.”

“Beetles have much fuel left. It’s okay. Tell me the angle to change.”

I check the readouts at the bottom of the screen. All we have to do is line up the Hail Mary with the current telescope angle. “Rotate yaw plus 13.72 degrees. Rotate pitch minus 9.14 degrees.”

“Yaw plus one three point seven two. Pitch minus nine point one four.” He grabs the beetle controls from their holders and gets to work. By turning the beetles on and off in order, he angles the ship toward the Blip-A.

I zero the telescope and zoom in to confirm. The difference between space and the ship is so small it’s hard to see. But it’s there. “Angle correct.”

He focuses hard on his texture screen. “I don’t see anything on-screen.”

“Light difference is very, very small. Need human eyes to see it. Angle is good.”

“Understand. What is the range?”

I switch to the Radar screen. Nothing. “Too far for my radar to see. At least ten thousand kilometers.”

“Accelerate to what speed?”

“How about…three kilometers per second? We’ll get to the Blip-A in about an hour.”

“Three thousand meters per second. Is the standard acceleration rate okay?”

“Yes. Fifteen meters per second per second.”

“Thrust for two hundred seconds. Start now.”

I brace for gravity.
