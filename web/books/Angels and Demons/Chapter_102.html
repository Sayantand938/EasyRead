Angels and Demons

Chapter 102

Piazza Navona. Fountain of the Four Rivers.

Nights in Rome, like desert nights, can be surprisingly cool, even after a warm day. Langdon was on the edge of Piazza Navona, pulling his jacket tighter. Like the distant sound of traffic, news reports echoed across the city. He checked his watch. Fifteen minutes. He was glad for a few moments to rest.

The piazza was empty. Bernini’s amazing fountain seemed to sizzle with a scary magic. The bubbling water sent a magical mist up, lit from below by lights. Langdon felt a cool electricity in the air.

The fountain’s most striking feature was its height. The center part alone was over twenty feet tall—a rough mountain of marble with caves and holes where the water rushed through. The whole mound was covered with pagan figures. On top of this was a tall stone pillar that rose another forty feet. Langdon looked up. On the pillar’s tip, a faint shadow covered the sky, a single pigeon perched silently.

A cross, Langdon thought, still amazed by how the markers were arranged across Rome. Bernini’s Fountain of the Four Rivers was the last altar of science. Just hours ago, Langdon had been in the Pantheon, thinking the Path of Illumination was broken and he would never get this far. It had been a silly mistake. In fact, the whole path was still there. Earth, Air, Fire, Water. And Langdon had followed it… from start to finish.

Not quite to the end, he reminded himself. The path had five stops, not four. This fourth fountain somehow pointed to the final destination—the Illuminati’s secret hideout—the Church of Illumination. Langdon wondered if the hideout was still there. He wondered if that was where the Hassassin had taken Vittoria.

Langdon looked closely at the figures in the fountain, searching for any clue about where the hideout was. Let angels guide you on your lofty quest. But almost at once, he felt uneasy. This fountain had no angels at all. Certainly none that Langdon could see from where he was… and none he had ever seen before. The Fountain of the Four Rivers was a pagan work. The carvings were all unholy—humans, animals, even an awkward armadillo. An angel here would be very out of place.

Is this the wrong place? He thought about the cross shape made by the four pillars. He clenched his fists. This fountain is perfect.

It was only 10:46 P.M. when a black van came out of the alley on the far side of the piazza. Langdon wouldn’t have noticed it except that the van had no headlights on. Like a shark moving around a bay at night, the van circled the edge of the piazza.

Langdon huddled down, crouching in the shadows by the huge stairway to the Church of St. Agnes in Agony. He looked out at the piazza, his heart racing.

After circling twice, the van turned toward Bernini’s fountain. It stopped beside the fountain, moving sideways until it was right against it. Then it parked, its sliding door just above the swirling water.

Mist rose up.

Langdon felt a sense of unease. Had the Hassassin come early? Had he come in a van? Langdon thought the killer would lead his last victim across the piazza on foot, like at St. Peter’s, giving Langdon a clear shot. But if the Hassassin came in a van, things were different now.

Suddenly, the van’s side door slid open.

On the floor of the van, twisted in pain, was a naked man. He was wrapped in heavy chains. He struggled against the iron links, but they were too strong. One of the links cut across the man’s mouth like a horse’s bit, silencing his cries. Then Langdon saw the second figure, moving behind the prisoner in the dark, as if getting ready.

Langdon knew he had only a few seconds to react.

He grabbed the gun, took off his jacket, and dropped it. He didn’t want the heavy jacket to slow him down, and he didn’t want to get Galileo’s Diagramma wet. The document would stay here, safe and dry.

Langdon moved quickly to his right. Going around the fountain, he stood directly across from the van. The fountain's large center blocked his view. He stood up and ran toward the fountain. He hoped the loud water hid his footsteps. When he reached the fountain, he climbed over the edge and jumped into the foamy pool.

The water was waist deep and freezing. Langdon gritted his teeth and pushed through the water. The bottom was slippery, made worse by a layer of coins tossed in for luck. Langdon felt he needed more than luck. As the mist rose around him, he wondered if the cold or the fear was making the gun shake in his hand.

He reached the inside of the fountain and circled back to his left. He pushed through the water, staying close to the marble statues. Hiding behind the large carved horse, Langdon peeked out. The van was only fifteen feet away. The Hassassin was kneeling in the van, hands on the cardinal’s chained body, ready to push him out the open door into the fountain.

Waist-deep in water, Robert Langdon raised his gun and stepped out of the mist, feeling like a waterlogged cowboy making a last stand. “Don’t move.” His voice was calmer than his gun.

The Hassassin looked up. For a moment he seemed puzzled, as if he'd seen a ghost. Then he smirked. He raised his arms in surrender. “And so it goes.”

“Get out of the van.”

“You look wet.”

“You’re early.”

“I am eager to get back to my reward.”

Langdon aimed the gun. “I won’t hesitate to shoot.”

“You’ve already hesitated.”

Langdon felt his finger squeeze the trigger. The cardinal was still now, looking tired and near death. “Untie him.”

“Forget him. You’re here for the woman. Don't act like you're not.”

Langdon wanted to end it right there. “Where is she?”

“Somewhere safe. Waiting for me to come back.”

She’s alive. Langdon felt a spark of hope. “At the Church of Illumination?”

The killer smiled. “You will never find it.”

Langdon couldn't believe it. Their hideout still exists. He pointed the gun. “Where?”

“The location has been a secret for centuries. I only found out recently. I would die before I betrayed that trust.”

“I can find it without you.”

“An arrogant idea.”

Langdon pointed to the fountain. “I’ve made it this far.”

“So have many. The last step is the hardest.”

Langdon moved closer, unsure of his footing in the water. The Hassassin looked very calm, sitting in the back of the van with his arms raised. Langdon aimed at his chest, wondering if he should just shoot and end it. No. He knows where Vittoria is. He knows where the antimatter is. I need information!

From the darkness of the van, the Hassassin looked at his attacker and felt a bit of pity. The American was brave, he had shown that. But he was also untrained. He had shown that too. Bravery without skill was suicide. There were rules for survival. Old rules. And the American was breaking them all.

You had the advantage—surprise. You wasted it.

The American was unsure… probably hoping for help… or maybe a slip of the tongue that would give away important information.

Never ask questions before you stop your enemy from fighting. An enemy with no escape is a dangerous enemy.

The American was talking again. Testing. Trying to trick him.

The killer almost laughed. This isn't one of your movies… there won't be long talks with a gun pointed before the final fight. This is the end. Now.

Without looking away, the killer moved his hands along the ceiling of the van until he found what he wanted. Still staring straight ahead, he grabbed it.

Then he made his move.

The move was totally unexpected. For a moment, Langdon thought the laws of physics had stopped working. The killer seemed to float weightlessly as his legs shot out, his boots hitting the cardinal's side and sending the chained body out the door. The cardinal landed with a splash, sending water everywhere.

Water splashed on Langdon's face, and he realized too late what had happened. The killer had grabbed one of the van's roll bars and used it to swing out. Now the Hassassin was flying toward him, feet-first through the spray.

Langdon pulled the trigger, and the silencer made a soft sound. The bullet went through the toe of the Hassassin's left boot. Instantly, Langdon felt the Hassassin's boots slam into his chest, pushing him back with a hard kick.

The two men fell into the water with a spray of blood and water.

As the cold water covered Langdon's body, his first feeling was pain. Then came the urge to survive. He realized he was no longer holding his gun. It had been knocked away. He dove down and felt around on the slimy bottom. His hand touched metal. A few coins. He dropped them. Opening his eyes, Langdon looked around at the glowing fountain. The water swirled around him like a cold Jacuzzi.

Even though he wanted to breathe, fear kept him at the bottom. He kept moving. He didn't know where the next attack would come from. He needed to find the gun! His hands reached out desperately.

You have the advantage, he told himself. You are in your element. Even in a wet turtleneck, Langdon was a good swimmer. Water is your element.

When Langdon's fingers found metal a second time, he was sure his luck had changed. The thing in his hand wasn't just a few coins. He grabbed it and tried to pull it toward him, but as he did, he found himself moving through the water. The thing was stuck.

Langdon realized, even before he floated over the cardinal's twisting body, that he had grabbed part of the metal chain that was weighing the man down. Langdon paused, frozen by the sight of the terrified face staring up at him from the bottom of the fountain.

Spurred by the life in the man's eyes, Langdon reached down and grabbed the chains, trying to lift him to the surface. The body came up slowly, like an anchor. Langdon pulled harder. When the cardinal's head came out of the water, the old man gasped, taking a few quick, desperate breaths. Then, his body suddenly rolled, and Langdon lost his grip on the slippery chains. Like a stone, Baggia went down again and disappeared under the foamy water.

Langdon dove, eyes wide in the murky water. He found the cardinal. This time, when Langdon grabbed on, the chains across Baggia’s chest moved… opening to show something even worse… a word burned into his skin.

Right away, two boots came into view. One was pouring blood.
