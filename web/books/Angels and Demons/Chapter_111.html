Angels and Demons

Chapter 111

Kohler felt disgusted by the lavishness of the Hallway of the Belvedere. The gold leaf on the ceiling probably could have paid for cancer research for a year. Rocher led Kohler up a ramp for disabled people, taking a winding path into the Apostolic Palace.

“No elevator?” Kohler asked.

“No power.” Rocher pointed to the candles burning around them in the darkened building. “It’s part of how we’re searching.”

“Searches that obviously didn’t work.”

Rocher nodded.

Kohler started coughing again and knew it might be one of his last times. It wasn't a completely bad thought.

When they got to the top floor and walked down the hall toward the Pope’s office, four Swiss Guards ran to them, looking worried. “Captain, what are you doing up here? I thought this man had information that-”

“He will only talk to the camerlegno.”

The guards stepped back, looking doubtful.

“Tell the camerlegno,” Rocher said firmly, “that the director of CERN, Maximilian Kohler, is here to see him. Right away.”

“Yes, sir!” One of the guards hurried toward the camerlegno’s office. The others stayed where they were. They looked at Rocher, seeming nervous. “Just a moment, captain. We'll announce your guest.”

Kohler, however, didn't stop. He turned quickly and steered his chair around the guards.

The guards turned and began to jog beside him. “Fermati! Sir! Stop!”

Kohler felt disgust for them. Even the best security in the world couldn't help but pity those with disabilities. If Kohler were able-bodied, the guards would have stopped him. People with disabilities are weak, Kohler thought. That’s what the world thinks.

Kohler knew he had little time to do what he came for. He also knew he might die tonight. He was surprised he didn't care more. Death was a price he was willing to pay. He had suffered too much to let someone like Camerlegno Ventresca ruin his work.

“Signore!” the guards shouted, running ahead and blocking the hallway. “You must stop!” One of them drew a gun and pointed it at Kohler.

Kohler stopped.

Rocher stepped forward, looking sorry. “Mr. Kohler, please. It will only be a moment. No one enters the Pope's Office without being announced.”

Kohler could see in Rocher’s eyes that he had to wait. Fine, Kohler thought. We wait.

The guards, seemingly cruel, had stopped Kohler next to a full-length gilded mirror. The sight of his own twisted form disgusted Kohler. The old rage welled up again. It gave him strength. He was among the enemy now. These were the people who had stolen his dignity. These were the people. Because of them he had never felt a woman’s touch… had never stood tall to accept an award. What truth do these people have? What proof, damn it! A book of old stories? Promises of miracles later? Science makes miracles every day!

Kohler stared for a moment into his own hard eyes. Tonight I may die because of religion, he thought. But it won’t be the first time.

For a moment, he was eleven years old again, lying in bed in his parents’ mansion in Frankfurt. The sheets beneath him were the best linen in Europe, but they were wet with sweat. Young Max felt like he was burning, the pain in his body unbearable. Kneeling beside his bed, where they had been for two days, were his mother and father. They were praying.

In the shadows stood three of Frankfurt’s best doctors.

“I beg you to think again!” one of the doctors said. “Look at the boy! His fever is getting higher. He is in terrible pain. And danger!”

But Max knew his mother’s answer before she spoke. “Gott wird ihn beschuetzen.”

Yes , Max thought. God will protect me. The belief in his mother’s voice gave him strength. God will protect me.

An hour later, Max felt like his whole body was being crushed under a car. He couldn’t even breathe to cry.

“Your son is suffering greatly,” another doctor said. “Let me at least lessen his pain. I have a simple injection in my bag that will-”

“Ruhe, bitte!” Max’s father stopped the doctor without opening his eyes. He just kept praying.

“Father, please!” Max wanted to scream. “Make the pain stop!” But his words were lost in a fit of coughing.

An hour later, the pain was worse.

“Your son could be paralyzed,” one of the doctors warned. “Or even die! We have medicines that can help!”

Frau and Herr Kohler would not allow it. They did not believe in medicine. Who were they to question God’s plan? They prayed harder. God had given them this boy, why would God take him away? His mother whispered to Max to be strong. She said God was testing him… like Abraham in the Bible… a test of his faith.

Max tried to have faith, but the pain was terrible.

“I can’t watch this!” one of the doctors finally said, running from the room.

By dawn, Max was barely awake. Every muscle in his body jerked in pain. Where is Jesus? he wondered. Doesn’t he love me? Max felt himself dying.

His mother had fallen asleep next to him, her hands still holding him. Max’s father stood across the room at the window watching the sunrise. He seemed lost in thought. Max could hear him quietly praying for mercy.

Then Max felt someone near him. An angel? Max could hardly see. His eyes were swollen shut. The person whispered in his ear, but it was not an angel. Max knew it was one of the doctors… the one who had stayed in the corner for two days, asking Max’s parents to let him give him a new drug from England.

“I will never forgive myself,” the doctor whispered, “if I don’t do this.” Then the doctor gently took Max’s weak arm. “I wish I had done it sooner.”

Max felt a tiny prick in his arm—barely noticeable through the pain.

Then the doctor quietly packed his things. Before he left, he put a hand on Max’s forehead. “This will save your life. I truly believe in the power of medicine.”

Within minutes, Max felt as if magic were flowing through his veins. The warmth spread through his body, numbing his pain. Finally, for the first time in days, Max slept.

When the fever broke, his mother and father declared it a miracle from God. But when they realized their son was crippled, they became sad. They wheeled their son into the church and asked the priest for guidance.

“It was only by God's grace,” the priest told them, “that this boy survived.”

Max listened, saying nothing.

“But our son cannot walk!” Frau Kohler was weeping.

The priest nodded sadly. “Yes. It seems God has punished him for not having enough faith.”

“Mr. Kohler?” It was the Swiss Guard who had run ahead. “The camerlegno says he will see you now.”

Kohler grunted, speeding up again down the hall.

“He’s surprised you came,” the guard said.

“I’m sure.” Kohler continued. “I want to see him alone.”

“Impossible,” the guard said. “No one-”

“Lieutenant,” Rocher snapped. “The meeting will happen as Mr. Kohler wants.”

The guard stared, clearly not believing it.

Outside the Pope’s office, Rocher let his guards take normal safety steps before letting Kohler in. Their metal detector didn’t work because of all the electronic devices on Kohler’s wheelchair. The guards patted him down but seemed too embarrassed by his disability to do a good job. They didn’t find the revolver attached under his chair. They also didn’t take away the other thing… the one Kohler knew would bring a memorable end to the night’s events.

When Kohler entered the Pope’s office, Camerlegno Ventresca was alone, kneeling in prayer by a dying fire. He didn’t open his eyes.

“Mr. Kohler,” the camerlegno said. “Have you come to make me a martyr?”
