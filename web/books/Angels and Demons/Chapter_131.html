Angels and Demons

Chapter 131

Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca stood in the aisle of the Sistine Chapel. The cardinals were all standing near the front of the church, turned, staring at him. Robert Langdon was on the altar beside a television that played the same scene over and over. The camerlegno knew the scene but couldn't imagine how it got there. Vittoria Vetra stood beside him, her face strained.

The camerlegno closed his eyes, hoping the morphine was making him see things and that the scene would be different when he opened them. But it wasn't.

They knew.

Strangely, he felt no fear. Show me the way, Father. Give me the words to help them see Your vision.

But the camerlegno heard no reply.

Father, we've come too far to fail now.

Silence.

They don't understand what we've done.

The camerlegno didn't know whose voice he heard in his mind, but the message was clear.

And the truth shall set you free…

And so it was that Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca held his head high as he walked toward the front of the Sistine Chapel. As he moved toward the cardinals, not even the soft light of the candles could soften the eyes staring at him. Explain yourself, the faces said. Make sense of this madness. Tell us our fears are wrong!

Truth, the camerlegno told himself. Only truth. There were too many secrets in these walls… one so dark it had driven him to madness. But from the madness had come the light.

“If you could give your own soul to save millions,” the camerlegno said, as he moved down the aisle, “ would you?”

The faces in the chapel simply stared. No one moved. No one spoke. Beyond the walls, the joyous songs could be heard in the square.

The camerlegno walked toward them. “Which is the greater sin? Killing one’s enemy? Or standing idle while your true love is strangled?” They are singing in St. Peter’s Square! The camerlegno stopped for a moment and gazed up at the ceiling of the Sistine. Michelangelo’s God was staring down from the darkened vault… and He seemed pleased.

“I could no longer stand by,” the camerlegno said. Still, as he drew nearer, he saw no sign of understanding in anyone’s eyes. Didn’t they see the clear simplicity of his deeds? Didn’t they see the utter necessity!

It had been so pure.

The Illuminati. Science and Satan as one.

Bring back the old fear. Then crush it.

Horror and Hope. Make them believe again.

Tonight, the Illuminati’s power had returned… with amazing results. The indifference was gone. Fear had spread worldwide like lightning, uniting people. Then, God's greatness had defeated the darkness.

I could not just stand there!

The inspiration seemed to come from God, like a light in the camerlegno’s painful night. Oh, this world with so little faith! Someone must save them. You. If not you, then who? You were saved for a reason. Show them the old fears. Remind them of what scares them. Indifference is death. Without darkness, there is no light. Without evil, there is no good. Make them choose. Dark or light. Where is the fear? Where are the heroes? If not now, when?

The camerlegno walked up the center aisle toward the crowd of standing cardinals. He felt like Moses as the red robes and hats parted, letting him pass. On the altar, Robert Langdon turned off the television, took Vittoria’s hand, and left the altar. The camerlegno knew that Robert Langdon's survival could only be God’s will. God had saved Robert Langdon. The camerlegno wondered why.

The silence was broken by the only woman in the Sistine Chapel. “You killed my father?” she said, stepping forward.

When the camerlegno turned to Vittoria Vetra, her face showed something he didn't understand—pain, yes, but anger? Surely she understood. Her father’s intelligence was dangerous. He had to be stopped. For the good of humanity.

“He was doing God’s work,” Vittoria said.

“God’s work is not done in a lab. It is done in the heart.”

“My father’s heart was pure! And his research proved-”

“His research proved again that man's thinking is faster than his soul!” The camerlegno’s voice was sharper than he had expected. He lowered his voice. “If a man as spiritual as your father could create a weapon like the one we saw tonight, imagine what an ordinary man will do with his technology.”

“A man like you?”

The camerlegno took a deep breath. Did she not see? Man's sense of right and wrong was not growing as fast as his science. Mankind was not spiritually ready for the powers he had. We have never created a weapon we have not used! And yet he knew that antimatter was nothing—another weapon in man’s already growing collection. Man could already destroy. Man learned to kill long ago. And his mother’s blood rained down. Leonardo Vetra’s genius was dangerous for another reason.

“For centuries,” the camerlegno said, “the church has stood by while science picked away at religion bit by bit. Disproving miracles. Teaching the mind to control the heart. Calling religion a drug for the masses. They say God is a hallucination—a false support for those too weak to accept that life is meaningless. I could not stand by while science dared to use the power of God himself! Proof, you say? Yes, proof of science’s ignorance! What is wrong with admitting that something exists beyond our understanding? The day science proves God in a lab is the day people stop needing faith!”

“You mean the day they stop needing the church,” Vittoria challenged, moving toward him. “Doubt is your last bit of control. It is doubt that brings souls to you. Our need to know that life has meaning. Man’s insecurity and need for a wise person assuring him everything is part of a plan. But the church is not the only wise influence on the planet! We all seek God in different ways. What are you afraid of? That God will show himself somewhere other than inside these walls? That people will find him in their own lives and leave your old rituals behind? Religions change! The mind finds answers, the heart struggles with new truths. My father was on your quest! A parallel path! Why couldn’t you see that? God is not some all-powerful boss looking down from above, threatening to throw us into hell if we disobey. God is the energy that flows through our nervous system and our hearts! God is in all things!”

“ Except science,” the camerlegno fired back, his eyes showing only pity. “Science, by definition, is without a soul. Separated from the heart. Intellectual miracles like antimatter arrive in this world with no ethical instructions attached. This in itself is dangerous! But when science announces its Godless pursuits as the enlightened path? Promising answers to questions whose beauty is that they have no answers?” He shook his head. “No.”

There was a moment of silence. The camerlegno felt suddenly tired as he returned Vittoria’s unyielding stare. This was not how it was supposed to be. Is this God’s final test?

It was Mortati who broke the silence. “The preferiti,” he said in a horrified whisper. “Baggia and the others. Please tell me you did not…”

The camerlegno turned to him, surprised by the pain in his voice. Surely Mortati could understand. Headlines carried science’s miracles every day. How long had it been for religion? Centuries? Religion needed a miracle! Something to wake up a sleeping world. Bring them back to the right path. Restore faith. The preferiti were not leaders anyway, they were changers—liberals ready to accept the new world and abandon the old ways! This was the only way. A new leader. Young. Powerful. Vibrant. Miraculous. The preferiti served the church far more effectively in death than they ever could alive. Horror and Hope. Offer four souls to save millions. The world would remember them forever as martyrs. The church would raise glorious tributes to their names. How many thousands have died for the glory of God? They are only four.

“The preferiti,” Mortati repeated.

“I felt their pain,” the camerlegno said, pointing to his chest. “And I too would die for God, but my work has only just begun. They are singing in St. Peter’s Square!”

The camerlegno saw the horror in Mortati’s eyes and was again confused. Was it the morphine? Mortati looked at him as if he himself had killed these men. I would even do that for God, the camerlegno thought, but he had not. The Hassassin had done it—a lost soul tricked into thinking he was working for the Illuminati. I am Janus, the camerlegno had told him. I will prove my power. And he had. The Hassassin’s hatred had made him God’s tool.

“Listen to the singing,” the camerlegno said, smiling, his heart full of joy. “Nothing unites people like the presence of evil. Burn a church, and the community rises up, holding hands, singing songs of defiance as they rebuild. Look how they gather tonight. Fear has brought them home. Create modern demons for modern man. No one is indifferent now. Show them the face of evil—Satanists hiding among us—running our governments, our banks, our schools, threatening to destroy the very House of God with their wrong science. Evil is everywhere. Man must be watchful. Seek goodness. Become goodness!”

In the silence, the camerlegno hoped they now understood. The Illuminati had not returned. They were long dead. Only their story lived on. The camerlegno had brought back the idea of the Illuminati as a reminder. Those who knew the history of the Illuminati remembered their evil. Those who did not, had learned about it and were amazed at how blind they had been. The old demons had been brought back to awaken a world that didn't care.

“But… the brands?” Mortati’s voice was tight with anger.

The camerlegno did not answer. Mortati could not know that the Vatican had taken the brands over a century ago. They had been locked away, forgotten and covered in dust, in the Pope’s private collection, deep inside his Borgia apartments. The Papal Vault held items the church thought were too dangerous for anyone but the Pope to see.

Why did they hide what inspired fear? Fear brought people to God!

The vault’s key was passed down from Pope to Pope. Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca had stolen the key and gone inside. The stories about what the vault contained were fascinating—the original pages for the fourteen unpublished books of the Bible known as the Apocrypha, the third prophecy of Fatima, the first two having come true and the third so frightening the church would never reveal it. He had also found the Illuminati Collection—all the secrets the church had discovered after kicking the group out of Rome… their disgusting Path of Illumination… the clever trickery of the Vatican’s main artist, Bernini… Europe’s best scientists making fun of religion as they secretly met in the Vatican’s own Castle St. Angelo. The collection included a pentagon box with iron brands, one of them the famous Illuminati Diamond. This was a part of Vatican history the old leaders thought best forgotten. The camerlegno did not agree.

“But the antimatter …” Vittoria demanded. “You risked destroying the Vatican!”

“There is no risk when God is on your side,” the camerlegno said. “This was His will.”

“You’re insane!” she hissed.

“Millions were saved.”

“People were killed!”

“Souls were saved.”

“Tell that to my father and Max Kohler!”

“CERN’s pride had to be exposed. A drop of liquid that can vaporize a half mile? And you call me crazy?” The camerlegno felt anger building. Did they think his job was easy? “Those who believe face great trials for God! God asked Abraham to sacrifice his son! God told Jesus to suffer crucifixion! And so we keep the crucifix before us—bloody, painful, agonizing—to remind us how strong evil is! To keep our hearts alert! The scars on Jesus’ body are a constant reminder of the power of darkness! My scars are a constant reminder! Evil exists, but God’s power will win!”

His shouts bounced off the back wall of the Sistine Chapel, and then a deep silence fell. Time seemed to stop. Michelangelo’s Last Judgment loomed behind him… Jesus throwing sinners into hell. Tears filled Mortati’s eyes.

“What have you done, Carlo?” Mortati asked quietly. He closed his eyes, and a tear fell. “His Holiness? ”

A shared sigh of pain rose up, as if everyone in the room had forgotten until that moment. The Pope. Poisoned.

“A terrible liar,” the camerlegno said.

Mortati looked shattered. “What do you mean? He was honest! He… loved you.”

“And I him.” Oh, how I loved him! But the deceit! The broken vows to God!

The camerlegno knew they did not understand now, but they would. When he told them, they would see! His Holiness was the worst deceiver the church had ever seen. The camerlegno still remembered that terrible night. He had returned from his trip to CERN with news of Vetra’s Genesis and of antimatter’s horrific power. The camerlegno was sure the Pope would see the dangers, but the Holy Father saw only hope in Vetra’s discovery. He even suggested the Vatican fund Vetra’s work as a gesture of goodwill toward spiritually based scientific research.

Madness! The church investing in research that threatened to make the church obsolete? Work that created weapons of mass destruction? The bomb that had killed his mother…

“But… you can’t!” the camerlegno had exclaimed.

“I owe a deep debt to science,” the Pope had replied. “Something I have hidden my entire life. Science gave me a gift when I was a young man. A gift I have never forgotten.”

“I don’t understand. What does science have to offer a man of God?”

“It is complicated,” the Pope had said. “I will need time to make you understand. But first, there is a simple fact about me that you must know. I have kept it hidden all these years. I believe it is time I told you.”

Then the Pope had told him the astonishing truth.
