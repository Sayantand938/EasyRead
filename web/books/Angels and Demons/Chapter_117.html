Angels and Demons

Chapter 117

Robert Langdon had no doubt that the chaos and panic spreading through St. Peter’s Square right now was greater than anything Vatican Hill had ever seen. No battle, no crucifixion, no pilgrimage, no mystical vision… nothing in the shrine’s 2,000-year history could compare to the size and intensity of this moment.

As the tragedy happened, Langdon felt strangely detached, as if floating next to Vittoria at the top of the stairs. The action seemed to stretch out, as if time were warped, all the madness slowing to a crawl…

The branded camerlegno… ranting for everyone to see…

The Illuminati Diamond… revealed in its evil brilliance…

The countdown clock showing the last twenty minutes of Vatican history…

But the drama had just started.

The camerlegno, as if in a daze after trauma, suddenly seemed powerful, taken over by demons. He began mumbling, whispering to invisible spirits, looking up at the sky and raising his arms to God.

“Speak!” the camerlegno yelled to the heavens. “Yes, I hear you!”

In that moment, Langdon understood. His heart sank like a rock.

Vittoria seemed to understand too. She went white. “He’s in shock,” she said. “He’s hallucinating. He thinks he’s talking to God!”

Somebody’s got to stop this , Langdon thought. It was a terrible and shameful end. Get this man to a hospital!

Below them on the stairs, Chinita Macri was ready and filming, having found her perfect spot. The images she filmed appeared right away across the square behind her on media screens… like endless drive-in movies all playing the same awful tragedy.

The whole scene felt huge. The camerlegno, in his torn cassock, with the burned mark on his chest, looked like a beaten hero who had fought through the circles of hell for this one moment of revealing truth. He shouted to the heavens.

“Ti sento, Dio! I hear you, God!”

Chartrand stepped back, looking awed.

The silence that fell on the crowd was quick and complete. For a moment it was as if the quiet had fallen across the entire planet… everyone in front of their TVs frozen, holding their breath together.

The camerlegno stood on the stairs, before the world, and held out his arms. He looked almost Christlike, bare and wounded before the world. He raised his arms to the heavens and, looking up, exclaimed, “Grazie! Grazie, Dio!”

The silence of the crowd never broke.

“Grazie, Dio!” the camerlegno cried out again. Like the sun breaking through a stormy sky, a look of joy spread across his face. “Grazie, Dio!”

Thank you, God? Langdon stared in wonder.

The camerlegno was glowing now, his strange change complete. He looked up at the sky, still nodding quickly. He shouted to the heavens, “Upon this rock I will build my church!”

Langdon knew the words, but he had no idea why the camerlegno was shouting them.

The camerlegno turned back to the crowd and yelled again into the night. “Upon this rock I will build my church!” Then he raised his hands to the sky and laughed out loud. “Grazie, Dio! Grazie!”

The man had clearly gone crazy.

The world watched, fascinated.

The end, however, was something no one expected.

With a final joyful cry, the camerlegno turned and ran back into St. Peter’s Basilica.
