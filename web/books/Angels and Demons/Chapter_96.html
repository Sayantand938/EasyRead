Angels and Demons

Chapter 96

Like a repeated part in a devilish song, the heavy darkness was back.

No light. No air. No way out.

Langdon was stuck under the flipped sarcophagus and felt his mind getting close to panic. Trying to think about anything but the tight space around him, Langdon tried to focus on something logical… math, music, anything. But he couldn't calm down. I can’t move! I can’t breathe!

The tight sleeve of his jacket had thankfully come loose when the casket fell, so Langdon could now move both arms. Still, when he pushed up on the ceiling of his small prison, it wouldn't budge. Strangely, he wished his sleeve was still stuck. At least it might make a gap for some air.

As Langdon pushed against the roof, his sleeve fell back to show the faint glow of an old friend. Mickey. The green cartoon face seemed to be making fun of him now.

Langdon searched the darkness for any other light, but the casket edge was flat against the floor. Damn Italian perfectionists, he thought, now in danger because of the same artistic skill he taught his students to admire… perfect edges, flawless lines, and of course, only using the best and strongest Carrara marble.

Perfection can trap you.

“Lift it,” he said out loud, pushing harder through the pile of bones. The box moved a little. He tightened his jaw and pushed again. The box felt like a huge rock, but this time it lifted a quarter of an inch. A quick flash of light came in, and then the casket fell back down. Langdon lay gasping in the dark. He tried to lift with his legs like before, but now that the sarcophagus was flat, he didn't even have room to straighten his knees.

As the trapped panic grew, Langdon imagined the sarcophagus getting smaller around him. Losing his mind, he fought the image with every bit of logic he had.

“Sarcophagus,” he said, trying to sound detached. But even knowledge seemed to fail him today. Sarcophagus comes from the Greek “sarx,” meaning “flesh,” and “phagein,” meaning “to eat.” I’m trapped in a box literally designed to “eat flesh.”

Images of flesh eaten from bone only reminded him that Langdon was covered in human remains. The thought made him sick and cold. But it also sparked an idea.

Groping around the coffin, Langdon found a piece of bone. A rib, maybe? He didn’t care. He just wanted a wedge. If he could lift the box, even a bit, and slide the bone under the edge, maybe enough air could…

Reaching across his body and pushing the pointed end of the bone into the crack between the floor and the coffin, Langdon reached up with his other hand and pushed upward. The box didn’t move. Not even a little. He tried again. For a moment, it seemed to shake slightly, but that was all.

With the awful smell and lack of air draining his strength, Langdon knew he only had time for one more try. He also knew he would need both arms.

Readjusting, he put the pointed edge of the bone against the crack, and shifting his body, he wedged the bone against his shoulder, holding it in place. Careful not to move it, he raised both hands above him. As the tight space began to suffocate him, he felt more and more panicked. It was the second time today he had been trapped with no air. Yelling, Langdon pushed upward with all his might. The casket lifted off the floor for a moment. But it was enough. The bone shard against his shoulder slipped further into the crack. When the casket fell again, the bone broke. But this time Langdon could see the casket was propped up. A tiny bit of light showed under the edge.

Exhausted, Langdon collapsed. Hoping the choking feeling in his throat would pass, he waited. But it only got worse as time went on. Whatever air was coming through the slit seemed too little to notice. Langdon wondered if it would be enough to keep him alive. And if so, for how long? If he passed out, who would know he was even in there?

With heavy arms, Langdon raised his watch again: 10:12 P.M. Struggling with shaky fingers, he fiddled with the watch and made his last move. He turned one of the small dials and pressed a button.

As he lost consciousness, and the walls felt closer, Langdon felt the old fears return. He tried to imagine, as he had so many times, that he was in an open field. But the image didn't help. The nightmare that had haunted him since he was young came rushing back…

The flowers here are like paintings, the child thought, laughing as he ran across the meadow. He wished his parents had come along. But his parents were busy setting up camp.

“Don’t go too far,” his mother had said.

He had pretended not to hear as he ran off into the woods.

Now, walking across this beautiful field, the boy saw a pile of stones. He guessed it was the base of an old house. He would not go near it. He knew better. Besides, his eyes were drawn to something else—a bright lady’s slipper—the rarest and most beautiful flower in New Hampshire. He had only ever seen them in books.

Excited, the boy moved toward the flower. He knelt down. The ground beneath him felt soft and hollow. He realized his flower had found a very rich spot. It was growing from a patch of rotting wood.

Thrilled by the thought of taking home his prize, the boy reached out… fingers stretching toward the stem.

He never reached it.

With a sickening crack, the earth broke open.

In the three seconds of dizzying terror as he fell, the boy knew he would die. Plunging downward, he braced for the bone-crushing hit. When it came, there was no pain. Only softness.

And cold.

He hit the deep liquid face first, falling into a narrow darkness. Spinning around, confused, he felt for the smooth walls that surrounded him. Somehow, as if by instinct, he sputtered to the surface.

Light.

Faint. Above him. It seemed miles above him.

His arms scratched at the water, trying to find something to grip on the hollow walls. Only smooth stone. He had fallen through the broken cover of an old well. He screamed for help, but his shouts echoed in the narrow space. He called out again and again. Above him, the torn hole grew dark.

Night fell.

Time twisted in the darkness. He grew numb as he floated in the deep pit, calling and crying. He imagined the walls caving in, burying him alive. His arms were tired and sore. A few times he thought he heard voices. He shouted, but his own voice was soft… like a dream.

As the night went on, the shaft seemed to get deeper. The walls slowly moved closer. The boy pushed against the enclosure, trying to keep it away. He was tired and wanted to quit. But the water held him up, cooling his hot fears until he felt nothing.

When the rescue team got there, the boy was barely awake. He had been floating for five hours. Two days later, the Boston Globe had a front-page story called “The Little Swimmer That Could.”
