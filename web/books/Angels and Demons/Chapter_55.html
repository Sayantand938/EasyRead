Angels and Demons

Chapter 55

Langdon and Vittoria burst into the courtyard outside the Secret Archives. The fresh air felt like a drug as it filled Langdon’s lungs. The purple spots in his vision quickly disappeared. The guilt, however, remained. He had just helped steal a priceless relic from the world’s most private vault. The camerlegno had said, I am giving you my trust.

“Hurry,” Vittoria said, still holding the folio and walking quickly across Via Borgia toward Olivetti’s office.

“If any water gets on that papyrus-”

“Calm down. When we figure this out, we can return their sacred Folio 5.”

Langdon sped up to keep pace. Besides feeling like a criminal, he was still stunned by the document’s incredible meaning. John Milton was an Illuminatus. He wrote the poem for Galileo to publish in Folio 5 … away from the Vatican's notice.

As they left the courtyard, Vittoria held out the folio to Langdon. “Think you can figure this out? Or did we just waste all those brain cells for nothing?”

Langdon carefully took the document. Without hesitating, he slipped it into a breast pocket of his tweed jacket, away from the sunlight and moisture. “I already figured it out.”

Vittoria stopped suddenly. “You what?”

Langdon kept walking.

Vittoria hurried to catch up. “You read it once! I thought it was supposed to be hard!”

Langdon knew she was right, yet he had understood the segno with just one look. A perfect verse in a natural rhythm, and the first altar of science had appeared clearly. Still, how easily he had done it made him uneasy. He believed in hard work. He could still hear his father say the old New England saying: If it wasn’t really hard, you did it wrong. Langdon hoped it wasn't true. “I understood it,” he said, walking faster. “I know where the first killing will happen. We have to warn Olivetti.”

Vittoria caught up to him. “How could you know already? Let me see that again.” Quick as a boxer, she slipped her hand into his pocket and took the folio again.

“Careful!” Langdon said. “You can’t-”

Vittoria didn’t listen. Holding the folio, she walked beside him, looking at the document in the evening light, checking the edges. As she started reading aloud, Langdon reached for the folio but was instead drawn in by Vittoria’s voice speaking the words in perfect rhythm with her steps.

Hearing the verse aloud, Langdon felt like he was traveling back in time… as if he were with Galileo, hearing the poem for the first time… knowing it was a test, a map, a clue showing the four altars of science… the four signs that marked a secret path across Rome. The verse came from Vittoria’s lips like a song.

From Santi’s earthly tomb with demon’s hole,

‘ Cross Rome the mystic elements unfold.

The path of light is laid, the sacred test,

Let angels guide you on your lofty quest.

Vittoria read it twice and then fell silent, as if letting the old words speak for themselves.

From Santi’s earthly tomb, Langdon repeated in his mind. The poem was very clear about that. The Path of Illumination began at Santi’s tomb. From there, across Rome, the markers showed the way.

From Santi’s earthly tomb with demon’s hole,

‘Cross Rome the mystic elements unfold.

Mystic elements. Also clear. Earth, Air, Fire, Water. Basic science, the four Illuminati markers hidden as religious sculpture.

“The first marker,” Vittoria said, “sounds like it’s at Santi’s tomb.”

Langdon smiled. “I told you it wasn’t that hard.”

“So who is Santi?” she asked, sounding suddenly excited. “And where’s his tomb?”

Langdon chuckled to himself. He was amazed how few people knew Santi, the last name of one of the most famous Renaissance artists ever. His first name was known worldwide… the child genius who at twenty-five was already working for Pope Julius II, and when he died at only thirty-eight, left behind the greatest collection of frescoes ever seen. Santi was a giant in the art world, and being known only by one’s first name was fame only a few had… people like Napoleon, Galileo, and Jesus… and, of course, the stars Langdon now heard from Harvard dorms-Sting, Madonna, Jewel, and the artist once known as Prince, who had changed his name to the symbol , causing Langdon to call him “The Tau Cross With Intersecting Hermaphroditic Ankh.”

“Santi,” Langdon said, “is the last name of the great Renaissance master, Raphael.”

Vittoria looked surprised. “Raphael? As in the Raphael?”

“The one and only.” Langdon kept walking toward the Office of the Swiss Guard.

“So the path starts at Raphael’s tomb?”

“It makes perfect sense,” Langdon said as they hurried. “The Illuminati often saw great artists and sculptors as honorary members. They could have chosen Raphael’s tomb as a tribute.” Langdon also knew that Raphael, like many religious artists, was thought to be secretly an atheist.

Vittoria carefully put the folio back in Langdon’s pocket. “So where is he buried?”

Langdon took a deep breath. “Believe it or not, Raphael’s buried in the Pantheon.”

Vittoria looked doubtful. “ The Pantheon?”

“The Raphael at the Pantheon.” Langdon had to admit, the Pantheon wasn't what he expected for the first marker. He thought the first altar of science would be in a quiet, hidden church, something subtle. Even in the 1600s, the Pantheon, with its huge, open dome, was one of the most famous places in Rome.

“Is the Pantheon even a church?” Vittoria asked.

“Oldest Catholic church in Rome.”

Vittoria shook her head. “But do you really think the first cardinal could be killed at the Pantheon? That’s got to be one of the most crowded tourist places in Rome.”

Langdon shrugged. “The Illuminati said they wanted the whole world watching. Killing a cardinal at the Pantheon would definitely get people’s attention.”

“But how does this guy expect to kill someone at the Pantheon and not be seen? It would be impossible.”

“As impossible as kidnapping four cardinals from Vatican City? The poem is exact.”

“And you’re certain Raphael is buried inside the Pantheon?”

“I’ve seen his tomb many times.”

Vittoria nodded, still looking worried. “What time is it?”

Langdon checked. “Seven-thirty.”

“Is the Pantheon far?”

“A mile maybe. We’ve got time.”

“The poem said Santi’s earthly tomb. Does that mean anything to you?”

Langdon quickly walked across the Courtyard of the Sentinel. “Earthly? Actually, there’s probably no place more earthly in Rome than the Pantheon. It got its name from the first religion practiced there-Pantheism-the worship of all gods, especially the pagan gods of Mother Earth.”

As an architecture student, Langdon was amazed to learn that the size of the Pantheon’s main room honored Gaea-the goddess of the Earth. The sizes were so precise that a giant ball could fit perfectly inside the building with hardly any space left over.

“Okay,” Vittoria said, sounding more convinced. “And demon’s hole? From Santi’s earthly tomb with demon’s hole?”

Langdon wasn't so sure about this. “Demon’s hole must mean the oculus,” he said, guessing logically. “The famous round opening in the Pantheon’s roof.”

“But it’s a church,” Vittoria said, walking easily beside him. “Why would they call the opening a demon’s hole?”

Langdon had been wondering that himself. He had never heard the term “demon’s hole,” but he remembered a famous sixth-century criticism of the Pantheon whose words seemed strangely fitting now. The Venerable Bede once wrote that the hole in the Pantheon’s roof was made by demons trying to escape the building when Boniface IV made it a church.

“And why,” Vittoria added as they entered a smaller courtyard, “why would the Illuminati use the name Santi if he was really known as Raphael?”

“You ask a lot of questions.”

“My dad used to say that.”

“Two possible reasons. One, the word Raphael has too many syllables. It would have ruined the poem’s iambic pentameter.”

“Sounds unlikely.”

Langdon agreed. “Okay, then maybe using ‘Santi’ was to make the clue harder, so only very knowledgeable men would recognize the reference to Raphael.”

Vittoria didn’t seem convinced. “I’m sure Raphael’s last name was very well known when he was alive.”

“Surprisingly, it wasn’t. Being known by one name was a status symbol. Raphael avoided his last name much like pop stars do today. Take Madonna, for example. She never uses her last name, Ciccone.”

Vittoria looked amused. “You know Madonna’s last name?”

Langdon regretted the example. It was amazing the kind of junk a mind gathered living with 10,000 teenagers.

As he and Vittoria passed the last gate toward the Office of the Swiss Guard, they were stopped suddenly.

“Para!” a voice roared behind them.

Langdon and Vittoria turned to find themselves staring at the barrel of a rifle.

“Attento!” Vittoria exclaimed, jumping back. “Watch it with-”

“Non sportarti!” the guard snapped, cocking the weapon.

“Soldato!” a voice commanded from across the courtyard. Olivetti was emerging from the security center. “Let them go!”

The guard looked bewildered. “Ma, signore, и una donna -”

“Inside!” he yelled at the guard.

“Signore, non posso -”

“Now! You have new orders. Captain Rocher will be briefing the corps in two minutes. We will be organizing a search.”

Looking bewildered, the guard hurried into the security center. Olivetti marched toward Langdon, rigid and steaming. “Our most secret archives? I want an explanation.”

“We have good news,” Langdon said.

Olivetti’s eyes narrowed. “It better be damn good.”
