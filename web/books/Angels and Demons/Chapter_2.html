Angels and Demons

Chapter 2

“Do I have your attention now?” the man’s voice said when Langdon finally answered the line.

“Yes, sir, you sure do. Want to explain yourself?”

“I tried to tell you before.” The voice was stiff, like a machine. “I’m a physicist. I run a research place. We had a murder. You saw the body.”

“How did you find me?” Langdon could hardly focus. His mind raced from the image on the fax.

“I already told you. The Internet. The site for your book, The Art of the Illuminati.”

Langdon tried to think clearly. His book wasn't well-known in main book circles, but it had gotten a lot of fans online. Still, the caller’s claim didn't make sense. “That page has no way to contact me,” Langdon argued. “I’m sure of it.”

“I have people here at the lab who are very good at getting user information from the Web.”

Langdon didn't believe it. “Sounds like your lab knows a lot about the Web.”

“We should,” the man snapped back. “We made it.”

Something in the man’s voice told Langdon he was serious.

“I must see you,” the caller insisted. “This is not something we can talk about on the phone. My lab is only an hour's flight from Boston.”

Langdon stood in the dim light of his study and studied the fax in his hand. The image was stunning, possibly the most important writing discovery of the century, a decade of his research proven by a single symbol.

“It’s urgent,” the voice pressed.

Langdon’s eyes were fixed on the mark. Illuminati, he read again and again. His work had always been based on the symbolic equivalent of fossils-old documents and historical stories-but this image before him was now. Today. He felt like a paleontologist seeing a living dinosaur.

“I’ve sent a plane for you,” the voice said. “It will be in Boston in twenty minutes.”

Langdon’s mouth felt dry. An hour’s flight …

“Please forgive me for taking the liberty,” the voice said. “I need you here.”

Langdon looked again at the fax-an old story confirmed in black and white. The meaning was scary. He stared blankly through the bay window. The first light of dawn was coming through the birch trees in his backyard, but the view looked different this morning. As a strange mix of fear and excitement filled him, Langdon knew he had to go.

“You win,” he said. “Tell me where to meet the plane.”
