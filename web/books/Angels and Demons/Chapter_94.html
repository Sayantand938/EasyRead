Angels and Demons

Chapter 94

Sitting in the Sistine Chapel among his shocked colleagues, Cardinal Mortati struggled to grasp what he was hearing. In front of him, lit only by candlelight, the camerlegno had just told a story of such hate and betrayal that Mortati was trembling. The camerlegno spoke of cardinals being kidnapped, branded, murdered. He spoke of the ancient Illuminati—a name that brought back old fears—and their return and vow to get revenge on the church. With pain in his voice, the camerlegno spoke of the Pope who had died… a victim of Illuminati poisoning. And finally, almost whispering, he spoke of a deadly new technology, antimatter, which in less than two hours threatened to destroy all of Vatican City.

When he finished, it was as if Satan himself had taken all the air from the room. Nobody could move. The camerlegno’s words hung in the darkness.

The only sound Mortati could hear now was the strange hum of a television camera in the back—an electronic presence no conclave had ever experienced—but a presence the camerlegno had demanded. To the cardinals’ great surprise, the camerlegno had entered the Sistine Chapel with two BBC reporters—a man and a woman—and announced that they would be broadcasting his serious statement, live to the world.

Now, speaking directly to the camera, the camerlegno stepped forward. “To the Illuminati,” he said, his voice getting deeper, “and to those of science, let me say this.” He paused. “You have won the war.”

The silence now spread to the farthest corners of the chapel. Mortati could hear his own heart pounding.

“Things have been moving for a long time,” the camerlegno said. “Your victory was inevitable. It has never been as clear as it is now. Science is the new God.”

What is he saying! Mortati thought. Has he gone mad? The whole world is hearing this!

“Medicine, electronic communications, space travel, genetic manipulation… these are the miracles we now tell our children about. These are the miracles we praise as proof that science will give us the answers. The old stories of virgin births, burning bushes, and seas parting are no longer important. God has become outdated. Science has won the battle. We give up.”

A ripple of confusion and shock went through the chapel.

“But science’s victory,” the camerlegno added, his voice intensifying, “has cost every one of us. And it has cost us deeply.”

Silence.

“Science may have eased the troubles of sickness and hard work and given us many gadgets for fun and ease, but it has left us in a world without wonder. Our sunsets are now just wavelengths and frequencies. The complexities of the universe have been broken down into math problems. Even how we value ourselves as people has been destroyed. Science says that Earth and its people are a meaningless speck in the big picture. A cosmic accident.” He paused. “Even the technology that promises to unite us, divides us. Each of us is now electronically connected to the world, and yet we feel completely alone. We are bombarded with violence, division, and betrayal. Doubt has become a good thing. Cynicism and needing proof has become smart thinking. Is it any wonder that people now feel more depressed and defeated than ever before? Does science hold anything sacred? Science looks for answers by examining unborn babies. Science even tries to change our own DNA. It breaks God’s world into smaller and smaller pieces looking for meaning… and all it finds is more questions.”

Mortati watched in awe. The camerlegno was almost hypnotic now. He had a physical strength in his movements and voice that Mortati had never seen at a Vatican altar. The man’s voice was full of conviction and sadness.

“The old war between science and religion is over,” the camerlegno said. “You have won. But you have not won fairly. You have not won by giving answers. You have won by so completely changing our society that the truths we once saw as guides now seem useless. Religion cannot keep up. Scientific growth is exponential. It feeds on itself like a virus. Every new discovery opens doors for new discoveries. It took mankind thousands of years to go from the wheel to the car. Yet only decades from the car to space. Now we measure scientific progress in weeks. We are spinning out of control. The gap between us grows deeper, and as religion is left behind, people find themselves spiritually empty. We cry out for meaning. And believe me, we do cry out. We see UFOs, try to contact spirits, have out-of-body experiences—all these strange ideas have a scientific appearance, but they are clearly irrational. They are the desperate cry of the modern soul, lonely and tormented, weakened by its own knowledge and its inability to find meaning in anything besides technology.”

Mortati could feel himself leaning forward in his seat. He and the other cardinals and people around the world were hanging on this priest’s every word. The camerlegno spoke without fancy words or anger. No mentions of scripture or Jesus Christ. He spoke in modern terms, simple and pure. Somehow, as if the words were coming from God himself, he spoke the modern language… delivering the ancient message. In that moment, Mortati saw one of the reasons the late Pope loved this young man so much. In a world of apathy, cynicism, and worship of technology, men like the camerlegno, realists who could speak to our souls like this man just had, were the church’s only hope.

The camerlegno was talking more forcefully now. “Science, you say, will save us. Science, I say, has destroyed us. Since the days of Galileo, the church has tried to slow the constant march of science, sometimes in the wrong ways, but always with good intentions. Even so, the temptations are too great for man to resist. I warn you, look around yourselves. The promises of science have not been kept. Promises of efficiency and simplicity have created nothing but pollution and chaos. We are a broken and frantic species… moving down a path of destruction.”

The camerlegno paused a long moment and then looked directly at the camera.

“Who is this God science? Who is the God who offers his people power but no morals to tell you how to use that power? What kind of God gives a child fire but does not warn the child of its dangers? The language of science does not tell us about good and bad. Science textbooks tell us how to create a nuclear reaction, and yet they don’t ask if it is a good idea.

“To science, I say this. The church is tired. We are exhausted from trying to guide you. Our resources are running out as we try to be the voice of balance while you blindly chase smaller chips and bigger profits. We don’t ask why you won’t control yourselves, but how can you? Your world moves so fast that if you stop even for a moment to think about what you’re doing, someone more efficient will pass you by. So you move on. You create weapons of mass destruction, but it is the Pope who travels the world begging leaders to show restraint. You clone living things, but it is the church reminding us to think about the moral implications. You encourage people to connect on phones, video screens, and computers, but it is the church who opens its doors and reminds us to gather in person as we were meant to do. You even kill unborn babies for research that will save lives. Again, it is the church who points out the flaw in this reasoning.

“And all the while, you say the church is ignorant. But who is more ignorant? The person who can't explain lightning, or the one who doesn't respect its power? This church is reaching out to you. Reaching out to everyone. Yet the more we reach, the more you push us away. 'Show me proof there is a God,' you say. I say, use your telescopes to look at the heavens, and tell me how there couldn't be a God!” The camerlegno had tears in his eyes now. “You ask what God looks like. I say, where did that question come from? The answers are the same. Don't you see God in your science? How can you miss Him! You say that even a tiny change in gravity or the weight of an atom would have made our universe a dead mist instead of our amazing sea of stars, and yet you don't see God's hand in this? Is it easier to believe we just picked the right card from a deck of billions? Have we become so empty inside that we'd rather believe in something mathematically impossible than in a power greater than us?

“Whether or not you believe in God,” the camerlegno said, his voice getting deeper, “you must believe this. When we stop trusting in the power greater than us, we lose our sense of responsibility. Faith… all faiths… are reminders that there's something we can't understand, something we're responsible to… With faith, we're responsible to each other, to ourselves, and to a higher truth. Religion has flaws, but only because people have flaws. If the world could see this church as I do… looking past the rituals of these walls… they'd see a modern miracle… a group of imperfect, simple people who just want to be a voice of kindness in a world spinning out of control.”

The camerlegno pointed out over the College of Cardinals, and the BBC camerawoman followed, showing the crowd.

“Are we outdated?” the camerlegno asked. “Are these men dinosaurs? Am I? Does the world really need someone to speak for the poor, the weak, the mistreated, the unborn child? Do we really need people like these who, even though they're not perfect, spend their lives asking us to read the signs of right and wrong and not lose our way?”

Mortati now saw that the camerlegno, whether he knew it or not, was making a smart move. By showing the cardinals, he was making the church personal. Vatican City wasn't just a building anymore, it was people -people like the camerlegno who had spent their lives doing good.

“Tonight, we're on the edge,” the camerlegno said. “None of us can ignore this. Whether you see this evil as Satan, corruption, or immorality… the dark force is alive and growing every day. Don't ignore it.” The camerlegno lowered his voice to a whisper, and the camera zoomed in. “The force, though strong, can be beaten. Goodness can win. Listen to your hearts. Listen to God. Together, we can step back from this cliff.”

Now Mortati understood. This was the reason. Conclave had been violated, but this was the only way. It was a strong and desperate call for help. The camerlegno was talking to both his enemy and his friends. He was asking anyone, friend or enemy, to see the truth and stop this madness. Surely someone listening would realize how crazy this plot was and come forward.

The camerlegno knelt at the altar. “Pray with me.”

The College of Cardinals knelt to pray with him. Outside in St. Peter’s Square and around the world… a shocked world knelt with them.
