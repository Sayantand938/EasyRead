Angels and Demons

Chapter 54

Inside Archive Vault 10 Robert Langdon said Italian numbers as he looked at the handwriting in front of him. Mille … centi … uno, duo, tre … cincuanta. I need a number! Anything, damn it!

When he got to the end of the page he was on, he lifted the spatula to turn the page. As he lined up the tool with the next page, he stumbled, struggling to hold it still. A few minutes later, he looked down and realized he had stopped using the spatula and was turning pages by hand. Oops, he thought, feeling a little guilty. The lack of oxygen was making him less careful. Looks like I’ll pay for this later.

“Finally,” Vittoria said, coughing, when she saw Langdon turning pages by hand. She dropped her spatula and started doing the same.

“Any luck?”

Vittoria shook her head. “Nothing that looks like just numbers. I’m skimming… but none of this seems like a clue.”

Langdon kept translating his pages, but it was getting harder. His Italian was not great, and the tiny writing and old-fashioned language was making it slow. Vittoria finished her stack before Langdon and looked sad as she flipped the pages back over. She got ready to look again more closely.

When Langdon finished his last page, he cursed quietly and looked over at Vittoria. She was frowning, staring at something on one of her pages. “What is it?” he asked.

Vittoria did not look up. “Did you see any notes at the bottom of your pages?”

“Not that I remember. Why?”

“This page has a footnote. It’s hidden in a fold.”

Langdon tried to see what she was looking at, but all he could see was the page number in the upper right corner. Page 5. It took a moment for the coincidence to sink in, and even then the connection seemed weak. Page Five. Five, Pythagoras, pentagrams, Illuminati. Langdon wondered if the Illuminati would have picked page five to hide their clue. Through the red haze around them, Langdon felt a small spark of hope. “Is the footnote about math?”

Vittoria shook her head. “Text. One line. Very small print. Almost impossible to read.”

His hopes dropped. “It should be math. Lingua pura.”

“Yeah, I know.” She paused. “I think you’ll want to hear this, though.” Langdon heard excitement in her voice.

“Go ahead.”

Squinting at the page, Vittoria read the line. “The path of light is laid, the sacred test.”

The words were nothing like what Langdon expected. “I’m sorry?”

Vittoria repeated the line. “The path of light is laid, the sacred test.”

“Path of light?” Langdon felt himself stand up straighter.

“That’s what it says. Path of light.”

As the words sank in, Langdon felt his confusion briefly replaced by clarity. The path of light is laid, the sacred test. He didn't know how it helped them, but the line was as clear a reference to the Path of Illumination as he could imagine. Path of light. Sacred test. His head felt like an engine struggling to run. “Are you sure about the translation?”

Vittoria hesitated. “Actually…” She glanced at him strangely. “It’s not really a translation. The line is written in English.”

For a moment, Langdon thought the room's acoustics were playing tricks on him. “English?”

Vittoria pushed the document to him, and Langdon read the tiny print at the bottom. “The path of light is laid, the sacred test. English? What is English doing in an Italian book?”

Vittoria shrugged. She looked a little unsteady too. “Maybe English is what they meant by lingua pura? It’s seen as the international language of science. It’s what we all speak at CERN.”

“But this was in the 1600s,” Langdon said. “Nobody spoke English in Italy, not even-” He stopped, realizing what he was about to say. “Not even… the clergy.” Langdon’s mind raced. “In the 1600s,” he said, speaking faster, “English was one language the Vatican hadn’t yet accepted. They used Italian, Latin, German, even Spanish and French, but English was completely foreign inside the Vatican. They thought of English as a tainted, rebellious language for common thinkers like Chaucer and Shakespeare.” Langdon suddenly thought of the Illuminati symbols of Earth, Air, Fire, Water. The idea that the symbols were in English now made a strange kind of sense.

“So you’re saying maybe Galileo saw English as la lingua pura because it was the one language the Vatican didn’t control?”

“Yes. Or maybe by putting the clue in English, Galileo was subtly keeping the Vatican from understanding it.”

“But it’s not even a clue,” Vittoria said. “The path of light is laid, the sacred test? What does that even mean?”

She’s right, Langdon thought. The line wasn’t helpful. But as he repeated the phrase, a strange fact struck him. That’s odd, he thought. What are the odds of that?

“We need to get out of here,” Vittoria said, sounding strained.

Langdon wasn’t listening. The path of light is laid, the sacred test. “It’s a line of iambic pentameter,” he said suddenly, counting the syllables. “Five pairs of alternating weak and strong beats.”

Vittoria looked confused. “Iambic what?”

Langdon was instantly back at Phillips Exeter Academy in Saturday English class. A nightmare. Peter Greer, the baseball star, couldn’t remember how many pairs were in a line of Shakespearean iambic pentameter. Their teacher, Mr. Bissell, jumped on the table and yelled, “Penta-meter, Greer! Think of home plate! A penta-gon! Five sides! Penta! Penta! Penta!”

Five pairs, Langdon thought. Each pair having two syllables. He couldn’t believe he’d never made the connection. Iambic pentameter was based on the Illuminati numbers of 5 and 2!

You’re grasping at straws! Langdon told himself, trying to dismiss it. A coincidence! But the thought remained. Five … for Pythagoras and the pentagram. Two … for the duality of everything.

Then, another realization made his legs feel weak. Iambic pentameter, because it was simple, was often called “pure verse.” La lingua pura? Could this be the pure language the Illuminati meant? The path of light is laid, the sacred test …

“Uh oh,” Vittoria said.

Langdon turned to see her flipping the page upside down. He felt a pang of dread. Not again. “That line can’t be an ambigram!”

“No, it’s not an ambigram… but it’s…” She kept turning the document, a quarter turn each time.

“It’s what?”

Vittoria looked up. “It’s not the only line.”

“There’s another?”

“There’s a different line on every edge. Top, bottom, left, and right. I think it’s a poem.”

“Four lines?” Langdon grew excited. Galileo was a poet? “Let me see!”

Vittoria did not let go of the page. She kept turning it a quarter turn at a time. “I didn’t see the lines before because they’re on the edges.” She tilted her head at the last line. “Huh. You know what? Galileo didn’t even write this.”

“What!”

“The poem is signed John Milton.”

“John Milton?” The famous English poet who wrote Paradise Lost lived at the same time as Galileo and was a genius who conspiracy fans think was one of the main Illuminati members. Langdon thought that the idea that Milton was linked to Galileo’s Illuminati might be true. Milton had famously traveled to Rome in 1638 to “talk with smart men,” and he had met with Galileo while the scientist was under house arrest. These meetings were shown in many Renaissance paintings, including Annibale Gatti’s famous Galileo and Milton, which still hung in the IMSS Museum in Florence.

“Milton knew Galileo, right?” Vittoria said, finally sliding the book to Langdon. “Maybe he wrote the poem as a favor?”

Langdon gritted his teeth as he took the covered document. Leaving it flat on the table, he read the line at the top. Then he turned the page, reading the line on the right side. Another turn, and he read the bottom. Another turn, the left. A final turn finished the circle. There were four lines in total. The first line Vittoria found was actually the third line of the poem. Totally amazed, he read the four lines again, in order: top, right, bottom, left. When he was done, he sighed. He was sure. “You found it, Ms. Vetra.”

She smiled a little. “Good, now can we get out of here?”

“I have to write these lines down. I need to find a pencil and paper.”

Vittoria shook her head. “No way, professor. No time to be a writer. Mickey’s ticking.” She took the page from him and went to the door.

Langdon stood up. “You can’t take that outside! It’s a-”

But Vittoria was already gone.
