Angels and Demons

Chapter 89

War had started in St. Peter’s Square.

The square had turned into a chaotic scene of aggression. Media trucks screeched to a halt like war vehicles claiming territory. Reporters unpacked their high-tech equipment like soldiers preparing for battle. All around the edge of the square, networks fought for the best spots as they hurried to set up the newest weapon in media wars—flat-screen displays.

Flat-screen displays were huge video screens that could be put together on top of trucks or portable frames. The screens acted like billboard ads for the network, showing that network’s coverage and logo like a drive-in movie. If a screen was in a good spot—like in front of the action—a rival network couldn't film the story without also showing an ad for their competitor.

The square was quickly becoming not just a multimedia spectacle, but also a busy public gathering. Onlookers streamed in from everywhere. Open space in the usually vast square was quickly becoming scarce. People gathered around the tall flat-screen displays, listening to live reports with shocked excitement.

Just a hundred yards away, inside the thick walls of St. Peter’s Basilica, everything was calm. Lieutenant Chartrand and three other guards moved through the darkness. Wearing their infrared goggles, they spread out across the main area, waving their detectors in front of them. The search of Vatican City’s public areas had so far found nothing.

“Better take off your goggles up here,” the senior guard said.

Chartrand was already doing it. They were getting close to the Niche of the Palliums—the sunken area in the center of the basilica. It was lit by ninety-nine oil lamps, and the amplified infrared light would have hurt their eyes.

Chartrand was glad to be out of the heavy goggles, and he stretched his neck as they went down into the sunken niche to scan the area. The room was beautiful… golden and glowing. He had not been down here before.

It seemed like every day since Chartrand had arrived in Vatican City, he had learned some new Vatican secret. These oil lamps were one of them. There were always exactly ninety-nine lamps burning. It was tradition. The clergy carefully refilled the lamps with sacred oils so that none ever went out. It was said they would burn until the end of time.

Or at least until midnight, Chartrand thought, his mouth dry again.

Chartrand swung his detector over the oil lamps. Nothing hidden here. He wasn't surprised; the video showed the canister was hidden in a dark area.

As he crossed the niche, he found a grate covering a hole in the floor. The hole led to a steep, narrow stairway going straight down. He'd heard stories about what was down there. Thankfully, they wouldn't have to go down. Rocher's orders were clear. Search only the public areas; ignore the white zones.

"What's that smell?" he asked, turning from the grate. The niche smelled sweet and strong.

"Fumes from the lamps," one of them replied.

Chartrand was surprised. "Smells more like cologne than kerosene."

"It's not kerosene. These lamps are near the papal altar, so they use a special mixture—ethanol, sugar, butane, and perfume."

“Butane?” Chartrand eyed the lamps warily.

The guard nodded. "Don't spill any. Smells like heaven, burns like hell."

The guards finished searching the Niche of the Palliums and were moving across the basilica when their walkie-talkies went off.

It was an update. The guards listened in shock.

Apparently, there was disturbing new information that couldn't be shared on the air. But the camerlegno had decided to break tradition and enter the conclave to speak to the cardinals. This had never happened before. Then again, Chartrand realized, the Vatican had never before possessed something like a new kind of nuclear weapon.

Chartrand felt better knowing the camerlegno was in charge. The camerlegno was the person inside Vatican City whom Chartrand respected most. Some guards saw the camerlegno as a beato—a religious fanatic whose love for God was almost obsessive—but even they agreed that when it came to fighting God's enemies, the camerlegno was the one man who would stand up and fight hard.

The Swiss Guards had seen the camerlegno often that week, as he prepared for the conclave. Everyone had remarked that he seemed a bit rough, his green eyes more intense than usual. This wasn't surprising; the camerlegno was not only responsible for planning the sacred conclave, but he had to do so right after the death of his mentor, the Pope.

Chartrand had only been at the Vatican for a few months when he heard the story of the bomb that killed the camerlegno's mother right in front of him. A bomb in church… and now it’s happening again. Sadly, the authorities never caught the people who planted the bomb. They said it was probably some anti-Christian hate group, and the case was forgotten. No wonder the camerlegno hated indifference.

A couple of months ago, on a quiet afternoon inside Vatican City, Chartrand had run into the camerlegno walking across the grounds. The camerlegno seemed to recognize Chartrand as a new guard and invited him to walk with him. They talked about everyday things, and the camerlegno made Chartrand feel welcome.

“Father,” Chartrand said, “may I ask you a strange question?”

The camerlegno smiled. “Only if I can give you a strange answer.”

Chartrand laughed. “I've asked every priest I know, and I still don’t understand.”

“What’s bothering you?” The camerlegno walked quickly, his robe swinging as he moved. His black, crepe-sole shoes seemed fitting, Chartrand thought, like reflections of the man himself—modern but humble, and showing wear.

Chartrand took a deep breath. “I don’t understand this all-powerful and good thing.”

The camerlegno smiled. “You’ve been reading Scripture.”

“I try.”

“You are confused because the Bible describes God as all-powerful and good.”

“Exactly.”

“All-powerful and good simply means that God has unlimited power and wants good for us.”

“I understand the concept. It’s just… there seems to be a contradiction.”

“Yes. The contradiction is pain. Man’s starvation, war, sickness…”

“Exactly!” Chartrand knew the camerlegno would understand. “Terrible things happen in this world. Human tragedy seems like proof that God could not possibly be both all-powerful and good. If He loves us and has the power to change our situation, He would prevent our pain, wouldn’t He?”

The camerlegno frowned. “Would He?”

Chartrand felt uneasy. Had he gone too far? Was this a religious question you just didn’t ask? “Well… if God loves us, and He can protect us, He would have to. It seems He is either all-powerful and doesn't care, or kind and unable to help.”

“Do you have children, Lieutenant?”

Chartrand blushed. “No, signore.”

“Imagine you had an eight-year-old son… would you love him?”

“Of course.”

“Would you do everything you could to keep him from being hurt?”

“Of course.”

“Would you let him skateboard?”

Chartrand was surprised. The camerlegno seemed surprisingly "aware" for a clergyman. “Yeah, I guess,” Chartrand said. “Sure, I’d let him skateboard, but I’d tell him to be careful.”

“So as this child’s father, you would give him some simple, good advice and then let him go and learn from his own mistakes?”

“I wouldn’t chase after him and baby him, if that’s what you mean.”

“But what if he fell and scraped his knee?”

“He would learn to be more careful.”

The camerlegno smiled. “So even though you could step in and stop your child from getting hurt, you’d rather show your love by letting him learn on his own?”

“Of course. Pain is part of growing up. It’s how we learn.”

The camerlegno nodded. “Exactly.”
