Angels and Demons

Chapter 47

Even though he knew time was short, Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca walked slowly. He needed the time alone to collect his thoughts before the opening prayer. So much was happening. As he walked in dim solitude down the Northern Wing, the challenge of the past fifteen days felt heavy in his bones.

He had followed his sacred duties exactly.

As was Vatican tradition, after the Pope’s death the camerlegno had personally confirmed it by placing his fingers on the Pope’s neck artery, listening for breath, and then calling the Pope’s name three times. By law there was no autopsy. Then he had sealed the Pope’s bedroom, destroyed the papal fisherman’s ring, shattered the mold used to make lead seals, and arranged the funeral. After that, he began preparations for the conclave.

Conclave, he thought. The final obstacle. It was one of the oldest traditions in Christianity. Nowadays, because the result of the conclave was usually known beforehand, the process was criticized as outdated—more of a show than an election. The camerlegno knew, however, this was a misunderstanding. Conclave was not an election. It was an ancient, mystical transfer of power. The tradition was timeless… the secrecy, the folded slips of paper, the burning of the ballots, the mixing of old chemicals, the smoke signals.

As the camerlegno walked through the Loggias of Gregory XIII, he wondered if Cardinal Mortati was panicking yet. Surely Mortati had noticed the favorites were missing. Without them, the voting would continue all night. The camerlegno felt sure that Mortati’s appointment as the Great Elector was a good one. The man was open-minded and could speak freely. The conclave would need a leader tonight more than ever.

As the camerlegno arrived at the top of the Royal Staircase, he felt as though he were standing on the edge of his life. Even from up here he could hear the noise of activity in the Sistine Chapel below—the nervous chatter of 165 cardinals.

One hundred sixty-one cardinals, he corrected.

For a moment the camerlegno felt like he was falling, plunging toward hell, people screaming, flames burning him, stones and blood falling from the sky.

And then silence.

When the child awoke, he was in heaven. Everything around him was white. The light was blinding and pure. Though some would say a ten year old couldn't understand heaven, young Carlo Ventresca understood it very well. He was in heaven right now. Where else would he be? Even in his short ten years on earth Carlo had felt the greatness of God—the loud pipe organs, the tall domes, the singing voices, the stained glass, shimmering bronze and gold. Carlo’s mother, Maria, brought him to Mass every day. The church was Carlo’s home.

“Why do we come to Mass every single day?” Carlo asked, not that he minded at all.

“Because I promised God I would,” she replied. “And a promise to God is the most important promise of all. Never break a promise to God.”

Carlo promised her he would never break a promise to God. He loved his mother more than anything in the world. She was his holy angel. Sometimes he called her Maria benedetta -the Blessed Mary-though she did not like that at all. He knelt with her as she prayed, smelling the sweet scent of her skin and listening to her quiet voice as she counted the rosary. Hail Mary, Mother of God … pray for us sinners … now and at the hour of our death.

“Where is my father?” Carlo asked, already knowing his father had died before he was born.

“God is your father, now,” she would always reply. “You are a child of the church.”

Carlo loved that.

“Whenever you feel frightened,” she said, “remember that God is your father now. He will watch over you and protect you forever. God has big plans for you, Carlo.” The boy knew she was right. He could already feel God in his blood.

Blood…

Blood raining from the sky!

Silence. Then heaven.

His heaven, Carlo learned when the bright lights were turned off, was the Intensive Care Unit in Santa Clara Hospital outside of Palermo. Carlo was the only one who lived through a terrorist bombing that had destroyed a chapel where he and his mother were at Mass on vacation. Thirty-seven people died, including Carlo’s mother. The newspapers called Carlo’s survival The Miracle of St. Francis. Just before the blast, Carlo had, for some reason, left his mother and gone into a safe alcove to look at a tapestry about St. Francis.

God called me there , he decided. He wanted to save me.

Carlo was in great pain and confused. He could still see his mother, kneeling at the pew, blowing him a kiss, and then with a loud roar, her sweet-smelling body was torn apart. He could still taste man’s evil. Blood rained down. His mother’s blood! The blessed Maria!

God will watch over you and protect you forever , his mother had told him.

But where was God now!

Then, like his mother’s words coming to life, a clergyman came to the hospital. He was not just any clergyman. He was a bishop. He prayed over Carlo. The Miracle of St. Francis. When Carlo got better, the bishop arranged for him to live in a small monastery connected to the cathedral where the bishop worked. Carlo lived and studied with the monks. He even became an altar boy for his new protector. The bishop suggested Carlo go to public school, but Carlo refused. He was very happy with his new home. He now truly lived in the house of God.

Every night Carlo prayed for his mother.

God saved me for a reason , he thought. What is the reason?

When Carlo turned sixteen, Italian law said he had to do two years of reserve military training. The bishop told Carlo that if he went to seminary he would not have to do this. Carlo told the priest that he planned to go to seminary but that first he needed to understand evil.

The bishop did not understand.

Carlo told him that if he wanted to spend his life in the church fighting evil, he needed to understand it first. He thought the best place to understand evil was in the army. The army used guns and bombs. A bomb killed my Blessed mother!

The bishop tried to talk him out of it, but Carlo had already decided.

“Be careful, my son,” the bishop had said. “And remember the church will be here when you come back.”

Carlo’s two years in the military had been awful. His youth had been quiet and thoughtful. But in the army, there was no quiet to think. Constant noise. Huge machines everywhere. Never a moment of peace. The soldiers went to Mass once a week at the barracks, but Carlo didn’t feel God in any of them. Their minds were too full of chaos to see God.

Carlo hated his new life and wanted to go home. But he was determined to stay. He hadn’t understood evil yet. He refused to fire a gun, so the military taught him how to fly a medical helicopter. Carlo hated the noise and the smell, but at least it let him fly up in the sky and be closer to his mother in heaven. When he learned his pilot training included parachuting, Carlo was terrified. Still, he had no choice.

God will protect me , he told himself.

Carlo’s first parachute jump was the most thrilling thing he had ever felt. It was like flying with God. Carlo couldn’t get enough… the silence… the floating… seeing his mother’s face in the big white clouds as he floated to earth. God has plans for you, Carlo. When he returned from the military, Carlo entered the seminary.

That had been twenty-three years ago.

Now, as Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca walked down the Royal Staircase, he tried to understand how he had arrived at this amazing turning point.

Let go of all fear, he told himself, and trust God with this night.

He could now see the large bronze door of the Sistine Chapel, carefully guarded by four Swiss Guards. The guards unlocked the door and pulled it open. Inside, everyone turned to look. The camerlegno looked out at the black robes and red sashes in front of him. He knew what God wanted him to do. The future of the church was now in his hands.

The camerlegno made the sign of the cross and walked inside.
