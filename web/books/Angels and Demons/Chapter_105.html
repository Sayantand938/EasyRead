Angels and Demons

Chapter 105

The fog of sadness faded slowly as Langdon walked away from the dead cardinal, back into deep water. Weak and alone in the fountain, Langdon almost expected to fall. But instead, he felt a new urge growing inside him. Impossible to ignore. Desperate. He felt his muscles tighten with a sudden strength. His mind, as if ignoring his heartache, pushed aside the past and focused on the single, urgent task ahead.

Find the Illuminati lair. Help Vittoria.

Turning to the huge center of Bernini’s fountain, Langdon found hope and began his search for the last Illuminati marker. He knew somewhere on this twisted group of figures was a clue pointing to the lair. But as Langdon looked around the fountain, his hope quickly disappeared. The words of the segno seemed to echo mockingly around him. Let angels guide you on your lofty quest. Langdon glared at the carved shapes before him. The fountain is pagan! It doesn’t have any angels!

When Langdon finished his useless search of the center, his eyes went to the tall stone pillar. Four markers, he thought, spread across Rome in a giant cross.

Looking at the hieroglyphs covering the obelisk, he wondered if there might be a clue hidden in the Egyptian symbols. He quickly dismissed the idea. The hieroglyphs were much older than Bernini, and they hadn’t even been understood until the Rosetta Stone was found. Still, Langdon thought, maybe Bernini had carved an extra symbol? One that would blend in with all the hieroglyphs?

Feeling a spark of hope, Langdon walked around the fountain again and studied all four sides of the obelisk. It took him two minutes, and when he reached the last side, his hope vanished. Nothing in the hieroglyphs seemed out of place. Certainly no angels.

Langdon checked his watch. It was exactly eleven. He couldn’t tell if time was passing quickly or slowly. Images of Vittoria and the Hassassin began to swirl in his mind as Langdon climbed around the fountain, his frustration growing as he made another pointless circle. Defeated and exhausted, Langdon felt like collapsing. He threw back his head to scream at the night sky.

The sound stopped in his throat.

Langdon was looking straight up the obelisk. The thing at the very top was something he had seen before and ignored. But now, it stopped him. It wasn’t an angel. Far from it. In fact, he hadn’t even seen it as part of Bernini’s fountain. He thought it was a living thing, another one of the city’s creatures perched on a high tower.

A pigeon.

Langdon squinted up at the shape, his eyes hazy from the bright mist around him. It was a pigeon, right? He could clearly see the head and beak outlined against some stars. But the bird hadn't moved since Langdon got there, even with the fight happening below. The bird was sitting exactly as it had been when Langdon came into the square. It was perched high on top of the obelisk, calmly looking west.

Langdon stared for a moment, then reached into the fountain and grabbed a handful of coins. He threw the coins up into the air. They clattered against the higher parts of the granite obelisk. The bird didn't move. He tried again. This time, one of the coins hit its target. A soft sound of metal hitting metal rang out in the square.

The darn pigeon was bronze.

You’re looking for an angel, not a pigeon, a voice reminded him. But it was too late. Langdon had made the connection. He realized the bird wasn't a pigeon at all.

It was a dove.

Hardly aware of what he was doing, Langdon splashed toward the middle of the fountain and started climbing up the travertine rock, scrambling over huge arms and heads, pulling himself higher. Halfway to the base of the obelisk, he came out of the mist and could see the bird's head more clearly.

There was no doubt. It was a dove. The bird's dark color was just Rome's pollution darkening the bronze. Then the meaning hit him. He had seen two doves earlier that day at the Pantheon. Two doves together meant nothing. But this dove was alone.

The lone dove is the pagan symbol for the Angel of Peace.

The truth almost lifted Langdon the rest of the way to the obelisk. Bernini had used the pagan symbol for the angel so he could hide it in a pagan fountain. Let angels guide you on your lofty quest. The dove is the angel! Langdon couldn't think of a higher place for the Illuminati's last marker than on top of this obelisk.

The bird was looking west. Langdon tried to see what it saw, but buildings blocked his view. He climbed higher. A quote from St. Gregory of Nyssa popped into his head. As the soul becomes enlightened… it takes the beautiful shape of the dove.

Langdon climbed toward the sky. Toward the dove. He felt like he was flying. He reached the platform where the obelisk stood and couldn't go any higher. But when he looked around, he realized he didn't need to. All of Rome was spread out before him. The view was amazing.

To his left, the bright media lights around St. Peter’s. To his right, the smoking dome of Santa Maria della Vittoria. Straight ahead, Piazza del Popolo in the distance. Below him, the fourth and final point. A huge cross made of obelisks.

Shaking, Langdon looked at the dove above. He turned to face the right direction, then looked down at the skyline.

In a moment, he saw it.

So obvious. So clear. So cleverly simple.

Looking at it now, Langdon couldn't believe the Illuminati hideout had stayed hidden for so long. The city seemed to disappear as he stared at the massive stone building across the river. It was one of the most famous buildings in Rome. It sat on the banks of the Tiber River, next to the Vatican. The building had a clear, geometric design: a round castle, inside a square fort, surrounded by a park shaped like a pentagram.

Soft lights lit up the old stone walls in front of him. On top of the castle was a giant bronze angel. The angel pointed its sword down at the exact center of the castle. And, as if that wasn't enough, the famous Bridge of Angels led straight to the castle's main entrance. It was a grand walkway decorated with twelve tall angels carved by Bernini himself.

Finally, Langdon had one more realization. Bernini's cross of obelisks across the city marked the fort in perfect Illuminati style. The middle part of the cross went directly through the center of the castle's bridge, splitting it in half.

Langdon picked up his tweed coat, holding it away from his wet body. Then he jumped into the stolen car, stomped on the gas with his soggy shoe, and sped off into the night.
