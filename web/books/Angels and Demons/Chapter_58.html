Angels and Demons

Chapter 58

Seven-forty-six and thirty… mark.“ Even speaking into his walkie-talkie, Olivetti’s voice never seemed to get louder than a whisper.

Langdon felt himself sweating in his tweed jacket in the back of the Alpha Romeo, which was running in Piazza de la Concorde, three blocks from the Pantheon. Vittoria sat beside him, looking focused on Olivetti, who was giving his final orders.

“Deployment will be an eight-point circle,” the commander said. “Full coverage around the area, focusing on the entrance. The person we’re after may know what you look like, so you need to be unseen. Use only non-lethal force. We need someone watching the roof. The main target is our priority. The other person is secondary.”

Jesus, Langdon thought, chilled by how easily Olivetti had just said the cardinal was replaceable. Asset secondary.

“I repeat. Non-lethal capture. We need the target alive. Go.” Olivetti turned off his walkie-talkie.

Vittoria looked shocked, almost angry. “Commander, is no one going in?”

Olivetti turned. “Inside?”

“Inside the Pantheon! Where this is supposed to happen?”

“Attento,” Olivetti said, his eyes turning cold. “If someone has told them who my men are, they may recognize us. Your colleague just warned me that this will be our only chance to catch the target. I don’t want to scare anyone away by sending my men inside.”

“But what if the killer is already inside?”

Olivetti checked his watch. “The target was specific. Eight o’clock. We have fifteen minutes.”

“He said he would kill the cardinal at eight o’clock. But what if he already got the victim inside somehow? What if your men see the target leave but don’t know who he is? Someone needs to check the inside is clear.”

“Too risky now.”

“Not if the person going in was hard to recognize.”

“Disguising agents takes time and-”

“I meant me,” Vittoria said.

Langdon turned and stared at her.

Olivetti shook his head. “Absolutely not.”

“He killed my father.”

“Exactly, so he might know who you are.”

“You heard him on the phone. He didn’t know Leonardo Vetra even had a daughter. He definitely doesn’t know what I look like. I could walk in like a tourist. If I see anything strange, I could walk into the square and signal your men to come in.”

“I’m sorry, I can’t allow that.”

“Comandante?” Olivetti’s receiver crackled. “We have a problem at the north point. The fountain is blocking our view. We can’t see the entrance unless we move into the open on the piazza. What do you want us to do? Be blind or exposed?”

Vittoria had clearly had enough. “That’s it. I’m going.” She opened her door and got out.

Olivetti dropped his walkie-talkie and jumped out of the car, moving in front of Vittoria.

Langdon got out too. What is she doing!

Olivetti blocked Vittoria’s path. “Ms. Vetra, your instincts are good, but I can’t let a civilian get involved.”

“Get involved? You can’t see anything. Let me help.”

“I’d love to have someone looking inside, but…”

“But what?” Vittoria asked. “But I’m a woman?”

Olivetti said nothing.

“That better not be what you were going to say, Commander, because you know this is a good idea, and if you let some old-fashioned macho nonsense-”

“Let us do our job.”

“Let me help.”

“Too dangerous. We couldn't talk to you. I can’t let you carry a walkie-talkie, it would give you away.”

Vittoria reached in her shirt pocket and took out her cell phone. “Lots of tourists carry phones.”

Olivetti frowned.

Vittoria turned on the phone and pretended to make a call. “Hi, honey, I’m standing in the Pantheon. You should see this place!” She turned the phone off and glared at Olivetti. “Who is going to know? There’s no risk. Let me be your eyes!” She pointed to the cell phone on Olivetti’s belt. “What’s your number?”

Olivetti did not reply.

The driver had been watching and seemed to have an idea. He got out of the car and spoke quietly to the commander for a few seconds. Olivetti nodded and came back. “Enter this number.” He started reading out numbers.

Vittoria entered the number on her phone.

“Now call it.”

Vittoria dialed. Olivetti’s phone rang on his belt. He answered it. “Go inside, Ms. Vetra, look around, come back out, and tell me what you see.”

Vittoria hung up. “Thank you, sir.”

Langdon suddenly felt worried for her. “Wait,” he said to Olivetti. “You’re sending her in there by herself?"

Vittoria frowned at him. “Robert, I’ll be okay.”

The Swiss Guard driver spoke to Olivetti again.

“It’s dangerous,” Langdon said to Vittoria.

“He’s right,” Olivetti said. “Even my best men work in pairs. My lieutenant just mentioned that it’ll look more real if you both go.”

Both of us? Langdon hesitated. Actually, what I meant -

“Both of you going in together,” Olivetti said, “will look like a couple on vacation. You can also support each other. I feel better about that.”

Vittoria shrugged. “Fine, but we need to be quick.”

Langdon groaned. Good move, tough guy.

Olivetti pointed down the street. “The first street you come to is Via degli Orfani. Turn left. It goes straight to the Pantheon. A two-minute walk at most. I’ll be here, guiding my men and waiting for your call. I want you to have protection.” He took out his pistol. “Do either of you know how to use a gun?”

Langdon’s heart jumped. We don’t need a gun!

Vittoria held out her hand. “I can hit a breaching porpoise from forty meters on a rocking ship.”

“Good.” Olivetti gave her the gun. “You’ll have to hide it.”

Vittoria looked down at her shorts. Then she looked at Langdon.

Oh no you don’t! Langdon thought, but Vittoria was too quick. She opened his jacket and put the weapon into one of his breast pockets. It felt like a rock dropping into his coat, his only comfort being that Diagramma was in the other pocket.

“We look harmless,” Vittoria said. “We’re leaving.” She took Langdon’s arm and started down the street.

The driver called out, “Arm in arm is good. Remember, you’re tourists. Newly married, even. Maybe you should hold hands?”

As they turned the corner Langdon thought he saw Vittoria almost smile.
