Angels and Demons

Chapter 136

The midmorning sky was still heavy with clouds when the Sistine Chapel's chimney sent up its first faint puffs of white smoke. The pearly wisps curled upward and slowly faded away.

Far below, in St. Peter's Square, reporter Gunther Glick watched in quiet thought. The final chapter…

Chinita Macri came up behind him and lifted her camera onto her shoulder. "It's time," she said.

Glick nodded sadly. He turned to her, smoothed his hair, and took a deep breath. My last broadcast, he thought. A small crowd had gathered around them to watch.

"Live in sixty seconds," Macri announced.

Glick glanced over his shoulder at the roof of the Sistine Chapel behind him. "Can you get the smoke?"

Macri patiently nodded. "I know how to frame a shot, Gunther."

Glick felt stupid. Of course she did. Macri's work behind the camera last night had probably won her the Pulitzer. His work, on the other hand… he didn't want to think about it. He was sure the BBC would fire him; no doubt they would have legal problems from many powerful groups… CERN and George Bush among them.

"You look good," Chinita said kindly, looking out from behind her camera now with a hint of worry. "I was wondering if I could offer you..." She hesitated, stopping herself.

“Some advice?”

Macri sighed. “I was only going to say that there’s no need to make a big scene.”

“I know,” he said. “You want it wrapped up simply.”

“The simplest ever. I’m trusting you.”

Glick smiled. A simple wrap-up? Is she crazy? A story like last night’s deserved so much more. A twist. A final surprise. A shocking truth revealed unexpectedly.

Fortunately, Glick had just the thing ready…

“You’re on in… five… four… three…”

As Chinita Macri looked through her camera, she sensed a sly glint in Glick’s eye. I was crazy to let him do this, she thought. What was I thinking?

But it was too late to change her mind. They were on.

“Live from Vatican City,” Glick announced on cue, “this is Gunther Glick reporting.” He stared solemnly at the camera as white smoke rose behind him from the Sistine Chapel. “Ladies and gentlemen, it is now official. Cardinal Saverio Mortati, a seventy-nine-year-old progressive, has just been elected the next Pope of Vatican City. Though an unlikely candidate, Mortati was chosen by an unprecedented unanimous vote by the College of Cardinals.”

As Macri watched him, she began to breathe easier. Glick seemed surprisingly professional today. Even serious. For the first time in his life, Glick actually looked and sounded somewhat like a newsman.

“And as we reported earlier,” Glick added, his voice intensifying perfectly, “the Vatican has yet to offer any statement whatsoever regarding the miraculous events of last night.”

Good. Chinita’s nervousness lessened. So far, so good.

Glick’s expression grew sorrowful now. “And though last night was a night of wonder, it was also a night of tragedy. Four cardinals died in yesterday’s conflict, along with Commander Olivetti and Captain Rocher of the Swiss Guard, both in the line of duty. Other casualties include Leonardo Vetra, the renowned CERN physicist and pioneer of antimatter technology, as well as Maximilian Kohler, the director of CERN, who apparently came to Vatican City to help but reportedly died in the process. No official report has been issued yet on Mr. Kohler’s death, but it is thought he died due to complications from a long-time illness.”

Macri nodded. The report was going perfectly. Just as they discussed.

“And following the explosion in the sky over the Vatican last night, CERN’s antimatter technology has become the hot topic among scientists, sparking excitement and controversy. A statement read by Mr. Kohler’s assistant in Geneva, Sylvie Baudeloque, announced this morning that CERN’s board of directors, though enthusiastic about antimatter’s potential, are stopping all research and licensing until further inquiries into its safety can be examined.”

Excellent, Macri thought. Home stretch.

“Notably absent from our screens tonight,” Glick reported, “is the face of Robert Langdon, the Harvard professor who came to Vatican City yesterday to lend his expertise during this Illuminati crisis. Though originally thought to have died in the antimatter blast, we now have reports that Langdon was spotted in St. Peter’s Square after the explosion. How he got there is still speculation, though a spokesman from Hospital Tiberina claims that Mr. Langdon fell out of the sky into the Tiber River shortly after midnight, was treated, and released.” Glick arched his eyebrows at the camera. “And if that is true… it was indeed a night of miracles.”

Perfect ending! Macri felt herself smiling broadly. Flawless wrap! Now sign off!

But Glick didn't stop there. Instead, he paused and then walked toward the camera, a strange smile on his face. “But before we finish…”

No!

“… I want to invite someone to join me.”

Chinita's hands froze on the camera. A guest? What is he doing? What guest! Finish! But she knew it was too late. Glick was committed.

“The man I'm about to introduce,” Glick said, “is an American… a well-known expert.”

Chinita paused. She held her breath as Glick turned to the small crowd and signaled for his guest to come forward. Macri silently prayed. Please let it be Robert Langdon… and not some crazy conspiracy theorist.

But as Glick’s guest appeared, Macri's heart dropped. It wasn't Robert Langdon. It was a bald man in jeans and a flannel shirt, with a cane and thick glasses. Macri felt fear. Crazy!

“Let me introduce,” Glick announced, “the famous Vatican expert from De Paul University in Chicago: Dr. Joseph Vanek.”

Macri hesitated as the man joined Glick on camera. This wasn't some crazy person; Macri had actually heard of him.

“Dr. Vanek,” Glick said, “you have some very surprising information about last night's meeting.”

“I do indeed,” Vanek said. “After a night of such surprises, it is hard to imagine there are any surprises left… and yet…” He paused.

Glick smiled. “And yet, there is a strange twist to all this.”

Vanek nodded. “Yes. As odd as this will sound, I believe the College of Cardinals unknowingly elected two Popes this weekend.”

Macri almost dropped the camera.

Glick gave a shrewd smile. “Two Popes, you say?”

The scholar nodded. “Yes. I should first say that I have spent my life studying the rules of papal election. Conclave law is very complex, and much of it is now forgotten or seen as outdated. Even the Great Elector probably doesn’t know what I am about to reveal. Nonetheless… according to the old forgotten laws in the Romano Pontifici Eligendo, Numero 63 … voting is not the only way a Pope can be elected. There is another, more divine way. It is called ‘Acclamation by Adoration.’ ” He paused. “And it happened last night.”

Glick gave his guest a riveted look. “Please, go on.”

“As you may recall,” the scholar continued, “last night, when Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca was standing on the roof of the basilica, all of the cardinals below began calling out his name together.”

“Yes, I recall.”

“With that image in mind, allow me to read directly from the old election laws.” The man pulled some papers from his pocket, cleared his throat, and began to read. “ ‘Election by Adoration occurs when… all the cardinals, as if inspired by the Holy Spirit, freely and without planning, unanimously and loudly, call out one person’s name.’ ”

Glick smiled. “So you’re saying that last night, when the cardinals chanted Carlo Ventresca’s name together, they actually elected him Pope?”

“That’s right. Also, the law says that Election by Adoration is more important than the usual rules about who can be elected, and it allows any clergyman—ordained priest, bishop, or cardinal—to be elected. So, as you can see, the camerlegno was perfectly qualified to be elected Pope this way.” Dr. Vanek looked straight at the camera now. “The facts are these… Carlo Ventresca was elected Pope last night. He was Pope for just under seventeen minutes. And if he hadn’t miraculously turned into a pillar of fire, he would now be buried in the Vatican Grottoes with the other Popes.”

“Thank you, doctor.” Glick turned to Macri with a mischievous wink. “Very informative…”
