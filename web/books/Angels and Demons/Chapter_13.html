Angels and Demons

Chapter 13

Langdon stared in confusion at the study before him. “What is this place?” Despite the welcome warmth on his face, he stepped through the door with worry.

Kohler said nothing as he followed Langdon inside.

Langdon looked around the room, not knowing what to think. It had the strangest mix of objects he had ever seen. On the far wall, the main feature was a huge wooden crucifix, which Langdon thought was from fourteenth-century Spain. Above the cross, hanging from the ceiling, was a metal model of the planets orbiting. To the left was an oil painting of the Virgin Mary, and next to that was a plastic-covered periodic table of elements. On the side wall, two more brass crosses were on either side of a poster of Albert Einstein, with his famous quote: GOD DOES NOT PLAY DICE WITH THE UNIVERSE.

Langdon moved into the room, looking around in amazement. A leather Bible sat on Vetra’s desk next to a plastic Bohr model of an atom and a small copy of Michelangelo’s Moses.

This is so strange, Langdon thought. The warmth felt good, but something about the decorations made him feel uneasy. He felt like he was seeing two great ideas fighting… a confusing mix of opposite forces. He looked at the titles on the bookshelf:

The God Particle The Tao of Physics God: The Evidence

One of the bookends had a quote carved into it:

TRUE SCIENCE DISCOVERS GOD WAITING BEHIND EVERY DOOR. -POPE PIUS XII

“Leonardo was a Catholic priest,” Kohler said.

Langdon turned. “A priest? I thought you said he was a physicist.”

“He was both. It’s not unheard of for people to be both scientists and religious. Leonardo da Vinci was one of them. He saw physics as ‘God’s natural law.’ He said God’s handwriting was visible in the natural world. He hoped to use science to prove God’s existence to those who doubted. He thought of himself as a theo-physicist.”

Theo-physicist? Langdon thought it sounded like a contradiction.

“The field of particle physics,” Kohler said, “has recently made some surprising discoveries that have spiritual implications. Leonardo was responsible for many of them.”

Langdon looked at CERN’s director, still trying to take in the strange surroundings. “Spirituality and physics?” Langdon had spent his career studying religious history, and it seemed like science and religion had always been enemies that don't mix.

“Vetra was a leader in particle physics,” Kohler said. “He was starting to combine science and religion, showing that they unexpectedly work together. He called this field New Physics.” Kohler took a book from the shelf and gave it to Langdon.

Langdon looked at the cover. God, Miracles, and the New Physics -by Leonardo Vetra.

“The field is small,” Kohler said, “but it’s offering new answers to old questions about the origin of the universe and the forces that connect us. Leonardo believed his research could lead millions to a more spiritual life. Last year he proved that an energy force unites us all. He showed that we are all physically connected and that a single force moves within us all.”

Langdon felt uneasy. And the power of God shall unite us all. “Did Mr. Vetra actually find a way to prove that particles are connected?”

“Definitive proof. A recent article in Scientific American said New Physics was a more reliable way to find God than religion.”

The comment struck a nerve. Langdon suddenly thought of the antireligious Illuminati. He reluctantly allowed himself to briefly consider the impossible. If the Illuminati were still active, would they have killed Leonardo to stop him from spreading his religious message? Langdon dismissed the thought. Ridiculous! The Illuminati are ancient history! All academics know that!

“Vetra had many enemies in the scientific world,” Kohler continued. “Many scientific purists despised him. Even here at CERN. They felt using physics to support religious ideas was a betrayal of science.”

“But aren’t scientists today a little less worried about the church?”

Kohler grunted in disgust. “Why should we be? The church may not be burning scientists anymore, but if you think they’ve given up control over science, ask yourself why half the schools in your country can’t teach evolution. Ask yourself why the U.S. Christian Coalition is the biggest obstacle to scientific progress in the world. The fight between science and religion continues, Mr. Langdon. It has moved from the battlefields to the boardrooms, but it still continues.”

Langdon knew Kohler was right. Just last week, the Harvard School of Divinity had protested at the Biology Building, objecting to the genetic engineering in the graduate program. The chairman of the Bio Department, famous bird expert Richard Aaronian, defended his program by hanging a huge banner from his office window. The banner showed the Christian “fish” with four little feet—a tribute, Aaronian said, to the African lungfishes’ move onto land. Beneath the fish, instead of “Jesus,” was the word “DARWIN!”

A sharp beep broke the silence, and Langdon looked up. Kohler reached down to the electronics on his wheelchair. He took a beeper out of its holder and read the message.

“Good. That’s Leonardo’s daughter. Ms. Vetra is arriving at the helipad now. We’ll meet her there. I think it’s better she doesn’t come up here and see her father like this.”

Langdon agreed. No child should see that.

“I’ll ask Ms. Vetra to explain the project she and her father worked on… maybe it will explain why he was murdered.”

“You think Vetra’s work is why he was killed?”

"Maybe," Kohler said. "Leonardo told me he was working on something big. That's all he said. He had become very private about the project. He had a private lab and wanted to be alone, which I let him do because he was so smart. His work had been using a lot of electricity lately, but I didn't ask him about it." Kohler turned toward the study door. "But, there's one more thing you need to know before we leave this apartment."

Langdon wasn't sure he wanted to hear it.

"Something was stolen from Vetra by his killer."

"Something?"

"Follow me."

The director moved his wheelchair back into the foggy living room. Langdon followed, unsure of what was coming. Kohler moved to right next to Vetra's body and stopped. He told Langdon to come closer. Hesitantly, Langdon approached, feeling sick from the smell of the victim's frozen urine.

"Look at his face," Kohler said.

Look at his face? Langdon frowned. I thought you said something was stolen.

Hesitantly, Langdon knelt down. He tried to see Vetra's face, but his head was twisted all the way around, his face pressed into the carpet.

Struggling because of his disability, Kohler reached down and carefully twisted Vetra's frozen head. With a loud crack, the corpse's face turned into view, twisted in pain. Kohler held it there for a moment.

“Sweet Jesus!” Langdon cried, stumbling back in horror. Vetra’s face was covered in blood. A single hazel eye stared lifelessly back at him. The other socket was ripped and empty. “They stole his eye? ”
