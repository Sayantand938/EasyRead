Angels and Demons

Chapter 93

Langdon didn't know where he was going. Instinct was his only guide, pushing him away from danger. His elbows and knees burned as he crawled under the pews. Still he struggled on. A voice in his head told him to go left. If you can reach the main aisle, you can run for the exit. He knew it was impossible. There’s a wall of flames blocking the main aisle! His mind searching for options, Langdon crawled blindly on. The footsteps grew closer to his right.

When it happened, Langdon was caught off guard. He thought he had another ten feet of pews before reaching the front of the church. He was wrong. Without warning, the cover above him ended. He froze for a moment, partly exposed at the front of the church. Rising in the alcove to his left, huge from this angle, was the very thing that had brought him here. He had completely forgotten. Bernini’s Ecstasy of St. Teresa stood like a sensual still life… the saint on her back, arching in pleasure, mouth open in a moan, and above her, an angel pointing his spear of fire.

A bullet exploded in the pew above Langdon’s head. He felt his body jolt like a sprinter starting a race. Driven by adrenaline, and barely aware of what he was doing, he was suddenly running, hunched over, head down, racing across the front of the church to his right. As the bullets flew behind him, Langdon dove again, sliding uncontrollably across the marble floor before crashing into the railing of a niche on the right-hand wall.

That's when he saw her. A crumpled figure near the back of the church. Vittoria! Her bare legs were twisted beneath her, but Langdon somehow knew she was breathing. He didn't have time to help her.

Instantly, the killer rounded the pews on the far left of the church and came relentlessly forward. Langdon knew it was over. The killer raised the weapon, and Langdon did the only thing he could. He rolled over the railing into the niche. As he hit the floor on the other side, the marble columns of the railing burst apart in a storm of bullets.

Langdon felt like a trapped animal as he crawled deeper into the semicircular niche. Standing before him, the niche’s only object seemed ironically fitting – a single sarcophagus. Maybe mine , Langdon thought. Even the casket seemed appropriate. It was a scаtola - a small, plain, marble box. Burial on a budget. The casket was raised off the floor on two marble blocks, and Langdon eyed the opening beneath it, wondering if he could slide through.

Footsteps echoed behind him.

With no other choice, Langdon pressed himself to the floor and crawled toward the casket. Grabbing the two marble supports, one in each hand, he pulled like a swimmer, dragging his body into the opening under the tomb. The gun fired.

Along with the loud bang of the gun, Langdon felt something he had never felt before – a bullet passing close to his skin. There was a rush of air, like the snap of a whip, as the bullet just missed him and shattered the marble with a puff of dust. Blood rushing, Langdon pulled his body the rest of the way under the casket. Scrambling across the marble floor, he pulled himself out from under the casket and to the other side.

Dead end.

Langdon was now right up against the back wall of the niche. He knew this small space behind the tomb would be his grave. And soon, he realized, seeing the gun barrel appear in the opening under the sarcophagus. The Hassassin held the gun level, aimed at Langdon’s stomach.

Impossible to miss.

Langdon felt a bit of instinct kick in. He twisted onto his stomach, in line with the casket. Facedown, he put his hands flat on the floor, the glass cut from the archives pinching open with a stab. Ignoring the pain, he pushed. Heaving his body upward in a clumsy push-up, Langdon arched his stomach off the floor just as the gun fired. He felt the shock wave of the bullets as they flew beneath him and smashed the soft travertine behind. Closing his eyes and fighting exhaustion, Langdon hoped the noise would stop.

And then it did.

The gunfire was replaced by the click of an empty gun.

Langdon opened his eyes slowly, afraid even his eyelids would make a sound. Fighting the pain, he held his position, arched like a cat. He didn’t even breathe. His ears ringing, Langdon listened for any sign of the killer leaving. Silence. He thought of Vittoria and wanted to help her.

The sound that followed was deafening. Barely human. A deep, strained roar.

The sarcophagus over Langdon’s head suddenly seemed to lift on its side. Langdon collapsed as hundreds of pounds leaned toward him. Gravity won, and the lid fell first, sliding off the tomb and crashing to the floor beside him. The casket came next, rolling off its supports and toppling upside down toward Langdon.

As the box rolled, Langdon knew he would either be buried in the space beneath it or crushed by one of the edges. Pulling in his legs and head, Langdon made himself as small as possible and yanked his arms to his sides. Then he closed his eyes and waited for the sickening crush.

When it came, the whole floor shook beneath him. The upper edge landed just millimeters from the top of his head, rattling his teeth. His right arm, which Langdon was sure would be crushed, miraculously still felt fine. He opened his eyes to see a beam of light. The right edge of the casket had not fallen completely to the floor and was still partly propped up. Directly above, though, Langdon found himself staring right into the face of death.

The original occupant of the tomb was hanging above him, having stuck, as decaying bodies often do, to the bottom of the casket. The skeleton hung for a moment, like a hesitant lover, and then with a sticky crack, it gave in to gravity and fell away. The corpse rushed down to embrace him, raining rotten bones and dust into Langdon’s eyes and mouth.

Before Langdon could react, a blind arm was sliding through the opening under the casket, searching through the corpse like a hungry python. It groped until it found Langdon’s neck and clamped down. Langdon tried to fight back against the iron grip now crushing his throat, but he found his left sleeve caught under the edge of the coffin. He had only one arm free, and the fight was a losing one.

Langdon’s legs bent in the only space he had, his feet searching for the casket floor above him. He found it. Coiling, he planted his feet. Then, as the hand around his neck squeezed tighter, Langdon closed his eyes and pushed his legs out like a ram. The casket shifted, just slightly, but enough.

With a rough grinding sound, the sarcophagus slid off the supports and landed on the floor. The casket edge crashed onto the killer’s arm, and there was a muffled scream of pain. The hand released Langdon’s neck, twisting and jerking away into the dark. When the killer finally pulled his arm free, the casket fell with a final thud against the flat marble floor.

Complete darkness. Again.

And silence.

There was no angry banging outside the overturned sarcophagus. No trying to get in. Nothing. As Langdon lay in the dark among a pile of bones, he fought the closing darkness and turned his thoughts to her.

Vittoria. Are you alive?

If Langdon had known the truth—the horror Vittoria would soon wake up to—he would have wished she were dead for her own sake.
