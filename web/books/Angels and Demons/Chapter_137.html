Angels and Demons

Chapter 137

High on the steps of the Roman Coliseum, Vittoria laughed and called down to him. “Robert, hurry! I knew I should have married someone younger!” Her smile was like magic.

He struggled to keep up, but his legs felt heavy as stone. “Wait,” he pleaded. “Please…”

His head was pounding.

Robert Langdon woke up suddenly.

Darkness.

He lay still for a long time in the soft, unfamiliar bed, unable to figure out where he was. The pillows were filled with goose down, big and comfortable. The air smelled of flower petals. Across the room, two glass doors opened to a fancy balcony, where a light breeze moved under a shiny, cloud-filled moon. Langdon tried to remember how he had gotten here… and where here was.

Strange bits of memory floated back into his mind…

A fire burning like magic… an angel appearing from the crowd… her gentle hand taking his and leading him into the night… guiding his tired, hurt body through the streets… leading him here… to this room… helping him, half-asleep, into a very hot shower… leading him to this bed… and watching him as he fell into a deep sleep.

In the dim light, Langdon could see another bed. The sheets were messy, but the bed was empty. From one of the rooms next door, he could hear the quiet sound of a shower running.

Looking at Vittoria’s bed, he noticed a fancy stitched logo on her pillowcase: HOTEL BERNINI. Langdon smiled. Vittoria had good taste. Old-fashioned luxury overlooking Bernini’s Triton Fountain… the perfect hotel in Rome.

As Langdon lay there, loud banging woke him. Someone was at the door, knocking harder and harder.

Confused, Langdon got up. Nobody knows we’re here, he thought, feeling uneasy. He put on a plush Hotel Bernini robe and walked to the suite’s front door. After a moment, he opened the heavy oak door.

A strong man in bright purple and yellow clothes stared down at him. “I am Lieutenant Chartrand,” the man said. “Vatican Swiss Guard.”

Langdon knew exactly who he was. “How… how did you find us?”

“I saw you leave the square last night. I followed you. I’m glad you’re still here.”

Langdon suddenly felt worried, wondering if the cardinals had sent Chartrand to take them back to Vatican City. They were the only ones besides the cardinals who knew the truth. That made them a risk.

“His Holiness asked me to give you this,” Chartrand said, handing over an envelope sealed with the Vatican symbol. Langdon opened it and read the note:

Mr. Langdon and Ms. Vetra,

I truly want to ask you to keep the events of the last day secret, but I can’t ask any more of you. I will step back and hope you do what feels right. The world seems better today… maybe the questions are more powerful than the answers.

My door is always open,

His Holiness, Saverio Mortati

Langdon read the message twice. The College of Cardinals had clearly chosen a kind and generous leader.

Before Langdon could speak, Chartrand held out a small package. “A thank-you gift from His Holiness.”

Langdon took the package. It was heavy, wrapped in brown paper.

“By his order,” Chartrand said, “this object is yours to keep as long as you live, borrowed from the sacred Papal Vault. His Holiness only asks that you make sure it is returned there when you die.”

Langdon opened the package and was speechless. It was the brand. The Illuminati Diamond.

Chartrand smiled. “Peace be with you.” He turned to leave.

“Thank… you,” Langdon said, his hands shaking as he held the valuable gift.

The guard paused in the hall. “Mr. Langdon, can I ask you something?”

“Of course.”

“My fellow guards and I are curious. Those last few minutes… what happened in the helicopter?”

Langdon felt anxious. He knew this was coming—the moment of truth. He and Vittoria had discussed it last night when they slipped away from St. Peter’s Square. And they had decided. Even before the Pope’s note.

Vittoria’s father had hoped his antimatter discovery would spark a spiritual awakening. Last night’s events were not what he wanted, but the fact remained… people worldwide were thinking about God in new ways. Langdon and Vittoria didn't know how long this would last, but they knew they couldn't ruin it with scandal and doubt. The Lord works in mysterious ways, Langdon thought, wondering if yesterday had been God’s will.

“Mr. Langdon?” Chartrand repeated. “About the helicopter?”

Langdon gave a sad smile. “Yes, I know…” He spoke from the heart, not his head. “Maybe it was the shock of the fall… but my memory… it’s all a blur…”

Chartrand seemed defeated. “You remember nothing?”

Langdon sighed. “I think it will always be a mystery.”

When Robert Langdon returned to the bedroom, what he saw stopped him. Vittoria stood on the balcony, looking at him. She looked like an angel… a bright figure with the moon behind her. She could have been a Roman goddess, in her white robe, tied tight, showing off her curves. Behind her, a light mist hung like a halo over Bernini’s Triton Fountain.

Langdon felt drawn to her… more than any woman he had known. He quietly put the Illuminati Diamond and the Pope’s letter on his bedside table. He would explain it all later. He went to her on the balcony.

Vittoria looked happy to see him. “You’re awake,” she said, in a playful whisper. “ Finally. ”

Langdon smiled. “Long day.”

She ran a hand through her thick hair, the neck of her robe falling open slightly. “And now… I guess you want your reward.”

The comment surprised Langdon. “I’m… sorry?”

“We’re adults, Robert. You can admit it. You feel a desire. I see it in your eyes. A deep, physical hunger.” She smiled. “I feel it too. And that craving is about to be satisfied.”

“It is?” He felt brave and took a step toward her.

“ Completely. ” She held up a room-service menu. “I ordered everything they’ve got.”

The feast was amazing. They ate together by moonlight… sitting on their balcony… enjoying frisйe, truffles, and risotto. They drank Dolcetto wine and talked late into the night.

Langdon didn't need to be a symbologist to see the signals Vittoria was giving him. During dessert of boysenberry cream with savoiardi and steaming Romcaffй, Vittoria pressed her bare legs against his beneath the table and gave him a seductive look. She seemed to be urging him to put down his fork and carry her off in his arms.

But Langdon did nothing. He remained the perfect gentleman. Two can play at this game, he thought, hiding a mischievous smile.

When all the food was eaten, Langdon went to the edge of his bed and sat alone, turning the Illuminati Diamond over and over in his hands, repeatedly commenting on its perfect symmetry. Vittoria stared at him, her confusion growing into obvious frustration.

“You find that ambigram very interesting, don’t you?” she asked.

Langdon nodded. “Fascinating.”

“Would you say it’s the most interesting thing in this room?”

Langdon scratched his head, pretending to think about it. “Well, there is one thing that interests me more.”

She smiled and took a step toward him. “Which is?”

“How you disproved that Einstein theory using tuna fish.”

Vittoria threw up her hands. “ Dio mмo! Enough with the tuna fish! Don’t tease me, I’m warning you.”

Langdon grinned. “Maybe for your next experiment, you could study flounders and prove the earth is flat.”

Vittoria was fuming now, but the first hints of an annoyed smile appeared on her lips. “For your information, professor, my next experiment will make scientific history. I plan to prove neutrinos have mass.”

“Neutrinos have mass? ” Langdon looked shocked. “I didn’t even know they were Catholic!”

In one quick move, she pinned him. “I hope you believe in life after death, Robert Langdon.” Vittoria laughed, straddling him, holding him down, her eyes sparkling with mischief.

“Actually,” he gasped, laughing more, “I’ve always struggled to imagine anything after this world.”

“Really? Never had a religious experience? A perfect moment of pure joy?”

Langdon shook his head. “No, and I doubt I’m the type to have one.”

Vittoria took off her robe. “You’ve never slept with a yoga master, have you?”
