Angels and Demons

Chapter 70

Gunther Glick and Chinita Macri sat in the BBC van, parked in the shadows at the far end of Piazza del Popolo. They'd arrived soon after the four Alpha Romeos, just in time to see an unbelievable series of events. Chinita still didn't know what it meant, but she'd made sure the camera was recording.

As soon as they'd arrived, Chinita and Glick had seen a huge number of young men get out of the Alpha Romeos and surround the church. Some had weapons out. One of them, a formal older man, led a group up the front steps. The soldiers pulled out guns and shot off the locks on the front doors. Macri heard nothing and guessed they had silencers. Then the soldiers went inside.

Chinita had suggested they stay put and film from the shadows. After all, guns were guns, and they had a good view of what was happening from the van. Glick hadn't argued. Now, across the piazza, men moved in and out of the church. They shouted to each other. Chinita adjusted her camera to follow a group as they searched the area. All of them, though in plain clothes, seemed to move like soldiers. "Who do you think they are?" she asked.

"I have no idea." Glick looked fascinated. "Are you getting all this?"

"Every bit of it."

Glick sounded pleased. "Still think we should go back to Pope-Watch?"

Chinita wasn't sure what to say. Something was clearly happening, but she'd been in journalism long enough to know that interesting events often had boring explanations. "This might be nothing," she said. "These guys might have gotten the same tip you got and are just checking it out. It could be a false alarm."

Glick grabbed her arm. "Over there! Focus." He pointed back to the church.

Chinita swung the camera back to the top of the stairs. "Well, well," she said, focusing on the man now coming out of the church.

“Who’s the fancy dresser?”

Chinita moved closer. “Never seen him before.” She focused on the man’s face and smiled. “But I wouldn’t mind seeing him again.”

Robert Langdon hurried down the stairs outside the church and into the center of the piazza. It was getting dark, the spring sun setting late in southern Rome. The sun had dipped below the nearby buildings, and shadows crossed the square.

“Okay, Bernini,” he said to himself. “Where is your angel pointing?”

He turned and studied the way the church was facing. He imagined the Chigi Chapel inside, and the angel sculpture inside that. He immediately turned west, toward the bright sunset. Time was running out.

“Southwest,” he said, frowning at the shops and apartments blocking his view. “The next sign is out there.”

Langdon tried to remember everything he knew about Italian art history. He knew Bernini’s work well, but the sculptor had done so much that no one could know it all. Still, because the first sign-Habakkuk and the Angel -was so famous, Langdon hoped he would recognize the second one.

Earth, Air, Fire, Water , he thought. Earth they had found-inside the Chapel of the Earth-Habakkuk, the prophet who predicted the earth’s destruction.

Air is next. Langdon told himself to think. A Bernini sculpture about Air! He couldn't think of anything. But he felt excited. I’m on the path of Illumination! It’s still there!

Looking southwest, Langdon tried to see a spire or cathedral tower above the buildings. He saw nothing. He needed a map. If they could find out what churches were southwest of here, maybe one would remind Langdon. Air , he repeated. Air. Bernini. Sculpture. Air. Think!

Langdon turned and walked back up the cathedral stairs. Vittoria and Olivetti were waiting for him under the scaffolding.

“Southwest,” Langdon said, out of breath. “The next church is southwest of here.”

Olivetti’s voice was cold. “Are you sure this time?”

Langdon didn’t react. “We need a map. One that shows all the churches in Rome.”

The commander looked at him for a moment, his face unchanging.

Langdon checked his watch. “We only have half an hour.”

Olivetti walked past Langdon down the stairs toward his car, parked right in front of the cathedral. Langdon hoped he was going to get a map.

Vittoria looked eager. “So the angel’s pointing southwest? No idea which churches are southwest?”

“I can’t see past the buildings.” Langdon turned and looked at the square again. “And I don’t know Rome’s churches well enou-” He stopped.

Vittoria looked surprised. “What?”

Langdon looked out at the piazza again. Having climbed the church stairs, he was now higher, and his view was better. He still couldn’t see anything, but he knew he was going the right way. His eyes went up the rickety tower of scaffolding above him. It rose six stories, almost to the top of the church’s rose window, much higher than the other buildings in the square. He knew right away where he was headed.

Across the square, Chinita Macri and Gunther Glick were glued to the windshield of the BBC van.

“You getting this?” Gunther asked.

Macri zoomed in on the man now climbing the scaffolding. “He’s a little too dressed up to be playing Spiderman if you ask me.”

“And who’s Ms. Spidey?”

Chinita glanced at the attractive woman below the scaffolding. “I bet you’d like to know.”

“Think I should call the office?”

“Not yet. Let’s watch. Better to have something ready before we admit we left the conclave.”

“You think somebody really killed one of the old guys in there?”

Chinita clucked. “You’re definitely going to hell.”

"And I'm taking the Pulitzer with me."
