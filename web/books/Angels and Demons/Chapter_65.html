Angels and Demons

Chapter 65

The inside of Santa Maria del Popolo was a dark cave in the fading light. It looked more like an unfinished subway station than a cathedral. The main area was full of torn flooring, brick stacks, piles of dirt, wheelbarrows, and even a rusty backhoe. Huge columns rose up, holding up a high, curved roof. Dust floated in the air in the soft light from the stained glass. Langdon stood with Vittoria under a large Pinturicchio fresco and looked around the damaged church.

Nothing moved. Complete silence.

Vittoria held the gun out in front of her with both hands. Langdon checked his watch: 8:04 P.M. We’re crazy to be in here, he thought. It’s too dangerous. Still, he knew if the killer was inside, he could leave through any door, making it pointless to guard the outside with only one gun. Catching him inside was the only way… if he was even still here. Langdon felt guilty about the mistake that had cost them their chance at the Pantheon. He couldn't insist on being careful now; he was the one who had gotten them into this situation.

Vittoria looked worried as she scanned the church. “So,” she whispered. “Where is this Chigi Chapel?”

Langdon looked through the dim, ghostly light toward the back of the cathedral and studied the outer walls. Unlike what many people think, Renaissance cathedrals always had many chapels, with huge cathedrals like Notre Dame having dozens. Chapels were less like rooms and more like spaces - curved areas holding tombs around the church’s outer wall.

This is bad, Langdon thought, seeing the four spaces on each side wall. There were eight chapels in all. Although eight wasn't a huge number, all eight openings were covered with large sheets of clear plastic because of the construction. The see-through covers seemed meant to keep dust off the tombs inside the spaces.

“It could be any of those covered spaces,” Langdon said. “No way to know which is the Chigi without looking inside each one. Maybe we should wait for Oliv-”

“Which is the secondary left apse?” she asked.

Langdon looked at her, surprised that she knew the architectural term. “Secondary left apse?”

Vittoria pointed at the wall behind him. A decorative tile was set into the stone. It had the same symbol they had seen outside—a pyramid under a shining star. The dirty plaque next to it read:

COAT OF ARMS OF ALEXANDER CHIGI

WHOSE TOMB IS LOCATED IN THE

SECONDARY LEFT APSE OF THIS CATHEDRAL

Langdon nodded. Chigi’s coat of arms was a pyramid and star? He wondered if the rich Chigi had been an Illuminatus. He nodded to Vittoria. “Nice work, Nancy Drew.”

“What?”

“Never mind. I-”

A piece of metal clattered to the floor nearby. The clang echoed through the church. Langdon pulled Vittoria behind a pillar as she aimed the gun toward the sound and held it there. Silence. They waited. Again there was sound, this time a rustling. Langdon held his breath. I never should have let us come in here! The sound moved closer, a broken scuffling, like a limping man. Suddenly, an object came into view around the base of the pillar.

“Figlio di puttana!” Vittoria cursed quietly, jumping back. Langdon fell back with her.

Beside the pillar, dragging a half-eaten sandwich in paper, was a huge rat. It stopped when it saw them, staring a long moment down the barrel of Vittoria’s weapon, and then, unmoved, kept dragging its prize into the church.

“Son of a…” Langdon gasped, his heart racing.

Vittoria lowered the gun, quickly calming herself. Langdon peeked around the column to see a workman’s lunchbox on the floor, apparently knocked off a sawhorse by the clever rodent.

Langdon looked around the basilica for movement and whispered, “If he’s here, he definitely heard that. You sure you don’t want to wait for Olivetti?”

“Secondary left apse,” Vittoria repeated. “Where is it?”

Reluctantly Langdon turned and tried to get his bearings. Cathedral terms were like stage directions—totally confusing. He faced the main altar. Stage center. Then he pointed with his thumb backward over his shoulder.

They both turned and looked where he was pointing.

It seemed the Chigi Chapel was in the third of four alcoves to their right. The good news was they were on the correct side of the church. The bad news was they were at the wrong end. They would have to cross the entire cathedral, passing three other chapels, each covered with see-through plastic.

“Wait,” Langdon said. “I’ll go first.”

“Forget it.”

“I’m the one who messed up at the Pantheon.”

She turned. “But I’m the one with the gun.”

In her eyes Langdon could see what she was really thinking… I’m the one who lost my father. I’m the one who helped build a weapon of mass destruction. This guy’s kneecaps are mine …

Langdon sensed it was pointless and let her go. He moved beside her, cautiously, down the east side of the basilica. As they passed the first shrouded alcove, Langdon felt tense, like a contestant on some strange game show. I’ll take curtain number three , he thought.

The church was quiet, the thick stone walls blocking out all hints of the outside world. As they hurried past one chapel after the other, pale humanoid forms flickered like ghosts behind the rustling plastic. Carved marble , Langdon told himself, hoping he was right. It was 8:06 P.M. Had the killer been on time and slipped out before Langdon and Vittoria had entered? Or was he still here? Langdon was unsure which situation he wanted more.

They passed the second apse, ominous in the slowly darkening cathedral. Night seemed to be falling quickly now, made worse by the musty tint of the stained-glass windows. As they pressed on, the plastic curtain beside them billowed suddenly, as if caught in a breeze. Langdon wondered if someone somewhere had opened a door.

Vittoria slowed as the third niche loomed before them. She held the gun before her, motioning with her head to the stele beside the apse. Carved in the granite block were two words:

CAPELLA CHIGI

Langdon nodded. Without a sound they moved to the corner of the opening, positioning themselves behind a wide pillar. Vittoria leveled the gun around a corner at the plastic. Then she signaled for Langdon to pull back the shroud.

A good time to start praying , he thought. Reluctantly, he reached over her shoulder. As carefully as possible, he began to pull the plastic aside. It moved an inch and then crinkled loudly. They both froze. Silence. After a moment, moving in slow motion, Vittoria leaned forward and peered through the narrow slit. Langdon looked over her shoulder.

For a moment, neither one of them breathed.

“Empty,” Vittoria finally said, lowering the gun. “We’re too late.”

Langdon didn’t hear. He was amazed, transported for a moment to another world. He had never imagined a chapel like this. Made entirely of chestnut marble, the Chigi Chapel was stunning. Langdon quickly took it all in. It was the most earthly chapel he could imagine, almost as if Galileo and the Illuminati had designed it themselves.

Overhead, the domed cupola shone with illuminated stars and the seven planets. Below were the twelve signs of the zodiac—pagan, earthly symbols based on astronomy. The zodiac was also tied to Earth, Air, Fire, Water… the quadrants representing power, intellect, passion, emotion. Earth is for power, Langdon remembered.

Farther down the wall, Langdon saw tributes to the Earth’s four seasons—primavera, estate, autunno, invйrno. But even more incredible were the two huge structures dominating the room. Langdon stared at them in silent wonder. It can’t be, he thought. It just can’t be! But it was. On either side of the chapel, perfectly symmetrical, were two ten-foot-high marble pyramids.

“I don’t see a cardinal,” Vittoria whispered. “Or an assassin.” She pulled aside the plastic and stepped in.

Langdon’s eyes were fixed on the pyramids. What are pyramids doing inside a Christian chapel? And there was more. In the center of each pyramid, set into their front sides, were gold medallions… medallions like few Langdon had ever seen… perfect ellipses. The shiny disks glimmered in the setting sun as it filtered through the cupola. Galileo’s ellipses? Pyramids? A cupola of stars? The room had more Illuminati meaning than any room Langdon could have imagined.

“Robert,” Vittoria blurted, her voice cracking. “Look!”

Langdon turned, reality returning as his eyes dropped to where she was pointing. “Bloody hell!” he shouted, jumping backward.

Staring up at them from the floor was a skeleton—a detailed, marble mosaic showing “death in flight.” The skeleton carried a tablet showing the same pyramid and stars they had seen outside. But it wasn’t the image that chilled Langdon. It was the fact that the mosaic was mounted on a circular stone—a cupermento—that had been lifted out of the floor like a manhole cover and was now sitting beside a dark opening in the floor.

“Demon’s hole,” Langdon gasped. He had been so taken with the ceiling he hadn’t even seen it. He cautiously moved toward the pit. The stench coming up was overwhelming.

Vittoria put a hand over her mouth. “ Che puzzo.”

“Smell,” Langdon said. “Vapors from decaying bone.” He breathed through his sleeve as he leaned out over the hole, peering down. Blackness. “I can’t see a thing.”

“You think anybody’s down there?”

“No way to know.”

Vittoria motioned to the far side of the hole where a rotting, wooden ladder went down into the darkness.

Langdon shook his head. “No way.”

“Maybe there’s a flashlight outside with those tools.” She sounded eager to get away from the smell. “I’ll look.”

“Careful!” Langdon warned. “We don’t know for sure that the Hassassin-”

But Vittoria was already gone.

A strong-willed woman , Langdon thought.

As he turned back to the pit, he felt dizzy from the fumes. Holding his breath, he lowered his head below the edge and looked deep into the darkness. Slowly, as his eyes adjusted, he began to see faint shapes below. The pit seemed to open into a small room. Demon’s hole. He wondered how many generations of Chigis had been carelessly thrown in. Langdon closed his eyes and waited, making his pupils grow so he could see better in the dark. When he opened his eyes again, a pale, dim figure floated below in the darkness. Langdon shivered but fought the urge to pull back. Am I imagining things? Is that a body? The figure disappeared. Langdon closed his eyes again and waited, longer this time, so his eyes would catch the faintest light.

Dizziness began to set in, and his thoughts drifted in the blackness. Just a few more seconds. He wasn’t sure if it was from breathing the fumes or holding his head down, but Langdon was definitely starting to feel sick. When he finally opened his eyes again, the image before him was completely unexplainable.

He was now staring at a tomb lit by an eerie bluish light. A faint hissing sound echoed in his ears. Light flickered on the steep walls of the shaft. Suddenly, a long shadow appeared over him. Surprised, Langdon scrambled up.

“Watch out!” someone yelled behind him.

Before Langdon could turn, he felt a sharp pain on the back of his neck. He spun to see Vittoria pulling a lit blowtorch away from him, the hissing flame casting blue light around the chapel.

Langdon grabbed his neck. “What are you doing?”

“I was giving you some light,” she said. “You backed right into me.”

Langdon glared at the portable blowtorch in her hand.

“It’s the best I could find,” she said. “No flashlights.”

Langdon rubbed his neck. “I didn’t hear you come in.”

Vittoria handed him the torch, wincing again at the crypt's stink. “Think those fumes could catch fire?”

“Let's hope not.”

He took the torch and moved slowly toward the hole. Carefully, he went to the edge and pointed the flame down, lighting the side wall. As he moved the light, his eyes followed the wall down. The crypt was round and about twenty feet wide. Thirty feet down, the light hit the floor. The ground was dark and patchy. Like dirt. Then Langdon saw the body.

He wanted to pull back. “He’s here,” Langdon said, making himself keep looking. The figure was a pale shape against the dirt floor. “I think he’s been stripped naked.” Langdon pictured Leonardo Vetra's naked body.

“Is it one of the cardinals?”

Langdon had no idea, but he couldn’t think who else it would be. He stared down at the pale blob. Still. Dead. And yet … Langdon paused. Something was very strange about how the figure was lying. He seemed to be…

Langdon called out. “Hello?”

“You think he’s alive?”

There was no answer from below.

“He’s not moving,” Langdon said. “But he looks…” No, impossible.

“He looks what?” Vittoria was peering over the edge now, too.

Langdon squinted into the darkness. “He looks like he’s standing.”

Vittoria held her breath and leaned over the edge to get a better look. After a moment, she pulled back. “You’re right. He’s standing! Maybe he’s alive and needs help!” She called into the hole. “Hello?! Can you hear me?”

There was no echo from inside the mossy space. Only silence.

Vittoria headed for the shaky ladder. “I’m going down.”

Langdon caught her arm. “No. It’s dangerous. I’ll go.”

This time Vittoria didn’t argue.
