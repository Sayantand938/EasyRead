Angels and Demons

Chapter 88

Even with its siren on and blaring, Olivetti’s Alpha Romeo seemed unnoticed as it sped across the bridge into old Rome. All the traffic was moving the other way, toward the Vatican, as if the Holy See had suddenly become the most exciting thing in Rome.

Langdon sat in the back, questions racing through his mind. He wondered if they would catch the killer this time, if he would tell them what they needed to know, if it was already too late. How long before the camerlegno told the crowd in St. Peter’s Square they were in danger? The incident in the vault still bothered him. A mistake.

Olivetti never braked as he steered the howling Alpha Romeo toward the Church of Santa Maria della Vittoria. Langdon knew he would have been terrified on any other day. But now, he felt numb. Only the throbbing in his hand reminded him where he was.

Overhead, the siren wailed. Great way to announce our arrival, Langdon thought. Yet they were moving incredibly fast. He guessed Olivetti would turn off the siren as they got closer.

Now with a moment to think, Langdon felt a bit amazed as the news of the Pope’s murder finally hit him. The idea was unbelievable, yet it seemed perfectly logical. Infiltration had always been the Illuminati's way—changing power from within. And Popes had been murdered before. There were many rumors of betrayal, but with no autopsy, none were confirmed. Until recently. Academics had recently gotten permission to X-ray the tomb of Pope Celestine V, who supposedly died at the hands of his ambitious successor, Boniface VIII. The researchers hoped the X-ray might show some sign of foul play—a broken bone perhaps. Incredibly, the X-ray showed a ten-inch nail in the Pope’s skull.

Langdon now recalled news clippings that other Illuminati fans had sent him years ago. At first, he thought they were a joke, so he went to the Harvard microfiche collection to check if the articles were real. Amazingly, they were. He now kept them on his bulletin board as examples of how even respected news organizations sometimes got carried away with Illuminati fears. Suddenly, the media’s suspicions seemed less crazy. Langdon could clearly see the articles in his mind…

THE BRITISH BROADCASTING CORPORATION

June 14, 1998

Pope John Paul I, who died in 1978, was murdered in a plot by the P2 Masonic Lodge… The secret society P2 decided to kill John Paul I when it saw he was going to fire American Archbishop Paul Marcinkus as President of the Vatican Bank. The Bank had been involved in suspicious financial deals with the Masonic Lodge…

THE NEW YORK TIMES

August 24, 1998

Why was John Paul I wearing his everyday shirt in bed when he died? Why was it ripped? And there are more questions. No medical tests were done. Cardinal Villot said no autopsy could be done because Popes never had them. Also, John Paul’s medicines disappeared from his bedside, along with his glasses, slippers, and his will.

LONDON DAILY MAIL

August 27, 1998

… a scheme involving a strong, cruel, and unlawful Masonic group that reached into the Vatican.

Vittoria’s phone rang, helpfully pushing the thoughts from Langdon’s head.

Vittoria answered, looking unsure who was calling. Even from a short distance, Langdon knew the sharp voice on the phone.

“Vittoria? This is Maximilian Kohler. Have you found the antimatter yet?”

“Max? You’re alright?”

“I saw the news. Nothing about CERN or the antimatter. This is good. What’s going on?”

“We haven’t found the canister yet. It’s complicated. Robert Langdon has been a big help. We have a clue about catching the man killing cardinals. Right now we are headed-”

“Ms. Vetra,” Olivetti cut in. “You’ve said too much.”

She covered the phone, looking annoyed. “Commander, this is the president of CERN. Surely he has a right to-”

“He has a right,” Olivetti snapped, “to be here dealing with this. You’re on a cell phone. You’ve said too much.”

Vittoria took a deep breath. “Max?”

“I might have some information for you,” Max said. “About your father… I might know who he told about the antimatter.”

Vittoria’s face darkened. “Max, my father said he told no one.”

“I’m afraid, Vittoria, your father did tell someone. I need to check some security records. I’ll be in touch soon.” The line went dead.

Vittoria looked pale as she put the phone back in her pocket.

“You okay?” Langdon asked.

Vittoria nodded, but her trembling fingers showed she was lying.

“The church is on Piazza Barberini,” Olivetti said, turning off the siren and checking his watch. “We have nine minutes.”

When Langdon first learned the location of the third marker, the name of the church had sounded familiar. Piazza Barberini. Something about the name seemed familiar, but he couldn't remember what it was. Now Langdon knew what it was. The piazza was the site of a controversial subway stop. Twenty years ago, building the subway station had upset art experts who worried that digging under Piazza Barberini might knock over the heavy obelisk in the center. City planners had moved the obelisk and replaced it with a small fountain called the Triton.

In Bernini’s time , Langdon now realized, Piazza Barberini had an obelisk! Any doubts Langdon had about this being the location of the third marker disappeared.

A block from the piazza, Olivetti turned into an alley, drove the car quickly, and stopped with a skid. He took off his suit jacket, rolled up his sleeves, and loaded his gun.

“We can’t risk anyone recognizing you,” he said. “You two were on television. I want you across the piazza, out of sight, watching the front entrance. I’m going in the back.” He took out a familiar pistol and gave it to Langdon. “Just in case.”

Langdon frowned. It was the second time that day he'd been given a gun. He put it in his breast pocket. Then he realized he was still carrying the folio from Diagramma. He couldn’t believe he had forgotten to leave it. He imagined the Vatican Curator being horrified at the thought of this priceless item being carried around Rome like a tourist map. Then Langdon thought of the shattered glass and scattered papers he'd left in the archives. The curator had other problems. If the archives even survive the night …

Olivetti got out of the car and pointed back up the alley. “The piazza is that way. Keep your eyes open and don’t let yourselves be seen.” He tapped the phone on his belt. “Ms. Vetra, let’s test our auto dial again.”

Vittoria took out her phone and pressed the auto dial number she and Olivetti had programmed at the Pantheon. Olivetti’s phone vibrated silently on his belt.

The commander nodded. “Good. If you see anything, let me know.” He readied his weapon. “I’ll be inside waiting. This enemy is mine.”

Just then, very close by, another cell phone rang.

The Hassassin answered. “Talk.”

“It is I,” the voice said. “Janus.”

The Hassassin smiled. “Hello, master.”

“Someone may know where you are. Someone is coming to stop you.”

“They are too late. I have already taken care of things here.”

“Good. Make sure you get away alive. There is more work to do.”

“Anyone who gets in my way will die.”

“Those who get in your way are smart.”

“You speak of an American scholar?”

“You know about him?”

The Hassassin chuckled. “Calm but inexperienced. He spoke to me on the phone earlier. He is with a woman who seems quite different.” The killer felt a stirring of arousal as he recalled the fiery temperament of Leonardo Vetra’s daughter.

There was a moment of silence on the line, the first hesitation the Hassassin had ever sensed from his Illuminati master. Finally, Janus spoke. “Get rid of them if you have to.”

The killer smiled. “It will be done.” He felt a warm anticipation spreading through his body. Although I may keep the woman as a prize.
