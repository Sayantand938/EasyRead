Angels and Demons

Chapter 134

Camerlegno Ventresca’s white robe flowed as he walked down the hallway away from the Sistine Chapel. The Swiss Guards seemed puzzled when he came out of the chapel alone and said he needed a moment to himself. But they did as he asked, letting him go.

Now as he turned the corner and they could no longer see him, the camerlegno felt a storm of emotions unlike anything he thought a person could feel. He had poisoned the man he called “Holy Father,” the man who called him “my son.” The camerlegno had always thought the words “father” and “son” were just religious tradition, but now he knew the awful truth—the words were literal.

Like that terrible night weeks ago, the camerlegno now felt himself spinning wildly through the darkness.

It was raining the morning the Vatican staff pounded on the camerlegno’s door, waking him from a restless sleep. The Pope, they said, was not answering his door or his phone. The clergy were scared. The camerlegno was the only one who could enter the Pope’s rooms without warning.

The camerlegno went in alone to find the Pope, as he was the night before, twisted and dead in his bed. His Holiness’s face looked like the Devil's. His tongue black like death. The Devil himself had been sleeping in the Pope’s bed.

The camerlegno felt no regret. God had spoken.

No one would see the betrayal… not yet. That would come later.

He announced the terrible news—His Holiness was dead from a stroke. Then the camerlegno prepared for the conclave.

Mother Maria’s voice was whispering in his ear. “Never break a promise to God.”

“I hear you, Mother,” he replied. “The world has lost its faith. They need to be guided back to what is right. Horror and Hope. It is the only way.”

“Yes,” she said. “If you don’t do it… who will? Who will lead the church out of darkness?”

Certainly not one of the preferiti. They were old… like walking dead… liberals who would follow the Pope, supporting science in his memory, trying to attract modern followers by giving up the old ways. Old men desperately behind the times, pretending they were not. They would fail, of course. The church’s strength was its tradition, not its changes. The whole world was changing. The church did not need to change, it simply needed to remind the world it still mattered! Evil lives! God will win!

The church needed a leader. Old men do not inspire! Jesus inspired! Young, vibrant, powerful… MIRACULOUS.

“Enjoy your tea,” the camerlegno told the four preferiti, leaving them in the Pope’s private library before the meeting to elect a new pope. “Your guide will be here soon.”

The preferiti thanked him, excited that they had been given a chance to enter the famous Passetto. Very unusual! The camerlegno, before leaving them, had unlocked the door to the Passetto, and right on time, the door had opened, and a foreign-looking priest with a torch had led the excited preferiti in.

The men had never come out.

They will be the Horror. I will be the Hope.

No… I am the horror.

The camerlegno stumbled now through the darkness of St. Peter’s Basilica. Somehow, through the madness and guilt, through the images of his father, through the pain and realization, even through the effect of the morphine… he had found a brilliant clarity. A sense of destiny. I know my purpose, he thought, amazed by how clear it was.

From the beginning, nothing tonight had gone as he planned. Unexpected problems had come up, but the camerlegno had changed his plans, making big adjustments. Still, he never thought tonight would end like this, but now he saw the destined greatness of it.

It could not end any other way.

Oh, the fear he had felt in the Sistine Chapel, wondering if God had left him! Oh, the things He had planned! He had fallen to his knees, full of doubt, listening for God's voice but hearing only silence. He had begged for a sign. Guidance. Direction. Was this God’s will? The church destroyed by scandal and sin? No! God was the one who had wanted the camerlegno to act! Hadn’t He?

Then he had seen it. On the altar. A sign. Divine communication—something normal seen in an amazing way. The crucifix. Simple, wooden. Jesus on the cross. In that moment, it was all clear… the camerlegno was not alone. He would never be alone.

This was His will… His meaning.

God had always asked for big sacrifices from those he loved most. Why had the camerlegno been so slow to understand? Was he too afraid? Too modest? It didn't matter. God had found a way. The camerlegno even understood now why Robert Langdon had been saved. It was to bring the truth. To make this ending happen.

This was the only way to save the church!

The camerlegno felt like he was floating as he went down into the Niche of the Palliums. The rush of morphine seemed constant now, but he knew God was guiding him.

In the distance, he could hear the cardinals shouting in confusion as they ran from the chapel, yelling orders to the Swiss Guard.

But they would never find him. Not in time.

The camerlegno felt drawn… faster… going down the stairs into the sunken area where the ninety-nine oil lamps shone brightly. God was bringing him back to Holy Ground. The camerlegno moved toward the grate covering the hole that led down to the Necropolis. The Necropolis is where this night would end. In the sacred darkness below. He lifted an oil lamp, ready to go down.

But as he moved across the Niche, the camerlegno paused. Something about this felt wrong. How did this serve God? A lonely and silent end? Jesus had suffered where the whole world could see. Surely this could not be God’s will! The camerlegno listened for the voice of his God, but heard only the blurring buzz of drugs.

“Carlo.” It was his mother. “God has plans for you.”

Confused, the camerlegno kept moving.

Then, without warning, God arrived.

The camerlegno stopped, staring. The light of the ninety-nine oil lanterns had thrown the camerlegno’s shadow on the marble wall beside him. Huge and scary. A blurry shape surrounded by golden light. With flames flickering all around him, the camerlegno looked like an angel going up to heaven. He stood a moment, raising his arms, watching his own image. Then he turned, looking back up the stairs.

God’s meaning was clear.

Three minutes had passed in the chaotic hallways outside the Sistine Chapel, and still nobody could find the camerlegno. It was as if the man had disappeared into the night. Mortati was about to demand a full search of Vatican City when a roar of joy burst out in St. Peter’s Square. The crowd was celebrating wildly. The cardinals all looked surprised.

Mortati closed his eyes. “God help us.”

For the second time that evening, the College of Cardinals rushed onto St. Peter’s Square. Langdon and Vittoria were caught in the crowd of cardinals, and they too came out into the night air. The media lights and cameras were all pointed toward the basilica. And there, having just stepped onto the sacred Papal Balcony in the exact center of the towering front of the building, Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca stood with his arms raised to the heavens. Even far away, he looked like pureness itself. A small figure. Dressed in white. Covered in light.

The energy in the square seemed to swell like a rising wave, and suddenly the Swiss Guard barriers broke. The crowd surged toward the basilica in a joyful rush of people. The wave crashed forward—people crying, singing, media cameras flashing. Chaos. As the people poured in around the front of the basilica, the disorder grew, until it seemed unstoppable.

And then something stopped it.

High above, the camerlegno made a tiny movement. He clasped his hands. Then he lowered his head in silent prayer. One by one, then dozens by dozens, then hundreds by hundreds, the people lowered their heads with him.

The square went silent… as if magic had been used.

In his mind, swirling and far away now, the camerlegno’s prayers were a flood of hopes and sorrows… forgive me, Father… Mother… full of grace… you are the church… may you understand this sacrifice of your only son.

Oh, my Jesus… save us from the fires of hell… take all souls to heaven, especially those who need your mercy most…

The camerlegno did not open his eyes to see the crowds below him, the television cameras, the whole world watching. He could feel it in his soul. Even in his pain, the unity of the moment was thrilling. It was as if a connecting web had shot out in all directions around the world. In front of televisions, at home, and in cars, the world prayed as one. Like pulses of a giant heart all beating together, the people reached for God, in dozens of languages, in hundreds of countries. The words they whispered were new and yet as familiar to them as their own voices… old truths… imprinted on the soul.

The harmony felt endless.

As the silence ended, the happy sounds of singing began to rise again.

He knew the moment had arrived.

Most Holy Trinity, I offer You the most precious Body, Blood, Soul… to make up for the insults, disrespect, and lack of concern…

The camerlegno already felt the physical pain starting. It was spreading across his skin like a disease, making him want to scratch at his flesh like he had weeks ago when God had first come to him. Do not forget how much Jesus suffered. He could taste the fumes now in his throat. Not even the morphine could lessen the sting.

My work here is done.

The Horror was his. The Hope was theirs.

In the Niche of the Palliums, the camerlegno had followed God’s will and anointed his body. His hair. His face. His linen robe. His flesh. He was soaking now with the sacred, glassy oils from the lamps. They smelled sweet like his mother, but they burned. His would be a kind rising to heaven. Miraculous and quick. And he would leave behind not scandal… but a new strength and amazement.

He slipped his hand into the pocket of his robe and felt the small, golden lighter he had brought with him from the Pallium incendiario.

He whispered a verse from Judgments. And when the flame went up toward heaven, the angel of the Lord ascended in the flame.

He positioned his thumb.

They were singing in St. Peter’s Square…

The vision the world witnessed no one would ever forget.

High above on the balcony, like a soul breaking free, a bright burst of flame shot up from the camerlegno. The fire leaped upward, covering his whole body at once. He did not scream. He lifted his arms and looked up. The fire roared around him, hiding him completely in a pillar of light. It burned for what felt like forever, as the world watched. The light grew brighter and brighter. Then, slowly, the flames faded. The camerlegno was gone. It was impossible to know if he had fallen behind the railing or vanished. All that remained was a trail of smoke rising into the sky over Vatican City.
