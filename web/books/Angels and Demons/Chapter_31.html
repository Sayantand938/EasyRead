Angels and Demons

Chapter 31

The X-33 space plane shot into the sky and headed south toward Rome. Langdon sat silently on board. The last fifteen minutes had passed in a blur. Now that he had explained the Illuminati and their vow against the Vatican to Vittoria, the gravity of the situation was starting to hit him.

What am I doing? Langdon wondered. I should have gone home when I could! But deep down, he knew he never really had a choice.

Langdon’s good sense had told him to go back to Boston. But his scholarly curiosity had won out over caution. Everything he had ever thought about the end of the Illuminati suddenly seemed like a clever lie. Part of him needed proof, confirmation. There was also the matter of his conscience. With Kohler sick and Vittoria alone, Langdon knew he had a moral duty to be there if his knowledge of the Illuminati could help.

But there was more to it. Langdon was ashamed to admit that his initial horror at hearing where the antimatter was hidden was not just about the danger to human life in Vatican City, but also about something else.

Art.

The world’s largest art collection was now sitting on a time bomb. The Vatican Museum held over 60,000 priceless artworks in 1,407 rooms—Michelangelo, da Vinci, Bernini, Botticelli. Langdon wondered if all the art could be moved in time if necessary. He knew it was impossible. Many of the pieces were sculptures weighing tons. And the greatest treasures were architectural—the Sistine Chapel, St. Peter’s Basilica, Michelangelo’s famous spiral staircase leading to the Musиo Vaticano—priceless examples of human creativity. Langdon wondered how much time was left on the canister.

“Thanks for coming,” Vittoria said quietly.

Langdon snapped out of his thoughts and looked up. Vittoria was sitting across the aisle. Even in the harsh light of the cabin, she seemed calm and composed, almost radiant. Her breathing seemed deeper, as if a will to survive had sparked within her, a desire for justice and revenge fueled by a daughter’s love.

Vittoria hadn’t had time to change from her shorts and sleeveless top, and her bare legs were now covered in goosebumps from the cold plane. Langdon automatically took off his jacket and offered it to her.

“American chivalry?” She agreed, her eyes thanking him silently.

The plane bumped through some rough air, and Langdon felt a rush of danger. The windowless cabin felt small again, and he tried to picture himself in an open field. The thought, he knew, was ironic. He had been in an open field when it happened. Crushing darkness. He pushed the memory away. Ancient history.

Vittoria was watching him. “Do you believe in God, Mr. Langdon?”

The question surprised him. The sincerity in Vittoria’s voice was even more unsettling than the question itself. Do I believe in God? He had hoped for an easier topic to fill the trip.

A spiritual puzzle, Langdon thought. That’s what my friends call me. Though he studied religion for years, Langdon was not a religious man. He respected the power of faith, the kindness of churches, the strength religion gave to so many… and yet, for him, the mental surrender needed to truly “believe” had always been too difficult for his academic mind. “I want to believe,” he heard himself say.

Vittoria’s reply held no judgment or challenge. “So why don’t you?”

He chuckled. “Well, it’s not that simple. Having faith requires taking faith, mentally accepting miracles—virgin births and divine acts. And then there are the rules. The Bible, the Koran, Buddhist writings… they all have similar demands—and similar punishments. They say that if I don’t live by a certain code I will go to hell. I can’t imagine a God who would act that way.”

“I hope you don’t let your students avoid questions that obviously.”

The comment surprised him. “What?”

“Mr. Langdon, I did not ask if you believe what man says about God. I asked if you believed in God. There is a difference. Holy writings are stories… legends and history of man’s search to understand his own need for meaning. I am not asking you to judge literature. I am asking if you believe in God. When you lie out under the stars, do you sense something divine? Do you feel deep down that you are looking at God’s work?”

Langdon paused, thinking.

“I’m being nosy,” Vittoria said.

“No, I just…”

“You must talk about faith in your classes.”

“All the time.”

“And you probably argue the opposite side just to stir things up.”

Langdon smiled. “You must be a teacher too.”

“No, but I learned from the best. My father could argue any point, no matter how strange.”

Langdon laughed, picturing a Mobius Strip – a twisted ring of paper with only one side. He had first seen the shape in M. C. Escher's art. “Can I ask you something, Ms. Vetra?”

“Call me Vittoria. Ms. Vetra makes me feel old.”

He sighed, feeling his age all at once. “Vittoria, I’m Robert.”

“You had a question.”

“Yes. As a scientist and the daughter of a Catholic priest, what do you think of religion?”

Vittoria paused, pushing a strand of hair from her face. “Religion is like language or clothing. We lean towards what we grew up with. But in the end, we’re all saying the same thing: life has meaning, and we’re thankful for the power that created us.”

Langdon was interested. “So you’re saying whether you’re Christian or Muslim just depends on where you were born?”

“Isn’t that clear? Look how religion spread around the world.”

“So faith is random?”

“Not at all. Faith is everywhere. How we understand it is just chance. Some pray to Jesus, some go to Mecca, some study tiny particles. We’re all just looking for truth, something bigger than us.”

Langdon wished his students could speak so plainly. He wished he could. “And God?” he asked. “Do you believe in God?”

Vittoria was quiet for a while. “Science says God must be real. My mind says I’ll never understand God. And my heart says I’m not supposed to.”

That's concise, he thought. “So you believe God is real, but we will never understand Her.”

“Her,” she said with a smile. “Your Native Americans were right.”

Langdon chuckled. “Mother Earth.”

“Gaea. The planet is alive. We are all cells with different jobs. But we are connected. Helping each other. Helping the whole.”

Looking at her, Langdon felt something awaken inside him that had been dormant for years. Her eyes were strikingly clear, her voice pure. He felt drawn to her.

“Robert, may I ask you another question?”

“Robert,” he said. Mr. Langdon makes me feel old. I am old!

“Robert, how did you get involved with the Illuminati?”

Langdon thought back. “Actually, it was money.”

Vittoria looked disappointed. “Money? You mean, as a consultant?”

Langdon laughed, understanding how that sounded. “No. Money as in cash.” He reached into his pocket and took out some money. He found a dollar bill. “I got really interested in the cult when I first learned that American money is full of Illuminati symbols.”

Vittoria’s eyes narrowed, unsure if he was serious.

Langdon gave her the bill. “Look at the back. See the Great Seal on the left?”

Vittoria flipped the dollar bill over. “You mean the pyramid?”

“The pyramid. Do you know why pyramids matter to American history?”

Vittoria shrugged.

“Exactly,” Langdon said. “They don’t matter at all.”

Vittoria frowned. “So why is it the main symbol of the Great Seal?”

“It’s a strange story,” Langdon said. “The pyramid is a hidden symbol for moving up to the ultimate source of light. See what’s above it?”

Vittoria looked closely at the bill. “An eye inside a triangle.”

“It’s called the trinacria. Have you ever seen that eye in a triangle anywhere else?”

Vittoria was silent a moment. “Actually, yes, but I’m not sure…”

“It’s displayed on Masonic lodges around the world.”

“The symbol is Masonic?”

“Actually, no. It’s Illuminati. They called it their ‘shining delta.’ A call for positive change. The eye means the Illuminati can sneak in and watch everything. The shining triangle stands for enlightenment. And the triangle is also the Greek letter delta, which is the math symbol for-”

“Change. Transition.”

Langdon smiled. “I forgot I was talking to a scientist.”

“So you’re saying the U.S. Great Seal is a call for positive, all-seeing change?”

“Some would call it a New World Order.”

Vittoria seemed startled. She glanced down at the bill again. “The writing under the pyramid says Novus … Ordo …”

“Novus Ordo Seculorum,” Langdon said. “It means New Secular Order.”

“Secular as in not religious?”

“Not religious. The phrase clearly states the Illuminati goal, and it also obviously goes against the phrase beside it: In God We Trust.”

Vittoria seemed worried. “But how could all these symbols end up on the world’s most important money?”

“Most experts think it was because of Vice President Henry Wallace. He was a high-ranking Mason and definitely had connections to the Illuminati. No one knows if he was a member or just unknowingly influenced by them. But Wallace was the one who gave the president the design of the Great Seal.”

“How? Why would the president have agreed to-”

“The president was Franklin D. Roosevelt. Wallace just told him Novus Ordo Seculorum meant New Deal.”

Vittoria seemed doubtful. “And Roosevelt didn’t have anyone else check the symbol before telling the Treasury to print it?”

“No need. He and Wallace were very close.”

“Close?”

"Look at your history books," Langdon said with a smile. "Franklin D. Roosevelt was a famous Mason."
