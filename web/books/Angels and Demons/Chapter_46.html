Angels and Demons

Chapter 46

The Secret Vatican Archives are at the far end of the Borgia Courtyard, up a hill from the Gate of Santa Ana. They have over 20,000 books and are said to hold treasures like Leonardo da Vinci’s missing diaries and even unpublished books of the Holy Bible.

Langdon walked quickly up the empty Via della Fondamenta toward the archives, hardly believing he was about to be allowed inside. Vittoria was beside him, easily keeping up. Her almond-scented hair blew gently in the breeze, and Langdon smelled it. He felt his thoughts wandering and pulled himself back.

Vittoria said, “Are you going to tell me what we’re looking for?”

“A small book by someone named Galileo.”

She seemed surprised. “You don’t do things halfway. What’s in it?”

“It’s supposed to have something called il segno.”

“The sign?”

“Sign, clue, signal… it depends on how you translate it.”

“Sign to what?”

Langdon sped up. “A secret place. Galileo’s Illuminati needed to be safe from the Vatican, so they created a super-secret meeting spot here in Rome. They called it The Church of Illumination.”

“Calling a satanic hideout a church is pretty daring.”

Langdon shook his head. “Galileo’s Illuminati were not at all satanic. They were scientists who valued knowledge. Their meeting place was simply where they could safely gather and talk about topics the Vatican banned. We know this secret lair existed, but no one has ever found it.”

“Sounds like the Illuminati know how to keep a secret.”

“Definitely. They never told anyone outside the group where their hideout was. This secrecy kept them safe, but it also made it hard to get new members.”

“They couldn’t grow if they couldn’t advertise,” Vittoria said, her legs and mind moving together perfectly.

“Exactly. News of Galileo’s group started to spread in the 1630s, and scientists from around the world secretly traveled to Rome hoping to join the Illuminati… eager to look through Galileo’s telescope and hear his ideas. But because the Illuminati were so secretive, scientists arriving in Rome never knew where the meetings were or who they could trust. The Illuminati wanted new members, but they couldn't risk revealing their location.”

Vittoria frowned. “Sounds like an impossible situation.”

“Exactly. A catch-22, as we would say.”

“So what did they do?”

They were scientists. They studied the problem and found a solution. A brilliant one, really. The Illuminati made a clever map to guide scientists to their hideout.

Vittoria looked doubtful and slowed down. “A map? Seems risky. If it fell into the wrong hands…”

“It couldn’t,” Langdon said. “There were no copies. It wasn’t a map on paper. It was huge. Like a marked trail across the city.”

Vittoria slowed even more. “Arrows on sidewalks?”

“Kind of, but more hidden. The map was a series of secret symbols in public places. One symbol led to the next, like a trail, leading to the Illuminati's lair.”

Vittoria looked at him sideways. “Sounds like a treasure hunt.”

Langdon chuckled. “In a way, it is. The Illuminati called their symbols ‘The Path of Illumination.’ Anyone who wanted to join had to follow it to the end. It was a test.”

“But if the Vatican wanted to find the Illuminati,” Vittoria said, “couldn’t they just follow the symbols?”

“No. The path was hidden. A puzzle that only certain people could solve to find the Illuminati church. The Illuminati meant it as a way to join, a security measure and a way to make sure only the smartest scientists found them.”

“I don’t believe it. In the 1600s, priests were very educated. If these symbols were in public, surely some Vatican members could have figured it out.”

“Sure,” Langdon said, “if they had known about the markers. But they didn’t. And they never noticed them because the Illuminati designed them so that priests would never suspect what they were. They used a method known in symbology as dissimulation.”

“Camouflage.”

Langdon was impressed. “You know the term.”

“Dissimulacione,” she said. “Nature’s best defense. Try spotting a trumpet fish floating vertically in seagrass.”

“Okay,” Langdon said. “The Illuminati used the same idea. They created markers that blended into the background of ancient Rome. They couldn’t use ambigrams or scientific symbols because it would be too obvious, so they called on an Illuminati artist-the same anonymous genius who had created their ambigram symbol ‘Illuminati’-and they asked him to carve four sculptures.”

“Illuminati sculptures?”

“Yes, sculptures with two rules. First, the sculptures had to look like the other art in Rome… art that the Vatican would never think belonged to the Illuminati.”

“ Religious art.”

Langdon nodded, feeling excited, talking faster now. “And the second rule was that the four sculptures had to have very specific themes. Each piece needed to be a subtle tribute to one of the four elements of science.”

“Four elements?” Vittoria said. “There are over a hundred.”

“Not in the 1600s,” Langdon reminded her. “Early alchemists thought the whole universe was made of only four things: Earth, Air, Fire, and Water.”

Langdon knew the early cross was a common symbol of the four elements—four arms for Earth, Air, Fire, and Water. Besides that, there were many symbolic uses of Earth, Air, Fire, and Water throughout history—the Pythagorean life cycles, the Chinese Hong-Fan, the Jungian ideas of male and female, the Zodiac signs, and even Muslims honored the four elements, calling them “squares, clouds, lightning, and waves.” But Langdon was always chilled by a more modern use—the Mason’s four levels of initiation: Earth, Air, Fire, and Water.

Vittoria looked puzzled. “So this Illuminati artist made four artworks that looked religious but were really about Earth, Air, Fire, and Water?”

“Exactly,” Langdon said, turning onto Via Sentinel toward the archives. “The artworks blended in with all the religious art in Rome. The brotherhood anonymously donated the art to specific churches and used their power to get them placed in chosen churches in Rome. Each artwork was a marker, subtly pointing to the next church, where the next marker waited. It was a trail of clues disguised as religious art. If an Illuminati candidate found the first church and the Earth marker, they could follow it to Air, then Fire, then Water, and finally to the Church of Illumination.”

Vittoria looked confused. “And this helps us catch the Illuminati assassin?”

Langdon smiled, playing his trump card. “Yes. The Illuminati had a special name for these four churches: The Altars of Science.”

Vittoria frowned. “I’m sorry, that doesn’t-” She stopped. “L’altare di scienza?” she exclaimed. “The Illuminati assassin warned that the cardinals would be sacrifices on the altars of science!”

Langdon smiled. “Four cardinals, four churches, the four altars of science.”

She looked shocked. “Are you saying the four churches where the cardinals will be sacrificed are the same four churches that mark the ancient Path of Illumination?”

“I think so, yes.”

“But why would the killer have given us that clue?”

“Why not?” Langdon replied. “Very few historians know about these sculptures. Even fewer believe they exist. And their locations have been secret for four hundred years. The Illuminati probably thought the secret was safe for another five hours. Besides, the Illuminati don’t need their Path of Illumination anymore. Their secret hideout is probably gone anyway. They live in the modern world. They meet in bank offices, clubs, private golf courses. Tonight they want to make their secrets public. This is their moment. Their big reveal.”

Langdon worried the Illuminati reveal would have a special symmetry to it that he hadn't mentioned. The four brands. The killer had said each cardinal would be branded with a different symbol. Proof the ancient legends are true, the killer had said. The legend of the four ambigrammatic brands was as old as the Illuminati itself: earth, air, fire, water-four words crafted in perfect symmetry. Just like the word Illuminati. Each cardinal was to be branded with one of the ancient elements of science. The rumor that the four brands were in English rather than Italian was still debated by historians. English seemed like a strange change from their usual language… and the Illuminati did nothing randomly.

Langdon turned up the brick pathway before the archive building. Horrible images flashed in his mind. The Illuminati plot was starting to show its patient scale. The brotherhood had promised to stay silent as long as it took, gaining enough influence and power that they could reappear without fear, take a stand, fight their cause openly. The Illuminati were no longer about hiding. They were about showing off their power, proving the conspiracy myths were true. Tonight was a global publicity stunt.

Vittoria said, “Here comes our escort.” Langdon looked up to see a Swiss Guard hurrying across a nearby lawn toward the front door.

When the guard saw them, he stopped. He stared at them, as if he were imagining things. Without a word he turned away and pulled out his walkie-talkie. Seeming not to believe what he was being asked to do, the guard spoke quickly to the person on the other end. The angry reply was unclear to Langdon, but its message was obvious. The guard slumped, put away the walkie-talkie, and turned to them with a look of annoyance.

Not a word was spoken as the guard led them into the building. They passed through four steel doors, two passkey entries, down a long stairwell, and into a foyer with two combination keypads. Passing through a high-tech series of electronic gates, they arrived at the end of a long hallway outside a set of wide oak double doors. The guard stopped, looked them over again and, mumbling, walked to a metal box on the wall. He unlocked it, reached inside, and pressed a code. The doors before them buzzed, and the deadbolt opened.

The guard turned, speaking to them for the first time. “The archives are beyond that door. I have been told to escort you this far and return for instructions on another matter.”

“You’re leaving?” Vittoria asked.

“Swiss Guards are not allowed access to the Secret Archives. You are here only because my commander received a direct order from the camerlegno.”

“But how do we get out?”

“One-way security. You will have no problems.” That was all he said. The guard turned and walked away down the hall.

Vittoria said something, but Langdon didn’t hear. He was staring at the double doors, wondering what secrets were hidden inside.
