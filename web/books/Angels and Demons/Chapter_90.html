Angels and Demons

Chapter 90

Langdon and Vittoria watched Piazza Barberini from the shadows of a small alley on the western corner. The church was across from them, its hazy dome rising from a small group of buildings across the square. The night was pleasantly cool, and Langdon was surprised to see the square empty. Above them, televisions blaring from open windows reminded Langdon where everyone was.

“… no comment yet from the Vatican… Illuminati murders of two cardinals… evil presence in Rome… rumors of more spies…”

The news had spread like wildfire. Rome was glued to their screens, and so was the rest of the world. Langdon wondered if they could really stop this out-of-control situation. As he looked around the square and waited, Langdon noticed that despite the modern buildings, the piazza still looked remarkably oval. High above, like a modern tribute to a past hero, a huge neon sign flashed on the roof of a fancy hotel. Vittoria had already shown it to Langdon. The sign seemed strangely fitting.

HOTEL BERNINI

“Five after ten,” Vittoria said, her eyes quickly scanning the square. As soon as she said it, she grabbed Langdon’s arm and pulled him back into the shadows. She pointed to the center of the square.

Langdon looked where she was pointing. When he saw it, he froze.

Two dark figures appeared, walking in front of them under a street lamp. Both were wearing cloaks, their heads covered with dark mantles , the traditional black head covering for Catholic widows. Langdon would have guessed they were women, but he couldn't be sure in the dark. One looked old and moved as if in pain, bent over. The other, bigger and stronger, was helping her.

“Give me the gun,” Vittoria said.

“You can’t just-”

Quick as a cat, Vittoria was in and out of his pocket again. The gun flashed in her hand. Then, in complete silence, as if her feet made no sound on the stones, she circled left in the shadows, moving across the square to approach the couple from behind. Langdon watched, unable to move, as Vittoria disappeared. Then, cursing under his breath, he hurried after her.

The couple was moving slowly, and it only took a short time for Langdon and Vittoria to get behind them, closing in. Vittoria hid the gun beneath her casually crossed arms, out of sight but ready to use quickly. She seemed to move faster as the space between them closed, and Langdon struggled to keep up. When his shoes bumped a stone, sending it sliding, Vittoria glared at him. But the couple didn't seem to hear. They were talking.

At thirty feet, Langdon could start to hear voices. Not words, just quiet murmurs. Beside him, Vittoria moved faster with each step. Her arms loosened, the gun starting to show. Twenty feet. The voices were clearer—one much louder than the other. Angry. Ranting. Langdon thought it was the voice of an old woman. Rough. Like a man's. He tried to hear what she was saying, but another voice cut through the night.

“Mi scusi!” Vittoria’s friendly tone lit up the square.

Langdon tensed as the cloaked couple stopped and turned. Vittoria kept walking toward them, even faster now, about to run into them. They wouldn't have time to react. Langdon realized he had stopped moving. From behind, he saw Vittoria’s arms loosening, her hand coming free, the gun swinging forward. Then, over her shoulder, he saw a face, lit by the street lamp. Panic shot through his legs, and he jumped forward. “Vittoria, no!”

Vittoria, however, seemed to be a step ahead. In a motion that was both fast and casual, Vittoria raised her arms again, the gun disappearing as she hugged herself like a woman in the cold. Langdon stumbled to her side, almost bumping into the cloaked couple in front of them.

“Buona sera,” Vittoria said quickly, her voice sounding surprised.

Langdon sighed in relief. Two elderly women stood before them, frowning from under their cloaks. One was so old she could barely stand. The other was helping her. Both held rosaries. They seemed confused by the sudden interruption.

Vittoria smiled, although she looked shaken. “Dov‘и la chiesa Santa Maria della Vittoria? Where is the Church of-”

The two women pointed together to a large, dark building on a sloping street, in the direction they had come. “И lа.”

“Grazie,” Langdon said, putting his hands on Vittoria’s shoulders and gently pulling her back. He couldn’t believe they’d almost attacked a pair of old ladies.

“Non si puу entrare,” one woman warned. “И chiusa temprano.”

“Closed early?” Vittoria looked surprised. “Perchи?”

Both women explained at once. They sounded annoyed. Langdon understood only parts of the mumbled Italian. It seemed the women had been inside the church fifteen minutes ago praying for the Vatican in its time of need, when some man had appeared and told them the church was closing early.

“Hanno conosciuto l’uomo?” Vittoria demanded, sounding tense. “Did you know the man?”

The women shook their heads. The man was a straniero crudo, they explained, and he had forced everyone inside to leave, even the young priest and janitor, who said they were calling the police. But the intruder had only laughed, telling them to be sure the police brought cameras.

Cameras? Langdon wondered.

The women clucked angrily and called the man a bar-аrabo. Then, grumbling, they continued on their way.

“Bar-аrabo?” Langdon asked Vittoria. “A barbarian?”

Vittoria looked suddenly strained. “Not quite. Bar-аrabo is a derogatory pun. It means Аrabo … Arab.”

Langdon shivered and turned to the church. He noticed something in the stained-glass windows that filled him with dread.

Vittoria, not noticing, took out her phone and speed-dialed. “I’m warning Olivetti.”

Langdon, speechless, touched her arm and pointed, his hand shaking, to the church.

Vittoria gasped.

Inside, glowing like evil eyes through the stained-glass windows… was the growing flash of flames.
