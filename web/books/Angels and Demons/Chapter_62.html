Angels and Demons

Chapter 62

Langdon’s walk around his side of the Pantheon was slowed down by the guide following him, still talking as Langdon got ready to check the last alcove.

“You really seem to like those niches!” the guide said, looking happy. “Did you know that the walls getting thinner is why the dome looks like it weighs nothing?”

Langdon nodded, not hearing anything as he prepared to look at another niche. Suddenly someone grabbed him from behind. It was Vittoria. She was out of breath and pulling at his arm. From the look of fear on her face, Langdon could only think of one thing. She found a body. He felt a wave of dread.

“Ah, your wife!” the guide exclaimed, clearly happy to have another person. He pointed to her short pants and hiking boots. “Now you I can tell are American!”

Vittoria’s eyes narrowed. “I’m Italian.”

The guide’s smile faded. “Oh, dear.”

“Robert,” Vittoria whispered, trying to turn away from the guide. “Galileo’s Diagramma. I need to see it.”

“Diagramma?” the guide said, pushing his way back in. “Wow! You two really know your history! Sadly, that document can’t be seen. It’s being secretly kept in the Vatican Arc-”

“Could you excuse us?” Langdon said. He was confused by Vittoria’s panic. He took her aside and reached in his pocket, carefully taking out the Diagramma paper. “What’s going on?”

“What’s the date on this?” Vittoria asked, looking over the sheet.

The guide was back, staring at the page, mouth open. “That’s not… really…”

“Tourist copy,” Langdon said. “Thank you for your help. Please, my wife and I want to be alone.”

The guide stepped back, still watching the paper.

“Date,” Vittoria repeated to Langdon. “When did Galileo publish…”

Langdon pointed to the Roman numeral at the bottom. “That’s the date it was published. What’s happening?”

Vittoria read the number. “1639?”

“Yes. What’s wrong?”

Vittoria looked worried. “We’re in trouble, Robert. Big trouble. The dates don’t match.”

“What dates don’t match?”

Raphael’s tomb. He wasn’t buried here until 1759. A century after Diagramma was published.”

Langdon stared at her, trying to understand. “No,” he replied. “Raphael died in 1520, long before Diagramma.”

“Yes, but he wasn’t buried here until much later.”

Langdon was lost. “What are you talking about?”

“I just read it. Raphael’s body was moved to the Pantheon in 1758. It was part of a historic tribute to important Italians.”

As the words sank in, Langdon felt like he'd been knocked off balance.

“When that poem was written,” Vittoria declared, “Raphael’s tomb was somewhere else. Back then, the Pantheon had nothing to do with Raphael!”

Langdon could not breathe. “But that… means…”

“Yes! It means we’re in the wrong place!”

Langdon felt himself sway. Impossible… I was certain …

Vittoria ran over and grabbed the docent, pulling him back. “Sir, excuse us. Where was Raphael’s body in the 1600s?”

“Urb… Urbino,” he stammered, now looking confused. “His birthplace.”

“Impossible!” Langdon muttered to himself. “The Illuminati's science spots were here in Rome. I’m sure of it!”

“Illuminati?” The docent gasped, looking again at the document in Langdon’s hand. “Who are you?”

Vittoria took over. “We’re looking for something called Santi’s earthly tomb. In Rome. Can you tell us what that might be?”

The docent looked uneasy. “This was Raphael’s only tomb in Rome.”

Langdon tried to think, but his mind wouldn't work. If Raphael’s tomb wasn’t in Rome in 1655, then what was the poem talking about? Santi’s earthly tomb with demon’s hole? What is it? Think!

“Was there another artist called Santi?” Vittoria asked.

The docent shrugged. “Not that I know of.”

“How about anyone famous at all? Maybe a scientist or a poet or an astronomer named Santi?”

The docent now looked like he wanted to leave. “No, ma’am. The only Santi I’ve ever heard of is Raphael the architect.”

“Architect?” Vittoria said. “I thought he was a painter!”

“He was both, of course. They all were. Michelangelo, da Vinci, Raphael.”

Langdon didn’t know if it was the docent’s words or the fancy tombs that made him realize it, but it didn't matter. The thought came to him. Santi was an architect. From there, his thoughts followed quickly. Renaissance architects lived for only two reasons—to honor God with big churches, and to honor important people with fancy tombs. Santi’s tomb. Could it be? The images came faster now…

da Vinci’s Mona Lisa.

Monet’s Water Lilies.

Michelangelo’s David.

Santi’s tomb on earth …

“Santi designed the tomb,” Langdon said.

Vittoria turned. “What?”

“It’s not about where Raphael is buried, it’s about a tomb he designed.”

“What are you talking about?”

“I misunderstood the clue. We’re not looking for Raphael’s grave, but a tomb Raphael designed for someone else. I can’t believe I missed it. A lot of sculptures in Renaissance and Baroque Rome were for funerals.” Langdon smiled, realizing this. “Raphael must have designed hundreds of tombs!”

Vittoria didn’t look happy. “Hundreds?”

Langdon’s smile disappeared. “Oh.”

“Any of them earthly , professor?”

Langdon suddenly felt unprepared. He knew very little about Raphael’s work. He could have helped with Michelangelo, but Raphael’s work never interested him. Langdon only knew a few of Raphael’s famous tombs, but he wasn’t sure what they looked like.

Seeing Langdon’s confusion, Vittoria turned to the docent, who was trying to move away. She grabbed his arm and pulled him back. “I need a tomb. Designed by Raphael. A tomb that could be called earthly.”

The docent looked worried. “A tomb by Raphael? I don’t know. He designed so many. And you probably mean a chapel by Raphael, not a tomb. Architects always designed the chapels with the tomb.”

Langdon realized he was right.

“Are any of Raphael’s tombs or chapels considered worldly?”

The man shrugged. “I’m sorry. I don’t know what you mean. Worldly really doesn’t describe anything I know of. I should be going.”

Vittoria held his arm and read from the top line of the folio. “From Santi’s worldly tomb with demon’s hole. Does that mean anything to you?”

“Not a thing.”

Langdon looked up suddenly. He had momentarily forgotten the second part of the line. Demon’s hole? “Yes!” he said to the docent. “That’s it! Do any of Raphael’s chapels have an oculus in them?”

The docent shook his head. “To my knowledge the Pantheon is unique.” He paused. “But…”

“But what!” Vittoria and Langdon said together.

Now the docent cocked his head, stepping toward them again. “A demon’s hole?” He muttered to himself and picked at his teeth. “Demon’s hole… that is… buco diаvolo?”

Vittoria nodded. “Literally, yes.”

The docent smiled faintly. “Now there’s a term I haven’t heard in a while. If I’m not mistaken, a buco diаvolo refers to an undercroft.”

“An undercroft?” Langdon asked. “Like a crypt?”

“Yes, but a specific crypt. I think a demon’s hole is an old name for a large burial space in a chapel… under another tomb.”

“An ossuary annex?” Langdon asked, quickly understanding what the man meant.

The docent seemed impressed. “Yes! That’s the term I wanted!”

Langdon thought about it. Ossuary annexes were a cheap church solution to a tricky problem. When churches honored important members with fancy tombs inside, the family members still alive often wanted to be buried together… so they could also have a good burial spot inside the church. But if the church didn't have the space or money to build tombs for the whole family, they sometimes dug an ossuary annex—a hole in the floor near the tomb where they buried the less important family members. The hole was then covered with something like a manhole cover from the Renaissance. Although helpful, the ossuary annex soon became unpopular because of the smell that often rose into the cathedral. Demon's hole, Langdon thought. He had never heard that name. It seemed strangely right.

Langdon’s heart was now beating fast. From Santi’s earthly tomb with demon’s hole. Only one question seemed left to ask. “Did Raphael design any tombs that had one of these demon’s holes?”

The docent scratched his head. “Actually… I can only think of one.”

Only one? Langdon couldn’t have hoped for a better answer.

“Where!” Vittoria almost yelled.

The docent looked at them strangely. “It’s called the Chigi Chapel. Tomb of Agostino Chigi and his brother, rich supporters of the arts and sciences.”

“Sciences?” Langdon said, exchanging looks with Vittoria.

“Where?” Vittoria asked again.

The docent ignored the question, seeming happy again to help. “As for whether the tomb is earthly or not, I don’t know, but it is… let’s say different.”

“Different?” Langdon said. “How?”

“It doesn’t match the architecture. Raphael was just the architect. Another sculptor did the inside decorations. I can’t remember who.”

Langdon was now very interested. The secret Illuminati leader, maybe?

“Whoever did the inside monuments had no taste,” the docent said. “My God! Awful! Who would want to be buried under pyramids?”

Langdon could hardly believe what he was hearing. “Pyramids? The chapel has pyramids?”

“I know,” the docent scoffed. “Terrible, right?”

Vittoria grabbed the docent’s arm. “Sir, where is this Chigi Chapel?”

“About a mile north. In the church of Santa Maria del Popolo.”

Vittoria exhaled. “Thank you. Let’s-”

“Hey,” the docent said, “I just thought of something. I feel so silly.”

Vittoria stopped short. “Please don’t tell me you made a mistake.”

He shook his head. “No, but I should have realized this sooner. The Chigi Chapel wasn't always called the Chigi. It used to be called Capella della Terra.”

“Chapel of the Land?” Langdon asked.

“No,” Vittoria said, heading for the door. “Chapel of the Earth.”

Vittoria Vetra pulled out her cell phone as she rushed into Piazza della Rotunda. “Commander Olivetti,” she said. “This is the wrong place!”

Olivetti sounded confused. “Wrong? What do you mean?”

“The first altar of science is at the Chigi Chapel!”

“Where?” Now Olivetti sounded angry. “But Mr. Langdon said-”

“Santa Maria del Popolo! One mile north. Get your men there now! We’ve got four minutes!”

“But my men are set up here! I can’t possibly-”

“Move!” Vittoria hung up.

Behind her, Langdon came out of the Pantheon, looking confused.

She grabbed his hand and pulled him to the line of taxis waiting by the curb with no drivers in them. She hit the hood of the first car. The sleeping driver jumped up with a surprised yell. Vittoria opened the back door and pushed Langdon inside. Then she jumped in after him.

“Santa Maria del Popolo,” she ordered. “ Quick!”

Looking lost and scared, the driver hit the gas and sped down the street.
