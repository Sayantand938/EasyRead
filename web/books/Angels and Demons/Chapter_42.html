Angels and Demons

Chapter 42

Cardinal Mortati was sweating in his black robe. The Sistine Chapel felt like a sauna, and conclave was starting in twenty minutes. The four missing cardinals hadn't been found. The other cardinals, first confused, were now openly worried.

Mortati couldn't imagine where they were. With the camerlegno maybe? He knew the camerlegno had held the usual private tea for the four favorites earlier, but that was hours ago. Were they sick? Something they ate? Mortati doubted it. Even near death, the favorites would be here. A cardinal rarely gets the chance to be elected Pope, and Vatican Law says he must be inside the Sistine Chapel when the vote happens. Otherwise, he can't be elected.

Though there were four favorites, most cardinals knew who the next Pope would be. For two weeks, faxes and calls had discussed candidates. As usual, four names were chosen as favorites, each meeting the requirements for Pope:

Fluent in Italian, Spanish, and English. No secrets. Between sixty-five and eighty years old.

Usually, one favorite becomes the top choice. Tonight, it was Cardinal Aldo Baggia from Milan. Baggia’s perfect record, language skills, and ability to communicate spirituality made him the clear favorite.

So where is he? Mortati wondered.

Mortati was worried because he was in charge of the conclave. A week ago, the College of Cardinals chose Mortati as The Great Elector - the conclave’s master of ceremonies. The camerlegno was the church’s highest official, but he was just a priest and didn't know the election process well. So, a cardinal was chosen to oversee the ceremony from inside the Sistine Chapel.

Cardinals joked that being The Great Elector was a terrible honor. It made one ineligible to be elected and required days of studying the Universi Dominici Gregis, reviewing the complex rules to ensure a proper election.

Mortati wasn't bitter. He knew he was the best choice. He was the senior cardinal and a confidant of the late Pope, which increased his respect. He was still young enough to be elected, but too old to be a serious candidate. At seventy-nine, he was past the age when the college trusted one’s health for the papacy. A Pope usually worked fourteen-hour days, seven days a week, and died of exhaustion in about 6.3 years. The joke was that becoming Pope was a cardinal’s “fastest way to heaven.”

Many thought Mortati could have been Pope when he was younger if he hadn't been so open-minded. When it came to becoming Pope, it was all about being conservative.

Mortati always found it amusing that the late Pope, may he rest in peace, had become surprisingly liberal once he was in office. Perhaps sensing the modern world moving away from the church, the Pope had tried to reach out, softening the church's stance on science and even donating to some scientific causes. Sadly, it had ruined him politically. Conservative Catholics called the Pope "senile," while scientific purists accused him of trying to spread the church's influence where it didn't belong.

"So where are they?"

Mortati turned.

One of the cardinals was nervously tapping him on the shoulder. "You know where they are, don't you?"

Mortati tried not to look too worried. "Perhaps they're still with the camerlegno."

"At this hour? That's very unusual!" The cardinal frowned suspiciously. "Perhaps the camerlegno lost track of time?"

Mortati doubted it, but he said nothing. He knew that most cardinals didn't like the camerlegno much, feeling he was too young to be so close to the Pope. Mortati suspected much of the cardinals' dislike was jealousy, and he actually admired the young man, secretly praising the late Pope's choice for chamberlain. Mortati saw only strong belief in the camerlegno's eyes, and unlike many of the cardinals, the camerlegno put the church and faith before petty politics. He was truly a man of God.

Throughout his time, the camerlegno's unwavering devotion had become famous. Many believed it was because of the miraculous event in his childhood—an event that would have affected anyone deeply. The miracle and wonder of it, Mortati thought, often wishing he had experienced something similar as a child that would have given him that kind of certain faith.

Unfortunately for the church, Mortati knew the camerlegno would never become Pope later in life. To become Pope, you needed some political ambition, which the young camerlegno seemed to lack. He had turned down the Pope's offers for higher positions many times, saying he preferred to serve the church as a simple man.

“What next?” The cardinal tapped Mortati, waiting.

Mortati looked up. “I’m sorry?”

“They’re late! What should we do!”

“What can we do?” Mortati replied. “We wait. And have faith.”

Looking completely unsatisfied with Mortati’s response, the cardinal retreated back into the shadows.

Mortati stood for a moment, dabbing his forehead and trying to clear his head. Indeed, what should we do? He looked past the altar up to Michelangelo’s famous fresco, “The Last Judgment.” The painting did nothing to ease his worry. It was a terrifying, fifty-foot-tall picture of Jesus Christ dividing mankind into the good and the bad, sending the bad to hell. There was skinned flesh, burning bodies, and even one of Michelangelo’s rivals sitting in hell with donkey ears. Guy de Maupassant had once written that the painting looked like something painted for a carnival wrestling booth by an uneducated coal worker.

Cardinal Mortati had to agree.
