Angels and Demons

Chapter 33

Rome from the air is a maze—a confusing web of old roads twisting around buildings, fountains, and ruins.

The Vatican helicopter stayed low as it moved northwest through the smog caused by the traffic below. Langdon looked down at the scooters, tour buses, and tiny Fiats driving around traffic circles in every direction. Koyaanisqatsi, he thought, remembering the Hopi word for “life out of balance.”

Vittoria sat silently and determined in the seat next to him.

The helicopter turned sharply.

His stomach sinking, Langdon looked farther into the distance. He saw the ruins of the Roman Coliseum. Langdon had always thought the Coliseum was one of history's greatest ironies. Now a respected symbol of human culture, the stadium had been built to host barbaric events for centuries—hungry lions tearing apart prisoners, slaves fighting to the death, gang rapes of women captured from other countries, and public beheadings and castrations. Langdon thought it was ironic, or maybe fitting, that the Coliseum had been the model for Harvard’s Soldier Field—the football stadium where savage traditions were reenacted every fall, with fans screaming for bloodshed as Harvard played Yale.

As the helicopter flew north, Langdon saw the Roman Forum—the center of Rome before Christianity. The crumbling columns looked like fallen gravestones in a cemetery that had somehow survived the city around it.

To the west, the Tiber River curved in large arcs across the city. Even from the air, Langdon could see the water was deep. The swirling currents were brown, filled with silt and foam from the rain.

“Straight ahead,” the pilot said, climbing higher.

Langdon and Vittoria looked out and saw it. Like a mountain rising from the morning fog, the huge dome of St. Peter’s Basilica appeared in the haze before them.

“Now that,” Langdon said to Vittoria, “is something Michelangelo got right.”

Langdon had never seen St. Peter’s from the air. The marble facade shone like fire in the afternoon sun. Covered with 140 statues of saints, martyrs, and angels, the massive building stretched two football fields wide and a staggering six long. The huge interior of the basilica could hold over 60,000 worshipers… over one hundred times the population of Vatican City, the smallest country in the world.

Incredibly, though, not even a stronghold this big could outdo the piazza in front of it. A wide open area of granite, St. Peter’s Square was a huge open space in the crowded city of Rome, like a classical Central Park. In front of the basilica, bordering the large oval space, 284 columns curved outward in four circles of decreasing size… an architectural trick used to make the piazza seem even grander.

As he stared at the magnificent shrine before him, Langdon wondered what St. Peter would think if he were here now. The Saint had suffered a terrible death, crucified upside down on this very spot. Now he rested in the most sacred of tombs, buried five stories down, directly beneath the central dome of the basilica.

“Vatican City,” the pilot said, sounding far from friendly.

Langdon looked out at the towering stone walls that rose ahead—strong defenses surrounding the complex… a surprisingly worldly protection for a spiritual world of secrets, power, and mystery.

“Look!” Vittoria said suddenly, grabbing Langdon’s arm. She pointed quickly downward toward St. Peter’s Square directly beneath them. Langdon put his face to the window and looked.

“Over there,” she said, pointing.

Langdon looked. The back of the piazza looked like a parking lot filled with a dozen or so trailer trucks. Huge satellite dishes pointed upward from the roof of every truck. The dishes were marked with familiar names:

TELEVISOR EUROPEA VIDEO ITALIA BBC UNITED PRESS INTERNATIONAL

Langdon felt suddenly confused, wondering if the news of the antimatter had already gotten out.

Vittoria seemed suddenly tense. “Why is the press here? What’s happening?”

The pilot turned and gave her an odd look over his shoulder. “What’s happening? You don’t know?”

“No,” she snapped, her accent husky and strong.

“Il Conclavo,” he said. “It will be sealed in about an hour. The whole world is watching.”

Il Conclavo.

The word echoed in Langdon’s ears before dropping like a brick to his stomach. Il Conclavo. The Vatican Conclave. How could he have forgotten? It had been in the news lately.

Fifteen days ago, the Pope, after a hugely popular twelve-year reign, had died. Every paper in the world had the story about the Pope’s fatal stroke while sleeping—a sudden and unexpected death many whispered was suspicious. But now, following the sacred tradition, fifteen days after the death of a Pope, the Vatican was holding Il Conclavo—the sacred ceremony in which the 165 cardinals of the world—the most powerful men in Christendom—gathered in Vatican City to elect the new Pope.

Every cardinal on the planet is here today, Langdon thought as the chopper passed over St. Peter’s Basilica. The vast inner world of Vatican City spread out beneath him. The entire power structure of the Roman Catholic Church is sitting on a time bomb.
