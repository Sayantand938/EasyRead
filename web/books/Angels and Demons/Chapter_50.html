Angels and Demons

Chapter 50

Robert Langdon stood outside Archive Vault 9 and read the labels on the stacks.

BRAHE… CLAVIUS… COPERNICUS… KEPLER… NEWTON…

As he read the names again, he felt a sudden uneasiness. Here are the scientists … but where is Galileo?

He turned to Vittoria, who was checking what was in a vault nearby. “I found the right theme, but Galileo’s missing.”

“No he isn’t,” she said, frowning as she pointed to the next vault. “He’s over here. But I hope you brought your reading glasses, because this entire vault is his.”

Langdon ran over. Vittoria was right. Every label in Vault 10 had the same keyword.

IL PROCESO GALILEANO

Langdon let out a low whistle, now realizing why Galileo had his own vault. “The Galileo Affair,” he marveled, looking through the glass at the dark shapes of the stacks. “The longest and most expensive legal case in Vatican history. Fourteen years and six hundred million lire. It’s all here.”

“Just a few legal documents.”

“I guess lawyers haven’t changed much over the centuries.”

“Neither have sharks.”

Langdon walked to a large yellow button on the side of the vault. He pressed it, and overhead lights hummed on inside. The lights were deep red, turning the cube into a glowing red cell… a maze of tall shelves.

“My God,” Vittoria said, looking scared. “Are we tanning or working?”

“Parchment and vellum fade, so vault lighting is always done with dark lights.”

“You could go crazy in here.”

Or worse, Langdon thought, walking toward the vault’s only entrance. “A quick warning. Oxygen causes things to decay, so sealed vaults contain very little of it. It’s partly empty inside. Your breathing will feel difficult.”

“Hey, if old cardinals can survive it.”

True, Langdon thought. Let’s hope we’re as lucky.

The vault entrance was a single electronic revolving door. Langdon saw the usual setup of four buttons inside the door, one in each section. When a button was pressed, the door would start moving and turn halfway before stopping-a normal way to keep the air inside pure.

“After I’m in,” Langdon said, “just press the button and follow me. It’s very dry inside, so your mouth will probably feel dry.”

Langdon stepped into the rotating compartment and pressed the button. The door buzzed loudly and began to turn. Following its motion, Langdon braced himself for the physical shock that always came with entering a sealed vault. Going into a sealed archive was like going from sea level to 20,000 feet instantly. Nausea and dizziness were common. Double vision, double over, he told himself, repeating the archivist’s saying. Langdon felt his ears pop. There was a hiss of air, and the door stopped spinning.

He was in.

Langdon’s first thought was that the air inside was thinner than he expected. The Vatican, it seemed, was very serious about its archives. Langdon fought the urge to gag and relaxed his chest while his lungs adjusted. The tightness went away quickly. Enter the Dolphin, he thought, glad his daily swims were helping. Breathing more easily, he looked around the vault. Despite the clear walls, he felt a familiar unease. I’m in a box, he thought. A blood red box.

The door buzzed behind him, and Langdon turned to watch Vittoria enter. As soon as she was inside, her eyes started watering, and she began to breathe hard.

“Give it a minute,” Langdon said. “If you feel dizzy, bend over.”

“I… feel…” Vittoria gasped, “like I’m… scuba diving… with the wrong… air.”

Langdon waited for her to adjust. He knew she would be okay. Vittoria Vetra was clearly in great shape, unlike the frail old Radcliffe graduates Langdon had once guided through Widener Library’s sealed vault. That tour had ended with Langdon giving mouth-to-mouth to an old woman who’d almost swallowed her dentures.

“Feeling better?” he asked.

Vittoria nodded.

“I rode your damn space plane, so I thought I owed you.”

This brought a smile. “ Touché.”

Langdon reached into the box beside the door and pulled out some white cotton gloves.

“Formal affair?” Vittoria asked.

“Finger acid. We can’t touch the documents without them. You’ll need a pair.”

Vittoria put on some gloves. “How long do we have?”

Langdon checked his Mickey Mouse watch. “It’s just past seven.”

“We have to find this thing within the hour.”

“Actually,” Langdon said, “we don’t have that much time.” He pointed overhead to a filtered duct. “Normally the curator would turn on a system to add oxygen when someone is inside the vault. Not today. In twenty minutes, we’ll both be gasping for air.”

Vittoria visibly paled in the reddish glow.

Langdon smiled and smoothed his gloves. “Prove it or be quiet, Ms. Vetra. Time is running out.”
