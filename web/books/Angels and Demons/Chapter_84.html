Angels and Demons

Chapter 84

The dim lights shone intensely in the archival vault. This vault was much smaller than the one Langdon had been in before. Less air. Less time. He wished he had asked Olivetti to turn on the air fans.

Langdon quickly found the section of items that held the records listing Belle Arti. It was hard to miss. It took up almost eight full shelves. The Catholic church owned millions of individual pieces around the world.

Langdon scanned the shelves looking for Gianlorenzo Bernini. He started his search about halfway down the first shelf, around where he thought the B 's would be. After a moment of panic when he thought the record was missing, he realized, with even more dismay, that the records were not in alphabetical order. Why am I not surprised?

It wasn't until Langdon went back to the beginning of the collection and climbed a rolling ladder to the top shelf that he understood how the vault was organized. High up on the upper shelves, he found the biggest records of all—those belonging to the masters of the Renaissance—Michelangelo, Raphael, da Vinci, Botticelli. Langdon now realized that, fittingly for a vault called "Vatican Assets," the records were arranged by the overall monetary value of each artist's collection. Between Raphael and Michelangelo, Langdon found the record marked Bernini. It was over five inches thick.

Already out of breath and struggling with the heavy book, Langdon climbed down the ladder. Then, like a kid with a comic book, he lay down on the floor and opened the cover.

The book was cloth-bound and very sturdy. The record was handwritten in Italian. Each page listed a single work, with a short description, date, location, cost of materials, and sometimes a rough drawing of the piece. Langdon flipped through the pages… over eight hundred in all. Bernini had been a busy man.

As a young art student, Langdon had wondered how single artists could create so much work in their lifetimes. Later he learned, to his disappointment, that famous artists actually created very little of their own work. They ran studios where they trained young artists to carry out their designs. Sculptors like Bernini created small models in clay and hired others to make them larger in marble. Langdon knew that if Bernini had had to personally complete all of his projects, he would still be working today.

"Index," he said aloud, trying to clear his head. He flipped to the back of the book, intending to look under the letter F for titles containing the word fuтco -fire-but the F 's were not together. Langdon swore quietly. What's with these people and not using alphabetical order?

The entries had apparently been logged in the order they were made, one by one, as Bernini created each new work. Everything was listed by date. Not helpful at all.

As Langdon stared at the list, another discouraging thought came to him. The sculpture he was looking for might not even have the word Fire in its title. The last two sculptures-Habakkuk and the Angel and West Ponente -didn't have the words Earth or Air in their titles.

He spent a minute or two flipping through the book, hoping a picture would catch his eye. But nothing did. He saw many unknown works, and also many familiar ones… Daniel and the Lion, Apollo and Daphne , and several fountains. The fountains made him think ahead. Water. He wondered if the fourth altar of science was a fountain. A fountain seemed like a good symbol for water. Langdon hoped they would catch the killer before he had to think about Water. Bernini had made many fountains in Rome, mostly in front of churches.

Langdon refocused on Fire. Looking through the book, he remembered Vittoria’s words. You knew the first two sculptures… you probably know this one too. He looked at the index again, scanning for titles he knew. Some were familiar, but none stood out. Langdon realized he couldn't finish his search before passing out. Against his better judgment, he decided to take the book out of the vault. It’s just a book, he told himself. I’m not taking an original Galilean paper. Langdon remembered the paper in his pocket and reminded himself to return it before leaving.

Hurrying, he reached down to pick up the book, but something caught his eye. There were many notes in the index, but one seemed strange.

The note said that the famous Bernini sculpture, The Ecstasy of St. Teresa , had been moved from its original spot in the Vatican shortly after it was revealed. This wasn't what caught Langdon’s eye. He already knew about the sculpture’s history. Some thought it was a masterpiece, but Pope Urban VIII thought The Ecstasy of St. Teresa was too sexual for the Vatican. He moved it to a small chapel across town. What caught Langdon’s eye was that it had been placed in one of the five churches on his list. And the note said it had been moved there per suggerimento del artista.

By suggestion of the artist? Langdon was puzzled. It didn't make sense that Bernini would want his masterpiece hidden in some out-of-the-way spot. Artists want their work displayed, not hidden in some remote-

Langdon paused. Unless …

He didn't want to even think about it. Was it possible? Had Bernini made the work so sexual that the Vatican had to hide it in a remote spot? A spot that Bernini himself could suggest? Maybe a remote church in line with West Ponente ‘s breath?

Langdon got excited, but he remembered that the statue had nothing to do with fire. Anyone who had seen it would say it was anything but scientific-pornographic maybe, but not scientific. An English critic had called The Ecstasy of St. Teresa “the most unsuitable thing to put in a Christian Church.” Langdon understood the controversy. The statue showed St. Teresa on her back, seeming to have an orgasm. Not exactly Vatican material.

Langdon quickly flipped to the book’s description of the work. When he saw the sketch, he felt a sudden surge of hope. In the sketch, St. Teresa did seem to be enjoying herself, but there was another figure in the statue that Langdon had forgotten.

An angel.

The dark story suddenly came back…

St. Teresa was a nun made a saint after saying an angel had happily visited her in her sleep. Critics later thought her experience was likely more sexual than holy. At the bottom of the ledger, Langdon saw a well-known piece. St. Teresa’s own words were very clear:

… his large golden spear… full of fire… stabbed me many times… went deep inside me… a pleasure so strong that you would never want it to end.

Langdon smiled. If that’s not a metaphor for serious sex, I don’t know what is. He also smiled at the ledger’s description of the work. Although the paragraph was in Italian, the word fire appeared many times:

… angel’s spear with a tip of fire …

… angel’s head giving off rays of fire …

… woman burning with passion’s fire …

Langdon wasn’t fully sure until he looked up at the sketch again. The angel’s fiery spear was held high, showing the way. Let angels lead you on your grand search. Even the kind of angel Bernini chose seemed important. It’s a seraphim , Langdon realized. Seraphim means "the fiery one."

Robert Langdon had never looked for proof from heaven, but when he read the name of the church where the sculpture now was, he thought he might start to believe.

Santa Maria della Vittoria.

Vittoria, he thought, grinning. Perfect.

Staggering to his feet, Langdon felt a rush of dizziness. He glanced up the ladder, wondering if he should replace the book. The hell with it, he thought. Father Jaqui can do it. He closed the book and left it neatly at the bottom of the shelf.

As he made his way toward the glowing button on the vault’s electronic exit, he was breathing in shallow gasps. Still, he felt renewed by his good luck.

His good luck, however, ran out before he reached the exit.

Without warning, the vault let out a pained sigh. The lights dimmed, and the exit button went dead. Then, like an enormous dying beast, the archive went totally black. Someone had just cut the power.
