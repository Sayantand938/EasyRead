Angels and Demons

Chapter 127

The images around him swam, fading in and out. Langdon’s eyes slowly cleared. His legs ached, and his body felt crushed. He was lying on his side on the ground. Something stunk, like vomit. He still heard the constant sound of water lapping. It didn't sound peaceful anymore. There were other sounds too—talking nearby. He saw blurry white shapes. Were they all wearing white? Langdon figured he was either in an asylum or heaven. From the burning in his throat, Langdon decided it wasn't heaven.

“He’s done vomiting,” one man said in Italian. “Turn him over.” The voice was steady and professional.

Langdon felt hands slowly rolling him onto his back. His head spun. He tried to sit up, but the hands gently pushed him back down. His body gave in. Then Langdon felt someone going through his pockets, taking things out.

Then he passed out completely.

Dr. Jacobus wasn't religious; the science of medicine had taken that away long ago. Yet, the events in Vatican City tonight had challenged his logical thinking. Now bodies are falling from the sky?

Dr. Jacobus felt the pulse of the soaked man they had just pulled from the Tiber River. The doctor thought that God himself had delivered this one to safety. The impact of hitting the water had knocked the man out, and if Jacobus and his crew hadn't been standing on the shore watching the show in the sky, this falling person would have gone unnoticed and drowned.

“An American,” a nurse said, looking through the man’s wallet after they pulled him ashore.

American? Romans often joked that there were so many Americans in Rome that hamburgers should be the official Italian food. But Americans falling from the sky? Jacobus shined a penlight in the man’s eyes, testing his reaction. “Sir? Can you hear me? Do you know where you are?”

The man was unconscious again. Jacobus wasn't surprised. The man had vomited a lot of water after Jacobus had done CPR.

“His name is Robert Langdon,” the nurse said, reading the man’s driver’s license.

The group on the dock all stopped suddenly.

“Impossible!” Jacobus declared. Robert Langdon was the man from the television—the American professor who had been helping the Vatican. Jacobus had seen Mr. Langdon just minutes ago, getting into a helicopter in St. Peter’s Square and flying high into the air. Jacobus and the others had run to the dock to see the antimatter explosion—a huge ball of light unlike anything they had ever seen. How could this be the same man!

“It’s him!” the nurse exclaimed, brushing his wet hair back. “And I recognize his tweed coat!”

Suddenly someone was yelling from the hospital entrance. It was one of the patients. She was screaming, going crazy, holding her radio up to the sky and praising God. Apparently Camerlegno Ventresca had just miraculously appeared on the roof of the Vatican.

Dr. Jacobus decided that when his shift ended at 8 A.M., he was going straight to church.

The lights above Langdon’s head were brighter now, sterile. He was on some kind of examination table. He smelled astringents, strange chemicals. Someone had just given him a shot, and they had taken off his clothes.

Definitely not gypsies, he thought in his half-conscious state. Aliens, maybe? Yes, he had heard about things like this. Luckily these beings would not harm him. All they wanted were his—

“No way!” Langdon sat straight up, eyes wide open.

“Careful!” one of the creatures yelled, steadying him. His badge read Dr. Jacobus. He looked very human.

Langdon stammered, “I… thought…”

“Easy, Mr. Langdon. You’re in a hospital.”

The fog began to lift. Langdon felt a wave of relief. He hated hospitals, but they were definitely better than aliens harvesting his testicles.

“My name is Dr. Jacobus,” the man said. He explained what had just happened. “You are very fortunate to be alive.”

Langdon did not feel fortunate. He could barely understand his own memories… the helicopter… the camerlegno. His body ached all over. They gave him some water, and he rinsed out his mouth. They placed a new gauze on his palm.

“Where are my clothes?” Langdon asked. He was wearing a paper robe.

One of the nurses pointed to a dripping pile of shredded khaki and tweed on the counter. “They were soaked. We had to cut them off you.”

Langdon looked at his shredded Harris tweed and frowned.

“You had some Kleenex in your pocket,” the nurse said.

It was then that Langdon saw the ruined pieces of parchment stuck all over the lining of his jacket. The folio from Galileo’s Diagramma. The last copy on earth had just dissolved. He was too numb to react. He just stared.

“We saved your things.” She held up a plastic bin. “Wallet, camcorder, and pen. I dried the camcorder as much as I could.”

“I don’t have a camcorder.”

The nurse frowned and held out the bin. Langdon looked at what was inside. Along with his wallet and pen was a small Sony RUVI camcorder. He remembered it now. Kohler had given it to him and asked him to give it to the media.

“We found it in your pocket. You’ll probably need a new one, though.” The nurse flipped open the small screen on the back. “The screen is cracked.” Then she looked happier. “The sound still works, though. Just barely.” She held the device to her ear. “It keeps playing something over and over.” She listened for a moment and then frowned, handing it to Langdon. “I think it’s two guys arguing.”

Confused, Langdon took the camcorder and held it to his ear. The voices were tinny and mechanical, but he could make them out. One was close. One was far away. Langdon knew who they both were.

Sitting there in his paper gown, Langdon listened to the conversation in shock. Even though he couldn’t see what was happening, when he heard the shocking end, he was glad he didn’t have to see it.

My God!

As the conversation started playing again, Langdon lowered the camcorder from his ear and sat there, horrified and confused. The antimatter… the helicopter… Langdon’s mind started racing.

But that means…

He felt like throwing up again. Filled with growing confusion and anger, Langdon got off the table and stood on shaky legs.

“Mr. Langdon!” the doctor said, trying to stop him.

“I need some clothes,” Langdon insisted, feeling the draft on his rear from the open-back gown.

“But, you need to rest.”

“I’m leaving. Now. I need some clothes.”

“But, sir, you-”

“Now!”

Everyone looked confused. “We have no clothes,” the doctor said. “Maybe a friend could bring you some tomorrow.”

Langdon took a slow, deep breath and looked the doctor in the eye. “Dr. Jacobus, I am walking out of here right now. I need clothes. I am going to Vatican City. You don’t go to Vatican City with your butt hanging out. Am I clear?”

Dr. Jacobus swallowed hard. “Get this man something to wear.”

When Langdon limped out of Hospital Tiberina, he felt like a giant Cub Scout. He was wearing a blue paramedic’s jumpsuit that zipped up the front and had cloth badges that seemed to show his many skills.

The woman with him was large and wore a similar suit. The doctor had promised Langdon she would get him to the Vatican very quickly.

“Molto traffico,” Langdon said, reminding her that the area around the Vatican was full of cars and people.

The woman seemed calm. She pointed proudly to one of her patches. “Sono conducente di ambulanza.”

“Ambulanza?” That made sense. Langdon felt like he could use an ambulance ride.

The woman led him around the side of the building. On a ledge over the water was a cement platform where her vehicle was waiting. When Langdon saw it, he stopped. It was an old medical helicopter. The side said Aero-Ambulanza.

He lowered his head.

The woman smiled. “Fly Vatican City. Very fast.”
