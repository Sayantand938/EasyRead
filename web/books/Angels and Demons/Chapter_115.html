Angels and Demons

Chapter 115

In St. Peter’s Square, the Swiss Guard pilot sat in the cockpit of the parked Vatican helicopter and rubbed his temples. The noise in the square around him was so loud that it drowned out the sound of his idling rotors. This was no quiet candlelight vigil. He was amazed a riot had not started yet.

With less than twenty-five minutes left until midnight, the people were still crowded together, some praying, some crying for the church, others screaming insults and saying that this was what the church deserved, still others chanting end-of-the-world Bible verses.

The pilot’s head pounded as the media lights reflected off his windshield. He squinted out at the noisy crowds. Banners waved over the crowd.

ANTIMATTER IS THE ANTICHRIST!

SCIENTIST=SATANIST

WHERE IS YOUR GOD NOW?

The pilot groaned, his headache worsening. He almost thought about grabbing the windshield’s vinyl cover and putting it up so he wouldn’t have to watch, but he knew he would be flying in a few minutes. Lieutenant Chartrand had just radioed with bad news. The camerlegno had been attacked by Maximilian Kohler and badly hurt. Chartrand, the American, and the woman were carrying the camerlegno out now so he could be taken to a hospital.

The pilot felt like he was to blame for the attack. He scolded himself for not trusting his instincts. Earlier, when he had picked up Kohler at the airport, he had sensed something in the scientist’s cold eyes. He couldn’t name it, but he didn’t like it. Not that it mattered. Rocher was in charge, and Rocher insisted this was the guy. Rocher had apparently been wrong.

A new burst of noise came from the crowd, and the pilot looked over to see a line of cardinals walking slowly out of the Vatican onto St. Peter’s Square. The cardinals’ relief to be leaving the center of the trouble seemed to quickly change to looks of confusion at the scene now happening outside the church.

The crowd noise grew louder again. The pilot’s head throbbed. He needed an aspirin. Maybe three. He didn’t like flying on medication, but a few aspirin would be better than this terrible headache. He reached for the first-aid kit, kept with maps and manuals in a cargo box bolted between the front seats. But when he tried to open the box, he found it locked. He looked around for the key and then gave up. Tonight was not his night. He went back to rubbing his temples.

Inside the dark church, Langdon, Vittoria, and the two guards hurried toward the main exit. Unable to find anything better, they were carrying the wounded camerlegno on a narrow table, balancing the limp body between them like a stretcher. Outside the doors, the distant roar of the crowd was now audible. The camerlegno was close to losing consciousness.

Time was running out.
