Angels and Demons

Chapter 21

I believe you've heard of antimatter, Mr. Langdon?" Vittoria studied him, her dark skin standing out against the white lab.

Langdon looked up, feeling suddenly foolish. "Yes. Well… kind of.”

A small smile appeared on her face. "You watch Star Trek.”

Langdon blushed. "Well, my students like…” He frowned. “Isn’t antimatter what powers the U.S.S. Enterprise? ”

She nodded. "Good science fiction comes from good science.”

“So antimatter is real?”

“A natural phenomenon. Everything has an opposite. Protons have electrons. Up-quarks have down-quarks. There's a cosmic balance at the subatomic level. Antimatter is yin to matter’s yang. It balances the physical equation.”

Langdon thought of Galileo’s belief in duality.

“Scientists have known since 1918,” Vittoria said, “that two kinds of matter were created in the Big Bang. One kind is what we see here on earth, making up rocks, trees, people. The other is its opposite—exactly like matter except that the charges of its particles are reversed.”

Kohler spoke as though waking from a daze. His voice sounded suddenly fragile. “But there are huge technical challenges to actually storing antimatter. What about neutralization?”

“My father created a vacuum with reversed power to draw the antimatter positrons from the accelerator before they broke down.”

Kohler frowned. “But a vacuum would also draw out the matter. There would be no way to tell the particles apart.”

“He used a magnetic field. Matter curved right, and antimatter curved left. They are opposites.”

Suddenly, Kohler’s doubt seemed to break. He looked at Vittoria, clearly amazed, and then suddenly began to cough. “Unbel… ievable…” he said, wiping his mouth, “and yet…” He still seemed doubtful. “Yet even if the vacuum worked , these canisters are made of matter. Antimatter cannot be stored in canisters made of matter. The antimatter would immediately react with-”

“The sample isn’t touching the canister,” Vittoria said, as if expecting the question. “The antimatter is floating. The canisters are called ‘antimatter traps’ because they trap the antimatter in the center, keeping it safely away from the sides and bottom.”

“Floating? But… how?”

“Between two crossing magnetic fields. Here, take a look.”

Vittoria walked across the room and picked up a large electronic device. It looked to Langdon like a cartoon ray gun—a wide, cannon-like barrel with a scope on top and wires hanging below. Vittoria lined up the scope with a canister, looked into the eyepiece, and adjusted some knobs. Then she stepped back, letting Kohler look.

Kohler looked puzzled. “You gathered visible amounts?”

“Five thousand nanograms,” Vittoria said. “A liquid plasma with millions of positrons.”

“Millions? But a few particles is all anyone has ever detected… anywhere.”

“Xenon,” Vittoria said plainly. “He sped the particle beam through xenon gas, stripping off the electrons. He wouldn't reveal the exact process, but it included injecting raw electrons into the accelerator at the same time.”

Langdon felt confused, wondering if they were still speaking English.

Kohler paused, his brow furrowing. He gasped. He slumped as if shot. “That would technically leave…”

Vittoria nodded. “Yes. A lot of it.”

Kohler looked back at the canister. Hesitantly, he lifted himself in his chair and looked through the viewer, staring inside. He stared silently for a long time. When he finally sat, his forehead was sweaty. The lines on his face were gone. His voice was a whisper. “My God… you actually did it.”

Vittoria nodded. “My father did it.”

“I… I don’t know what to say.”

Vittoria turned to Langdon. “Would you like to look?” She pointed to the viewer.

Unsure what he would find, Langdon moved closer. From just a couple of feet away, the canister looked empty. Whatever was inside was extremely small. Langdon put his eye up to the viewer. It took a second for the image to become clear.

Then he saw it.

The object wasn't at the bottom of the container, like he thought it would be. Instead, it was floating in the middle—hanging in the air—a shiny drop of liquid that looked like mercury. As if by magic, the liquid floated and spun in place. Tiny metallic waves moved across the surface of the droplet. The floating fluid reminded Langdon of a video he once saw of a water droplet in zero gravity. Even though he knew the droplet was microscopic, he could see every changing curve and ripple as the ball of plasma turned slowly in the air.

"It's… floating," he said.

"It needs to be," Vittoria answered. "Antimatter is very unstable. Energy-wise, antimatter is the opposite of matter, so the two destroy each other instantly if they touch. Keeping antimatter away from matter is hard because everything on earth is made of matter. The samples have to be stored without touching anything—not even air."

Langdon was impressed. Now that's working in a vacuum.

"These antimatter traps?" Kohler asked, looking amazed as he touched the base of one with a pale finger. "Your father designed these?"

"Actually," she said, "I did."

Kohler looked up.

Vittoria's voice was calm. "My father made the first particles of antimatter but couldn't figure out how to store them. I suggested these. Airtight shells made of nanocomposites with electromagnets that push against each other at each end."

"Looks like you got your smarts from your father."

"Not really. I got the idea from nature. Portuguese man-of-war use stinging cells to trap fish in their tentacles. Same idea here. Each container has two electromagnets, one on each end. Their opposite magnetic fields meet in the middle of the container and hold the antimatter there, floating in a vacuum."

Langdon looked again at the container. Antimatter floating in a vacuum, not touching anything. Kohler was right. It was brilliant.

"Where does the power for the magnets come from?" Kohler asked.

Vittoria pointed. "In the pillar under the trap. The containers are screwed into a port that constantly recharges them so the magnets never stop working."

"And if the field stops working?"

"The obvious. The antimatter falls, hits the bottom of the trap, and we get annihilation."

Langdon perked up. "Annihilation?" He didn't like the sound of that.

Vittoria seemed calm. "Yes. If antimatter and matter touch, both are instantly destroyed. Physicists call it 'annihilation.' "

Langdon nodded. "Oh."

“It’s nature's simplest reaction. A piece of matter and a piece of antimatter join to release two new pieces—called photons. A photon is like a tiny burst of light.”

Langdon had read about photons—light pieces—the purest energy. He decided not to ask about Captain Kirk using photon torpedoes against the Klingons. “So if the antimatter falls, we see a tiny burst of light?”

Vittoria shrugged. “Depends on what you mean by tiny. Here, let me show you.” She reached for the canister and began to unscrew it from its charging base.

Suddenly, Kohler cried out in fear and jumped forward, pushing her hands away. “Vittoria! Are you crazy!”
