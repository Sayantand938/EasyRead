Angels and Demons

Chapter 23

Kohler stared into the annihilation chamber, utterly amazed by what he'd just witnessed. Robert Langdon stood beside him, even more stunned.

“I want to see my father,” Vittoria insisted. “I showed you the lab. Now I want to see my father.”

Kohler turned slowly, seeming not to hear her. “Why did you wait so long, Vittoria? You and your father should have told me about this discovery right away.”

Vittoria stared at him. How many reasons do you need? “Director, we can argue about this later. Right now, I want to see my father.”

“Do you know what this technology means?”

“Sure,” Vittoria retorted. “Money for CERN. A lot of it. Now I want-”

“Is that why you kept it secret?” Kohler asked, clearly trying to provoke her. “Because you were afraid the board and I would vote to license it?”

“It should be licensed,” Vittoria snapped back, feeling herself drawn into the argument. “Antimatter is important technology. But it’s also dangerous. My father and I wanted time to improve the procedures and make it safe.”

“In other words, you didn’t trust the board of directors to value careful science over making money.”

Vittoria was surprised by Kohler’s indifferent tone. “There were other reasons too,” she said. “My father wanted time to present antimatter in the right way.”

“Meaning?”

What do you think I mean? “Matter from energy? Something from nothing? It’s almost proof that Genesis is scientifically possible.”

“So he didn’t want the religious meaning of his discovery lost in a wave of commercialism?”

“In a way.”

“And you?”

Ironically, Vittoria’s concerns were almost the opposite. Commercialism was key to the success of any new energy source. Although antimatter technology had amazing potential as a clean and efficient energy source, if revealed too soon, it could be destroyed by the politics and PR disasters that had killed nuclear and solar power. Nuclear spread before it was safe, and there were accidents. Solar spread before it was efficient, and people lost money. Both technologies got bad reputations and died out.

“My interests,” Vittoria said, “were a little less grand than uniting science and religion.”

“The environment,” Kohler guessed confidently.

“Endless energy. No strip mining. No pollution. No radiation. Antimatter technology could save the planet.”

“Or destroy it,” Kohler joked. “It depends on who uses it and how.” Vittoria felt a chill coming from Kohler’s twisted body. “Who else knew about this?” he asked.

“No one,” Vittoria said. “I told you that.”

“Then why do you think your father was killed?”

Vittoria’s muscles tensed. “I have no idea. He had enemies at CERN, you know that, but it couldn’t have been about antimatter. We promised each other to keep it secret for a few more months, until we were ready.”

“And you’re sure your father kept his promise to be silent?”

Vittoria was getting angry now. “My father has kept harder promises than that!”

“And you told no one?”

“Of course not!”

Kohler sighed. He paused, as if carefully choosing his next words. “Let’s say someone did find out. And let’s say someone got into this lab. What do you think they would want? Did your father write notes down here? Did he keep records of his work?”

“Director, I’ve been patient. I need answers now. You keep talking about a break-in, but you saw the eye scan. My father was always very careful about secrecy and security.”

“Just tell me,” Kohler snapped, startling her. “What would be gone?”

“I have no idea.” Vittoria angrily looked around the lab. All the antimatter samples were there. Her father’s workspace looked neat. “Nobody came in here,” she said. “Everything here looks fine.”

Kohler looked surprised. “ Up here?”

Vittoria had said it without thinking. “Yes, here in the upper lab.”

“You’re using the lower lab too?”

“For storage.”

Kohler rolled toward her, coughing again. “You’re using the Haz-Mat chamber for storage? Storage of what? ”

Dangerous stuff, what else! Vittoria was losing patience. “Antimatter.”

Kohler lifted himself on the arms of his chair. “There are more samples? Why didn’t you tell me!”

“I just did,” Vittoria shot back. “And you’ve barely let me!”

“We need to check those specimens,” Kohler said. “Now.”

“Specimen,” Vittoria corrected. “Singular. And it’s fine. Nobody could ever-”

“Only one?” Kohler hesitated. “Why isn’t it up here?”

“My father wanted it below the bedrock as a precaution. It’s larger than the others.”

Vittoria noticed the alarmed look that passed between Kohler and Langdon. Kohler rolled toward her again. “You created a specimen larger than five hundred nanograms?”

“It was necessary,” Vittoria defended. “We had to prove we could safely get more out than we put in.” She knew that with new fuel sources, the main question was always input vs. yield—how much money it cost to get the fuel out. Building an oil rig to get just one barrel of oil was a waste. But if that same rig could deliver millions of barrels with little extra cost, it was worth it. Antimatter was the same. Using sixteen miles of electromagnets to create a tiny bit of antimatter used more energy than the antimatter contained. To prove antimatter was efficient and worth it, they had to create larger amounts.

Vittoria had pushed her father to create a large specimen, even though he was unsure. She argued that to make people take antimatter seriously, they had to prove two things. First, that they could produce it at a reasonable cost. And second, that they could store it safely. She finally convinced him, even though he wasn't sure it was the right thing to do. But he set strict rules about secrecy and access. He insisted the antimatter be stored in Haz-Mat—a small granite space seventy-five feet underground. The specimen would be their secret. And only they would have access.

“Vittoria?” Kohler asked, his voice tight. “How large a specimen did you and your father create?”

Vittoria felt a strange satisfaction. She knew the amount would shock even Maximilian Kohler. She imagined the antimatter below. It was an incredible sight. A tiny sphere of antimatter floated inside the trap, easily visible. It wasn't a microscopic speck. It was a droplet the size of a BB.

Vittoria took a deep breath. “A full quarter of a gram.”

The blood drained from Kohler’s face. “What!” He coughed violently. “A quarter of a gram? That’s like… almost five kilotons!”

Kilotons. Vittoria hated the word. She and her father never used it. A kiloton was equal to 1,000 metric tons of TNT. Kilotons were for weapons. Payload. Destructive power. She and her father spoke in electron volts and joules—constructive energy output.

“That much antimatter could literally destroy everything within a half-mile!” Kohler exclaimed.

“Yes, if it all blew up at once,” Vittoria replied, “which no one would ever do!”

“Unless they didn’t know better. Or if your power went out!” Kohler was already going to the elevator.

“That’s why my father kept it in Haz-Mat under a backup power and extra security.”

Kohler turned, looking hopeful. “You have more security on Haz-Mat?”

“Yes. A second retina scan.”

Kohler said only two words. “Downstairs. Now.”

The freight elevator dropped fast.

Another seventy-five feet into the earth.

Vittoria felt fear in both men as the elevator went deeper. Kohler’s usually blank face was tight. I know, Vittoria thought, the sample is huge, but the precautions we’ve taken are -

They reached the bottom.

The elevator opened, and Vittoria led the way down the dim corridor. Ahead, the corridor ended at a huge steel door. HAZ-MAT. The retina scanner beside the door was like the one upstairs. She walked up to it. Carefully, she put her eye to the lens.

She pulled back. Something was wrong. The normally clean lens was covered… smeared with something that looked like… blood? Confused, she turned to the two men, but they looked pale. Both Kohler and Langdon were white, their eyes on the floor at her feet.

Vittoria looked where they were looking… down.

“No!” Langdon yelled, reaching for her. But it was too late.

Vittoria’s eyes locked on the thing on the floor. It was both strange and familiar.

It took only a moment.

Then, with a wave of horror, she knew. Staring up at her from the floor, thrown away like trash, was an eyeball. She knew that hazel color anywhere.
