Angels and Demons

Chapter 35

The Vatican’s helipad, for safety and to keep noise down, is on the northwest edge of Vatican City, as far from St. Peter’s Basilica as it could be.

“Solid ground,” the pilot said as they landed. He got out and opened the sliding door for Langdon and Vittoria.

Langdon got out of the aircraft and turned to help Vittoria, but she had already jumped to the ground easily. Every muscle in her seemed focused on one thing—finding the antimatter before it caused something terrible.

After putting a shiny sun cover across the cockpit window, the pilot led them to a large electric golf cart waiting near the helipad. The cart quietly took them along the country’s western border—a fifty-foot-tall concrete wall thick enough to stop even tanks. Inside the wall, Swiss Guards stood every fifty meters, watching the grounds. The cart turned sharply right onto Via della Osservatorio. Signs pointed everywhere:

PALAZZIO GOVERNATORIO COLLEGIO ETHIOPIANA BASILICA SAN PIETRO CAPELLA SISTINA

They sped up the neat road past a short building marked RADIO VATICANA. Langdon was surprised to realize this was the center of the world’s most popular radio station—Radio Vaticana—spreading God’s word to millions of listeners worldwide.

“Attenzione,” the pilot said, turning quickly into a roundabout.

As the cart went around, Langdon could hardly believe what he was seeing. Giardini Vaticani, he thought. The heart of Vatican City. Straight ahead was the back of St. Peter’s Basilica, a view Langdon knew most people never saw. To the right was the Palace of the Tribunal, the fancy papal home that only Versailles could compete with in its elaborate design. The plain-looking Governatorato building, where Vatican City’s government was, was now behind them. And ahead on the left, the huge rectangular building of the Vatican Museum. Langdon knew there wouldn’t be time to visit the museum this time.

“Where is everyone?” Vittoria asked, looking at the empty lawns and paths.

The guard checked his black, military-style watch—it seemed out of place with his puffy sleeve. “The cardinals are meeting in the Sistine Chapel. The conclave starts in just under an hour.”

Langdon nodded, remembering that before the conclave, the cardinals spent two hours in the Sistine Chapel, quietly reflecting and visiting with cardinals from around the world. This time was meant to refresh old friendships and make the election less intense. “What about the other residents and staff?”

“They’re not allowed in the city for secrecy and security until the conclave ends.”

“And when will that be?”

The guard shrugged. “Only God knows.” The words seemed unusually literal.

After parking the cart on the large lawn behind St. Peter’s Basilica, the guard led Langdon and Vittoria up a stone slope to a marble plaza behind the basilica. They crossed the plaza, went along the back wall of the basilica through a triangular courtyard, across Via Belvedere, and into a group of buildings close together. Langdon knew enough Italian from art history to recognize signs for the Vatican Printing Office, the Tapestry Restoration Lab, Post Office Management, and the Church of St. Ann. They crossed another small square and reached their destination.

The Office of the Swiss Guard is next to Il Corpo di Vigilanza, northeast of St. Peter’s Basilica. The office is a short, stone building. Two guards stood on either side of the entrance like stone statues.

Langdon had to admit, these guards didn't look funny. They also wore the blue and gold uniform, but each carried the traditional “Vatican long sword”—an eight-foot spear with a sharp blade—said to have beheaded many Muslims while defending the Christian crusaders in the fifteenth century.

As Langdon and Vittoria came closer, the two guards stepped forward, crossing their long swords to block the entrance. One looked at the pilot, confused. “I pantaloni,” he said, pointing to Vittoria’s shorts.

The pilot waved them away. “ Il comandante vuole vederli subito.”

The guards frowned. Reluctantly they stepped aside.

Inside, the air was cool. It looked nothing like the security offices Langdon expected. The hallways were ornate and beautifully furnished, with paintings that Langdon was sure any museum would be proud to display.

The pilot pointed down a steep set of stairs. “Down, please.”

Langdon and Vittoria followed the white marble steps down between rows of nude male sculptures. Each statue had a fig leaf that was lighter than the rest of the body.

The Great Castration , Langdon thought.

It was one of the worst tragedies in Renaissance art. In 1857, Pope Pius IX decided that showing the male body accurately might cause lust inside the Vatican. So he took a chisel and mallet and cut off the genitals of every male statue in Vatican City. He damaged works by Michelangelo, Bramante, and Bernini. Plaster fig leaves were used to cover the damage. Hundreds of sculptures had been castrated. Langdon had often wondered if there was a huge crate of stone penises somewhere.

“Here,” the guard announced.

They reached the bottom of the stairs and stopped at a heavy, steel door. The guard typed in a code, and the door slid open. Langdon and Vittoria entered.

Beyond the door was complete chaos.
