Angels and Demons

Chapter 15

Langdon quietly followed Vittoria and Kohler as they returned to the main hall where his strange visit had begun. Vittoria moved with efficient, fluid strides, like an Olympic diver, a strength, Langdon thought, likely from her yoga practice. He heard her breathing slowly and deeply, as if trying to control her sorrow.

Langdon wanted to speak to her, to offer his sympathy. He too had felt the sudden emptiness of losing a parent unexpectedly. He mostly remembered the funeral, rainy and gray. It was two days after his twelfth birthday. The house was full of gray-suited men from his father's work, men who squeezed his hand too hard when they shook it. They mumbled words like cardiac and stress. His mother joked through teary eyes that she'd always known the stock market by holding her husband's hand… his pulse her own private stock ticker.

Once, when his father was alive, Langdon had heard his mom begging him to "slow down and enjoy life." That year, Langdon bought his father a small glass rose for Christmas. It was the most beautiful thing Langdon had ever seen, the way the sun hit it, casting colorful rainbows on the wall. "It's lovely," his father had said, kissing Robert's forehead. "Let's find a safe place for it." Then his father carefully put the rose on a high, dusty shelf in the darkest corner of the living room. A few days later, Langdon got a stool, took the rose back, and returned it to the store. His father never noticed it was gone.

The ding of an elevator brought Langdon back to the present. Vittoria and Kohler were ahead of him, getting into the lift. Langdon paused outside the open doors.

"Is something wrong?" Kohler asked, sounding more impatient than worried.

"Not at all," Langdon said, forcing himself into the small space. He only used elevators when he had to. He liked the more open feeling of stairwells.

"Dr. Vetra's lab is underground," Kohler said.

Great , Langdon thought as he stepped across the gap, feeling a cold wind rise from the depths of the shaft. The doors closed, and the car began to go down.

"Six stories," Kohler said flatly, like a computer.

Langdon pictured the dark, empty space below them. He tried to ignore it by watching the numbers change as they moved between floors. Strangely, the elevator only showed two stops: GROUND LEVEL and LHC.

“What does LHC stand for?” Langdon asked, trying not to seem worried.

“Large Hadron Collider,” Kohler said. “It speeds up tiny particles.”

Speeds up tiny particles? Langdon had heard the term before, but he didn't know much about it. He remembered hearing it at dinner with friends at Dunster House in Cambridge. A physicist friend, Bob Brownell, had shown up for dinner one night, furious.

“The jerks canceled it!” Brownell yelled.

“Canceled what?” they asked.

“The SSC!”

“The what?”

“The Superconducting Super Collider!”

Someone shrugged. “I didn’t know Harvard was building one.”

“Not Harvard!” he shouted. “The U.S.! It was going to be the world’s most powerful particle accelerator! One of the most important science projects of the century! Two billion dollars spent, and the Senate cancels it! Damn religious lobbyists!”

When Brownell finally calmed down, he explained that a particle accelerator was a big, circular tube where tiny particles were sped up. Magnets in the tube switched on and off quickly to "push" particles around and around until they went incredibly fast. Fully sped-up particles circled the tube at over 180,000 miles per second.

“But that’s almost the speed of light,” one of the professors exclaimed.

“Damn right,” Brownell said. He explained that by speeding up two particles in opposite directions around the tube and then crashing them together, scientists could break the particles into smaller pieces and see nature's most basic parts. “Particle accelerators,” Brownell declared, “are vital to the future of science. Crashing particles together is the key to understanding the universe's building blocks.”

Harvard’s Poet in Residence , a quiet man named Charles Pratt, didn’t seem impressed. “It sounds to me,” he said, “like a crude way to do science… like smashing clocks to see how they work.”

Brownell dropped his fork and stormed out of the room.

So CERN has a particle accelerator? Langdon thought as the elevator went down. A circular tube for smashing particles. He wondered why they had buried it underground.

When the elevator stopped, Langdon was glad to be on solid ground. But when the doors opened, his relief disappeared. Robert Langdon found himself again in a completely strange place.

The passageway stretched out forever in both directions, left and right. It was a smooth cement tunnel, wide enough for a large truck to drive through. Brightly lit where they stood, the corridor was pitch black further down. A damp wind came out of the darkness—a disturbing reminder that they were now deep underground. Langdon could almost feel the weight of the dirt and stone above him. For a moment, he was nine years old… the darkness pulling him back… back to the five hours of crushing blackness that still haunted him. Clenching his fists, he fought it off.

Vittoria remained silent as she left the elevator and walked confidently into the darkness without them. Above, the fluorescent lights flickered on to light her way. The effect was unsettling, Langdon thought, as if the tunnel were alive… anticipating her every move. Langdon and Kohler followed, staying a short distance behind. The lights went out automatically behind them.

“This particle accelerator,” Langdon said quietly. “It’s down this tunnel somewhere?”

“That’s it there.” Kohler pointed to his left where a shiny, chrome tube ran along the tunnel's wall.

Langdon looked at the tube, confused. “That’s the accelerator?” It didn't look like what he had imagined. It was perfectly straight, about three feet wide, and went on as far as he could see before disappearing into the darkness. Looks more like a high-tech sewer, Langdon thought. “I thought particle accelerators were circular.”

“This accelerator is a circle,” Kohler said. “It looks straight, but that's an illusion. The tunnel is so big that the curve is hard to see—like the earth.”

Langdon was amazed. This is a circle? “But… it must be huge!”

“The LHC is the biggest machine in the world.”

Langdon looked again, surprised. He remembered the CERN driver mentioning a huge machine buried in the earth. But -

“It is over eight kilometers across… and twenty-seven kilometers around.”

Langdon turned his head quickly. “Twenty-seven kilometers?” He stared at the director and then turned to look into the dark tunnel ahead. “This tunnel is twenty-seven kilometers long? That’s… that’s over sixteen miles!”

Kohler nodded. “Dug in a perfect circle. It goes all the way into France before turning back here. Particles will circle the tube more than ten thousand times a second before they hit each other.”

Langdon's legs felt weak as he stared down the wide tunnel. "You're saying CERN removed tons of earth just to smash tiny particles?"

Kohler shrugged. "Sometimes to find truth, one must move mountains."
