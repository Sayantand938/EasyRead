Angels and Demons

Chapter 37

Vittoria glared at the Swiss Guard standing outside Olivetti’s locked door. The guard glared back, his bright costume making his grim look even more unsettling.

“What a mess,” Vittoria thought. Held hostage by an armed man in pajamas.

Langdon was quiet, and Vittoria hoped he was using his Harvard brain to find a way out. But from his face, she guessed he was more shocked than thoughtful. She wished she hadn't gotten him so involved.

Vittoria wanted to grab her cell phone and call Kohler, but she knew it was pointless. First, the guard would probably come in and take her phone. Second, if Kohler's attack was like usual, he was probably still unable to help. Not that it mattered… Olivetti didn't seem like he would trust anyone right now.

Remember! she told herself. Remember how to solve this!

Remembrance was a trick used by a Buddhist philosopher. Instead of asking her mind to find a solution to a problem that seemed impossible, Vittoria asked her mind to simply remember it. The idea that she once knew the answer made her believe that the answer must exist … removing the feeling of hopelessness. Vittoria often used this to solve scientific problems… the ones that most people thought were unsolvable.

But right now, her trick wasn't working. So she considered her choices… her needs. She needed to warn someone. Someone at the Vatican needed to believe her. But who? The camerlegno? How? She was stuck with only one way out.

Tools, she told herself. There are always tools. Look around.

Without thinking, she relaxed her shoulders and eyes, and took three deep breaths. She felt her heart slow and her muscles relax. The panic in her mind faded. Okay, she thought, clear your mind. What's good about this situation? What do I have to work with?

Vittoria Vetra had a sharp, logical mind that, once settled, was very effective. She quickly understood that being locked in was actually how they could get away.

“I need to make a call,” she said without warning.

Langdon looked up. “I was going to tell you to call Kohler, but…”

“Not Kohler. Someone else.”

“Who?”

“The camerlegno.”

Langdon looked confused. “You’re calling the chamberlain? How?”

“Olivetti mentioned the camerlegno was in the Pope’s office.”

“Okay. Do you know the Pope’s private number?”

“No. But I’m not using my phone.” She pointed to the advanced phone system on Olivetti’s desk, covered in speed dial buttons. “The head of security has to have a direct line to the Pope’s office.”

“He also has a weight lifter with a gun standing right there.”

“And we’re locked in.”

“I knew that already.”

“I mean the guard is locked out. This is Olivetti’s private office. I doubt anyone else has a key.”

Langdon looked at the guard. “That glass is pretty thin, and that gun is pretty big.”

“What’s he going to do, shoot me for using the phone?”

“Who knows! This place is strange, and with how things are-”

“Or,” Vittoria said, “we can spend the next five hours and forty-eight minutes in Vatican Prison. At least we’ll be able to see the antimatter explode.”

Langdon turned white. “But the guard will get Olivetti the second you pick up that phone. Besides, there are twenty buttons on it. And I don’t see any labels. Are you going to try them all and hope you pick the right one?”

“No,” she said, walking to the phone. “Just one.” Vittoria picked up the phone and pressed the top button. “Number one. I bet you one of those Illuminati U.S. dollars you have that this is the Pope’s office. What else would be most important for a Swiss Guard commander?”

Langdon didn't have time to answer. The guard outside the door started banging on the glass with the end of his gun. He signaled for her to put the phone down.

Vittoria winked at him. The guard looked like he was about to explode with anger.

Langdon moved away from the door and turned back to Vittoria. "You better be right, because this guy looks furious!"

"Damn!" she said, listening to the phone. "A recording."

"Recording?" Langdon asked. "The Pope has an answering machine?"

"It wasn't the Pope's office," Vittoria said, hanging up. "It was the stupid weekly menu for the Vatican cafeteria."

Langdon gave a weak smile to the guard outside who was now glaring angrily through the glass while he called Olivetti on his walkie-talkie.
