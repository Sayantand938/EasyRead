Angels and Demons

Chapter 43

Langdon stood still at the Pope’s bulletproof window and looked down at the busy media trailers in St. Peter’s Square. The strange phone call had left him feeling heavy… swollen somehow. Not himself.

The Illuminati, like a snake from the forgotten depths of history, had risen and wrapped themselves around an old enemy. No demands. No negotiation. Just revenge. Pure evil. Squeezing. A revenge 400 years in the making. It seemed that after centuries of being hunted, science had struck back.

The camerlegno stood at his desk, staring blankly at the phone. Olivetti was the first to break the silence. “Carlo,” he said, using the camerlegno’s first name and sounding more like a tired friend than an officer. “For twenty-six years, I have sworn my life to protect this office. It seems tonight I am shamed.”

The camerlegno shook his head. “You and I serve God in different ways, but service always brings honor.”

“These events… I can’t imagine how… this situation…” Olivetti looked lost.

“You realize we have only one choice. I am responsible for the safety of the College of Cardinals.”

“I fear that responsibility was mine, signore.”

“Then your men will handle the immediate evacuation.”

“Signore?”

“Other actions can wait—searching for the device, hunting down the missing cardinals and their captors. But first, the cardinals must be safe. Human life is the most important thing. Those men are the heart of this church.”

“Are you saying we should cancel the conclave now?”

“Do I have a choice?”

“What about your duty to choose a new Pope?”

The young chamberlain sighed, looking out the window at the city of Rome. “His Holiness told me a Pope is caught between two worlds… the real world and the spiritual. He said any church that ignores reality will not last long enough to enjoy the spiritual.” He sounded older than he was. “The real world is here tonight. We would be foolish to ignore it. Pride and tradition shouldn't outweigh common sense.”

Olivetti nodded, impressed. “I have underestimated you, sir.”

The camerlegno didn’t seem to hear, still staring out the window.

“I will be honest, sir. The real world is my world. I deal with its ugliness every day so others don't have to and can seek something better. Let me advise you. This is what I'm trained for. Your instincts, though admirable… could be a disaster.”

The camerlegno turned.

Olivetti sighed. “Evacuating the College of Cardinals from the Sistine Chapel is the worst thing you could do right now.”

The camerlegno didn't seem angry, just confused. "What do you suggest?"

"Say nothing to the cardinals. Seal the conclave. It will give us time to explore other choices."

The camerlegno looked worried. "Are you suggesting I lock all the cardinals on top of a bomb?"

"Yes, signore. For now. Later, if we have to, we can arrange an evacuation."

The camerlegno shook his head. "Delaying the ceremony before it starts is enough reason for an investigation, but after the doors are sealed, nothing can interfere. Conclave rules require-"

"Real world, signore. You're in it tonight. Listen closely." Olivetti now spoke with the sharp tone of a field officer. "Leading one hundred sixty-five cardinals unprepared and unprotected into Rome would be foolish. It would confuse and panic some very old men, and frankly, one fatal stroke this month is enough."

One fatal stroke. The commander's words brought back the headlines Langdon had seen while eating dinner with students at Harvard: POPE SUFFERS STROKE. DIES IN SLEEP.

"Also," Olivetti said, "the Sistine Chapel is a fortress. Although we don't talk about it, the building is strongly reinforced and can withstand any attack except missiles. To prepare, we searched every part of the chapel this afternoon, looking for bugs and other spying devices. The chapel is clean, a safe place, and I'm sure the antimatter isn't inside. There's no safer place for those men right now. We can always talk about emergency evacuation later if necessary."

Langdon was impressed. Olivetti's cold, sharp logic reminded him of Kohler.

"Commander," Vittoria said, her voice tight, "there are other things to consider. No one has ever created this much antimatter. I can only guess the explosion range. Some of the area around Rome might be in danger. If the canister is in one of your main buildings or underground, the impact outside these walls might be small, but if the canister is near the edge… in this building, for example…" She glanced nervously out the window at the crowd in St. Peter's Square.

“I know my duties to the world,” Olivetti answered, “but that doesn’t make this any more serious. Protecting this place has been my only job for over twenty years. I don’t plan to let that weapon explode.”

Camerlegno Ventresca looked up. “You think you can find it?”

“Let me talk about our choices with my tech experts. If we cut off power to Vatican City, we might get rid of the extra signals and get a clear reading of the canister’s magnetic field.”

Vittoria looked surprised, then impressed. “You want to black out Vatican City?”

“Maybe. I don’t know yet if it’s possible, but I want to look into it.”

“The cardinals would wonder what happened,” Vittoria said.

Olivetti shook his head. “Conclaves use candles. The cardinals wouldn't know. After the conclave is locked, I could take back all but a few of my guards and start searching. A hundred men could cover a lot of ground in five hours.”

“Four hours,” Vittoria corrected. “I need to fly the canister back to CERN. It will explode if I don’t recharge it.”

“Can’t you recharge it here?”

Vittoria shook her head. “It’s too complicated. I would have brought it if I could.”

“Four hours then,” Olivetti said, frowning. “Still enough time. Panicking helps no one. Sir, you have ten minutes. Go to the chapel, start the conclave. Give my men time to work. As we get closer to the most important time, we will make the most important choices.”

Langdon wondered how close to “the most important time” Olivetti would let things get.

The camerlegno looked worried. “But the cardinals will ask about the favorites… especially Baggia… where they are.”

“Then you will have to think of something, sir. Tell them you gave the four cardinals something at tea that made them sick.”

The camerlegno looked angry. “Stand on the altar of the Sistine Chapel and lie to the College of Cardinals?”

“For their own safety. Una bugia veniale. A small lie. Your job will be to keep things calm.” Olivetti headed for the door. “Now if you will excuse me, I need to get started.”

“Comandante,” the camerlegno urged, “we can’t just ignore missing cardinals.”

Olivetti stopped in the doorway. “Baggia and the others are currently out of our reach. We must let them go… for the good of everyone. The military calls it triage.”

“Don’t you mean giving up?”

His voice hardened. “If there were any way, sir… any way possible to find those four cardinals, I would give my life to do it. And yet…” He pointed across the room at the window where the early evening sun shone on an endless sea of Roman rooftops. “Searching a city of five million is beyond my ability. I will not waste valuable time trying to ease my guilt with something pointless. I’m sorry.”

Vittoria spoke suddenly. “But if we caught the killer, couldn’t you make him talk?”

Olivetti frowned at her. “Soldiers can’t be saints, Ms. Vetra. Believe me, I understand why you want to catch this man.”

“It’s not just personal,” she said. “The killer knows where the antimatter is… and the missing cardinals. If we could somehow find him…”

“Do what they want?” Olivetti said. “Believe me, removing all protection from Vatican City to watch hundreds of churches is what the Illuminati hope we will do… wasting time and men when we should be searching… or worse, leaving the Vatican Bank unprotected. Not to mention the remaining cardinals.”

That made sense.

“What about the Roman Police?” the camerlegno asked. “We could tell the city police about the crisis. Ask them to help find the cardinals’ captor.”

“Another mistake,” Olivetti said. “You know how the Roman Carbonieri feel about us. They’d barely help, and they’d sell our crisis to the media. Exactly what our enemies want. We’ll have to deal with the media soon enough.”

I will make your cardinals famous, Langdon thought, remembering the killer’s words. The first cardinal's body appears at eight o’clock. Then one every hour. The press will love it.

The camerlegno was talking again, sounding angry. “Commander, we can't just do nothing about the missing cardinals!”

Olivetti looked the camerlegno in the eye. “The prayer of St. Francis, signore. Do you remember it?”

The young priest said the line with pain in his voice. “God, give me strength to accept what I can't change.”

“Trust me,” Olivetti said. “This is one of those things.” Then he left.
