Angels and Demons

Chapter 56

The four unmarked Alpha Romeo 155 T-Sparks sped down Via dei Coronari like fighter jets taking off. The cars carried twelve plainclothed Swiss Guards with Cherchi-Pardini semiautomatics, local-radius nerve gas, and long-range stun guns. The three sharpshooters had laser-sighted rifles.

In the passenger seat of the first car, Olivetti turned to Langdon and Vittoria. His eyes burned with anger. “You promised me a good explanation, and this is what you give me?”

Langdon felt squeezed in the small car. “I understand your-”

“No, you don’t!” Olivetti didn’t raise his voice, but his intensity grew. “I've just pulled a dozen of my best men from Vatican City right before the conclave. And I've done it to watch the Pantheon because of some American I don't know who just figured out a four-hundred-year-old poem. I've also left the search for this antimatter weapon to less important officers.”

Langdon stopped himself from pulling Folio 5 out of his pocket and showing it to Olivetti. “All I know is that the information we found talks about Raphael’s tomb, and Raphael’s tomb is in the Pantheon.”

The driver nodded. “He’s right, commander. My wife and I-”

“Drive,” Olivetti ordered. He turned back to Langdon. “How could a killer kill someone in such a crowded place and get away?”

“I don’t know,” Langdon said. “But the Illuminati are very clever. They broke into CERN and Vatican City. We’re only lucky we know where the first killing will be. The Pantheon is your only chance to catch this guy.”

“More mixed messages,” Olivetti said. “One chance? I thought you said there was a path. A series of signs. If the Pantheon is right, we can follow the path to the other signs. We'll have four chances to catch this guy.”

“I had hoped so,” Langdon said. “And we would have… a century ago.”

Langdon’s discovery that the Pantheon was science's first altar was a sad moment. History often tricks those who seek it. It was unlikely the Path of Illumination would still be complete after so long, with all its statues, but Langdon had imagined following it to the end and finding the secret Illuminati hideout. But, he realized, it wasn't meant to be. “The Vatican had all the statues in the Pantheon taken away and destroyed in the late 1800s.”

Vittoria looked surprised. “Why?”

“The statues were pagan gods. Sadly, that means the first marker is gone… and with it-”

“Any hope,” Vittoria said, “of finding the Path of Illumination and more markers?”

Langdon shook his head. “We have one chance. The Pantheon. After that, the path is lost.”

Olivetti stared at them for a while, then turned forward. “Pull over,” he ordered the driver.

The driver steered the car to the side and stopped. Three other Alpha Romeos stopped quickly behind them. The Swiss Guard convoy stopped with a screech.

“What are you doing!” Vittoria asked.

“My job,” Olivetti said, turning, his voice hard. “Mr. Langdon, when you said you'd explain on the way, I thought I'd arrive at the Pantheon understanding why my men are here. That's not the case. Because I'm leaving important duties to be here, and because I don't understand your theory about virgin sacrifices and old poems, I can't continue. I'm stopping this mission now.” He took out his walkie-talkie and turned it on.

Vittoria reached across the seat and grabbed his arm. “You can’t!”

Olivetti slammed down the walkie-talkie and stared at her, his eyes blazing. “Have you been to the Pantheon, Ms. Vetra?”

“No, but I-”

“Let me tell you something. The Pantheon is just one room. A round space made of stone and cement. It has one entrance. No windows. One small entrance. That entrance always has at least four armed Roman police officers guarding it, protecting it from people who damage art, anti-Christian terrorists, and tourist scams.”

“What’s your point?” she said calmly.

“My point?” Olivetti gripped the seat tightly. “My point is that what you just told me is completely impossible! Can you give me one likely way someone could kill a cardinal inside the Pantheon? How could anyone even get a hostage past the guards into the Pantheon in the first place? And then kill him and escape?” Olivetti leaned over the seat, his coffee breath now in Langdon’s face. “How, Mr. Langdon? One likely way.”

Langdon felt the small car closing in on him. I have no idea! I’m not a killer! I don’t know how he’ll do it! I just know -

“One way?” Vittoria said quickly, her voice calm. “How about this? The killer flies over in a helicopter and drops a screaming, branded cardinal through the hole in the roof. The cardinal hits the marble floor and dies.”

Everyone in the car turned and stared at Vittoria. Langdon didn’t know what to think. You have a twisted imagination, lady, but you’re fast.

Olivetti frowned. “Possible, I guess… but not very-”

“Or the killer drugs the cardinal,” Vittoria said, “and brings him to the Pantheon in a wheelchair, like an old tourist. He wheels him inside, quietly slits his throat, and then walks out.”

This seemed to wake Olivetti up a bit.

Not bad! Langdon thought.

“Or,” she said, “the killer could-”

“I heard you,” Olivetti said. “Enough.” He took a deep breath and let it out. Someone knocked hard on the window, and everyone jumped. It was a soldier from one of the other cars. Olivetti rolled down the window.

“Everything okay, commander?” The soldier was in plain clothes. He pulled back the sleeve of his denim shirt to show a black military watch. “Seven-forty, commander. We need time to get in place.”

Olivetti nodded but didn't say anything for a while. He ran a finger across the dash, making a line in the dust. He looked at Langdon in the side mirror, and Langdon felt like he was being judged. Finally, Olivetti turned back to the guard. He sounded hesitant. “I want separate routes. Cars to Piazza della Rotunda, Via delgi Orfani, Piazza Sant’Ignacio, and Sant’Eustachio. No closer than two blocks. Once you’re parked, get ready and wait for my orders. Three minutes.”

“Yes, sir.” The soldier went back to his car.

Langdon nodded at Vittoria, impressed. She smiled back, and for a moment Langdon felt a surprising connection… a spark between them.

The commander turned and looked straight at Langdon. “Mr. Langdon, this better not backfire.”

Langdon smiled, feeling unsure. How could it?
