Angels and Demons

Chapter 36

The Office of the Swiss Guard.

Langdon stood in the doorway, looking at the mix of old and new before them. Mixed media. The room was a beautiful Renaissance library with detailed bookshelves, oriental carpets, and colorful tapestries… but it also had high-tech equipment—rows of computers, faxes, electronic maps of the Vatican, and TVs showing CNN. Men in bright pants typed quickly on computers and listened closely through futuristic headphones.

“Wait here,” the guard said.

Langdon and Vittoria waited as the guard walked across the room to a very tall, thin man in a dark blue military uniform. He was talking on a cell phone and stood so straight he seemed to lean back. The guard spoke to him, and the man glanced at Langdon and Vittoria. He nodded, then turned away and kept talking on the phone.

The guard came back. “Commander Olivetti will see you in a moment.”

“Thank you.”

The guard left and went back up the stairs.

Langdon looked at Commander Olivetti, realizing he was the head of the armed forces of a whole country. Vittoria and Langdon waited, watching the activity. Guards in bright clothing hurried around, shouting orders in Italian.

“Continua cercando!” one yelled into a telephone.

“Did you check the museum?” another asked.

Langdon didn't need to speak Italian well to know the security center was searching hard. That was good. The bad news was they clearly hadn't found the antimatter yet.

“You okay?” Langdon asked Vittoria.

She shrugged and gave a tired smile.

When the commander finished his call and walked over, he seemed to get bigger with each step. Langdon was tall and not used to looking up, but Commander Olivetti made him. Langdon could tell the commander had been through a lot. His face was strong and tough. His dark hair was cut short, and his eyes showed a strong will that came from years of training. He moved very precisely, and the earpiece hidden behind his ear made him look more like U.S. Secret Service than Swiss Guard.

The commander spoke to them in accented English. His voice was surprisingly soft for such a big man, almost a whisper. It was sharp and efficient. “Good afternoon,” he said. “I am Commander Olivetti-Comandante Principale of the Swiss Guard. I called your director.”

Vittoria looked up at him. “Thank you for seeing us, sir.”

The commander didn't answer. He told them to follow him and led them through the mess of electronics to a door in the wall. “Enter,” he said, holding the door open.

Langdon and Vittoria went inside. They were in a dark control room with a wall of video screens showing black-and-white pictures of the building. A young guard watched the screens closely.

“Out,” Olivetti said.

The guard packed up and left.

Olivetti walked over to one of the screens and pointed. Then he turned to his guests. “This image is from a hidden camera inside Vatican City. I want an explanation.”

Langdon and Vittoria looked at the screen and gasped. The image was clear. No question. It was CERN’s antimatter canister. Inside, a shimmering drop of liquid metal hung in the air, lit by the blinking LED clock. The area around the canister was dark, as if the antimatter were in a closet or dark room. At the top of the screen flashed the words: LIVE FEED-CAMERA #86.

Vittoria looked at the time left on the canister. “Under six hours,” she whispered to Langdon, her face tight.

Langdon checked his watch. “So we have until…” He stopped, his stomach clenching.

“Midnight,” Vittoria said, looking grim.

Midnight, Langdon thought. Someone likes drama. Whoever stole the canister had timed it perfectly. He felt a sense of dread, realizing he was at ground zero.

Olivetti’s whisper sounded like a hiss. “Does this object belong to you?”

Vittoria nodded. “Yes, sir. It was stolen from us. It contains a very explosive substance called antimatter.”

Olivetti looked calm. “I know about explosives, Ms. Vetra. I have not heard of antimatter.”

“It’s new technology. We need to find it now or evacuate Vatican City.”

Olivetti closed his eyes slowly and reopened them, as if looking at Vittoria again might change what he just heard. “Evacuate? Do you know what's happening here tonight?”

“Yes, sir. And your cardinals' lives are in danger. We have about six hours. Have you started looking for the canister?”

Olivetti shook his head. “We haven’t started looking.”

Vittoria was shocked. “What? But we clearly heard your guards talking about searching the-”

“Searching, yes,” Olivetti said, “but not for your canister. My men are looking for something else that doesn't concern you.”

Vittoria’s voice broke. “You haven’t even started looking for this canister?”

Olivetti’s pupils seemed to shrink. He had the cold look of an insect. “Ms. Vetra, is it? Let me explain something. The director of your facility wouldn't give me any details about this object over the phone except to say I needed to find it immediately. We are very busy, and I can't dedicate people to a situation until I get some facts.”

“There is only one important fact right now, sir,” Vittoria said, “which is that in six hours that device is going to destroy this entire complex.”

Olivetti stood still. “Ms. Vetra, there is something you need to know.” His tone sounded condescending. “Even though Vatican City looks old, every entrance, both public and private, has the most advanced sensing equipment. If someone tried to enter with any kind of bomb, it would be detected instantly. We have radioactive isotope scanners, smell filters designed by the American DEA to detect the faintest chemical smells of things that burn and toxins. We also use the most advanced metal detectors and X-ray scanners available.”

“Very impressive,” Vittoria said, staying as calm as Olivetti. “But antimatter isn't radioactive, it's chemically like pure hydrogen, and the container is plastic. None of those devices could have found it.”

“But the device has a power source,” Olivetti said, pointing to the blinking light. “Even a tiny bit of nickel-cadmium would show up as-”

“The batteries are also plastic.”

Olivetti was clearly losing patience. “Plastic batteries?”

“Polymer gel electrolyte with Teflon.”

Olivetti leaned toward her, as if to use his height to his advantage. “Signorina, the Vatican gets bomb threats all the time. I teach every Swiss Guard about modern explosives myself. I know that nothing on earth is powerful enough to do what you're saying, unless you mean a nuclear bomb with a fuel core as big as a baseball.”

Vittoria looked at him intensely. “Nature still has many secrets.”

Olivetti leaned closer. “May I ask who you are exactly? What do you do at CERN?”

“I'm a senior researcher and the person in charge of talking to the Vatican during this crisis.”

“Sorry for being rude, but if this is a crisis, why am I talking to you and not your boss? And why are you being disrespectful by coming to Vatican City in shorts?”

Langdon groaned. He couldn’t believe that, given the situation, the man was so focused on the dress code. Then again, he realized, if stone penises could cause lustful thoughts in Vatican residents, Vittoria Vetra in shorts could certainly be a threat to national security.

“Commander Olivetti,” Langdon intervened, trying to calm what seemed like another problem about to start. “My name is Robert Langdon. I’m a professor of religious studies in the U.S. and not connected to CERN. I have seen antimatter demonstrated and will confirm Ms. Vetra’s claim that it is extremely dangerous. We have reason to believe it was placed inside your complex by a cult against religion, hoping to disrupt your meeting.”

Olivetti turned, looking down at Langdon. “I have a woman in shorts telling me that a drop of liquid will blow up Vatican City, and I have an American professor telling me we are being targeted by some cult against religion. What exactly do you expect me to do?”

“Find the canister,” Vittoria said. “Right away.”

“Impossible. That device could be anywhere. Vatican City is huge.”

“Your cameras don’t have GPS trackers?”

“They are not usually stolen. This missing camera will take days to find.”

“We don’t have days,” Vittoria said firmly. “We have six hours.”

“Six hours until what, Ms. Vetra?” Olivetti’s voice suddenly got louder. He pointed to the image on the screen. “Until these numbers count down? Until Vatican City disappears? Believe me, I don't like people messing with my security system. Nor do I like strange devices appearing mysteriously inside my walls. I am worried. It is my job to be worried. But what you have told me here is unacceptable.”

Langdon spoke without thinking. “Have you heard of the Illuminati?”

The commander's coldness broke for a moment. His eyes widened, like a shark about to strike. "I'm warning you. I don't have time for this."

"So you have heard of the Illuminati?"

Olivetti's eyes were sharp and piercing. "I am a dedicated defender of the Catholic Church. Of course I've heard of the Illuminati. They've been gone for decades."

Langdon reached into his pocket and took out the fax of Leonardo Vetra's branded body. He gave it to Olivetti.

"I study the Illuminati," Langdon said as Olivetti looked at the picture. "I find it hard to believe they're still around, but this brand, and the fact they're known to hate Vatican City, has made me change my mind."

"A fake made on a computer." Olivetti gave the fax back to Langdon.

Langdon stared, unable to believe it. "Fake? Look at how perfect it is! You should know how real it is because of-"

"Real is exactly what it isn't. Maybe Ms. Vetra hasn't told you, but scientists at CERN have been criticizing the Vatican for years. They often ask us to take back our Creationist ideas, apologize for what happened to Galileo and Copernicus, and stop criticizing research they think is dangerous or wrong. What's more likely—that a four-hundred-year-old evil group is back with a powerful weapon, or that someone at CERN is trying to ruin a holy Vatican event with a clever trick?"

"That photo," Vittoria said, her voice full of anger, "is of my father. Murdered. You think this is a joke?"

"I don't know, Ms. Vetra. But I know that until I get answers that make sense, I won't cause any alarm. It's my job to be careful and quiet… so that spiritual things can happen here peacefully. Especially today."

Langdon said, “At least put it off.”

“Put it off?” Olivetti’s jaw dropped. “Such nerve! A conclave isn’t some American baseball game you call off because of rain. This is a sacred event with a strict code and process. Never mind that one billion Catholics in the world are waiting for a leader. Never mind that the world media is outside. The rules for this event are holy—not able to be changed. Since 1179, conclaves have survived earthquakes, famines, and even the plague. Believe me, it’s not going to be canceled because of a murdered scientist and a drop of God knows what.”

“Take me to the head person,” Vittoria demanded.

Olivetti glared. “You’re looking at him.”

“No,” she said. “Someone in the clergy.”

The veins on Olivetti’s forehead began to show. “The clergy is gone. Except for the Swiss Guard, the only people in Vatican City right now are the College of Cardinals. And they are inside the Sistine Chapel.”

“What about the chamberlain?” Langdon stated clearly.

“Who?”

“The late Pope’s chamberlain.” Langdon repeated the word confidently, hoping he remembered correctly. He recalled reading about the strange way Vatican power works after a Pope dies. If Langdon was right, in the time between Popes, all power goes to the late Pope’s personal assistant—his chamberlain—a secretarial helper who watches over the conclave until the cardinals pick the new Holy Father. “I believe the chamberlain is the one in charge right now.”

“Il camerlegno?” Olivetti frowned. “The camerlegno is just a priest here. He isn’t even a saint. He is the late Pope’s servant.”

“But he is here. And you answer to him.”

Olivetti crossed his arms. “Mr. Langdon, it’s true that Vatican rules say the camerlegno takes over as chief executive during the conclave, but only because he can’t be elected pope, which makes the election fair. It’s like if your president died, and one of his aides filled in temporarily. The camerlegno is young, and doesn’t know much about security, or anything else. So, really, I’m in charge here.”

“Take us to him,” Vittoria said.

“Impossible. The conclave starts in forty minutes. The camerlegno is in the Pope’s Office preparing. I’m not going to bother him with security issues.”

Vittoria started to reply but was interrupted by a knock on the door. Olivetti opened it.

A guard in uniform stood outside, pointing to his watch. “It’s time, Commander.”

Olivetti checked his own watch and nodded. He turned back to Langdon and Vittoria like a judge deciding what to do with them. “Follow me.” He led them out of the monitoring room, across the security center, to a small glass cubicle against the back wall. “My office.” Olivetti showed them inside. The room was plain—a messy desk, file cabinets, folding chairs, a water cooler. “I’ll be back in ten minutes. I suggest you use the time to decide what you want to do.”

Vittoria turned quickly. “You can’t just leave! That canister is-”

“I don’t have time for this,” Olivetti said angrily. “Maybe I should hold you until after the conclave, when I do have time.”

“Signore,” the guard urged, pointing to his watch again. “Clearing of the chapel.”

Olivetti nodded and started to leave.

“Spazzare di capella?” Vittoria demanded. “You’re leaving to sweep the chapel?”

Olivetti turned, his eyes piercing her. “We sweep for electronic bugs, Miss Vetra—it's confidential.” He motioned to her legs. “Not something I think you’d understand.”

With that he slammed the door, rattling the heavy glass. In one quick move he produced a key, inserted it, and twisted. A heavy deadbolt slid into place.

“Idiтta!” Vittoria yelled. “You can’t keep us in here!”

Through the glass, Langdon could see Olivetti say something to the guard. The sentinel nodded. As Olivetti strode out of the room, the guard spun and faced them on the other side of the glass, arms crossed, a large sidearm visible on his hip.

Perfect, Langdon thought. Just bloody perfect.
