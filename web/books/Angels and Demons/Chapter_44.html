Angels and Demons

Chapter 44

The main office of the British Broadcast Corporation (BBC) is in London, just west of Piccadilly Circus. The switchboard phone rang, and a young editor answered.

“BBC,” she said, putting out her Dunhill cigarette.

The voice on the line was rough, with a Middle Eastern accent. “I have a big story your network might want.”

The editor grabbed a pen and a standard form. “About?”

“The Pope's election.”

She frowned, tiredly. The BBC had already run a story yesterday that didn't do well. People didn't seem to care much about Vatican City. “What’s new?”

“Do you have a TV reporter in Rome covering the election?”

“I think so.”

“I need to talk to him right away.”

“I’m sorry, but I cannot give you that number without some idea-”

“There is a threat to the group. That is all I can tell you.”

The editor took notes. “Your name?”

“My name doesn't matter.”

The editor was not surprised. “And you have proof of this claim?”

“I do.”

“I would be happy to take the information, but it is not our policy to give out our reporters’ numbers unless-”

“I understand. I will call another network. Thank you for your time. Good-b-”

“Just a moment,” she said. “Can you hold?”

The editor put the caller on hold and stretched her neck. The skill of filtering out potential crazy calls was not perfect, but this caller had just passed the BBC’s two unspoken tests for a real phone source. He had refused to give his name, and he was eager to get off the phone. Frauds and attention seekers usually whined and begged.

Luckily for her, reporters always worried about missing important news, so they rarely got mad at her for giving them a crazy lead now and then. Wasting five minutes of a reporter’s time was okay. Missing a big story was not.

Yawning, she looked at her computer and typed in “Vatican City.” When she saw the name of the reporter covering the papal election, she smiled. He was a new guy the BBC had just pulled from some cheap London paper to do some of their simpler stories. They had clearly started him with the easiest job.

He was probably very bored, waiting all night to record his short video. He would likely be happy for something to break up the boredom.

The BBC editor wrote down the reporter’s satellite number in Vatican City. Then, lighting another cigarette, she gave the anonymous caller the reporter’s number.
