Angels and Demons

Chapter 61

The air inside the Pantheon was cool and damp, heavy with history. The vast ceiling hung overhead as if weightless—the 141-foot span bigger than the dome at St. Peter’s. As always, Langdon felt a chill as he entered the huge room. It was an amazing mix of engineering and art. Above them, the famous circular hole in the roof shone with a narrow beam of evening sun. The oculus, Langdon thought. The demon’s hole.

They had arrived.

Langdon’s eyes followed the curve of the ceiling sloping out to the walls with columns, and then down to the shiny marble floor. The soft echo of footsteps and tourist chatter filled the dome. Langdon looked at the dozen or so tourists wandering around in the shadows. Are you here?

“Looks pretty quiet,” Vittoria said, still holding his hand.

Langdon nodded.

“Where’s Raphael’s tomb?”

Langdon thought for a moment, trying to get his bearings. He looked around the room. Tombs. Altars. Pillars. Niches. He pointed to a very fancy tomb across the dome and to the left. “I think that’s Raphael’s over there.”

Vittoria looked around the rest of the room. “I don’t see anyone who looks like an assassin about to kill a cardinal. Should we look around?”

Langdon nodded. “There’s only one place in here where someone could hide. We should check the rientranze.”

“The niches?”

“Yes.” Langdon pointed. “The niches in the wall.”

Around the edge, mixed in with the tombs, were semicircular niches carved into the wall. The niches weren't huge, but big enough to hide someone in the shadows. Sadly, Langdon knew they once held statues of the Olympian gods, but the pagan sculptures were destroyed when the Vatican turned the Pantheon into a Christian church. He felt frustrated knowing he stood at science's first altar, now missing its marker. He wondered which statue it was, and where it pointed. Langdon couldn't imagine anything more exciting than finding an Illuminati marker—a statue secretly pointing down the Path of Illumination. Again, he wondered who the anonymous Illuminati sculptor was.

“I’ll take the left side,” Vittoria said, pointing to the left half. “You go right. See you in a hundred and eighty degrees.”

Langdon smiled tightly.

As Vittoria left, Langdon felt the eerie horror of the situation creeping back into his mind. Turning to the right, the killer’s voice seemed to whisper in the empty space. Eight o’clock. Virgin sacrifices on the altars of science. A mathematical progression of death. Eight, nine, ten, eleven… and at midnight. Langdon checked his watch: 7:52. Eight minutes.

As Langdon moved toward the first niche, he passed the tomb of an Italian Catholic king. The sarcophagus, like many in Rome, was crooked against the wall, positioned strangely. A group of visitors seemed confused. Langdon didn’t stop to explain. Formal Christian tombs were often misaligned with the architecture so they could face east. It was an old superstition that Langdon’s Symbology 212 class had discussed just last month.

“That’s totally weird!” a female student in the front had blurted when Langdon explained why tombs faced east. “Why would Christians want their tombs to face the rising sun? This is Christianity… not sun worship!”

Langdon smiled, walking in front of the blackboard, chewing an apple. “Mr. Hitzrot!” he shouted.

A young man dozing in the back woke up with a jolt. “What! Me?”

Langdon pointed to a Renaissance art poster on the wall. “Who is that man kneeling before God?”

“Um… some saint?”

“Great. And how do you know he’s a saint?”

“He’s got a halo?”

“Great, and does that golden halo remind you of anything?”

Hitzrot smiled. “Yeah! Those Egyptian things we studied last term. Those… um… sun disks!”

“Thank you, Hitzrot. Go back to sleep.” Langdon turned back to the class. “Halos, like much of Christian symbolism, were taken from the ancient Egyptian religion of sun worship. Christianity is full of examples of sun worship.”

“Excuse me?” the girl in front said. “I go to church all the time, and I don’t see much sun worshiping going on!”

“Really? What do you celebrate on December twenty-fifth?”

“Christmas. The birth of Jesus Christ.”

“And yet, the Bible says Christ was born in March, so why do we celebrate in late December?”

Silence.

Langdon smiled. “December twenty-fifth, my friends, is the old pagan holiday of sol invictus -Unconquered Sun- which happens at the same time as the winter solstice. It’s that great time of year when the sun comes back, and the days start getting longer.”

Langdon took another bite of apple.

“When religions take over,” he continued, “they often use existing holidays to make switching easier. It’s called transmutation. It helps people get used to the new faith. Worshipers keep the same holy dates, pray in the same sacred places, use similar symbols… and they just switch to a different god.”

Now the girl in front looked angry. “You’re saying Christianity is just… repackaged sun worship!”

“Not at all. Christianity didn't borrow only from sun worship. The Christian canonization ritual comes from the old ‘god-making’ rite of Euhemerus. The practice of ‘god-eating’—that is, Holy Communion—was borrowed from the Aztecs. Even the idea of Christ dying for our sins may not be only Christian; the self-sacrifice of a young man to forgive the sins of his people appears in the earliest story of Quetzalcoatl.”

The girl glared. “So, is anything in Christianity new?”

“Very little in any organized faith is truly new. Religions aren't born from nothing. They grow from each other. Modern religion is a collage… a collection of historical records of man's search to understand God.”

“Um… wait,” Hitzrot said, sounding awake now. “I know something Christian that's new. What about our image of God? Christian art never shows God as a hawk sun god, or as an Aztec, or as anything weird. It always shows God as an old man with a white beard. So our image of God is new, right?”

Langdon smiled. “When the first Christians left their old gods—pagan, Roman, Greek, sun, Mithraic, all of them—they asked the church what their new Christian God looked like. Smartly, the church picked the most feared, powerful… and well-known face in history.”

Hitzrot looked doubtful. “An old man with a long, white beard?”

Langdon pointed to a row of old gods on the wall. At the top was an old man with a long, white beard. “Does Zeus look familiar?”

The class ended just as planned.

“Good evening,” a man said.

Langdon jumped. He was back in the Pantheon. He turned to see an old man in a blue cape with a red cross on it. The man smiled, showing gray teeth.

“You’re English, yes?” The man’s accent was strongly Tuscan.

Langdon blinked, confused. “Actually, no. I’m American.”

The man looked ashamed. “Oh dear, I’m sorry. You were dressed so well, I just guessed… I apologize.”

“Can I help you?” Langdon asked, his heart racing.

“Actually I thought perhaps I could help you. I am the guide here.” The man pointed proudly to his city badge. “It is my job to make your visit to Rome more interesting.”

More interesting? Langdon was sure this visit to Rome was plenty interesting.

“You look like a refined man,” the guide flattered, “no doubt more interested in culture than most. Perhaps I can give you some history on this fascinating building.”

Langdon smiled politely. “That’s kind, but I’m an art historian myself, and-”

“Great!” The man’s eyes lit up like he’d won big. “Then you will no doubt find this delightful!”

“I think I’d prefer to-”

“The Pantheon,” the man declared, starting his memorized speech, “was built by Marcus Agrippa in 27 B.C.”

“Yes,” Langdon interrupted, “and rebuilt by Hadrian in 119 A.D.”

“It was the world’s largest free-standing dome until 1960 when the Superdome in New Orleans beat it!”

Langdon groaned. The man would not stop.

And a fifth-century theologian once called the Pantheon the House of the Devil , warning that the hole in the roof was an entrance for demons!

Langdon ignored him. His eyes went up to the oculus, and the memory of Vittoria’s idea flashed a chilling image in his mind… a branded cardinal falling through the hole and hitting the marble floor. Now that would be a media event. Langdon looked around the Pantheon for reporters. None. He took a deep breath. It was a ridiculous idea. The logistics of pulling off a stunt like that would be absurd.

As Langdon moved on to keep looking around, the chatty guide followed like a puppy. Remind me , Langdon thought, there’s nothing worse than an overly enthusiastic art historian.

Across the room, Vittoria was deep in her own search. Standing alone for the first time since she had heard the news of her father, she felt the harsh reality of the last eight hours closing in. Her father had been murdered—cruelly and suddenly. Almost as painful was that her father’s creation had been corrupted—now a tool of terrorists. Vittoria felt guilty thinking that it was her invention that had allowed the antimatter to be transported… her canister that was now counting down inside the Vatican. Trying to support her father’s search for the simplicity of truth… she had become a partner in chaos.

Strangely, the only thing that felt right in her life at the moment was the presence of a complete stranger. Robert Langdon. She found an inexplicable comfort in his eyes… like the peace of the oceans she had left behind that morning. She was glad he was there. He had not only been a source of strength and hope for her, but Langdon had also used his quick mind to provide this one chance to catch her father’s killer.

Vittoria breathed deeply as she continued her search, walking around the edge of the room. She was overwhelmed by the sudden images of personal revenge that had filled her thoughts all day. Even as someone who loved all life… she wanted this killer dead. No amount of good karma could make her forgive and forget today. Alarmed and excited, she felt something running through her Italian blood that she had never felt before… the voices of Sicilian ancestors defending family honor with violent justice. Vendetta , Vittoria thought, and for the first time in her life understood.

Thoughts of revenge pushed her on. She approached the tomb of Raphael Santi. Even from a distance she could tell this guy was special. His casket, unlike the others, was protected by a Plexiglas shield and set back into the wall. Through the barrier she could see the front of the sarcophagus.

RAPHAEL SANTI, 1483-1520

Vittoria studied the grave and then read the one-sentence description next to Raphael’s tomb.

Then she read it again.

Then… she read it again.

A moment later, she was running in horror across the floor. “Robert! Robert!”
