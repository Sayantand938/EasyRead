Angels and Demons

Chapter 8

When Kohler and Langdon came out of CERN’s main building into the bright Swiss sunlight, Langdon felt like he was back home. The scene in front of him looked like an Ivy League school.

A grassy hill sloped down to a wide open area where groups of sugar maples stood in squares surrounded by brick dorms and paths. People who looked like professors hurried in and out of buildings with books. Adding to the college feel, two hippies with long hair threw a Frisbee back and forth while Mahler’s Fourth Symphony played loudly from a dorm window.

“These are our dorms,” Kohler said as he sped down the path toward the buildings in his wheelchair. “We have over three thousand physicists here. CERN alone employs more than half of the world’s particle physicists—the smartest people on earth—Germans, Japanese, Italians, Dutch, you name it. Our physicists come from over five hundred universities and sixty countries.”

Langdon was amazed. “How do they all communicate?”

“English, of course. The universal language of science.”

Langdon had always heard math was the universal language of science, but he was too tired to argue. He followed Kohler down the path.

Halfway down, a young man jogged past. His T-shirt said: NO GUT, NO GLORY!

Langdon watched him, confused. “Gut?”

“General Unified Theory,” Kohler joked. “The theory of everything.”

“I see,” Langdon said, though he didn't really.

“Are you familiar with particle physics, Mr. Langdon?”

Langdon shrugged. “I know a bit about regular physics—things falling, that kind of stuff.” His years of diving had taught him to really respect gravity. “Particle physics is about atoms, right?”

Kohler shook his head. “Atoms are huge compared to what we study. We focus on the atom's nucleus—which is tiny, only a ten-thousandth of the atom's size.” He coughed again, sounding unwell. “The people at CERN are trying to answer the same questions humans have always asked. Where did we come from? What are we made of?”

“And you find these answers in a physics lab?”

“You seem surprised.”

“I am. Those questions seem more spiritual.”

“Mr. Langdon, all questions used to be spiritual. Since the beginning, people have used spirituality and religion to explain what science couldn't. The sun rising and setting was once blamed on Helios and his chariot. Earthquakes and waves were Poseidon's anger. Science has shown those gods aren't real. Soon all Gods will be proven false. Science has answered almost every question. Only a few big ones are left. Where do we come from? Why are we here? What's the meaning of life and everything?”

Langdon was surprised. “And CERN is trying to answer those?”

“Actually, we are answering them.”

Langdon fell silent as the two men walked through the residential areas. As they walked, a Frisbee flew over them and stopped right in front of them. Kohler ignored it and kept walking.

A voice called out from across the area. “ S’il vous plaоt!”

Langdon looked over. An elderly white-haired man in a COLLEGE PARIS sweatshirt waved to him. Langdon picked up the Frisbee and threw it back perfectly. The old man caught it on one finger and bounced it a few times before throwing it over his shoulder to his partner. “Merci!” he called to Langdon.

“Congratulations,” Kohler said when Langdon finally caught up. “You just played catch with a Nobel Prize winner, Georges Charpak, inventor of the multiwire proportional chamber.”

Langdon nodded. My lucky day.

It took Langdon and Kohler three more minutes to reach where they were going—a large, well-kept dormitory in a group of aspens. Compared to the other dorms, this building seemed fancy. The carved stone sign in front read BUILDING C.

Creative name, Langdon thought.

But even with its boring name, Building C looked good to Langdon's eye for architecture—simple and strong. It had a red brick front, a fancy railing, and was surrounded by neatly shaped hedges. As the two men walked up the stone path toward the entrance, they passed under a gateway made of two marble columns. Someone had put a sticky note on one of them.

THIS COLUMN IS IONIC

Physicist humor? Langdon wondered, looking at the column and chuckling. “It’s good to know that even smart physicists make mistakes.”

Kohler looked over. “What do you mean?”

“Whoever wrote that note made a mistake. That column isn’t Ionic. Ionic columns are the same width all the way up. That one gets narrower. It’s Doric-the Greek version. It’s a common mistake.”

Kohler did not smile. “The writer meant it as a joke, Mr. Langdon. Ionic means it has ions-electrically charged particles. Most things have them.”

Langdon looked back at the column and groaned.

Langdon still felt foolish when he got off the elevator on the top floor of Building C. He followed Kohler down a nice hallway. The decorations were a surprise-old-fashioned colonial French-a cherry couch, a porcelain vase, and carved woodwork.

“We like our senior scientists to be comfortable,” Kohler said.

Obviously , Langdon thought. “So the man who sent the fax lived up here? One of your higher-up employees?”

“Yes,” Kohler said. “He didn’t come to a meeting with me this morning and didn’t respond to my page. I came up here to find him and found him dead in his living room.”

Langdon felt a sudden chill as he realized he was about to see a dead body. His stomach had never been very strong. He'd learned this as an art student when the teacher told the class that Leonardo da Vinci had become an expert on the human body by digging up dead bodies and cutting up their muscles.

Kohler led the way to the end of the hallway. There was only one door. “The Penthouse, as you might call it,” Kohler announced, wiping a drop of sweat from his forehead.

Langdon looked at the single oak door. The name plate said:

LEONARDO VETRA

“Leonardo Vetra,” Kohler said, “would have been fifty-eight next week. He was one of the most brilliant scientists of our time. His death is a great loss for science.”

For a moment, Langdon thought he saw feeling in Kohler’s stern face. But it vanished quickly. Kohler reached into his pocket and started looking through a large key ring.

A strange thought struck Langdon. The building seemed empty. “Where is everyone?” he asked. He didn't expect it to be so quiet, since they were about to enter a murder scene.

“The residents are in their labs,” Kohler replied, finding the key.

“I mean the police,” Langdon said clearly. “Have they left already?”

Kohler stopped, his key halfway in the lock. “Police?”

Langdon looked the director in the eye. “Police. You sent me a fax about a murder. You must have called the police.”

“I most certainly have not.”

“What?”

Kohler’s gray eyes narrowed. “The situation is complicated, Mr. Langdon.”

Langdon felt a wave of worry. “But… surely someone else knows about this!”

“Yes. Leonardo’s adopted daughter. She is also a physicist here at CERN. She and her father share a lab. They are partners. Ms. Vetra has been away this week doing research in the field. I have told her about her father’s death, and she is returning now.”

“But a man has been murd-”

“An official investigation,” Kohler said, his voice firm, “will happen. However, it will almost certainly involve searching Vetra’s lab, a space he and his daughter keep very private. Therefore, it will wait until Ms. Vetra arrives. I think I owe her at least that small amount of privacy.”

Kohler turned the key.

As the door opened, a blast of cold air rushed into the hall and hit Langdon in the face. He stepped back, confused. He was looking across the doorway at a strange world. The apartment in front of him was covered in a thick, white fog. The mist swirled in smoky circles around the furniture and hid the room in a cloudy haze.

“What the… ?” Langdon stuttered.

“Freon cooling system,” Kohler replied. “I chilled the apartment to keep the body preserved.”

Langdon buttoned his tweed jacket against the cold. I’m in Oz, he thought. And I forgot my ruby shoes.
