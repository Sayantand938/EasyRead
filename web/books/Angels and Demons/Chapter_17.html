Angels and Demons

Chapter 17

Not many children could say they remembered the day they met their father, but Vittoria Vetra could. She was eight years old, living where she always had, Orfanotrofio di Siena , a Catholic orphanage near Florence, left by parents she never knew. It was raining that day. The nuns had called for her twice to come to dinner, but as always she pretended not to hear. She lay outside in the courtyard, staring up at the raindrops… feeling them hit her body… trying to guess where one would land next. The nuns called again, warning that pneumonia might make an unbearably stubborn child a lot less curious about nature.

I can’t hear you , Vittoria thought.

She was soaked when the young priest came out to get her. She didn’t know him. He was new there. Vittoria waited for him to grab her and drag her back inside. But he didn’t. Instead, to her surprise, he lay down beside her, soaking his robes in a puddle.

“They say you ask a lot of questions,” the young man said.

Vittoria scowled. “Are questions bad?”

He laughed. “Guess they were right.”

“What are you doing out here?”

“Same thing you’re doing… wondering why raindrops fall.”

“I’m not wondering why they fall! I already know!”

The priest looked at her, surprised. “Really?”

“Sister Francisca says raindrops are angels crying, washing away our sins.”

“Wow!” he said, sounding amazed. “So that’s why.”

“No, it’s not!” the girl snapped. “Raindrops fall because everything falls! Everything falls! Not just rain!”

The priest scratched his head, looking puzzled. “You know, you’re right. Everything does fall. It must be gravity.”

“It must be what?”

He looked at her, surprised. “You haven’t heard of gravity?”

“No.”

The priest shrugged, looking sad. “Too bad. Gravity explains a lot.”

Vittoria sat up. “What’s gravity?” she asked. “Tell me!”

The priest winked at her. “How about I tell you over dinner?”

The young priest was Leonardo Vetra. He had been a prize-winning physics student in college, but he felt a different calling and joined the seminary. Leonardo and Vittoria became close friends in the isolated world of nuns and rules. Vittoria made Leonardo laugh, and he guided her, explaining that beautiful things like rainbows and rivers had many explanations. He taught her about light, planets, stars, and all of nature through the eyes of both God and science. Vittoria's natural intelligence and curiosity made her a fascinating student. Leonardo protected her like a daughter.

Vittoria was happy too. She had never known the joy of having a father. While other adults scolded her for asking questions, Leonardo spent hours showing her books. He even asked what her thoughts were. Vittoria hoped Leonardo would stay with her forever. Then one day, her worst fear came true. Father Leonardo told her he was leaving the orphanage.

“I’m moving to Switzerland,” Leonardo said. “I have money to study physics at the University of Geneva.”

“Physics?” Vittoria cried. “I thought you loved God!”

“I do, very much. That’s why I want to study his divine rules. The laws of physics are the foundation God used to create his masterpiece.”

Vittoria was heartbroken. But Father Leonardo had more news. He told Vittoria he had spoken to his bosses, and they agreed that Father Leonardo could adopt her.

“Would you like me to adopt you?” Leonardo asked.

“What does adopt mean?” Vittoria said.

Father Leonardo explained it to her.

Vittoria hugged him for five minutes, crying tears of joy. “Oh yes! Yes!”

Leonardo told her he had to leave for a while and get their new home set up in Switzerland, but he promised to send for her in six months. It was the longest wait of Vittoria’s life, but Leonardo kept his word. Five days before her ninth birthday, Vittoria moved to Geneva. She went to Geneva International School during the day and learned from her father at night.

Three years later Leonardo Vetra was hired by CERN. Vittoria and Leonardo moved to a wonderland unlike anything the young Vittoria had ever imagined.

Vittoria Vetra’s body felt numb as she walked down the LHC tunnel. She saw her faint reflection in the LHC and felt her father’s absence. Normally she was very calm, at peace with the world around her. But now, suddenly, nothing made sense. The last three hours had been a blur.

It had been 10 A.M. in the Balearic Islands when Kohler’s call came. Your father has been murdered. Come home immediately. Despite the sweltering heat on the deck of the dive boat, the words had chilled her to the bone, Kohler’s emotionless tone hurting as much as the news.

Now she had returned home. But home to what? CERN, her world since she was twelve, seemed suddenly strange. Her father, the man who had made it magical, was gone.

Deep breaths, she told herself, but she couldn’t calm her mind. The questions went around and around faster. Who killed her father? And why? Who was this American “specialist”? Why was Kohler insisting on seeing the lab?

Kohler had said there was evidence that her father’s murder was related to the current project. What evidence? Nobody knew what we were working on! And even if someone found out, why would they kill him?

As she moved down the LHC tunnel toward her father’s lab, Vittoria realized she was about to reveal her father’s greatest achievement without him there. She had imagined this moment very differently. She had imagined her father calling CERN’s top scientists to his lab, showing them his discovery, watching their amazed faces. Then he would beam with fatherly pride as he explained to them how it had been one of Vittoria’s ideas that had helped him make the project a reality… that his daughter had been essential in his breakthrough. Vittoria felt a lump in her throat. My father and I were supposed to share this moment together. But here she was alone. No colleagues. No happy faces. Just an American stranger and Maximilian Kohler.

Maximilian Kohler. Der Kцnig.

Even as a child, Vittoria disliked the man. Although she eventually respected his sharp mind, his cold way always seemed inhuman, the opposite of her father’s warmth. Kohler studied science for its perfect logic… her father for its spiritual wonder. Yet, there always seemed to be an unspoken respect between them. Genius , someone had once told her, accepts genius without question.

Genius , she thought. My father… Dad. Dead.

The entrance to Leonardo Vetra’s lab was a long, white-tiled hallway. Langdon felt like he was entering an underground mental hospital. Along the corridor were many framed, black-and-white pictures. Langdon had studied images for a living, but these were strange to him. They looked like messy negatives of random lines and swirls. Modern art? he wondered. Jackson Pollock on drugs?

“Scatter plots,” Vittoria said, noticing Langdon’s interest. “Computer images of particle crashes. That’s the Z-particle,” she said, pointing to a faint line almost hidden in the mess. “My father discovered it five years ago. Pure energy-no mass. It may be the smallest piece of nature. Matter is just trapped energy.”

Matter is energy? Langdon tilted his head. Sounds Zen. He looked at the tiny line in the picture and wondered what his friends in the Harvard physics department would say when he told them he’d spent the weekend in a Large Hadron Collider admiring Z-particles.

“Vittoria,” Kohler said, as they neared the lab’s large steel door, “I should tell you I came here this morning looking for your father.”

Vittoria blushed slightly. “You did?”

“Yes. And imagine my surprise when I saw he had replaced CERN’s regular keypad lock with something else.” Kohler pointed to a complex electronic device beside the door.

“I’m sorry,” she said. “You know how he was about privacy. He only wanted the two of us to have access.”

Kohler said, “Fine. Open the door.”

Vittoria stood for a long moment. Then, taking a deep breath, she walked to the device on the wall.

Langdon was completely unprepared for what happened next.

Vittoria stepped up to the device and carefully lined up her right eye with a lens that stuck out like a telescope. Then she pressed a button. Inside the machine, something clicked. A beam of light moved back and forth, scanning her eyeball like a copy machine.

“It’s a retina scan,” she said. “Perfect security. It only allows two retina patterns. Mine and my father’s.”

Robert Langdon stood in horrified realization. The image of Leonardo Vetra flashed back in gruesome detail—the bloody face, the single hazel eye staring back, and the empty eye socket. He tried to deny the obvious truth, but then he saw it… beneath the scanner on the white tile floor… faint spots of red. Dried blood.

Vittoria, thankfully, didn’t notice.

The steel door slid open, and she walked through.

Kohler stared intently at Langdon. His message was clear: As I told you… the missing eye has a greater purpose.
