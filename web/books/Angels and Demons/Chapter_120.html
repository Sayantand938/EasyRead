Angels and Demons

Chapter 120

Eleven-fifty-one P.M.

Necropolis literally means City of the Dead.

Nothing Robert Langdon had ever read about this place prepared him for seeing it. The huge underground space was full of crumbling tombs, like small houses on the floor of a cave. The air smelled dead. An uneven network of narrow paths twisted between the decaying memorials, most of which were broken brick with marble covering. Like columns of dust, many pillars of unexcavated earth rose up, holding up a dirt sky, which hung low over the shadowy village.

City of the dead , Langdon thought, feeling stuck between academic wonder and pure fear. He and the others hurried deeper down the winding paths. Did I make the wrong choice?

Chartrand had been the first to fall for the camerlegno’s influence, yanking open the gate and declaring his faith in the camerlegno. Glick and Macri, at the camerlegno’s suggestion, had generously agreed to provide light for the quest, although considering what rewards awaited them if they got out of here alive, their reasons were certainly questionable. Vittoria had been the least eager of all, and Langdon had seen in her eyes a wariness that looked, disturbingly, a lot like female intuition.

It’s too late now , he thought, he and Vittoria hurrying after the others. We’re committed.

Vittoria was silent, but Langdon knew they were thinking the same thing. Nine minutes is not enough time to get the hell out of Vatican City if the camerlegno is wrong.

As they ran on through the tombs, Langdon felt his legs getting tired, noticing to his surprise that the group was going up a steady slope. The explanation, when he realized it, sent shivers down his spine. The ground beneath his feet was like it was in Christ’s time. He was running up the original Vatican Hill! Langdon had heard Vatican scholars say that St. Peter’s tomb was near the top of Vatican Hill, and he had always wondered how they knew. Now he understood. The damn hill is still here!

Langdon felt like he was running through the pages of history. Somewhere ahead was St. Peter’s tomb-the Christian relic. It was hard to imagine that the original grave had been marked only with a simple shrine. Not anymore. As Peter’s importance spread, new shrines were built on top of the old, and now, the tribute stretched 440 feet overhead to the top of Michelangelo’s dome, the peak positioned directly over the original tomb within a fraction of an inch.

They kept climbing the winding paths. Langdon glanced at his watch. Eight minutes. He wondered if he and Vittoria would end up staying with the dead forever.

“Watch out!” Glick shouted from behind. “Snake holes!”

Langdon saw them just in time. A bunch of small holes dotted the path ahead. He jumped, just clearing them.

Vittoria jumped too, barely missing the small openings. She looked worried as they ran on. “Snake holes?”

“Snack holes, actually,” Langdon said. “Trust me, you don’t want to know.” He had just realized the holes were libation tubes. The early Christians believed the body would rise again, and they used the holes to “feed the dead” by pouring milk and honey into the tombs below.

The camerlegno felt faint.

He ran on, his duty to God and people giving him strength. Almost there. He was in terrible pain. The mind can cause more pain than the body. He still felt tired. He knew he didn't have much time left.

“I will save your church, Father. I promise.”

Despite the BBC lights behind him, which he appreciated, the camerlegno held his oil lamp high. I am a light in the dark. I am the light. The lamp swayed as he ran, and for a moment he worried the oil would spill and burn him. He had had enough burned skin for one night.

As he neared the top of the hill, he was covered in sweat and could barely breathe. But when he reached the top, he felt new again. He stumbled onto the flat ground where he had stood many times. Here the path stopped. The necropolis ended suddenly at a wall of dirt. A small sign read: Mausoleum S.

La tomba di San Pietro.

Before him, at waist level, was an opening in the wall. There was no fancy sign here. No big deal. Just a simple hole in the wall, leading to a small grotto and a plain, decaying coffin. The camerlegno looked into the hole and smiled wearily. He could hear the others coming up the hill behind him. He set down his oil lamp and knelt to pray.

Thank you, God. It is almost over.

Outside in the square, surrounded by amazed cardinals, Cardinal Mortati stared up at the media screen and watched the drama unfold in the crypt below. He no longer knew what to believe. Had the entire world just seen what he had seen? Had God truly spoken to the camerlegno? Was the antimatter really going to appear on St. Peter’s-

“Look!” A gasp went up from the crowd.

“There!” Everyone was suddenly pointing at the screen. “It’s a miracle!”

Mortati looked up. The camera angle was shaky, but it was clear enough. The image was unforgettable.

Filmed from behind, the camerlegno was kneeling in prayer on the dirt floor. In front of him was a roughly made hole in the wall. Inside the hollow, among the rubble of old stone, was a terra cotta casket. Although Mortati had seen the coffin only once in his life, he knew without a doubt what it contained.

San Pietro.

Mortati was not innocent enough to think that the shouts of joy and amazement now roaring through the crowd were praises for seeing one of Christianity’s most sacred relics. St. Peter’s tomb was not what had people falling to their knees in sudden prayer and gratitude. It was the object on top of his tomb.

The antimatter canister. It was there… where it had been all day… hidden in the darkness of the Necropolis. Sleek. Relentless. Deadly. The camerlegno’s discovery was correct.

Mortati stared in wonder at the clear cylinder. The drop of liquid still floated at its center. The grotto around the canister blinked red as the LED counted down to its final five minutes.

Also on the tomb, inches from the canister, was the wireless Swiss Guard security camera that had been pointed at the canister and transmitting the whole time.

Mortati crossed himself, sure this was the most frightening image he had ever seen. He realized, a moment later, however, that it was about to get worse.

The camerlegno stood suddenly. He grabbed the antimatter in his hands and turned toward the others. His face showed complete focus. He pushed past the others and began going down the Necropolis the way he had come, running down the hill.

The camera caught Vittoria Vetra, frozen in terror. “Where are you going! Camerlegno! I thought you said-”

“Have faith!” he shouted as he ran off.

Vittoria spun toward Langdon. “What do we do?”

Robert Langdon tried to stop the camerlegno, but Chartrand was now blocking him, apparently trusting the camerlegno’s belief.

The picture from the BBC camera was like a roller coaster now, winding, twisting. Quick glimpses of confusion and terror as the chaotic group stumbled through the shadows back toward the Necropolis entrance.

Out in the square, Mortati gasped in fear. “Is he bringing that up here?”

On televisions everywhere, bigger than life, the camerlegno raced up from the Necropolis with the antimatter. “There will be no more death tonight!”

But the camerlegno was wrong.
