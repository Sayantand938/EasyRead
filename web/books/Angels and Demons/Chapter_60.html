Angels and Demons

Chapter 60

Two blocks from the Pantheon, Langdon and Vittoria walked past a line of taxis, their drivers sleeping. Nap time seemed endless in the Eternal City—the common sight of people dozing a perfect continuation of the afternoon siestas that began in ancient Spain.

Langdon struggled to think clearly, but the situation was too strange to understand easily. Six hours ago, he had been fast asleep in Cambridge. Now he was in Europe, caught in a bizarre fight of ancient powers, carrying a semiautomatic in his Harris tweed, and holding hands with a woman he had just met.

He looked at Vittoria. She was looking straight ahead. He felt the strength of an independent and determined woman in her grip. Her fingers held his with the ease of natural acceptance. No hesitation. Langdon felt more and more drawn to her. Be realistic, he told himself.

Vittoria seemed to feel his discomfort. “Relax,” she said, without turning her head. “We’re supposed to look like newlyweds.”

“I’m relaxed.”

“You’re squeezing my hand too hard.”

Langdon blushed and loosened his grip.

“Breathe through your eyes,” she said.

“Excuse me?”

“It relaxes the muscles. It’s called pranayama.”

“Piranha?”

“Not the fish. Pranayama. Forget it.”

As they turned the corner into Piazza della Rotunda, the Pantheon came into view. Langdon admired it, as always, with awe. The Pantheon. Temple to all gods. Pagan gods. Gods of Nature and Earth. It looked more square from the outside than he remembered. The upright pillars and triangular pronaus almost hid the round dome behind it. Still, the strong inscription over the entrance told him they were in the right place. M AGRIPPA L F COS TERTIUM FECIT. Langdon translated it, as always, with amusement. Marcus Agrippa, Consul for the third time, built this.

So much for being humble, he thought, looking around. A few tourists with video cameras walked around. Others sat enjoying Rome’s best iced coffee at La Tazza di Oro’s outdoor cafe. Four armed Roman policemen stood guard outside the Pantheon's entrance, just as Olivetti had said.

“Looks pretty calm,” Vittoria said.

Langdon nodded, but he felt uneasy. Now that he was here, it all seemed unreal. Despite Vittoria’s belief that he was right, Langdon knew he had put everyone at risk. The Illuminati poem stuck in his mind. From Santi’s earthly tomb with demon’s hole. YES, he told himself. This was the place. Santi’s tomb. He had been here many times under the Pantheon’s oculus and stood before the grave of the great Raphael.

“What time is it?” Vittoria asked.

Langdon checked his watch. “Seven-fifty. Ten minutes until it starts.”

“Hope these guys are good,” Vittoria said, watching the tourists enter the Pantheon. “If anything happens inside that dome, we’ll all be caught in the middle.”

Langdon sighed as they walked toward the entrance. The gun felt heavy in his pocket. He wondered what would happen if the police searched him and found it, but they didn't even glance at them. It seemed the disguise worked.

Langdon whispered to Vittoria. “Ever shoot anything besides a tranquilizer gun?”

“Don’t you trust me?”

“Trust you? I hardly know you.”

Vittoria frowned. “And I thought we were newlyweds.”
