Angels and Demons

Chapter 73

Gunther Glick slammed the gas pedal in the BBC van and weaved through traffic, following the four fast Alpha Romeos across the Tiber River on Ponte Margherita. Usually, Glick would have tried to stay out of sight, but today he could barely keep up. These guys were flying.

Macri sat in her work area in the back of the van, finishing a phone call with London. She hung up and shouted to Glick over the traffic noise. “You want the good news or the bad news?”

Glick frowned. Nothing was ever easy when dealing with the home office. “Bad news.”

“Editorial is angry we left our spot.”

“Surprise.”

“They also think your source is a fake.”

“Of course.”

“And the boss just told me you’re not playing with a full deck.”

Glick scowled. “Great. And the good news?”

“They agreed to watch the footage we just shot.”

Glick felt his frown turn into a grin. I guess we’ll see who’s wrong. “So play it.”

“Can’t send it until we stop and get a clear cell signal.”

Glick sped the van onto Via Cola di Rienzo. “Can’t stop now.” He followed the Alpha Romeos through a sharp left turn around Piazza Risorgimento.

Macri held on to her computer in back as everything slid. “Break my transmitter,” she warned, “and we’ll have to carry this footage to London.”

“Hold on tight, love. Something tells me we’re almost there.”

Macri looked up. “Where?”

Glick looked out at the familiar dome now directly in front of them. He smiled. “Right back where we started.”

The four Alpha Romeos smoothly entered the traffic around St. Peter’s Square. They separated and spread out along the square's edge, quietly dropping off men at specific spots. The exiting guards moved into the crowd of tourists and media vans at the square's edge and immediately disappeared. Some of the guards went into the rows of pillars around the colonnade. They also seemed to vanish into the surroundings. As Langdon watched through the windshield, he felt a trap closing around St. Peter’s.

Besides the men Olivetti had just sent, the commander had called the Vatican and sent more undercover guards to the center where Bernini’s West Ponente was. As Langdon looked out at the open space of St. Peter’s Square, a familiar question bothered him. How does the Illuminati assassin plan to escape? How will he get a cardinal through all these people and kill him in public? Langdon checked his Mickey Mouse watch. It was 8:54 P.M. Six minutes.

In the front seat, Olivetti turned to Langdon and Vittoria. “I want you two right on top of this Bernini brick or block or whatever it is. Same as before. You’re tourists. Use the phone if you see anything.”

Before Langdon could answer, Vittoria grabbed his hand and pulled him out of the car.

The spring sun was setting behind St. Peter’s Basilica, and a huge shadow spread, covering the piazza. Langdon felt a bad chill as he and Vittoria stepped into the cool, dark shadow. Weaving through the crowd, Langdon looked at every face, wondering if the killer was among them. Vittoria’s hand felt warm.

As they crossed the open space of St. Peter’s Square, Langdon felt Bernini’s large piazza having the exact effect the artist intended - to "humble all those who entered." Langdon definitely felt humbled at the moment. Humbled and hungry , he thought, surprised such a normal thought could come to him at a time like this.

“To the obelisk?” Vittoria asked.

Langdon nodded, turning left across the piazza.

“Time?” Vittoria asked, walking quickly, but casually.

“Five ‘til.”

Vittoria said nothing, but Langdon felt her grip tighten. He still had the gun. He hoped Vittoria wouldn't want it. He couldn't imagine her pulling out a weapon in St. Peter’s Square and shooting the kneecaps off some killer while the world watched. Then again, that would be nothing compared to branding and murdering a cardinal out here.

Air , Langdon thought. The second element of science. He tried to picture the brand. The method of murder. Again he scanned the large granite space beneath his feet - St. Peter’s Square - an open desert surrounded by Swiss Guard. If the Hassassin really dared to do this, Langdon couldn't imagine how he would escape.

In the center of the piazza stood Caligula’s massive 350-ton Egyptian obelisk. It rose eighty-one feet into the air, topped by a pyramid-shaped point with a hollow iron cross. High enough to catch the last sunlight, the cross seemed to glow magically, supposedly holding pieces of the cross on which Christ died.

Two fountains stood on either side of the obelisk, perfectly balanced. Art historians knew the fountains marked the precise center points of Bernini’s oval piazza, but Langdon had never really thought about this architectural detail until today. It seemed Rome was suddenly full of ovals, pyramids, and surprising geometry.

As they got closer to the obelisk, Vittoria slowed down. She breathed out deeply, as if trying to get Langdon to relax with her. Langdon tried, lowering his shoulders and loosening his tight jaw.

Near the obelisk, prominently placed outside the world’s largest church, was the second altar of science—Bernini’s West Ponente —an oval block in St. Peter’s Square.

Gunther Glick watched from the shadows of the pillars around St. Peter’s Square. On any other day, the man in the tweed jacket and the woman in khaki shorts wouldn't have interested him at all. They looked like tourists enjoying the square. But today was different. Today had been a day of phone calls, dead bodies, unmarked cars speeding through Rome, and men in tweed jackets climbing scaffolding looking for something only God knew. Glick would stay with them.

He looked across the square and saw Macri. She was exactly where he had told her to be, on the other side of the couple, watching them. Macri held her video camera casually, but despite trying to look like a bored reporter, she stood out more than Glick wanted. No other reporters were in that corner of the square, and the letters “BBC” on her camera were attracting attention from tourists.

The video Macri had taken earlier of the naked body in the trunk was playing right now on the VCR transmitter in the van. Glick knew the images were being sent over his head to London. He wondered what the editors would say.

He wished he and Macri had gotten to the body sooner, before the plainclothes soldiers had stepped in. He knew the same soldiers had now spread out and surrounded the piazza. Something big was about to happen.

The media is the right arm of anarchy , the killer had said. Glick wondered if he had missed his chance for a big story. He looked at the other media vans in the distance and watched Macri follow the mysterious couple across the piazza. Something told Glick he was still in the game.
