Angels and Demons

Chapter 108

In the instant Langdon took to look around, he knew he was in a sacred place. The decorations in the long room, though old and worn, were full of familiar symbols. Star tiles. Planet paintings. Doves. Pyramids.

The Church of Illumination. Simple and pure. He was here.

Right in front of him, in the balcony opening, was the Hassassin. He was shirtless, standing over Vittoria, who was tied up but alive. Langdon felt relieved to see her. For a moment, their eyes met, and a flood of feelings passed—thanks, despair, and sorrow.

“We meet again,” the Hassassin said. He looked at the bar in Langdon’s hand and laughed. “And this time you come at me with that?”

“Untie her.”

The Hassassin put the knife to Vittoria’s throat. “I will kill her.”

Langdon knew the Hassassin could do it. He made his voice calm. “I think she would want it… considering what else could happen.”

The Hassassin smiled at the insult. “You’re right. She has a lot to offer. It would be a waste.”

Langdon stepped forward, holding the rusty bar, and pointed the broken end at the Hassassin. The cut on his hand stung. “Let her go.”

The Hassassin seemed to consider it for a moment. He exhaled and his shoulders dropped. It was a clear sign of surrender, but at that instant, the Hassassin’s arm seemed to speed up suddenly. There was a blur of dark muscle, and a blade tore through the air toward Langdon’s chest.

Whether it was instinct or exhaustion that made Langdon’s knees buckle, he didn’t know, but the knife flew past his left ear and clattered to the floor behind him. The Hassassin seemed unaffected. He smiled at Langdon, who was kneeling now, holding the metal bar. The killer stepped away from Vittoria and moved toward Langdon like a stalking lion.

As Langdon scrambled to his feet, lifting the bar again, his wet turtleneck and pants felt more restricting. The Hassassin, half-clothed, seemed to move much faster, the wound on his foot not slowing him at all. Langdon sensed this was a man used to pain. For the first time in his life, Langdon wished he were holding a very big gun.

The Hassassin circled slowly, as if enjoying himself, always just out of reach, moving toward the knife on the floor. Langdon cut him off. Then the killer moved back toward Vittoria. Again, Langdon cut him off.

“There’s still time,” Langdon said. “Tell me where the canister is. The Vatican will pay more than the Illuminati ever could.”

“You are naive.”

Langdon jabbed with the bar. The Hassassin dodged. He moved around a bench, holding the weapon in front of him, trying to corner the Hassassin in the oval room. This damn room has no corners! Oddly, the Hassassin didn’t seem interested in attacking or fleeing. He was simply playing Langdon’s game, coolly waiting.

Waiting for what? The killer kept circling, expertly positioning himself. It was like an endless game of chess. The weapon in Langdon’s hand was getting heavy, and he suddenly sensed he knew what the Hassassin was waiting for. He’s tiring me out. It was working, too. Langdon was hit by a wave of weariness, the adrenaline alone no longer enough to keep him alert. He knew he had to make a move.

The Hassassin seemed to read Langdon’s mind, shifting again, as if intentionally leading Langdon toward a table in the middle of the room. Langdon could tell there was something on the table. Something glinted in the torchlight. A weapon? Langdon kept his eyes focused on the Hassassin and moved closer to the table. When the Hassassin glanced at the table, a long, innocent look, Langdon tried to ignore the obvious bait. But instinct won. He stole a glance. The damage was done.

It was not a weapon at all. The sight momentarily froze him.

On the table was a simple copper chest, covered in old green film. The chest had five sides. The lid was open. Inside, in five padded spots, were five stamps. The stamps were made of iron—big tools for pressing designs with strong wooden handles. Langdon knew what they said.

ILLUMINATI, EARTH, AIR, FIRE, WATER.

Langdon quickly looked up, afraid the Hassassin would attack. He didn’t. The killer was waiting, as if he enjoyed the game. Langdon tried to focus, looking at his enemy again, ready to strike with the pipe. But the image of the box stayed in his mind. The stamps were fascinating—items that few Illuminati experts even thought existed—but Langdon suddenly realized that something else about the box had made him feel uneasy. As the Hassassin moved again, Langdon glanced down again.

My God!

In the chest, the five stamps were in spots around the edge. But in the center, there was another spot. This space was empty, but it was clearly made for another stamp… a stamp much bigger than the others, and perfectly square.

The attack happened fast.

The Hassassin rushed at him like a hawk. Langdon, his attention taken away, tried to defend himself, but the pipe felt heavy. He was too slow to block the attack. The Hassassin moved out of the way. As Langdon tried to pull the bar back, the Hassassin grabbed it. The man’s grip was strong, and his injured arm didn’t seem to bother him anymore. The two men fought hard. Langdon felt the bar pulled away, and a sharp pain went through his hand. A moment later, Langdon was staring at the broken point of the weapon. The hunter had become the hunted.

Langdon felt like he’d been hit hard. The Hassassin moved around him, smiling, pushing Langdon against the wall. “What is your American saying?” he teased. “Something about being too curious?”

Langdon could barely think. He was angry at his own carelessness as the Hassassin came closer. Nothing made sense. A sixth Illuminati stamp? Annoyed, he said, “I’ve never read about a sixth Illuminati stamp!”

“I think you probably have.” The killer chuckled as he moved Langdon around the curved wall.

Langdon was lost. He definitely had not. There were five Illuminati marks. He retreated, looking around for any weapon.

“A perfect mix of the old elements,” the Hassassin said. “The last mark is the brightest of all. I don’t think you’ll ever see it, though.”

Langdon felt he wouldn’t be seeing much longer. He kept backing away, looking for a way out. “And you’ve seen this last mark?” Langdon asked, trying to stall.

“Maybe someday they’ll honor me. When I prove myself.” He poked at Langdon, like he was enjoying a game.

Langdon slid back again. He felt the Hassassin was guiding him around the wall to somewhere he couldn't see. Where? Langdon couldn't look behind him. “The mark?” he asked. “Where is it?”

“Not here. Only Janus seems to have it.”

“Janus?” Langdon didn’t know the name.

“The Illuminati leader. He’ll be here soon.”

“The Illuminati leader is coming here?”

“To do the last marking.”

Langdon glanced at Vittoria, scared. She looked strangely calm, her eyes closed, breathing slowly and deeply. Was she the last victim? Was he?

“Such arrogance,” the Hassassin sneered, watching Langdon’s eyes. “You two are nothing. You will die, of course, that’s certain. But the final victim I'm talking about is a truly dangerous enemy.”

Langdon tried to understand the Hassassin’s words. A dangerous enemy? The top cardinals were all dead. The Pope was dead. The Illuminati had wiped them all out. Langdon found the answer in the emptiness of the Hassassin’s eyes.

The camerlegno.

Camerlegno Ventresca was the one man who had been a beacon of hope for the world through this whole ordeal. The camerlegno had done more to condemn the Illuminati tonight than decades of conspiracy theorists. Apparently, he would pay the price. He was the Illuminati’s final target.

“You’ll never get to him,” Langdon challenged.

“Not I,” the Hassassin replied, pushing Langdon farther back around the wall. “That honor is for Janus himself.”

“The Illuminati leader himself intends to brand the camerlegno?”

“Power has its privileges.”

“But no one could get into Vatican City right now!”

The Hassassin looked smug. “Not unless he had an appointment.”

Langdon was confused. The only person expected at the Vatican right now was the person the press was calling the 11th Hour Samaritan—the person Rocher said had information that could save—

Langdon stopped short. Good God!

The Hassassin smirked, clearly enjoying Langdon’s sickening realization. “I too wondered how Janus would get in. Then in the van I heard the radio—a report about an 11th hour Samaritan.” He smiled. “The Vatican will welcome Janus with open arms.”

Langdon almost stumbled backward. Janus is the Samaritan! It was an unthinkable trick. The Illuminati leader would get a royal escort directly to the camerlegno’s chambers. But how did Janus fool Rocher? Or was Rocher somehow involved? Langdon felt a chill. Ever since he had almost suffocated in the secret archives, Langdon had not entirely trusted Rocher.

The Hassassin jabbed suddenly, nicking Langdon in the side.

Langdon jumped back, his temper flaring. “Janus will never get out alive!”

The Hassassin shrugged. “Some causes are worth dying for.”

Langdon sensed the killer was serious. Janus coming to Vatican City on a suicide mission? A question of honor? For an instant, Langdon’s mind took in the entire terrifying cycle. The Illuminati plot had come full circle. The priest whom the Illuminati had accidentally brought to power by killing the Pope had emerged as a worthy enemy. In a final act of defiance, the Illuminati leader would destroy him.

Suddenly, Langdon felt the wall behind him disappear. There was a rush of cool air, and he staggered backward into the night. The balcony! He now realized what the Hassassin had in mind.

Langdon immediately felt the drop behind him—a hundred feet down to the courtyard. He'd noticed it on the way in. The Hassassin didn't wait. With a strong push, he attacked. The spear aimed for Langdon’s stomach. Langdon jumped back, and the spear missed, only catching his shirt. Again, the spear came at him. Langdon moved back more, feeling the railing right behind him. Knowing the next hit would kill him, Langdon tried something crazy. He spun to the side, reached out, and grabbed the spear, feeling pain shoot through his hand. Langdon held tight.

The Hassassin didn't seem bothered. They struggled against each other, face to face, the Hassassin's breath stinking in Langdon’s face. The railing started to give way. The Hassassin was too strong. In a last desperate move, Langdon stretched out his leg, losing balance as he tried to stomp on the Hassassin’s hurt toe. But the man was skilled and moved to protect himself.

Langdon had just played his last chance. And he knew he had lost.

The Hassassin's arms shot up, pushing Langdon back against the railing. Langdon felt only empty space behind him as the railing hit just below his rear. The Hassassin held the spear across and pushed it into Langdon’s chest. Langdon’s back bent over the edge.

“Ma’assalamah,” the Hassassin said with a nasty smile. "Good-bye."

With a cruel look, the Hassassin gave a final push. Langdon’s balance shifted, and his feet went up in the air. With only one chance to live, Langdon grabbed the railing as he fell over. His left hand slipped, but his right hand held on. He was now hanging upside down by his legs and one hand… struggling to hold on.

Standing over him, the Hassassin raised the spear, ready to bring it crashing down. As the spear started to move, Langdon saw something. Maybe it was because he was about to die, or just pure fear, but he suddenly felt a strange glow around the Hassassin. A bright light seemed to appear out of nowhere behind him… like a coming fireball.

Halfway through his swing, the Hassassin dropped the spear and screamed in pain.

The spear fell past Langdon into the night. The Hassassin turned away from him, and Langdon saw a burning mark on the killer’s back. Langdon pulled himself up to see Vittoria, her eyes shining, now facing the Hassassin.

Vittoria waved a torch, her face showing anger in the firelight. Langdon didn't know or care how she had escaped. He started climbing back over the railing.

The battle would be quick. The Hassassin was a lethal opponent. Yelling with rage, the killer charged at her. She tried to evade him, but he was on her, holding the torch and trying to take it. Langdon didn't hesitate. Jumping off the railing, Langdon slammed his fist into the raw burn on the Hassassin’s back.

The scream seemed to fill the entire Vatican.

The Hassassin froze briefly, his back arched in pain. He dropped the torch, and Vittoria shoved it forcefully into his face. Flesh sizzled as his left eye burned. He screamed again, raising his hands to his face.

“An eye for an eye,” Vittoria hissed. She swung the torch like a bat, and when it hit, the Hassassin stumbled back against the railing. Langdon and Vittoria attacked him together, both pushing with all their might. The Hassassin’s body flew backward over the railing into the night. There was no scream. The only sound was his spine breaking as he landed on a pile of cannonballs far below.

Langdon turned and stared at Vittoria in shock. Loose ropes hung off her waist and shoulders. Her eyes burned like fire.

“Houdini knew yoga.”
