Angels and Demons

Chapter 63

Gunther Glick had taken over the computer from Chinita Macri, who now stood hunched in the back of the small BBC van, staring in confusion over Glick’s shoulder.

“I told you,” Glick said, typing some more keys. “The British Tattler isn’t the only paper that publishes stories on these guys.”

Macri peered closer. Glick was right. The BBC database showed their respected network had picked up and run six stories in the past ten years on the group called the Illuminati. Well, I’ll be, she thought. “Who are the journalists who wrote the stories?” Macri asked. “Sensationalists?”

“BBC doesn’t hire sensationalists.”

“They hired you.”

Glick frowned. “I don’t know why you’re so doubtful. The Illuminati are well known throughout history.”

“So are witches, UFOs, and the Loch Ness Monster.”

Glick read the list of stories. “You ever heard of a guy called Winston Churchill?”

“Sounds familiar.”

"The BBC once made a historical show about Churchill's life. He was a strong Catholic, by the way. Did you know that in 1920, Churchill put out a statement criticizing the Illuminati and telling British people about a global plot against morals?"

Macri was doubtful. "Where was it printed? In the British Tattler?"

Glick smiled. "London Herald. February 8, 1920."

"No way."

"Take a look."

Macri looked closer at the clipping. London Herald. Feb. 8, 1920. I had no idea. "Well, Churchill was paranoid."

"He wasn't the only one," Glick said, reading on. "It seems Woodrow Wilson gave three radio talks in 1921, warning about the Illuminati gaining control over the U.S. banking system. Want me to read you a quote from the radio recording?"

"Not really."

Glick read one to her anyway. "He said, 'There is a power so well organized, so clever, so thorough, so widespread, that no one should dare to speak out against it.' "

"I've never heard of any of this."

“Maybe because in 1921 you were just a kid.”

“Charming.” Macri didn't let the comment bother her. She knew she was getting older. At forty-three, her thick black hair had gray strands. She was too proud to dye it. Her mom, a Southern Baptist, had taught Chinita to be happy and respect herself. When you’re a black woman , her mother said, you can’t hide who you are. The day you try, is the day you die. Stand tall, smile bright, and let them wonder what secret makes you laugh.

“Ever heard of Cecil Rhodes?” Glick asked.

Macri looked up. “The British banker?”

“Yeah. He started the Rhodes Scholarships.”

“Don’t tell me-”

“Illuminatus.”

“That’s not true.”

“The BBC says so. November 16, 1984.”

“We said Cecil Rhodes was Illuminati?”

“Sure did. And our network says the Rhodes Scholarships were funds started long ago to bring the world's smartest young people into the Illuminati.”

“That’s ridiculous! My uncle was a Rhodes Scholar!”

Glick winked. “So was Bill Clinton.”

Macri was getting angry. She hated careless, sensational reporting. But she knew the BBC researched and checked every story carefully.

“Here’s one you’ll remember,” Glick said. “BBC, March 5, 1998. Parliament Committee Chair, Chris Mullin, made all British Parliament members who were Masons say they were members.”

Macri remembered it. The rule later included policemen and judges. “Why was that?”

Glick read, “… concern that secret groups within the Masons had a lot of control over political and financial systems.”

“That’s right.”

“It caused a stir. The Masons in parliament were angry. They had a right to be. Most were innocent men who joined the Masons for networking and charity. They didn't know about the brotherhood's past connections.”

“Possible connections.”

“Whatever.” Glick scanned the articles. “Look at this stuff. Stories tracing the Illuminati back to Galileo, the Guerenets of France, the Alumbrados of Spain. Even Karl Marx and the Russian Revolution.”

“History tends to get rewritten.”

“Fine, you want something recent? Look at this. Here’s an Illuminati mention from a recent Wall Street Journal.”

This got Macri’s attention. “The Journal?”

“Guess what the most popular online computer game in America is right now?”

“Pin the tail on Pamela Anderson.”

“Close. It’s called, Illuminati: New World Order.”

Macri looked at the summary. “Steve Jackson Games has a huge hit… a sort-of historical game where an old evil group from Bavaria tries to take over the world. You can find them online at …” Macri looked up, feeling sick. “Why do these Illuminati guys hate Christianity so much?”

“Not just Christianity,” Glick said. “Religion in general.” Glick tilted his head and grinned. “Though from the phone call we just had, it seems they really do dislike the Vatican.”

“Oh, come on. You don’t actually believe that the guy who called is who he says he is, do you?”

“An Illuminati messenger? About to kill four cardinals?” Glick smiled. “I really hope so.”
