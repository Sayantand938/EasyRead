Angels and Demons

Chapter 14

Langdon stepped out of Building C into the open air, glad to be outside Vetra’s apartment. The sun helped erase the image of the empty eye socket burned into his mind.

“This way, please,” Kohler said, turning onto a steep path. The electric wheelchair seemed to speed up easily. “Ms. Vetra will be arriving any moment.”

Langdon hurried to keep up.

“So,” Kohler asked. “Do you still doubt the Illuminati are involved?”

Langdon didn't know what to think anymore. Vetra’s religious connections were definitely worrying, but Langdon couldn't abandon all the academic evidence he had researched. Besides, there was the eye…

“I still believe,” Langdon said, more strongly than he meant to, “that the Illuminati are not responsible for this murder. The missing eye proves it.”

“What?”

“Random mutilation,” Langdon explained, “is not something the Illuminati would do. Experts on cults see unplanned damage from inexperienced, extreme groups—zealots who commit random acts of terrorism—but the Illuminati have always been more calculated.”

“Calculated? Surgically removing someone’s eyeball is not calculated?”

“It sends no clear message. It serves no higher purpose.”

Kohler’s wheelchair stopped at the top of the hill. He turned. “Mr. Langdon, believe me, that missing eye does serve a higher purpose… a much higher purpose.”

As the two men walked across the grassy hill, the sound of helicopter blades grew louder from the west. A chopper came into view, flying across the open valley toward them. It turned sharply and then hovered over a helipad painted on the grass.

Langdon watched, feeling distant, his mind racing like the blades, wondering if a full night’s sleep would make his confusion any better. He doubted it.

As the landing gear touched down, a pilot jumped out and began unloading gear. There was a lot of it – duffel bags, waterproof bags, scuba tanks, and crates of what looked like advanced diving equipment.

Langdon was puzzled. “Is that Ms. Vetra’s gear?” he yelled to Kohler over the loud engines.

Kohler nodded and yelled back, “She was doing biological research in the Balearic Sea.”

“I thought you said she was a physicist!”

“She is. She’s a Bio Entanglement Physicist. She studies how life systems connect. Her work is closely related to her father’s work in particle physics. Recently, she disproved one of Einstein’s main theories by using atomically synchronized cameras to watch a school of tuna fish.”

Langdon looked at his host’s face for any sign of a joke. Einstein and tuna fish? He was beginning to think the X-33 space plane had dropped him off on the wrong planet.

A moment later, Vittoria Vetra came out of the helicopter. Robert Langdon knew today would be full of surprises. Coming down from the chopper in her khaki shorts and white sleeveless top, Vittoria Vetra didn't look like the scholarly physicist he had imagined. She was slender and graceful, tall with brown skin and long black hair that blew in the rotor wind. Her face was clearly Italian-not stunning, but with strong, natural features that seemed to radiate a pure sensuality, even from twenty yards away. As the wind pushed against her, her clothes stuck to her body, showing off her slim waist and small breasts.

“Ms. Vetra is a very strong person,” Kohler said, as if sensing Langdon’s fascination. “She spends months working in risky natural environments. She is a strict vegetarian and CERN’s expert in Hatha yoga.”

Hatha yoga? Langdon wondered. The old Buddhist practice of meditative stretching seemed like an unusual skill for the physicist daughter of a Catholic priest.

Langdon watched Vittoria walk over. She had clearly been crying, her dark eyes full of feelings Langdon couldn't name. Still, she moved toward them with energy and authority. Her arms and legs were strong and firm, glowing with the healthy radiance of Mediterranean skin that had spent a lot of time in the sun.

“Vittoria,” Kohler said as she got closer. “I'm so sorry for your loss. It’s a terrible blow for science… for everyone here at CERN.”

Vittoria nodded in thanks. When she spoke, her voice was soft-a husky, accented English. “Do you know who did this yet?”

“We’re still trying to find out.”

She turned to Langdon, offering a slim hand. “My name is Vittoria Vetra. You’re with Interpol, I guess?”

Langdon took her hand, briefly captivated by the depth of her teary eyes. “Robert Langdon.” He didn't know what else to say.

“Mr. Langdon isn’t with the police,” Kohler explained. “He’s an expert from the U.S. He’s here to help us find out who is responsible for this.”

Vittoria looked unsure. “And the police?”

Kohler sighed but said nothing.

“Where is his body?” she asked.

“Being taken care of.”

The small lie surprised Langdon.

“I want to see him,” Vittoria said.

“Vittoria,” Kohler pleaded, “your father was killed in a horrible way. It would be better to remember him as he was.”

Vittoria started to speak but was cut off.

“Hey, Vittoria!” voices called from afar. “Welcome home!”

She turned. A group of scientists walking by the helipad waved cheerfully.

“Disprove any more of Einstein’s theories?” one shouted.

Another added, “Your dad must be proud!”

Vittoria gave the men an awkward wave as they passed. Then she turned to Kohler, her face now showing confusion. “Nobody knows yet?”

“I thought it best to be discreet.”

“You haven’t told the staff my father was murdered?” Her confused tone now had an edge of anger.

Kohler’s tone turned stern. “Perhaps you forget, Ms. Vetra, that as soon as I report your father’s murder, there will be a CERN investigation. Including a close look at his lab. I have always tried to respect your father’s privacy. Your father told me only two things about your current project. One, that it could bring CERN millions in licensing deals in the next ten years. And two, that it’s not ready to be public because the technology is still dangerous. Given these two things, I'd rather strangers not snoop around his lab and either steal his work or accidentally kill themselves, making CERN responsible. Am I understood?”

Vittoria stared, silent. Langdon sensed she reluctantly respected and accepted Kohler’s reasoning.

“Before we tell the authorities anything,” Kohler said, “I need to know what you two were working on. I need you to take us to your lab.”

“The lab has nothing to do with this,” Vittoria said. “Nobody knew what my father and I were doing. The experiment couldn’t possibly have anything to do with my father’s murder.”

Kohler breathed out a rough, unhealthy breath. “The evidence suggests otherwise.”

“Evidence? What evidence?”

Langdon was wondering the same thing.

Kohler dabbed his mouth again. “You’ll just have to trust me.”

Vittoria’s angry stare made it clear that she did not.
