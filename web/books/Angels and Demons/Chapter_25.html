Angels and Demons

Chapter 25

Haz-Mat. Fifty meters below ground.

Vittoria Vetra stumbled forward, almost falling into the retina scan. She felt the American rush to help her, holding her, supporting her. On the floor at her feet, her father’s eyeball stared up. She felt like she couldn't breathe. They cut out his eye! Her world spun. Kohler pressed close behind, speaking. Langdon guided her. As if in a dream, she found herself gazing into the retina scan. The mechanism beeped.

The door slid open.

Even with the terror of her father’s eye piercing her soul, Vittoria sensed another horror waiting inside. When she focused her blurry gaze into the room, she saw the next stage of the nightmare. Before her, the solitary recharging podium was empty.

The canister was gone. They had cut out her father’s eye to steal it. The implications came too fast for her to fully understand. Everything had gone wrong. The specimen meant to prove antimatter was a safe energy source had been stolen. But nobody knew this specimen existed! The truth was clear. Someone had found out. Vittoria couldn't imagine who. Even Kohler, who supposedly knew everything at CERN, clearly had no idea about the project.

Her father was dead. Murdered for his genius.

As the grief tore through her heart, a new emotion surged into Vittoria’s mind. This one was far worse. Crushing. Stabbing at her. The emotion was guilt. Uncontrollable, constant guilt. Vittoria knew she had convinced her father to create the specimen. Against his better judgment. And he had been killed for it.

A quarter of a gram…

Like any powerful technology—fire, gunpowder, the combustion engine—antimatter in the wrong hands could be deadly. Very deadly. Antimatter was a lethal weapon. Powerful, and unstoppable. Once removed from its recharging platform at CERN, the canister would count down without stopping. A runaway train.

And when time ran out…

A blinding light. The roar of thunder. Instant burning. Just the flash… and an empty crater. A big empty crater.

The thought of her father’s quiet genius used as a weapon of destruction was like poison in her blood. Antimatter was the perfect terrorist weapon. It had no metal parts to set off detectors, no chemical smell for dogs to find, no fuse to cut if the police found it. The countdown had begun…

Langdon didn’t know what to do. He took his handkerchief and laid it on the floor over Leonardo Vetra’s eyeball. Vittoria was now standing in the doorway of the empty Haz-Mat chamber, her face full of grief and fear. Langdon moved toward her again, without thinking, but Kohler stopped him.

“Mr. Langdon?” Kohler’s face showed nothing. He motioned Langdon away from Vittoria. Langdon reluctantly followed, leaving Vittoria alone. “You’re the expert,” Kohler said, his whisper intense. “I want to know what these Illuminati are going to do with this antimatter.”

Langdon tried to focus. Despite the chaos around him, his first thought was logical. Academic disagreement. Kohler was still making assumptions. Impossible assumptions. “The Illuminati are gone, Mr. Kohler. I still believe that. This crime could be anything—maybe even another CERN employee who learned about Mr. Vetra’s discovery and thought the project was too dangerous.”

Kohler looked shocked. “You think this is a crime of conscience, Mr. Langdon? Ridiculous. Whoever killed Leonardo wanted one thing—the antimatter. And they definitely have plans for it.”

“You mean terrorism.”

“Clearly.”

“But the Illuminati weren’t terrorists.”

“Tell that to Leonardo Vetra.”

Langdon felt the truth in the statement hit him hard. Leonardo Vetra had indeed been branded with the Illuminati symbol. Where did it come from? The sacred brand seemed too hard to fake if someone was just trying to mislead investigators. There had to be another reason.

Again, Langdon forced himself to think about the unlikely. If the Illuminati were still around, and if they stole the antimatter, what would they want to do? What would they attack? The answer came to him right away. Langdon quickly dismissed it. It was true the Illuminati had a clear enemy, but a huge terrorist attack against that enemy didn't make sense. It wasn't their style. Yes, the Illuminati had killed people, but individuals, carefully chosen targets. Mass destruction seemed too crude. Langdon paused. Then again, he thought, there would be a grand statement in it—antimatter, the greatest scientific achievement, being used to destroy—

He refused to believe the absurd idea. “There is,” he said suddenly, “a logical reason besides terrorism.”

Kohler stared, clearly waiting.

Langdon tried to organize his thoughts. The Illuminati had always had a lot of power because of money. They controlled banks. They owned gold. They were even said to have the most valuable gem on earth—the Illuminati Diamond, a perfect diamond of huge size. “Money,” Langdon said. “The antimatter could have been stolen to make money.”

Kohler looked doubtful. “Money? Who would buy a drop of antimatter?”

“Not the antimatter itself,” Langdon replied. “The technology. Antimatter technology must be worth a lot. Maybe someone stole the antimatter to study it and do research.”

“Industrial spying? But that canister only has twenty-four hours before it runs out of power. The researchers would blow themselves up before they learned anything.”

“They could recharge it before it explodes. They could build a recharging station like the ones here at CERN.”

“In twenty-four hours?” Kohler questioned. “Even if they stole the plans, a recharger like that would take months to build, not hours!”

“He’s right.” Vittoria’s voice was weak.

Both men turned. Vittoria was moving toward them, walking as shakily as she spoke.

“He’s right. Nobody could rebuild a recharger that fast. The interface alone would take weeks. Flux filters, servo-coils, power conditioning alloys, all set for the specific energy level of the area.”

Langdon frowned. He understood. An antimatter trap was not something you could just plug into a wall. Once taken from CERN, the canister was on a one-way, twenty-four-hour trip to destruction.

Which left only one, very worrying, idea.

“We need to call Interpol,” Vittoria said. Even to herself, her voice sounded far away. “We need to call the right people. Now.”

Kohler shook his head. “Absolutely not.”

The words shocked her. “No? What do you mean?”

“You and your father have made things very hard for me.”

“Director, we need help. We need to find that trap and get it back here before someone gets hurt. It’s our duty!”

“Our duty is to think,” Kohler said, his voice turning sharp. “This could have very, very serious consequences for CERN.”

“You’re worried about CERN’s image? Do you know what that canister could do to a city? It can destroy everything within a half mile! Nine city blocks!”

“Maybe you and your father should have thought about that before you made it.”

Vittoria felt like she’d been stabbed. “But… we were as careful as possible.”

“Clearly, it wasn’t enough.”

“But nobody knew about the antimatter.” She knew it was a silly thing to say. Of course someone knew. Someone had found out.

Vittoria had told no one. That meant only two things. Either her father had told someone without telling her, which didn’t make sense because her father had made them promise to keep it secret, or she and her father had been watched. Their cell phone, maybe? She knew they had talked a few times while Vittoria was traveling. Had they said too much? Maybe. Or their email. But they had been careful, right? CERN’s security? Had they been watched without knowing it? She knew it didn’t matter now. What was done, was done. My father is dead.

The thought made her move. She took her cell phone from her shorts pocket.

Kohler hurried toward her, coughing hard, his eyes full of anger. “Who… are you calling?”

“CERN’s switchboard. They can connect us to Interpol.”

“Think!” Kohler choked, screeching to a halt in front of her. “Are you really so simple? That canister could be anywhere in the world by now. No intelligence agency on earth could possibly move fast enough to find it in time.”

“So we do nothing? ” Vittoria felt bad for challenging a man in such poor health, but the director was so out of line she didn’t even know him anymore.

“We do what is smart ,” Kohler said. “We don’t risk CERN’s reputation by involving authorities who can’t help anyway. Not yet. Not without thinking.”

Vittoria knew there was logic somewhere in Kohler’s argument, but she also knew that logic, by its very nature, lacked moral responsibility. Her father had lived for moral responsibility-careful science, accountability, faith in man’s natural goodness. Vittoria believed in those things too, but she saw them in terms of karma. Turning away from Kohler, she snapped open her phone.

“You can’t do that,” he said.

“Just try and stop me.”

Kohler did not move.

An instant later, Vittoria realized why. This far underground, her cell phone had no signal.

Fuming, she headed for the elevator.
