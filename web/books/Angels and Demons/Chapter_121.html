Angels and Demons

Chapter 121

The camerlegno burst through the doors of St. Peter’s Basilica at exactly 11:56 P.M. He stumbled into the bright glare of the world spotlight, carrying the antimatter before him like a holy offering. Through burning eyes he could see himself, half-naked and wounded, looking huge on the media screens around the square. The roar from the crowd in St. Peter’s Square was like nothing he had ever heard—crying, screaming, chanting, praying… a mix of worship and fear.

Save us from evil, he whispered.

He felt completely drained from his race out of the Necropolis. It had almost been a disaster. Robert Langdon and Vittoria Vetra had wanted to stop him, to throw the canister back into its underground hiding place, to run outside for cover. Blind fools!

The camerlegno now understood, with frightening clarity, that on any other night, he would never have won the race. But tonight, God was with him again. Robert Langdon, about to pass the camerlegno, had been grabbed by Chartrand, always trusting and obedient to the camerlegno’s orders to have faith. The reporters, of course, were mesmerized and carrying too much equipment to interfere.

God works in mysterious ways.

The camerlegno could hear the others behind him now… see them on the screens, getting closer. Gathering the last of his strength, he raised the antimatter high over his head. Then, throwing back his bare shoulders in defiance of the Illuminati mark on his chest, he ran down the stairs.

There was one final thing to do.

Godspeed, he thought. Godspeed.

Four minutes…

Langdon could barely see as he burst out of the basilica. Again the sea of media lights burned into his eyes. All he could make out was the blurry shape of the camerlegno, directly ahead of him, running down the stairs. For an instant, glowing in the media lights, the camerlegno looked like an angel, like some kind of modern god. His cassock was at his waist like a burial cloth. His body was scarred and wounded by his enemies, and still he kept going. The camerlegno ran on, standing tall, calling out to the world to have faith, running toward the crowd carrying this weapon of destruction.

Langdon ran down the stairs after him. What is he doing? He will kill them all!

“Satan’s work,” the camerlegno screamed, “has no place in the House of God!” He ran on toward a now terrified crowd.

“Father!” Langdon screamed, behind him. “There’s nowhere to go!”

“Look to the heavens! We forget to look to the heavens!”

In that moment, as Langdon saw where the camerlegno was headed, the wonderful truth came flooding all around him. Although Langdon could not see it because of the lights, he knew their salvation was directly overhead.

A star-filled Italian sky. The escape route.

The helicopter the camerlegno had called to take him to the hospital sat right ahead, pilot already in the cockpit, blades already humming. As the camerlegno ran toward it, Langdon felt a sudden rush of excitement.

The thoughts that tore through Langdon’s mind came quickly…

First he pictured the wide-open Mediterranean Sea. How far was it? Five miles? Ten? He knew the beach at Fiumocino was only about seven minutes by train. But by helicopter, 200 miles an hour, no stops… If they could fly the canister far enough out to sea, and drop it… There were other options too, he realized, feeling almost weightless as he ran. La Cava Romana! The marble quarries north of the city were less than three miles away. How large were they? Two square miles? Surely they were empty at this hour! Dropping the canister there…

“Everyone back!” the camerlegno yelled. His chest ached as he ran. “Get away! Now!”

The Swiss Guard standing around the chopper stared in disbelief as the camerlegno approached them.

“Back!” the priest screamed.

The guards moved back.

With the entire world watching in wonder, the camerlegno ran around the chopper to the pilot’s door and yanked it open. “Out, son! Now!”

The guard jumped out.

The camerlegno looked at the high cockpit seat and knew that in his exhausted state, he would need both hands to pull himself up. He turned to the pilot, trembling beside him, and thrust the canister into his hands. “Hold this. Hand it back when I’m in.”

As the camerlegno pulled himself up, he could hear Robert Langdon yelling excitedly, running toward the craft. Now you understand , the camerlegno thought. Now you have faith!

The camerlegno pulled himself up into the cockpit, adjusted a few familiar levers, and then turned back to his window for the canister.

But the guard to whom he had given the canister stood empty-handed. “He took it!” the guard yelled.

The camerlegno's heart clenched. “Who!”

The guard pointed. “Him!”

Robert Langdon was surprised by the canister's weight. He ran to the other side of the chopper and jumped into the back where he and Vittoria had been just hours before. He left the door open and buckled in. Then he yelled to the camerlegno in the front.

“Fly, Father!”

The camerlegno twisted back to Langdon, his face pale with fear. “What are you doing!”

“You fly! I’ll throw!” Langdon snapped. “There’s no time! Just fly the damn chopper!”

The camerlegno seemed frozen for a moment, the bright media lights in the cockpit deepening the lines on his face. “I can do this alone,” he whispered. “I am supposed to do this alone.”

Langdon wasn’t listening. Fly! he heard himself shout. Now! I’m here to help you! Langdon glanced at the canister and his breath caught when he saw the numbers. “Three minutes, Father! Three!”

The number seemed to shock the camerlegno back to his senses. Without a second thought, he turned back to the controls. With a loud roar, the helicopter rose.

Through a cloud of dust, Langdon saw Vittoria running toward the chopper. Their eyes met, and then she fell away like a stone.

Inside the chopper, the engine's whine and the wind from the open door hit Langdon with deafening noise. He braced himself against the heavy pull of gravity as the camerlegno sped the craft straight up. The glow of St. Peter’s Square shrank below them until it was a blurry, glowing oval shining in a sea of city lights.

The antimatter canister felt heavy in Langdon’s hands. He gripped it tighter, his palms now slick with sweat and blood. Inside the trap, the bit of antimatter floated calmly, pulsing red in the glow of the LED countdown clock.

“Two minutes!” Langdon yelled, wondering where the camerlegno planned to drop the canister.

The city lights below them spread out in all directions. In the distance to the west, Langdon could see the twinkling line of the Mediterranean coast—a rough border of light beyond which was an endless dark space of nothing. The sea looked farther now than Langdon had thought. Also, the number of lights at the coast was a stark reminder that even far out at sea an explosion could be devastating. Langdon hadn't even considered the effects of a ten-kiloton tidal wave hitting the coast.

When Langdon turned and looked straight ahead through the cockpit window, he felt more hopeful. Directly in front of them, the rolling shadows of the Roman foothills rose in the night. The hills were dotted with lights—the villas of the very rich—but a mile or so north, the hills went dark. There were no lights at all—just a huge pocket of blackness. Nothing.

The quarries! Langdon thought. La Cava Romana!

Staring hard at the empty pocket of land, Langdon felt that it was large enough. It seemed close, too. Much closer than the ocean. Excitement rushed through him. This was clearly where the camerlegno planned to take the antimatter! The chopper was heading right toward it! The quarries! But strangely, as the engines roared louder and the chopper sped through the air, Langdon could see that the quarries weren't getting any closer. Confused, he glanced out the side door to get his bearings. What he saw drowned his excitement in a wave of panic. Directly below them, thousands of feet straight down, glowed the media lights in St. Peter’s Square.

We’re still over the Vatican!

“Camerlegno!” Langdon choked. “Go forward! We’re high enough! You need to start moving forward! We can’t drop the canister back over Vatican City!”

The camerlegno didn't reply. He seemed focused on flying the chopper.

"We've got less than two minutes!" Langdon yelled, holding up the canister. "I see them! La Cava Romana! A couple of miles north! We don't have-"

"No," the camerlegno said. "It's too dangerous. I'm sorry." As the chopper kept climbing, the camerlegno turned and gave Langdon a sad smile. "I wish you hadn't come, my friend. You've made the ultimate sacrifice."

Langdon looked into the camerlegno's tired eyes and suddenly understood. His blood ran cold. "But... there must be somewhere we can go!"

“Up,” the camerlegno replied, his voice defeated. "It's the only sure thing."

Langdon could barely think. He had completely misunderstood the camerlegno’s plan. Look to the heavens!

Heaven, Langdon now realized, was literally where he was going. The camerlegno never planned to drop the antimatter. He was just getting it as far from Vatican City as possible.

This was a one-way trip.
