Angels and Demons

Chapter 79

Vittoria Vetra took a sip of water and nibbled without interest on some tea scones just brought out by one of the Swiss Guards. She knew she should eat, but she wasn't hungry. The Pope's Office was now full of activity, the sound of worried talk filling the air. Captain Rocher, Commander Olivetti, and several guards were looking at the damage and discussing what to do next.

Robert Langdon stood nearby, gazing out at St. Peter’s Square. He looked sad. Vittoria walked over to him. “Any ideas?”

He shook his head.

“Scone?”

His mood seemed to improve at the sight of food. “Yes, please. Thanks.” He ate quickly.

The conversation behind them suddenly stopped when two Swiss Guards led Camerlegno Ventresca through the door. If the chamberlain had looked worn out before, Vittoria thought, he now looked completely empty.

“What happened?” the camerlegno asked Olivetti. From the look on the camerlegno’s face, he seemed to already know the worst of it.

Olivetti’s official report sounded like a list of casualties from a battle. He stated the facts plainly and efficiently. “Cardinal Ebner was found dead in the church of Santa Maria del Popolo just after eight o’clock. He had been suffocated and branded with the ambigram word ‘Earth.’ Cardinal Lamassé was murdered in St. Peter’s Square ten minutes ago. He died from wounds to the chest. He was branded with the word ‘Air,’ also an ambigram. The killer got away in both cases.”

The camerlegno walked across the room and sat heavily behind the Pope’s desk. He lowered his head.

Cardinals Guidera and Baggia, however, are still alive.

The camerlegno’s head shot up, looking hurt. “Is that supposed to make me feel better? Two cardinals have been killed, commander. And the other two won't live much longer if you don't find them.”

“We will find them,” Olivetti promised. “I feel hopeful.”

“Hopeful? We've only failed.”

“That’s not true. We’ve lost two battles, but we’re winning the war. The Illuminati wanted to make tonight a media spectacle. We’ve stopped them so far. Both cardinals' bodies have been found without trouble. Also,” Olivetti added, “Captain Rocher says he's making good progress on finding the antimatter.”

Captain Rocher stepped forward in his red beret. Vittoria thought he seemed more human than the other guards—serious but not stiff. Rocher’s voice was full of feeling and clear, like a violin. “I hope we'll have the canister for you within an hour.”

“Captain,” the camerlegno said, “forgive me if I don't seem hopeful, but I thought searching Vatican City would take much longer than we have.”

“A full search would. But after looking at the situation, I believe the antimatter canister is in one of our white zones—the parts of the Vatican open to public tours—like the museums and St. Peter’s Basilica. We’ve already cut off power in those zones and are scanning them.”

“You're only searching a small part of Vatican City?”

“Yes. It’s not likely that someone got into the inner parts of Vatican City. The fact that the missing security camera was taken from a public area—a stairwell in one of the museums—suggests the intruder had limited access. So, he could only have moved the camera and antimatter to another public area. That’s where we’re focusing our search.”

“But the intruder kidnapped four cardinals. That suggests they're deeper inside than we thought.”

“Not necessarily. Remember, the cardinals spent much of today in the Vatican museums and St. Peter’s Basilica, enjoying those areas without the crowds. It's likely the missing cardinals were taken in one of these areas.”

“But how were they taken out of our walls?”

“We are still figuring that out.”

“I see.” The camerlegno sighed and stood up. He walked over to Olivetti. “Commander, tell me your evacuation plan.”

“We are still finalizing it, signore. In the meantime, I trust Captain Rocher will find the canister.”

Rocher clicked his boots as if he appreciated the trust. “My men have already scanned two-thirds of the white zones. We're very hopeful.”

The camerlegno did not seem to share that hope.

At that moment the guard with a scar beneath one eye came through the door carrying a clipboard and a map. He walked toward Langdon. “Mr. Langdon? I have the information you asked for on the West Ponente.”

Langdon swallowed his scone. “Good. Let’s take a look.”

The others kept talking while Vittoria joined Robert and the guard as they spread out the map on the Pope’s desk.

The soldier pointed to St. Peter’s Square. “This is where we are. The main line of West Ponente ‘s wind points directly east, straight out of Vatican City.” The guard traced a line with his finger from St. Peter’s Square across the Tiber River and into the center of old Rome. “As you can see, the line goes through almost all of Rome. There are about twenty Catholic churches that are close to this line.”

Langdon frowned. “ Twenty?”

“Maybe more.”

“Do any of the churches sit right on the line?”

“Some look closer than others,” the guard said, “but putting the exact direction of the West Ponente on a map can have errors.”

Langdon looked out at St. Peter’s Square for a moment. Then he scowled, rubbing his chin. “What about fire? Does any of them have Bernini artwork about fire?”

Silence.

“What about obelisks?” he asked. “Are any of the churches near obelisks?”

The guard started looking at the map.

Vittoria saw a glimmer of hope in Langdon’s eyes and realized what he was thinking. He’s right! The first two markers had been found on or near squares with obelisks! Maybe obelisks were a pattern? Tall pyramids marking the Illuminati path? The more Vittoria thought about it, the more fitting it seemed… four towering beacons rising over Rome to mark the altars of science.

“It’s a long shot,” Langdon said, “but I know that many of Rome’s obelisks were put up or moved when Bernini was in power. He likely had a say in where they were placed.”

“Or,” Vittoria added, “Bernini could have put his markers near existing obelisks.”

Langdon nodded. “True.”

“Bad news,” the guard said. “No obelisks on the line.” He pointed to the map. “None even close. Nothing.”

Langdon sighed.

Vittoria’s shoulders slumped. She’d thought it was a good idea. It seemed this wasn’t going to be as easy as they’d hoped. She tried to stay positive. “Robert, think. You must know a Bernini statue about fire. Anything at all.”

“Believe me, I’ve been thinking. Bernini made a lot of art. Hundreds of works. I was hoping West Ponente would point to a single church. Something that would stand out.”

“Fuтco,” she pressed. “Fire. No Bernini titles come to mind?”

Langdon shrugged. “There are his famous sketches of Fireworks, but they’re not sculptures, and they’re in Leipzig, Germany.”

Vittoria frowned. “And you’re sure the breath shows the direction?”

“You saw the carving, Vittoria. The design was completely balanced. The only clue to direction was the breath.”

Vittoria knew he was right.

“Besides,” he added, “since the West Ponente means Air , following the breath feels like the right thing to do.”

Vittoria nodded. So we follow the breath. But where?

Olivetti came over. “What do you have?”

“Too many churches,” the soldier said. “About two dozen. I guess we could put four men at each church-”

“Forget it,” Olivetti said. “We missed this guy twice when we knew exactly where he would be. A huge stakeout means leaving Vatican City unprotected and stopping the search.”

“We need a reference book,” Vittoria said. “A list of Bernini’s work. If we can check titles, maybe something will stand out.”

“I don’t know,” Langdon said. “If Bernini made it just for the Illuminati, it might be very hidden. It probably won’t be in any book.”

Vittoria didn't want to believe it. “The other two sculptures were pretty famous. You’d heard of them both.”

Langdon shrugged. “Yeah.”

“If we look for titles that mention ‘fire,’ maybe we’ll find a statue that’s said to be in the right direction.”

Langdon seemed to think it was worth trying. He turned to Olivetti. “I need a list of all Bernini’s work. You probably don’t have a big picture book of Bernini here, do you?”

“Big picture book?” Olivetti didn't seem to know what that meant.

“Never mind. Any list. What about the Vatican Museum? They must have information on Bernini.”

The guard with the scar frowned. “The museum has no power, and the records room is huge. Without the staff to help-”

“The Bernini work,” Olivetti interrupted. “Would it have been made while Bernini worked here at the Vatican?”

“Almost certainly,” Langdon said. “He was here almost his whole career. And definitely during the time of the Galileo problem.”

Olivetti nodded. “Then there’s another place to look.”

Vittoria felt a spark of hope. “Where?”

The commander didn't answer. He pulled his guard aside and spoke quietly. The guard looked unsure but nodded. When Olivetti finished, the guard turned to Langdon.

“This way, Mr. Langdon. It’s nine-fifteen. We need to hurry.”

Langdon and the guard went toward the door.

Vittoria followed them. “I’ll help.”

Olivetti stopped her, holding her arm. “No, Ms. Vetra. I need to talk to you.” His grip was firm.

Langdon and the guard left. Olivetti’s face was blank as he led Vittoria away. But Olivetti didn't get to say what he wanted. His walkie-talkie buzzed loudly. “Commandante?”

Everyone in the room turned.

The voice on the radio was serious. “You should turn on the TV.”
