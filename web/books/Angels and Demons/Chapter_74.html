Angels and Demons

Chapter 74

Langdon spotted what he wanted about ten yards ahead. Through the crowd of tourists, the white marble oval of Bernini’s West Ponente stood out against the gray stone blocks of the piazza. Vittoria must have seen it too. Her hand tightened.

“Relax,” Langdon whispered. “Do your thing.”

Vittoria loosened her grip.

As they got closer, everything seemed perfectly normal. Tourists strolled around, nuns chatted by the edge of the piazza, a girl fed pigeons at the bottom of the obelisk.

Langdon didn't check his watch. He knew the time was almost here.

The oval stone was now beneath them, and Langdon and Vittoria slowed down-not too eagerly-just two tourists stopping briefly at something mildly interesting.

“West Ponente,” Vittoria said, reading the words on the stone.

Langdon looked down at the marble carving and felt suddenly inexperienced. Not in his art books, not in his many trips to Rome, not ever had West Ponente’s importance struck him.

Not until now.

The relief was oval, about three feet long, and carved with a simple face—a depiction of the West Wind as an angel-like face. Bernini had drawn a strong breath of air blowing from the angel’s mouth, outward away from the Vatican… the breath of God. This was Bernini’s tribute to the second element… Air… a light breeze blown from angel’s lips. As Langdon stared, he realized the relief meant even more. Bernini had carved the air in five separate gusts… five! Also, on either side of the medallion were two shining stars. Langdon thought of Galileo. Two stars, five gusts, ovals, symmetry … He felt empty. His head hurt.

Vittoria started walking again right away, leading Langdon away from the relief. “I think someone’s following us,” she said.

Langdon looked up. “Where?”

Vittoria walked about thirty yards before speaking. She pointed up at the Vatican as if showing Langdon something on the dome. “The same person has been behind us all the way across the square.” Vittoria glanced over her shoulder casually. “Still on us. Keep moving.”

“You think it’s the Hassassin?”

Vittoria shook her head. “Not unless the Illuminati hires women with BBC cameras.”

When the bells of St. Peter’s began their deafening ringing, Langdon and Vittoria both jumped. It was time. They had walked away from West Ponente to try to lose the reporter but were now moving back toward the relief.

Despite the loud bells, the area seemed perfectly calm. Tourists walked around. A homeless drunk slept awkwardly at the base of the obelisk. A little girl fed pigeons. Langdon wondered if the reporter had scared the killer off. Unlikely , he decided, remembering the killer’s promise. I will make your cardinals famous.

As the echo of the ninth bell faded away, a peaceful silence came over the square.

Then… the little girl began to scream.
