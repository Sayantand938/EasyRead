Angels and Demons

Chapter 19

Vetra’s lab looked incredibly advanced.

Bright white and surrounded by computers and specialized equipment, it resembled an operating room. Langdon wondered what secrets this place could hold that would make someone cut out an eye just to get in.

Kohler seemed uneasy as they entered, his eyes darting around as if looking for an intruder. But the lab was empty. Vittoria also moved slowly… as if she felt lost without her father there.

Langdon’s eyes went straight to the center of the room, where several short pillars rose from the floor. Like a small Stonehenge, about a dozen polished steel columns stood in a circle. The pillars were about three feet tall, reminding Langdon of museum displays for precious jewels. But these pillars clearly weren't for jewels. Each held a thick, clear container about the size of a tennis ball can. They looked empty.

Kohler glanced at the containers, looking puzzled. He seemed to decide to ignore them for now. He turned to Vittoria. “Has anything been taken?”

“Taken? How?” she asked. “Only we can get in with the retina scan.”

“Just look around.”

Vittoria sighed and looked around the room for a few moments. She shrugged. “Everything looks the way my father always leaves it. Organized mess.”

Langdon felt Kohler considering his choices, as if wondering how much to tell Vittoria. He seemed to decide to wait for now. He moved his wheelchair toward the center of the room and looked at the strange group of empty containers.

“Secrets,” Kohler finally said, “are a luxury we can no longer afford.”

Vittoria nodded, looking suddenly emotional, as if being here brought back many memories.

Give her a minute, Langdon thought.

As if preparing to speak, Vittoria closed her eyes and breathed. Then she breathed again. And again. And again…

Langdon watched her, suddenly concerned. Is she okay? He glanced at Kohler, who seemed calm, as if he'd seen this before. Ten seconds passed before Vittoria opened her eyes.

Langdon couldn't believe the change. Vittoria Vetra seemed transformed. Her lips were relaxed, her shoulders down, and her eyes soft and accepting. It was as if she had adjusted her body to accept the situation. The anger and personal pain had somehow been replaced by a deeper, calm coolness.

“Where to begin…” she said, her accent steady.

“At the beginning,” Kohler said. “Tell us about your father’s experiment.”

“Reconciling science with religion has been my father’s dream,” Vittoria said. “He hoped to prove that science and religion are compatible—two ways of finding the same truth.” She paused as if unable to believe what she was about to say. “And recently… he thought of a way to do that.”

Kohler said nothing.

“He planned an experiment, hoping it would resolve one of the most heated arguments in science and religion.”

Langdon wondered what argument she meant. There were so many.

“Creationism,” Vittoria said. “The fight over how the universe began.”

Oh, Langdon thought. That debate.

“The Bible says God created the universe,” she explained. “God said, ‘Let there be light,’ and everything appeared from nothing. But a basic law of physics says matter can’t be created from nothing.”

Langdon had heard of this impasse. The idea that God created “something from nothing” went against the laws of physics, so scientists said Genesis was scientifically wrong.

“Mr. Langdon,” Vittoria said, turning, “I assume you know about the Big Bang Theory?”

Langdon shrugged. “More or less.” He knew the Big Bang was the accepted scientific idea for how the universe was created. He didn’t really get it, but the theory said a small point of energy exploded, expanding to form the universe. Or something like that.

Vittoria continued. “When the Catholic Church first suggested the Big Bang Theory in 1927, the-”

“I’m sorry?” Langdon interrupted, without thinking. “You’re saying the Big Bang was a Catholic idea?”

Vittoria looked surprised by his question “Of course. Suggested by a Catholic monk, Georges Lemaоtre in 1927.”

“But, I thought…” he hesitated. “Wasn’t the Big Bang suggested by Harvard astronomer Edwin Hubble?”

Kohler glared. “Again, American scientific arrogance. Hubble published in 1929, two years after Lemaоtre.”

Langdon frowned. It’s called the Hubble Telescope, sir-I’ve never heard of any Lemaоtre Telescope!

“Mr. Kohler is right,” Vittoria said, “the idea belonged to Lemaоtre. Hubble only confirmed it by finding the solid evidence that proved the Big Bang was scientifically likely.”

“Oh,” Langdon said, wondering if the Hubble-lovers in the Harvard Astronomy Department ever mentioned Lemaоtre in their lectures.

“When Lemaоtre first suggested the Big Bang Theory,” Vittoria continued, “scientists said it was completely absurd. Science said matter could not be created from nothing. So, when Hubble shocked the world by scientifically proving the Big Bang was correct, the church claimed victory, announcing this as proof that the Bible was scientifically correct. The divine truth.”

Langdon nodded, focusing intently now.

“Of course, scientists didn't like having their discoveries used by the church to promote religion, so they immediately used math to explain the Big Bang Theory, removed all religious meanings, and claimed it as their own. Unfortunately for science, however, their equations, even today, have one serious problem that the church likes to point out.”

Kohler grunted. “The singularity.” He spoke the word as if it were the worst thing ever.

“Yes, the singularity,” Vittoria said. “The exact moment of creation. Time zero.” She looked at Langdon. “Even today, science can't understand the first moment of creation. Our equations explain the early universe well, but as we go back in time, toward time zero, our math falls apart, and everything stops making sense.”

“Correct,” Kohler said, sounding impatient, “and the church uses this failure as proof of God’s miraculous involvement. Get to your point.”

Vittoria looked off into the distance. “My point is that my father always believed in God’s role in the Big Bang. Even though science couldn't understand the divine moment of creation, he believed someday it would.” She pointed sadly to a laser-printed memo on her father’s work area. “My dad would wave that in my face every time I doubted.”

Langdon read the message:

SCIENCE AND RELIGION ARE NOT ENEMIES. SCIENCE IS JUST TOO YOUNG TO UNDERSTAND.

“My dad wanted to improve science,” Vittoria said, “so it would support the idea of God.” She ran a hand through her long hair, looking sad. “He tried to do something no scientist had ever tried. Something no one has ever had the technology to do.” She paused, as if unsure how to continue. “He designed an experiment to prove Genesis was possible.”

Prove Genesis? Langdon wondered. Let there be light? Matter from nothing?

Kohler stared across the room with his lifeless eyes. “Excuse me?”

“My father created a universe… from nothing.”

Kohler turned sharply. “What!”

“Or, he remade the Big Bang.”

Kohler looked like he might leap up.

Langdon was completely confused. Making a universe? Remaking the Big Bang?

“It was smaller, of course,” Vittoria said, speaking quickly. “The way he did it was quite simple. He sped up two very thin streams of particles in opposite directions inside the accelerator tube. The two streams crashed together at great speeds, packing all their energy into one tiny spot. He got incredibly high energy levels.” She started listing units, and the director looked even more surprised.

Langdon tried to follow. So Leonardo Vetra was copying the packed point of energy where the universe supposedly began.

“The result,” Vittoria said, “was amazing. When it’s released, it will change everything in modern physics.” She spoke carefully, enjoying the importance of her news. “Suddenly, inside the accelerator tube, at this spot of focused energy, bits of matter started to appear from nowhere.”

Kohler didn’t react. He just stared.

“ Matter ,” Vittoria repeated. “Appearing from nothing. An amazing show of tiny sparks. A small universe coming to life. He showed that matter can be made from nothing, and that the Big Bang and Genesis can be explained by accepting that there’s a huge source of energy.”

“You mean God?” Kohler asked.

“God, Buddha, The Force, Yahweh, the singularity, the point of oneness—whatever you call it—it’s all the same. Science and religion agree—pure energy is the source of creation.”

When Kohler finally spoke, his voice was serious. “Vittoria, I’m confused. Are you saying your father created matter… from nothing?”

“Yes.” Vittoria pointed to the canisters. “And there’s the proof. In those canisters are samples of the matter he created.”

Kohler coughed and moved toward the canisters like a cautious animal circling something he instinctively felt was wrong. “I’ve clearly missed something,” he said. “How can you expect anyone to believe these canisters hold particles of matter your father actually created? They could be particles from anywhere.”

“Actually,” Vittoria said, sounding sure, “they can’t. These particles are unique. They’re a type of matter that doesn’t exist anywhere on Earth… so they had to be created.”

Kohler’s face grew darker. “Vittoria, what do you mean a certain type of matter? There’s only one type of matter, and it-” Kohler stopped.

Vittoria looked pleased. “You’ve taught about it yourself, Director. The universe has two kinds of matter. It’s a scientific fact.” Vittoria turned to Langdon. “Mr. Langdon, what does the Bible say about the Creation? What did God create?”

Langdon felt strange, unsure what this had to do with anything. “Um, God created… light and dark, heaven and hell-”

“Exactly,” Vittoria said. “He created everything in opposites. Symmetry. Perfect balance.” She turned back to Kohler. “Director, science says the same thing as religion, that the Big Bang created everything in the universe with an opposite.”

“Including matter itself,” Kohler whispered, as if to himself.

Vittoria nodded. “And when my father did his experiment, as expected, two kinds of matter appeared.”

Langdon wondered what this meant. Did Leonardo Vetra create matter’s opposite?

Kohler looked angry. “The substance you’re talking about only exists in other places in the universe. Not on Earth. Maybe not even in our galaxy!”

“Exactly,” Vittoria replied, “which proves the particles in these canisters had to be made.”

Kohler’s face hardened. “Vittoria, you can’t be saying those canisters contain real samples?”

“I am.” She looked proudly at the canisters. “Director, you’re looking at the world’s first samples of antimatter.”
