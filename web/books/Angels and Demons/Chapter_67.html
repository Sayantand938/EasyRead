Angels and Demons

Chapter 67

The descent was slow.

Langdon went down the creaking ladder, rung by rung… deeper and deeper below the Chigi Chapel floor. Into the Demon’s hole, he thought. He faced the side wall, his back to the chamber, and wondered how many more dark, cramped spaces this day could hold. The ladder groaned with each step, and the strong smell of rotting flesh and dampness was almost suffocating. Langdon wondered where Olivetti was.

Vittoria’s shape was still visible above, holding the blowtorch inside the hole, lighting Langdon’s way. As he went deeper into the darkness, the bluish light from above grew fainter. The only thing that grew stronger was the stench.

Twelve rungs down, it happened. Langdon’s foot hit a spot that was slippery with rot, and he stumbled. Lunging forward, he grabbed the ladder with his forearms to avoid falling to the bottom. Cursing the bruises now throbbing on his arms, he pulled himself back onto the ladder and started down again.

Three rungs deeper, he almost fell again, but this time it wasn't a rung that caused it. It was a jolt of fear. He had gone past a hollowed niche in the wall and suddenly found himself face to face with a collection of skulls. Catching his breath and looking around, he saw that the wall at this level was filled with shelflike openings—burial niches—all filled with skeletons. In the dim light, it created an eerie display of empty eye sockets and decaying rib cages flickering around him.

Skeletons by firelight, he grimaced, realizing he had, by chance, had a similar evening just last month. An evening of bones and flames. The New York Museum of Archeology’s candlelight dinner—salmon flambé in the shadow of a brontosaurus skeleton. He had gone at the invitation of Rebecca Strauss—former model, now art critic from the Times, a whirlwind of black velvet, cigarettes, and obviously enhanced breasts. She’d called him twice since. Langdon had not returned her calls. Not very gentlemanly, he thought, wondering how long Rebecca Strauss would last in a stink-pit like this.

Langdon was relieved to feel the last rung give way to the soft earth at the bottom. The ground under his shoes felt damp. Making sure the walls weren't going to close in on him, he turned into the crypt. It was circular, about twenty feet across. Breathing through his sleeve again, Langdon looked at the body. In the gloom, the image was blurry. A white, fleshy shape. Facing the other way. Motionless. Silent.

Moving through the murkiness of the crypt, Langdon tried to understand what he was seeing. The man had his back to Langdon, and Langdon couldn't see his face, but he did seem to be standing.

“Hello?” Langdon choked through his sleeve. Nothing. As he got closer, he realized the man was very short. Too short…

“What’s happening?” Vittoria called from above, moving the light.

Langdon didn’t answer. He was close enough now to see everything. He understood with a shudder of disgust. The chamber seemed to shrink around him. Rising like a demon from the dirt floor was an old man… or at least half of him. He was buried in the earth up to his waist, standing straight with half his body underground. He was naked, his hands tied behind his back with a red cardinal’s sash. He was held upright, his back arched like a horrible punching bag. The man’s head was tilted back, his eyes looking up as if begging God for help.

“Is he dead?” Vittoria called.

Langdon moved toward the body. I hope so, for his sake. As he got closer, he looked down at the upturned eyes. They were bulging, blue and bloodshot. Langdon leaned down to listen for breath but immediately pulled back. “For Christ’s sake!”

“What!”

Langdon almost choked. “He’s definitely dead. I just saw how he died.” The sight was awful. The man’s mouth had been forced open and filled with dirt. “Someone shoved a handful of dirt down his throat. He suffocated.”

“Dirt?” Vittoria said. “As in… earth?”

Langdon paused. Earth. He had almost forgotten. The brands. Earth, Air, Fire, Water. The killer had threatened to mark each victim with one of the ancient elements of science. The first element was Earth. From Santi’s earthly tomb. Feeling faint from the fumes, Langdon walked around to the front of the body. As he did, the symbologist in him remembered the artistic challenge of creating the mythical ambigram. Earth? How? Then, in an instant, it was before him. Centuries of Illuminati legend flooded his mind. The mark on the cardinal’s chest was burned and oozing. The flesh was charred black. La lingua pura …

Langdon stared at the brand as the room began to spin.

“Earth,” he whispered, tilting his head to see the symbol upside down. “Earth.”

Then, in a wave of horror, he realized one last thing. There are three more.
