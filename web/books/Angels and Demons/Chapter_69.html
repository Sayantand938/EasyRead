Angels and Demons

Chapter 69

Almost suffocating from the fumes, Langdon climbed the ladder toward the light at the top of the pit. He heard voices above him, but they made no sense. His head was spinning with images of the branded cardinal.

Earth … Earth …

As he climbed, his vision blurred, and he felt he might pass out. Two rungs from the top, he lost his balance. He reached up, trying to grab the edge, but it was too far. He lost his grip on the ladder and nearly fell back into the darkness. A sharp pain shot under his arms, and suddenly Langdon was in the air, legs kicking over the pit.

Two Swiss Guards grabbed him tightly under his arms and pulled him upward. A moment later, Langdon's head came out of the Demon's hole, choking and gasping for air. The guards pulled him over the edge, across the floor, and laid him down on the cold marble floor.

For a moment, Langdon didn't know where he was. Above, he saw stars and orbiting planets. Blurry figures rushed past. People were yelling. He tried to sit up. He was lying at the base of a stone pyramid. The familiar sting of an angry voice echoed inside the chapel, and then Langdon knew.

Olivetti was yelling at Vittoria. “Why didn’t you figure that out sooner!”

Vittoria was trying to explain.

Olivetti stopped her in the middle of her sentence and barked orders to his men. “Get that body out of there! Search the rest of the building!”

Langdon tried to sit up. The Chigi Chapel was full of Swiss Guards. The plastic curtain over the chapel opening had been torn from the entryway, and fresh air filled Langdon’s lungs. As he slowly became aware of his surroundings, Langdon saw Vittoria coming toward him. She knelt down, her face like an angel.

“You okay?” Vittoria took his arm and felt his pulse. Her hands were gentle on his skin.

“Thanks.” Langdon sat up. “Olivetti’s angry.”

Vittoria nodded. “He has a right to be. We messed up.”

“You mean I messed up.”

“Then make it up to him. Get it right next time.”

Next time? Langdon thought it was a harsh thing to say. There is no next time! We missed our chance!

Vittoria checked Langdon’s watch. “Mickey says we have forty minutes. Focus and help me find the next clue.”

“I told you, Vittoria, the sculptures are gone. The Path of Illumination is-” Langdon stopped.

Vittoria smiled gently.

Suddenly Langdon was jumping to his feet. He spun around, looking at the artwork around him. Pyramids, stars, planets, ellipses. Suddenly he remembered everything. This is the first altar of science! Not the Pantheon! He realized now how truly Illuminati the chapel was, far more subtle and exclusive than the famous Pantheon. The Chigi was a hidden alcove, a literal hole-in-the-wall, a tribute to a great supporter of science, decorated with earthly symbols. Perfect.

Langdon leaned on the wall and looked up at the huge pyramid sculptures. Vittoria was absolutely right. If this chapel was the first altar of science, it might still have the Illuminati sculpture that was the first marker. Langdon felt a jolt of hope realizing they still had a chance. If the marker was here, and they could follow it to the next altar of science, they might have another chance to catch the killer.

Vittoria came closer. “I found out who the unknown Illuminati sculptor was.”

Langdon turned sharply. “You what?”

“Now we just need to figure out which sculpture here is the-”

“Wait! You know who the Illuminati sculptor was?” He had spent years trying to find that out.

Vittoria smiled. “It was Bernini.” She paused. “ The Bernini.”

Langdon knew instantly she was wrong. Bernini was impossible. Gianlorenzo Bernini was the second most famous sculptor ever, only Michelangelo was more famous. In the 1600s, Bernini made more sculptures than any other artist. But the man they were looking for was supposed to be unknown, a nobody.

Vittoria frowned. “You don’t seem happy.”

“Bernini is impossible.”

“Why? Bernini lived at the same time as Galileo. He was a great sculptor.”

“He was a very famous man and a Catholic.”

“Yes,” Vittoria said. “Just like Galileo.”

“No,” Langdon argued. “Not like Galileo. Galileo was a problem for the Vatican. Bernini was the Vatican’s star. The church loved Bernini. He was chosen as the Vatican’s main artistic leader. He basically lived in Vatican City his whole life!”

“A perfect disguise. Illuminati getting in.”

Langdon felt bothered. “Vittoria, the Illuminati called their secret artist il maestro ignoto - the unknown master.”

“Yes, unknown to them. Think of the Masons' secrets - only the leaders knew everything. Galileo could have kept Bernini’s real identity hidden from most members… to protect Bernini. That way, the Vatican would never find out.”

Langdon wasn't convinced but had to agree Vittoria's idea made odd sense. The Illuminati were known for keeping secret information separate, only telling the truth to leaders. It was key to their secrecy… very few knew the full story.

“And Bernini being with the Illuminati,” Vittoria added, smiling, “explains why he designed those two pyramids.”

Langdon turned to the big, sculpted pyramids and shook his head. “Bernini was a religious sculptor. He didn't carve those pyramids.”

Vittoria shrugged. “Tell that to the sign behind you.”

Langdon turned to the plaque:

ART OF THE CHIGI CHAPEL

Although Raphael designed the architecture, Gianlorenzo Bernini created all the interior decorations.

Langdon read the plaque twice, still not convinced. Gianlorenzo Bernini was famous for his detailed, sacred sculptures of the Virgin Mary, angels, prophets, and Popes. What was he doing carving pyramids?

Langdon looked up at the towering monuments, feeling completely lost. Two pyramids, each with a bright, oval medallion. They were about as un-Christian as sculptures could be. The pyramids, the stars above, the signs of the Zodiac. All interior decorations are the work of Gianlorenzo Bernini. If that were true, Langdon realized, it meant Vittoria had to be right. It meant that Bernini was, by default, the Illuminati’s unknown master, because no one else had added artwork to this chapel! Langdon could hardly keep up with the implications.

Bernini was an Illuminatus.

Bernini designed the Illuminati ambigrams.

Bernini planned the path of Illumination.

Langdon could barely speak. Could it be that here, in this small Chigi Chapel, the world-famous Bernini had placed a sculpture that pointed across Rome to the next altar of science?

“Bernini,” he said. “I never would have guessed.”

"Who but a famous Vatican artist would have had the power to place his art in specific Catholic chapels around Rome and create the Path of Illumination? Certainly not someone unknown."

Langdon thought about it. He looked at the pyramids, wondering if one of them could be the marker. Maybe both of them? "The pyramids face opposite directions," Langdon said, unsure what to think. "They're also identical, so I don't know which..."

"I don't think the pyramids are what we're looking for."

"But they're the only sculptures here."

Vittoria stopped him by pointing to Olivetti and some of his guards who were gathered near the demon's hole.

Langdon followed her hand to the far wall. At first, he saw nothing. Then someone moved, and he caught a glimpse. White marble. An arm. A torso. And then a sculpted face, partly hidden in its niche. Two life-size human figures intertwined. Langdon's heart raced. He had been so focused on the pyramids and demon's hole that he hadn't even seen this sculpture. He moved across the room, through the crowd. As he got closer, Langdon recognized the work as pure Bernini—the intense artistic design, the detailed faces, and flowing clothing, all from the purest white marble that Vatican money could buy. It wasn't until he was almost right in front of it that Langdon recognized the sculpture itself. He stared up at the two faces and gasped.

"Who are they?" Vittoria asked, coming up behind him.

Langdon stood amazed. "Habakkuk and the Angel," he said, his voice barely audible. The piece was a fairly well-known Bernini work that was in some art history books. Langdon had forgotten it was here.

"Habakkuk?"

"Yes. The prophet who predicted the end of the earth."

Vittoria looked uneasy. “You think this is the marker?”

Langdon nodded in amazement. He had never been so sure of anything. This was the first Illuminati marker, without a doubt. Langdon had expected the sculpture to "point" to the next altar of science somehow, but not this literally. Both the angel and Habakkuk had their arms outstretched, pointing into the distance.

Langdon smiled. “Not too subtle, is it?”

Vittoria looked excited but confused. “I see them pointing, but they disagree. The angel points one way, and the prophet the other.”

Langdon chuckled. It was true. They were pointing in opposite directions. But Langdon had already solved that. With a burst of energy, he headed for the door.

“Where are you going?” Vittoria called.

“Outside!” Langdon felt light as he ran toward the door. “I need to see where that sculpture is pointing!”

“Wait! How do you know which finger to follow?”

“The poem,” he called back. “The last line!”

“ ‘Let angels guide you on your lofty quest?’ ” She looked up at the angel's outstretched finger, her eyes misting. “Well I’ll be damned!”
