Angels and Demons

Chapter 125

Robert Langdon was no longer falling.

The terror was gone. So was the pain. Even the sound of the rushing wind had disappeared. There was only the soft sound of water, like he was sleeping peacefully on a beach.

Strangely aware, Langdon knew this was death. He was happy for it. He let the spreading numbness take over completely. He let it carry him wherever he was going. His pain and fear were gone, and he didn't want them back, no matter what. His last memory had been a nightmare.

Take me. Please…

But the gentle lapping that brought him a distant sense of peace was also pulling him back. It was trying to wake him from a dream. No! Leave me alone! He didn't want to wake up. He felt evil closing in on his happiness, trying to destroy his joy. Blurry images appeared. Voices shouted. The wind roared. No, please! The more he resisted, the more the chaos broke through.

Then, suddenly, he was reliving it all…

The helicopter was climbing almost straight up, making him dizzy. He was stuck inside. Outside the open door, the lights of Rome looked farther away every second. His instinct to survive told him to drop the canister right now. Langdon knew it would take less than twenty seconds for the canister to fall half a mile. But it would be falling toward a city full of people.

Higher! Higher!

Langdon wondered how high they were. He knew small planes flew at about four miles high. This helicopter had to be pretty high up by now. Two miles? Three? There was still a chance. If they dropped it at the right time, the canister would only fall partway to the ground, exploding far above and away from the helicopter. Langdon looked out at the city spreading below them.

“And what if you’re wrong?” the camerlegno said.

Langdon turned, surprised. The camerlegno wasn’t even looking at him, seeming to read Langdon’s thoughts in the windshield’s faint reflection. Strangely, the camerlegno wasn’t focused on the controls anymore. His hands weren’t even on the throttle. The chopper, it seemed, was now in autopilot, climbing steadily. The camerlegno reached up to the cockpit ceiling, behind some cables, and took out a key taped there, hidden from sight.

Langdon watched, confused, as the camerlegno quickly unlocked the metal box bolted between the seats. He took out a large, black, nylon pack and put it on the seat beside him. Langdon’s mind raced. The camerlegno seemed calm, as if he had a plan.

“Give me the canister,” the camerlegno said, his voice calm.

Langdon was completely bewildered. He handed the canister to the camerlegno. “Ninety seconds!”

Langdon was shocked by what the camerlegno did with the antimatter. Carefully holding the canister, the camerlegno put it in the cargo box. Then he closed the heavy lid and locked it.

“What are you doing!” Langdon asked.

“Leading us from temptation.” The camerlegno threw the key out the open window.

As the key fell into the night, Langdon felt like his soul was falling with it.

The camerlegno then took the nylon pack and put his arms through the straps. He fastened a waist clamp and tightened it like a backpack. He turned to a stunned Robert Langdon.

“I’m sorry,” the camerlegno said. “It wasn’t supposed to happen like this.” Then he opened his door and threw himself into the night.

The image stuck in Langdon’s mind, and with it came the pain. Real pain. Physical pain. Aching. Burning. He begged for it to end, but as the water grew louder in his ears, new images flashed. His hell had only just begun. He saw bits and pieces. Quick flashes of pure panic. He was halfway between death and nightmare, begging to be saved, but the pictures grew brighter in his mind.

The antimatter canister was locked away. It counted down as the chopper rose. Fifty seconds. Higher. Higher. Langdon spun in the cabin, trying to understand what he had just seen. Forty-five seconds. He searched under seats for another parachute. Forty seconds. There was none! There had to be a way out! Thirty-five seconds. He raced to the open doorway of the chopper and stood in the raging wind, looking down at the lights of Rome below. Thirty-two seconds.

And then he made the choice.

The unbelievable choice…

With no parachute, Robert Langdon had jumped out the door. As the night swallowed his falling body, the helicopter seemed to shoot off above him, the sound of its rotors fading in the loud rush of his fall.

As he fell toward earth, Robert Langdon felt something he had not felt since his diving days-the strong pull of gravity during a straight drop. The faster he fell, the harder the earth seemed to pull him down. But this time, the drop was not fifty feet into a pool. The drop was thousands of feet into a city-an endless stretch of pavement and concrete.

Somewhere in the wind and desperation, Kohler’s voice echoed… words he had spoken earlier that morning at CERN’s free-fall tube. One square yard of drag will slow a falling body almost twenty percent. Twenty percent, Langdon now realized, was not enough to survive a fall like this. Still, more out of instinct than hope, he clenched in his hands the only thing he had grabbed from the chopper on his way out. It was a strange souvenir, but it had given him a brief moment of hope.

The windshield tarp had been in the back of the helicopter. It was a curved rectangle-about four yards by two-like a large fitted sheet… a very basic parachute. It had no harness, only loops at each end to attach it to the windshield. Langdon had grabbed it, slid his hands through the loops, held on, and jumped into the void.

His last act of youthful defiance.

He had no dreams of surviving this.

Langdon fell like a rock. Feet first. Arms raised. His hands gripping the loops. The tarp puffed out like a mushroom over him. The wind rushed past him hard.

As he dropped toward the ground, there was a loud explosion somewhere above. It seemed farther away than he thought it would be. Almost right away, the shock wave hit. He felt like he couldn't breathe. There was a sudden warmth all around him. He struggled to hold on. A wave of heat rushed down from above. The top of the tarp began to smoke… but held.

Langdon sped downward, on the edge of a light that was billowing out, feeling like a surfer trying to escape a huge tidal wave. Then suddenly, the heat faded.

He was falling again through the cool darkness.

For a moment, Langdon felt hope. But a moment later, that hope faded like the heat above. Even though his arms told him the tarp was slowing him down, the wind still rushed past him with a deafening sound. Langdon knew he was still moving too fast to live through the fall. He would be crushed when he hit the ground.

Numbers ran through his head, but he was too numb to understand them… one square yard of drag… 20 percent slower. All Langdon could tell was that the tarp over his head was big enough to slow him down more than 20 percent. But, he could tell from the wind rushing past him that the tarp wasn't helping enough. He was still falling fast… he wouldn't survive hitting the concrete below.

Beneath him, the lights of Rome spread out everywhere. The city looked like a huge starlit sky that Langdon was falling into. The perfect sky of stars was broken only by a dark strip that cut the city in two-a wide, dark ribbon that twisted through the lights like a fat snake. Langdon stared down at the winding black shape.

Suddenly, like a wave rising out of nowhere, hope filled him again.

With wild energy, Langdon yanked down hard on the right side of the tarp. The tarp suddenly flapped louder, billowing, trying to find the easiest way to move. Langdon felt himself drifting sideways. He pulled again, harder, ignoring the pain in his hand. The tarp flared, and Langdon felt his body sliding to the side. Not much. But some! He looked below him again, at the twisting snake of black. It was off to the right, but he was still pretty high up. Had he waited too long? He pulled with all his strength and accepted that it was now up to God. He focused on the widest part of the snake and… for the first time in his life, prayed for a miracle.

The rest was a blur.

The darkness rushing up beneath him… his diving instincts returning… his spine locking, toes pointing without thought… his lungs filling with air to protect him… his legs tensing like a ram… and finally… relief that the winding Tiber River was raging… making its waters foamy and full of air… and much softer than still water.

Then impact… and blackness.

The loud flapping of the parachute drew the group’s eyes from the fireball in the sky. The sky above Rome had been full of sights tonight… a helicopter shooting up, a huge explosion, and now this odd object that had fallen into the churning Tiber River, right off the shore of the river’s small island, Isola Tiberina.

Ever since the island was used to keep the sick separate during the Roman plague of A.D. 1656, people believed it had magical healing powers. Because of this, the island later became the location for Rome’s Hospital Tiberina.

The body was badly hurt when they pulled it ashore. The man still had a weak pulse, which they found incredible. They wondered if Isola Tiberina’s legendary healing powers had somehow kept his heart beating. Minutes later, when the man started coughing and slowly woke up, the group decided the island must truly be magical.
