Angels and Demons

Chapter 9

The dead body on the floor in front of Langdon was gruesome. Leonardo Vetra lay on his back, naked, his skin a bluish-gray color. His broken neck bones stuck out, and his head was twisted completely around, facing the wrong way. His face was hidden, pressed against the floor. The man lay in a frozen pool of his own urine, the hair around his shrunken genitals covered in frost.

Trying to control his nausea, Langdon looked at the victim’s chest. Even though Langdon had stared at the symmetrical wound many times on the fax, the burn was much more striking in person. The raised, burned flesh was perfectly outlined… the symbol perfectly shaped.

Langdon wondered if the intense chill he now felt was from the air-conditioning or his complete shock at the importance of what he was looking at.

His heart beat fast as he walked around the body, reading the word upside down, confirming the brilliance of the symmetry. The symbol seemed even harder to believe now that he was staring at it.

“Mr. Langdon?”

Langdon didn’t hear. He was lost in his own world… his area of expertise, a world where history, myth, and fact came together, overwhelming him. He started thinking.

“Mr. Langdon?” Kohler watched him, waiting for an answer.

Langdon didn’t look up. He was now even more determined, completely focused. “How much do you already know?”

“Only what I had time to read on your website. The word Illuminati means ‘the enlightened ones.’ It’s the name of some kind of old group.”

Langdon nodded. “Had you heard the name before?”

“Not until I saw it marked on Mr. Vetra.”

“So you searched for it online?”

“Yes.”

“And the word gave you many results, I’m sure.”

“Thousands,” Kohler said. “But yours mentioned Harvard, Oxford, a good publisher, and a list of related books. As a scientist, I know that information is only as good as where it comes from. Your background seemed real.”

Langdon’s eyes were still fixed on the body.

Kohler said nothing. He just stared, waiting for Langdon to explain what they were seeing.

Langdon looked up, glancing around the cold room. “Maybe we should talk about this somewhere warmer?”

“This room is fine.” Kohler seemed unaware of the cold. “We’ll talk here.”

Langdon frowned. The Illuminati story was not simple. I’ll freeze trying to explain it. He looked again at the brand, feeling awe again.

Although stories of the Illuminati symbol were famous in modern symbolism, no expert had ever seen it. Old papers said the symbol was an ambigram- ambi meaning “both”-showing it could be read both ways. Ambigrams were common in symbols-swastikas, yin yang, Jewish stars, simple crosses-but the idea that a word could be made into an ambigram seemed impossible. Modern symbol experts had tried for years to make the word “Illuminati” perfectly symmetrical, but they failed. Most experts now thought the symbol was a myth.

“So who are the Illuminati?” Kohler asked.

Yes , Langdon thought, who indeed? He began his story.

“Since history began,” Langdon said, “science and religion have been deeply divided. Scientists like Copernicus-”

“Were murdered,” Kohler interrupted. “The church murdered them for showing scientific truths. Religion has always hurt science.”

“Yes. But in the 1500s, a group in Rome fought back against the church. Some of Italy’s smartest men-physicists, mathematicians, astronomers-met secretly to share their worries about the church’s wrong teachings. They feared the church’s control of ‘truth’ threatened learning worldwide. They started the world’s first science think tank, calling themselves ‘the enlightened ones.’ ”

“The Illuminati.”

“Yes,” Langdon said. “Europe’s smartest people… dedicated to finding scientific truth.”

Kohler fell silent.

“Of course, the Catholic Church hunted the Illuminati without mercy. Only by keeping their rituals extremely secret did the scientists stay safe. News spread through secret academic circles, and the Illuminati group grew to include scholars from all over Europe. The scientists met regularly in Rome at a super-secret hideout they called the Church of Illumination.”

Kohler coughed and shifted in his chair.

“Many of the Illuminati,” Langdon continued, “wanted to fight the church’s oppression with violence, but their most respected member convinced them not to. He was a pacifist, and also one of history’s most famous scientists.”

Langdon was sure Kohler would know the name. Even people who weren't scientists knew about the unlucky astronomer who was arrested and almost killed by the church for saying that the sun, not the earth, was the center of the solar system. Even though his evidence was undeniable, the astronomer was severely punished for suggesting that God hadn't put mankind at the center of His universe.

“His name was Galileo Galilei,” Langdon said.

Kohler looked up. “Galileo?”

“Yes. Galileo was an Illuminatus. And he was also a devout Catholic. He tried to ease the church’s stance on science by saying that science didn't weaken the idea of God, but actually strengthened it. He once wrote that when he looked through his telescope at the spinning planets, he could hear God’s voice in the music of the spheres. He believed that science and religion weren't enemies, but allies -two different languages telling the same story, a story of balance and order… heaven and hell, night and day, hot and cold, God and Satan. Both science and religion celebrated God’s symmetry… the endless battle of light and dark.” Langdon paused, stomping his feet to stay warm.

Kohler simply sat in his wheelchair and stared.

“Unfortunately,” Langdon added, “the church didn't want science and religion to unite.”

“Of course not,” Kohler interrupted. “The union would have canceled the church’s claim as the only way man could understand God. So the church tried Galileo as a heretic, found him guilty, and put him under house arrest for life. I know scientific history, Mr. Langdon. But this was centuries ago. What does it have to do with Leonardo Vetra?”

The main question. Langdon got to the point. “Galileo’s arrest greatly disturbed the Illuminati. Mistakes happened, and the church found the identities of four members, who they caught and questioned. But the four scientists revealed nothing… even when tortured.”

“Torture?”

Langdon nodded. “They were branded alive. On the chest. With the symbol of a cross.”

Kohler’s eyes widened, and he looked worriedly at Vetra’s body.

“Then the scientists were brutally killed, their bodies left in the streets of Rome as a warning to others thinking of joining the Illuminati. With the church closing in, the remaining Illuminati ran from Italy.”

Langdon paused to emphasize his point. He looked directly into Kohler’s lifeless eyes. “The Illuminati went into hiding, where they joined other refugee groups running from the Catholic purges—mystics, alchemists, occultists, Muslims, Jews. Over the years, the Illuminati began accepting new members. A new Illuminati appeared. A darker Illuminati. A very anti-Christian Illuminati. They became very powerful, using mysterious rituals, deadly secrecy, promising to rise again someday and get revenge on the Catholic Church. Their power grew so much that the church considered them the most dangerous anti-Christian group on earth. The Vatican called the brotherhood Shaitan.”

“Shaitan?”

“It’s Islamic. It means ‘enemy’… God’s enemy. The church chose the name from Islam because it was a language they considered impure.” Langdon hesitated. “Shaitan is the origin of an English word… Satan.”

Kohler looked uneasy.

Langdon's voice was serious. "Mr. Kohler, I don't know how this mark got on this man's chest... or why... but you're looking at the long-lost symbol of the world's oldest and most powerful satanic group."
