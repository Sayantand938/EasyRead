Angels and Demons

Chapter 40

"Is this true?" the camerlegno asked, looking amazed as he turned from Vittoria to Olivetti.

"Signore," Olivetti said, "I'll admit there's something here. We can see it on one of our security monitors, but I can't say if Ms. Vetra's claims about its power are true."

"Wait," the camerlegno said. "You can see this thing?"

"Yes, signore. On wireless camera #86."

"Then why haven't you gotten it?" The camerlegno's voice was angry now.

"It's very difficult, signore." Olivetti stood straight as he explained.

The camerlegno listened, and Vittoria felt his worry grow. "Are you sure it's inside Vatican City?" the camerlegno asked. "Maybe someone took the camera outside and is sending the signal from somewhere else."

"Impossible," Olivetti said. "Our outside walls block signals to protect our communications inside. The signal can only be coming from inside, or we wouldn't be getting it."

"And I assume," he said, "you're now looking for this missing camera with everyone you have?"

Olivetti shook his head. “No, signore. Finding that camera could take a lot of time. We have other security issues right now, and with all respect to Ms. Vetra, this droplet she mentions is very small. It couldn't be as explosive as she says.”

Vittoria lost her patience. “That droplet is enough to destroy Vatican City! Did you even hear what I said?”

“Ma’am,” Olivetti said, his voice hard, “I have a lot of experience with explosives.”

“Your experience is outdated,” she snapped back, just as tough. “Despite my clothes, which I know you don't like, I'm a senior physicist at the world’s most advanced atomic research center. I designed the antimatter trap that's stopping that sample from exploding right now. And I'm warning you that if you don't find that canister in the next six hours, your guards will have nothing to protect for the next century but a big hole in the ground.”

Olivetti turned to the camerlegno, his small eyes flashing anger. “Signore, I can't let this go on. Your time is being wasted by jokers. The Illuminati? A droplet that will destroy us all?”

“Basta,” the camerlegno declared. He said the word quietly, but it seemed to echo in the room. Then there was silence. He continued in a whisper. “Dangerous or not, Illuminati or not, whatever this thing is, it shouldn't be inside Vatican City… especially right before the conclave. I want it found and removed. Start a search right away.”

Olivetti argued. “Signore, even if we used all the guards to search the area, it could take days to find this camera. Also, after talking to Ms. Vetra, I had one of my guards check our best guide to weapons for any mention of this thing called antimatter. There was nothing about it. Nothing.”

Arrogant jerk, Vittoria thought. A weapons guide? Did you try a general reference book? Under A!

Olivetti was still talking. “Signore, if you're suggesting we search all of Vatican City by hand, I have to disagree.”

“Commander.” The camerlegno’s voice was full of anger. “Let me remind you that when you speak to me, you're speaking to this office. I know you don't respect my position—but by law, I'm in charge. If I'm not wrong, the cardinals are now safe in the Sistine Chapel, and your security concerns are minimal until the conclave ends. I don't know why you don't want to look for this device. If I didn't know better, it would seem like you're intentionally endangering this conclave.”

Olivetti looked scornful. “How dare you! I have served your Pope for twelve years! And the Pope before that for fourteen years! Since 1438 the Swiss Guard have-”

The walkie-talkie on Olivetti’s belt squawked loudly, cutting him off. “Comandante?”

Olivetti snatched it up and pressed the transmitter. “I’m busy! What do you want!!”

“Sorry,” the Swiss Guard on the radio said. “Communications here. I thought you would want to know that we have received a bomb threat.”

Olivetti could not have looked less interested. “Then handle it! Do the usual trace, and write it up.”

“We did, sir, but the caller…” The guard paused. “I wouldn’t bother you, commander, except that he mentioned the substance you just asked me to research. Antimatter.”

Everyone in the room exchanged stunned looks.

“He mentioned what?” Olivetti stammered.

“Antimatter, sir. While we were trying to run a trace, I did some more research on his claim. The information on antimatter is… well, frankly, it’s quite worrying.”

“I thought you said the ballistics guide didn't mention it.”

“I found it online.”

Alleluia, Vittoria thought.

“The stuff seems very explosive,” the guard said. “I doubt this is right, but it says here that antimatter, ounce for ounce, is way more powerful than an atom bomb.”

Olivetti sagged. It was like seeing a mountain fall apart. Vittoria’s joy vanished when she saw the camerlegno’s horrified look.

“Did you track the call?” Olivetti stammered.

“No luck. Cellphone, heavily encrypted. The satellite lines are mixed, so we can’t pinpoint the location. The signal suggests he’s somewhere in Rome, but we can’t really trace him.”

“Did he ask for anything?” Olivetti said quietly.

“No, sir. Just warned us that there’s antimatter hidden here. He seemed surprised I didn’t know. Asked if I’d seen it yet. You’d asked me about antimatter, so I told you.”

“You did well,” Olivetti said. “I’ll be down in a minute. Tell me right away if he calls back.”

There was a pause on the walkie-talkie. “The caller’s still on the line, sir.”

Olivetti looked like he’d just been shocked. “The line is open?”

“Yes, sir. We’ve been trying to trace him for ten minutes, but we can’t get a signal. He must know we can’t touch him because he won’t hang up until he speaks to the camerlegno.”

“Patch him through,” the camerlegno ordered. “Now!”

Olivetti turned quickly. “Father, no. A trained Swiss Guard negotiator is much better at this.”

“Now!”

Olivetti gave the order.

A moment later, the phone on Camerlegno Ventresca’s desk began to ring. The camerlegno slammed his finger down on the speakerphone button. “Who do you think you are?”
