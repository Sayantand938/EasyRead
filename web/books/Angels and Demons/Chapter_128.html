Angels and Demons

Chapter 128

The College of Cardinals buzzed with excitement as they returned to the Sistine Chapel. In contrast, Mortati felt a rising confusion that threatened to overwhelm him. He believed in the ancient miracles of the Scriptures, yet what he had just seen was beyond his understanding. After a lifetime of devotion, seventy-nine years, Mortati knew these events should fill him with religious joy… a strong and vibrant faith. Yet all he felt was a growing, ghostly unease. Something felt wrong.

“Signore Mortati!” a Swiss Guard yelled, running down the hall. “We went to the roof as you asked. The camerlegno is… real! He is a true man! He is not a ghost! He is exactly as we knew him!”

“Did he talk to you?”

“He kneels in silent prayer! We are afraid to touch him!”

Mortati was bewildered. “Tell him… his cardinals await.”

“Signore, because he is a man …” the guard hesitated.

“What is it?”

“His chest… it is burned. Should we bandage his wounds? He must be in pain.”

Mortati considered it. Nothing in his long service to the church had prepared him for this. “He is a man, so treat him as a man. Bathe him. Bandage his wounds. Dress him in fresh robes. We await his arrival in the Sistine Chapel.”

The guard ran off.

Mortati headed for the chapel. The other cardinals were inside now. As he walked down the hall, he saw Vittoria Vetra slumped alone on a bench at the bottom of the Royal Staircase. He saw her pain and loneliness and wanted to go to her, but he knew he had to wait. He had work to do… although he had no idea what that work could be.

Mortati entered the chapel. It was filled with wild excitement. He closed the door. God help me.

Hospital Tiberina’s twin-rotor Aero-Ambulanza circled behind Vatican City, and Langdon clenched his teeth, swearing to God this was the last helicopter ride of his life.

After convincing the pilot that the rules about Vatican airspace didn't matter to the Vatican right now, he guided her in, unseen, over the back wall, and landed on the Vatican’s helipad.

“Grazie,” he said, getting out onto the ground with effort. She blew him a kiss and quickly took off, disappearing back over the wall and into the night.

Langdon exhaled, trying to clear his head, hoping to understand what he was about to do. With the camcorder in hand, he got on the same golf cart he had ridden earlier that day. It had not been charged, and the battery meter was almost empty. Langdon drove without headlights to save power.

He also preferred no one see him coming.

At the back of the Sistine Chapel, Cardinal Mortati stood in a daze as he watched the chaos before him.

“It was a miracle!” one of the cardinals shouted. “The work of God!”

“Yes!” others exclaimed. “God has shown us what He wants!”

“The camerlegno will be our Pope!” another shouted. “He is not a cardinal, but God has sent a miracle!”

“Yes!” someone agreed. “The laws of conclave are man’s laws. God’s will is before us! I call for a vote right now!”

“A vote?” Mortati asked, moving toward them. “I believe that is my job.”

Everyone turned.

Mortati could feel the cardinals watching him. They seemed far away, confused, bothered by his seriousness. Mortati wished he could feel the wonderful joy he saw on their faces. But he did not. He felt a strange pain in his soul… a deep sadness he could not explain. He had promised to lead this process with a pure heart, and he could not ignore this doubt he felt.

“My friends,” Mortati said, stepping to the altar. His voice sounded strange to him. “I think I will struggle for the rest of my life to understand what I have seen tonight. And yet, what you are suggesting about the camerlegno… it cannot be God’s will.”

The room went silent.

“How… can you say that?” one of the cardinals finally asked. “The camerlegno saved the church. God spoke to him directly! He survived death itself! What more proof do we need!”

“The camerlegno is coming to us now,” Mortati said. “Let us wait. Let us hear what he has to say before we vote. There may be an explanation.”

“An explanation?”

“As your Great Elector, I have promised to defend the rules of conclave. You know that Holy Law says the camerlegno cannot be elected pope. He is not a cardinal. He is a priest… a chamberlain. Also, he is too young.” Mortati felt the stares getting colder. “By even allowing a vote, I would be asking you to support a man who Vatican Law says cannot be elected. I would be asking you to break a sacred promise.”

“But what happened here tonight,” someone stammered, “it definitely goes beyond our laws!”

“Does it?” Mortati roared, not even knowing where his words were coming from. “Is God telling us to ignore the rules of the church? Is God telling us to forget reason and act wildly?”

“But did you not see what we saw?” another asked angrily. “How can you question that kind of power!”

Mortati’s voice boomed louder than he had ever heard it before. “I am not questioning God’s power! God gave us reason and caution! We serve God by being careful!”
