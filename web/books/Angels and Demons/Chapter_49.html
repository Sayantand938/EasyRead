Angels and Demons

Chapter 49

Langdon and Vittoria were now alone outside the double doors to the Secret Archives. The colonnade's decor was a strange mix of carpets over marble floors and security cameras watching from beside carved cherubs on the ceiling. Langdon called it Sterile Renaissance. Beside the arched entrance was a small bronze plaque.

ARCHIVIO VATICANO

Curatore, Padre Jaqui Tomaso

Father Jaqui Tomaso. Langdon knew the curator’s name from the rejection letters in his desk. Dear Mr. Langdon, I regret to inform you that I must deny …

Regret. Lies. Since Jaqui Tomaso became curator, Langdon hadn’t met a single non-Catholic American scholar allowed into the Secret Vatican Archives. Il gaurdiano, historians called him. Jaqui Tomaso was the world's strictest librarian.

As Langdon pushed the doors open and entered the inner sanctum, he almost expected to see Father Jaqui in military gear, standing guard with a bazooka. But the space was empty.

Silence. Soft lighting.

Archivio Vaticano. A lifelong dream.

As Langdon looked around the sacred chamber, he felt embarrassed. He realized how naive he was. His long-held images of this room were completely wrong. He had imagined dusty shelves filled with old books, priests cataloging by candlelight and stained-glass windows, monks studying scrolls…

Not even close.

At first glance, the room looked like a dark airplane hangar where someone had built a dozen separate racquetball courts. Langdon knew what the glass boxes were. He wasn't surprised to see them; dampness and heat damaged old papers, so they needed sealed rooms like these to keep them safe—airtight boxes that kept out moisture and natural acids in the air. Langdon had been inside these rooms many times, but it always felt strange… like entering an airtight container where the air was controlled by a librarian.

The rooms were dark, even ghostly, lit by small lights at the end of each row. In the darkness of each cell, Langdon felt the presence of giant rows of stacks, full of history. This was quite a collection.

Vittoria also seemed amazed. She stood beside him, staring silently at the giant clear cubes.

Time was short, and Langdon quickly looked around the dim room for a book catalog—a book that listed everything in the library. He only saw a few computer screens scattered around. "Looks like they have a Biblion. Their index is on the computer."

Vittoria seemed hopeful. "That should make things faster."

Langdon wished he felt the same, but he felt it was bad news. He walked to a computer and started typing. His fears were immediately confirmed. "The old way would have been better."

"Why?"

He stepped away from the screen. "Because real books don't have password protection. I guess physicists aren't natural hackers?"

Vittoria shook her head. "I can open oysters, that's about it."

Langdon took a deep breath and turned to face the strange collection of clear vaults. He walked to the closest one and peered into the dim inside. Behind the glass were vague shapes that Langdon recognized as the usual bookshelves, parchment bins, and examination tables. He looked up at the indicator tabs glowing at the end of each stack. Like in all libraries, the tabs showed what was in that row. He read the headings as he moved down the clear barrier.

PIETRO IL ERIMITO… LE CROCIATE… URBANO II… LEVANT…

“They’re labeled,” he said, still walking. “But it’s not by author’s name.” He wasn’t surprised. Old archives were almost never listed alphabetically because so many of the authors were unknown. Titles didn’t work either because many historical documents were untitled letters or pieces of parchment. Most lists were in order by date. But oddly, this arrangement didn’t seem to be in order by date.

Langdon felt valuable time already running out. “Looks like the Vatican has its own system.”

“What a surprise.”

He looked at the labels again. The documents covered centuries, but all the keywords, he noticed, were related. “I think it’s sorted by topic.”

“Topic?” Vittoria said, sounding like a disapproving scientist. “Sounds inefficient.”

Actually … Langdon thought, thinking about it more. This might be the smartest way to list things I’ve ever seen. He had always told his students to understand the overall feelings and ideas of an artistic time rather than getting caught up in the details of dates and specific works. The Vatican Archives, it seemed, were listed using a similar idea. Big picture …

“Everything in this vault,” Langdon said, feeling more sure of himself now, “centuries of material, has to do with the Crusades. That’s this vault’s topic.” It was all here, he realized. Historical stories, letters, artwork, social and political facts, modern studies. All in one place … helping people understand a topic better. Smart.

Vittoria frowned. “But data can relate to more than one topic at the same time.”

“Which is why they check with related clues.” Langdon pointed through the glass to the colorful plastic tabs inserted among the documents. “Those show other documents with the same topics, but located somewhere else.”

“Sure,” she said, apparently dropping the subject. She put her hands on her hips and looked around the huge space. Then she looked at Langdon. “So, Professor, what’s the name of this Galileo thing we’re looking for?”

Langdon couldn’t help but smile. He still couldn’t believe he was standing in this room. It’s in here, he thought. Somewhere in the dark, it’s waiting.

“Follow me,” Langdon said. He started quickly down the first aisle, looking at the indicator tabs of each vault. “Remember how I told you about the Path of Illumination? How the Illuminati brought in new members using a complicated test?”

“The treasure hunt,” Vittoria said, following closely.

“The Illuminati had a problem: after they put the markers in place, they needed a way to tell the scientific community that the path was there.”

“Makes sense,” Vittoria said. “Otherwise nobody would know to look for it.”

“Yes, and even if they knew the path was there, scientists would have no way of knowing where it started. Rome is huge.”

“Okay.”

Langdon went down the next aisle, looking at the tabs as he talked. “About fifteen years ago, some historians at the Sorbonne and I found a series of Illuminati letters filled with references to the segno.”

“The sign. The announcement about the path and where it started.”

“Yes. And since then, many Illuminati scholars, myself included, have found other mentions of the segno. It's generally believed now that the clue exists and that Galileo spread it widely to the scientific world without the Vatican ever finding out.”

“How?”

“We don’t know for sure, but probably through printed works. He put out many books and newsletters over the years.”

“Which the Vatican surely saw. Sounds risky.”

“Indeed. Still, the segno was spread around.”

“But no one has ever really found it?”

“No. But strangely, wherever references to the segno show up—Masonic diaries, old science journals, Illuminati letters—it’s often called by a number.”

“666?”

Langdon smiled. “Actually, it’s 503.”

“Meaning?”

“None of us could ever understand it. I got obsessed with 503, trying everything to find meaning in the number—numerology, map coordinates, latitudes.” Langdon reached the end of the aisle, turned the corner, and hurried to scan the next row of tabs as he spoke. “For years, the only clue seemed to be that 503 started with the number five… one of the Illuminati’s sacred numbers.” He paused.

“Something tells me you recently figured it out, and that’s why we’re here.”

“Correct,” Langdon said, letting himself feel a rare moment of pride in his work. “Are you familiar with a book by Galileo called Diаlogo?”

“Of course. Famous among scientists as the ultimate scientific sellout.”

Sellout wasn’t exactly the word Langdon would have used, but he knew what Vittoria meant. In the early 1630s, Galileo had wanted to publish a book supporting the Copernican heliocentric model of the solar system, but the Vatican wouldn’t allow the book to be released unless Galileo included equally convincing evidence for the church’s geo centric model—a model Galileo knew was completely wrong. Galileo had no choice but to give in to the church’s demands and publish a book giving equal attention to both the correct and incorrect models.

“As you probably know,” Langdon said, “even though Galileo compromised, Diаlogo was still considered heretical, and the Vatican put him under house arrest.”

“No good deed goes unpunished.”

Langdon smiled. “So true. And yet Galileo didn’t give up. While under house arrest, he secretly wrote a lesser-known manuscript that scholars often mistake for Diаlogo. That book is called Discorsi.”

Vittoria nodded. “I’ve heard of it. Discourses on the Tides.”

Langdon stopped, surprised she knew about the little-known book about planets and tides.

“Hey,” she said, “I’m an Italian ocean scientist whose father loved Galileo.”

Langdon laughed. Discorsi wasn't what they needed. Langdon explained that Discorsi wasn't Galileo's only work while stuck at home. Historians thought he also wrote a small, unknown book called Diagramma.

“ Diagramma della Veritа ,” Langdon said. “ Diagram of Truth.”

“Never heard of it.”

“I’m not shocked. Diagramma was Galileo’s most secret work—said to be a paper on scientific truths he couldn't share. Like some of Galileo’s other papers, Diagramma was snuck out of Rome by a friend and quietly printed in Holland. It became very popular in Europe's secret science world. Then the Vatican found out and burned all the copies they could find.”

Vittoria seemed interested now. “And you think Diagramma had the clue? The segno. The information about the Path of Illumination.”

“Diagramma is how Galileo spread the word. I’m sure of it.” Langdon walked into the third row of vaults and kept looking at the labels. “Archivists have looked for a copy of Diagramma for years. But because the Vatican burned them and the book wasn't made to last, it has vanished.”

“Made to last?”

“Durability. Archivists rate documents from one to ten based on how strong they are. Diagramma was printed on thin paper. Like tissue paper. It only lasts about a hundred years.”

“Why not something stronger?”

“Galileo’s order. To protect his followers. This way, any scientists caught with a copy could just drop it in water, and the booklet would dissolve. It was great for destroying evidence, but awful for record keepers. It’s thought that only one copy of Diagramma lasted past the eighteenth century.”

“One?” Vittoria looked amazed as she glanced around the room. “And it’s here?”

“The Vatican took it from the Netherlands shortly after Galileo died. I’ve been asking to see it for years. Ever since I knew what was in it.”

As if she knew what Langdon was thinking, Vittoria moved across the aisle and started checking the next section of vaults, speeding up.

“Thanks,” he said. “Look for reference labels about Galileo, science, or scientists. You’ll know it when you see it.”

“Okay, but you still haven’t said how you figured out Diagramma had the clue. It had something to do with the number you kept seeing in Illuminati letters? 503?”

Langdon smiled. “Yes. It took a while, but I finally realized that 503 is a simple code. It clearly points to Diagramma.”

For a moment, Langdon remembered the unexpected moment he figured it out: August 16. Two years ago. He was standing by a lake at a colleague's son’s wedding. Bagpipes played on the water as the wedding party made their unusual entrance… across the lake on a barge. The boat was decorated with flowers and wreaths. It had a Roman numeral painted on the hull—DCII.

Confused by the marking, Langdon asked the father of the bride, “Why 602?”

“602?”

Langdon pointed to the barge. “DCII is the Roman numeral for 602.”

The man laughed. “That’s not a Roman numeral. That’s the name of the barge.”

“The DCII?”

The man nodded. “ The Dick and Connie II.”

Langdon felt silly. Dick and Connie were the couple getting married. The barge was obviously named after them. “What happened to the DCI?”

The man groaned. “It sank yesterday during the rehearsal lunch.”

Langdon laughed. “Sorry to hear that.” He looked back at the barge. The DCII, he thought. Like a tiny QEII. A second later, it clicked.

Now Langdon turned to Vittoria. “503,” he said, “is a code, like I said. It’s an Illuminati trick to hide a Roman numeral. 503 in Roman numerals is-”

“DIII.”

Langdon looked up. “That was fast. Please don’t tell me you’re one of the Illuminati.”

She laughed. “I use Roman numerals to label ocean layers.”

Of course, Langdon thought. Don’t we all.

Vittoria looked over. “So what does DIII mean?”

“DI, DII, and DIII are very old abbreviations. Ancient scientists used them to tell apart the three Galilean documents that were often mixed up.”

Vittoria took a quick breath. “ Dialogue … Discourses … Diagram.”

“D-one. D-two. D-three. All scientific. All argued about. 503 is DIII. Diagram. The third of his books.”

Vittoria looked worried. “But something still doesn’t make sense. If this segno, this clue, this hint about the Path of Illumination was really in Galileo’s Diagram, why didn’t the Vatican see it when they took back all the copies?”

“They might have seen it but not noticed. Remember the Illuminati markers? Hiding things in plain sight? Deception? The segno was likely hidden the same way—right out in the open. Invisible to those not looking for it. And also invisible to those who didn’t understand it.”

“Meaning?”

“Meaning Galileo hid it well. According to historical records, the sign was revealed in a way the Illuminati called lingua pura.”

“The pure language?”

“Yes.”

“Mathematics?”

“That’s my guess. It seems pretty clear. Galileo was a scientist, after all, and he was writing for scientists. Math would be a sensible language to use for the clue. The booklet is called Diagramma, so mathematical diagrams might also be part of the code.”

Vittoria sounded only a little more hopeful. “I guess Galileo could have made some kind of mathematical code that the clergy didn't notice.”

“You don’t sound convinced,” Langdon said, moving down the row.

“I’m not. Mostly because you aren’t. If you were so sure about DIII, why didn’t you publish it? Then someone who did have access to the Vatican Archives could have come here and looked at Diagramma a long time ago.”

“I didn’t want to publish it,” Langdon said. “I had worked hard to find the information and-” He stopped himself, embarrassed.

“You wanted the credit.”

Langdon felt himself blush. “Kind of. It’s just that-”

“Don’t be so embarrassed. You’re talking to a scientist. Publish or die. At CERN we call it ‘Prove it or perish.’ ”

“It wasn’t just wanting to be first. I also worried that if the wrong people found the information in Diagramma, it might vanish.”

“The wrong people being the Vatican?”

“Not that they are wrong exactly, but the church has always dismissed the Illuminati threat. In the early 1900s the Vatican even said the Illuminati were imaginary. The clergy felt, maybe rightly so, that the last thing Christians needed was to know a strong anti-Christian group was invading their banks, politics, and universities.” Present tense, Robert, he thought. There IS a strong anti-Christian force invading their banks, politics, and universities.

“So you think the Vatican would have hidden any proof of the Illuminati threat?”

“Very likely. Any threat, real or not, weakens faith in the church’s power.”

“One more question.” Vittoria stopped and looked at him like he was from another planet. “Are you serious?”

Langdon stopped. “What do you mean?”

“I mean, is this really your plan to save the day?”

Langdon couldn’t tell if she looked amused, sorry for him, or just terrified. “You mean finding Diagramma?”

“No, I mean finding Diagramma, finding a four-hundred-year-old segno, figuring out a math code, and following an old trail of art that only the smartest scientists ever have… all in the next four hours.”

Langdon shrugged. “I’m all ears if you have other ideas.”
