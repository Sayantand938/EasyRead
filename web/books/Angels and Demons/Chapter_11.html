Angels and Demons

Chapter 11

Satanic?" Kohler wiped his mouth and shifted uncomfortably. "Is this the symbol of a satanic cult?"

Langdon paced the frozen room to keep warm. "The Illuminati were satanic. But not in the way we think of it today."

Langdon quickly explained that most people see satanic cults as devil-worshiping monsters, but historically, Satanists were educated men who opposed the church. Shaitan. The stories of satanic black-magic animal sacrifices and the pentagram ritual were just lies spread by the church to discredit their enemies. Later, people who opposed the church and wanted to be like the Illuminati started believing these lies and acting them out. That's how modern Satanism began.

Kohler grunted suddenly. "This is all old news. I want to know how this symbol got here."

Langdon took a deep breath. "The symbol itself was created by an unknown Illuminati artist in the 16th century to honor Galileo's love of symmetry—a kind of special Illuminati logo. The brotherhood kept the design secret, planning to reveal it only when they had gained enough power to reappear and achieve their final goal."

Kohler looked uneasy. "So this symbol means the Illuminati brotherhood is reappearing?"

Langdon frowned. "That's impossible. There's one part of Illuminati history I haven't explained yet."

Kohler's voice grew stronger. "Tell me."

Langdon rubbed his hands together, mentally going through the many documents he had read or written about the Illuminati. "The Illuminati were survivors," he explained. "When they fled Rome, they traveled across Europe looking for a safe place to gather again. They were taken in by another secret society… a brotherhood of wealthy Bavarian stone workers called the Freemasons."

Kohler looked startled. “The Masons?”

Langdon nodded, not surprised Kohler knew of them. The Masons had over five million members worldwide, half in the United States, and over a million in Europe.

“Surely the Masons aren't satanic,” Kohler said, sounding doubtful.

“Not at all. The Masons were victims of their own kindness. After sheltering the scientists in the 1700s, the Masons unknowingly became a cover for the Illuminati. The Illuminati grew within them, slowly taking over leadership roles. They quietly rebuilt their science group inside the Masons—a secret society within a secret society. Then the Illuminati used the Masons' global network to spread their influence.”

Langdon took a breath and hurried on. “Destroying Catholicism was the Illuminati's main goal. They believed the Church's superstitious teachings were humanity's greatest enemy. They feared that if religion kept pushing myths as fact, science would stop, and humanity would be stuck in an ignorant future of pointless religious wars.”

“Like we see today.”

Langdon frowned. Kohler was right. Religious wars were still in the news. My God is better than your God. It seemed believers and death tolls were always linked.

“Go on,” Kohler said.

Langdon gathered his thoughts. “The Illuminati gained power in Europe and targeted America, a new government where many leaders were Masons—George Washington, Ben Franklin—honest men who didn't know the Illuminati controlled the Masons. The Illuminati used this control to help start banks, universities, and industries to fund their main goal.” Langdon paused. “Creating a single, united world government—a secular New World Order.”

Kohler didn't move.

“A New World Order,” Langdon repeated, “based on scientific understanding. They called it their Luciferian Doctrine. The church said Lucifer was the devil, but the brotherhood insisted Lucifer meant, in Latin, bringer of light. Or Illuminator.”

Kohler sighed, and his voice turned serious. “Mr. Langdon, please sit down.”

Langdon sat carefully on a cold chair.

Kohler moved his wheelchair closer. “I’m not sure I understand everything you’ve said, but I understand this. Leonardo Vetra was one of CERN’s best people. He was also a friend. I need you to help me find the Illuminati.”

Langdon didn’t know what to say. “Find the Illuminati?” He’s joking, right? “I’m afraid, sir, that’s completely impossible.”

Kohler frowned. “What do you mean? You won’t-”

“Mr. Kohler.” Langdon leaned toward him, unsure how to explain. “I didn’t finish my story. Despite how it looks, it’s very unlikely the Illuminati put this mark here. There’s been no sign of them for over fifty years, and most experts agree the Illuminati have been gone for many years.”

The words hung in the air. Kohler stared through the fog, looking somewhere between shocked and angry. “How can you say this group is gone when their name is burned into this man!”

Langdon had been asking himself that all morning. The Illuminati symbol was amazing. Symbol experts everywhere would be impressed. And yet, Langdon knew that the symbol’s reappearance didn’t prove anything about the Illuminati.

“Symbols,” Langdon said, “don’t prove that the people who made them are still around.”

“What is that supposed to mean?”

“It means when structured groups like the Illuminati disappear, their symbols stay… able to be used by other groups. It’s called transference. It happens a lot with symbols. The Nazis took the swastika from the Hindus, the Christians took the cross shape from the Egyptians, the-”

“This morning,” Kohler argued, “when I typed ‘Illuminati’ into the computer, I got thousands of current results. It seems a lot of people think this group is still around.”

“Conspiracy fans,” Langdon replied. He had always been bothered by the many conspiracy theories in modern pop culture. The media wanted dramatic headlines, and so-called “cult experts” were still profiting from the millennium hype with made-up stories that the Illuminati were alive and planning their New World Order. Recently, the New York Times had written about the strange Masonic connections of many famous men—Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, the Duke of Kent, Peter Sellers, Irving Berlin, Prince Philip, Louis Armstrong, and many well-known modern business leaders and bankers.

Kohler pointed angrily at Vetra’s body. “Looking at the evidence, I’d say the conspiracy fans might be right.”

“I know how it looks,” Langdon said carefully. “But a more likely explanation is that some other group has taken the Illuminati name and is using it for their own goals.”

“What goals? What does this murder prove?”

Good question, Langdon thought. He also couldn’t imagine how anyone could have found the Illuminati name after 400 years. “All I can say is that even if the Illuminati were still active today, which I’m almost sure they’re not, they wouldn’t be involved in Leonardo Vetra’s death.”

“No?”

“No. The Illuminati may have wanted to end Christianity, but they used political and financial power, not terrorism. Also, the Illuminati had strict rules about who they saw as enemies. They highly respected scientists. There’s no way they would have murdered a fellow scientist like Leonardo Vetra.”

Kohler’s eyes turned to ice. “Maybe I didn’t make it clear that Leonardo Vetra was no ordinary scientist.”

Langdon sighed, trying to be patient. “Mr. Kohler, I’m sure Leonardo Vetra was brilliant in many ways, but the fact is-”

Suddenly, Kohler spun his wheelchair and sped out of the living room, leaving mist swirling behind him as he vanished down a hallway.

Good God, Langdon groaned. He followed. Kohler was waiting in a small space at the end of the hallway.

“This is Leonardo’s study,” Kohler said, pointing to the sliding door. “Maybe seeing it will change your mind.” With a strained grunt, Kohler pushed, and the door slid open.

Langdon looked into the study and instantly felt his skin crawl. Jesus Christ, he thought.
