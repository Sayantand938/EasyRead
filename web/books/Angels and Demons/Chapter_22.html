Angels and Demons

Chapter 22

Kohler, unbelievably, was standing for a moment, swaying on his thin legs. His face was pale with fear. “Vittoria! You can’t take that trap off!”

Langdon watched, confused by the director’s sudden worry.

“Five hundred nanograms!” Kohler said. “If you break the magnetic field-”

“Director,” Vittoria said calmly, “it’s completely safe. Every trap has a backup-a battery in case it’s taken off its charger. The sample stays floating even if I remove the container.”

Kohler looked unsure. Then, slowly, he sat back down in his chair.

“The batteries turn on by themselves,” Vittoria said, “when the trap is moved from the charger. They last for twenty-four hours. Like a spare tank of gas.” She turned to Langdon, as if seeing his unease. “Antimatter has some amazing properties, Mr. Langdon, that make it very dangerous. A ten milligram sample-the size of a grain of sand-is said to have as much energy as about two hundred metric tons of regular rocket fuel.”

Langdon’s head was spinning again.

“It’s the energy source of the future. A thousand times stronger than nuclear energy. Completely efficient. No waste. No radiation. No pollution. A few grams could power a big city for a week.”

Grams? Langdon took a step back from the podium, feeling uneasy.

“Don’t worry,” Vittoria said. “ These samples are tiny bits of a gram-millionths. Relatively harmless.” She reached for the canister again and twisted it from its docking platform.

Kohler twitched but did not interfere. As the trap came free, there was a sharp beep, and a small LED display turned on near the base of the trap. The red digits blinked, counting down from twenty-four hours.

24:00:00…

23:59:59…

23:59:58…

Langdon studied the dropping counter and decided it looked disturbingly like a time bomb.

“The battery,” Vittoria explained, “will last for the full twenty-four hours before dying. It can be recharged by placing the trap back on the podium. It’s designed as a safety measure, but it’s also handy for transport.”

“Transport?” Kohler looked shocked. “You take this stuff out of the lab?”

“Of course not,” Vittoria said. “But the ability to move it allows us to study it.”

Vittoria led Langdon and Kohler to the far end of the room. She pulled a curtain aside to reveal a window, beyond which was a large room. The walls, floors, and ceiling were entirely covered in steel. The room reminded Langdon of the holding tank of an oil freighter he had once taken to Papua New Guinea to study Hanta body art.

“It’s an annihilation tank,” Vittoria said.

Kohler looked up. “You actually watch annihilations?”

“My father was fascinated by the physics of the Big Bang—huge amounts of energy from tiny bits of matter.” Vittoria pulled open a steel drawer under the window. She put the trap inside and closed the drawer. Then she pulled a lever next to the drawer. A moment later, the trap appeared on the other side of the glass, rolling smoothly in a wide curve across the metal floor until it stopped near the center of the room.

Vittoria smiled tightly. “You’re about to see your first antimatter-matter annihilation. A few millionths of a gram. A very small sample.”

Langdon looked at the antimatter trap sitting alone on the floor of the huge tank. Kohler also turned toward the window, looking unsure.

“Normally,” Vittoria explained, “we’d have to wait a full twenty-four hours until the batteries died, but this chamber has magnets under the floor that can control the trap, pulling the antimatter out of suspension. And when the matter and antimatter touch…”

“Annihilation,” Kohler whispered.

“One more thing,” Vittoria said. “Antimatter releases pure energy. A one hundred percent conversion of mass to photons. So don’t look right at the sample. Shield your eyes.”

Langdon was cautious, but he now felt Vittoria was exaggerating. Don’t look right at the canister? The device was more than thirty yards away, behind a very thick wall of tinted Plexiglas. Also, the speck in the canister was invisible, microscopic. Shield my eyes? Langdon thought. How much energy could that speck possibly -

Vittoria pressed the button.

Instantly, Langdon went blind. A bright light appeared in the canister and then burst outward in a wave that spread everywhere, crashing against the window with a loud boom. He stepped back as the blast shook the vault. The light burned bright for a moment, searing, and then quickly rushed back in, shrinking into a tiny dot that vanished. Langdon blinked in pain, slowly able to see again. He squinted into the smoky room. The canister on the floor was gone. Vaporized. No sign of it.

He stared in amazement. “G… God.”

Vittoria nodded sadly. “That’s exactly what my father said.”
