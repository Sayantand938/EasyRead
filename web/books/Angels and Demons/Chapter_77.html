Angels and Demons

Chapter 77

Robert Langdon stumbled into the private bathroom next to the Pope's Office. He wiped the blood from his face and lips. The blood wasn't his. It belonged to Cardinal Lamassé, who had just died horribly in the crowded square outside the Vatican. Virgin sacrifices on the altars of science. So far, the Hassassin had done what he threatened.

Langdon felt helpless as he looked in the mirror. His eyes were tired, and his face was getting dark with stubble. The room around him was spotless and luxurious—black marble with gold details, soft cotton towels, and nice-smelling soaps.

Langdon tried to forget the bloody mark he had just seen. Air. The image stayed in his mind. He had seen three ambigrams since waking up this morning… and he knew there were two more to come.

Outside the door, it sounded like Olivetti, the camerlegno, and Captain Rocher were arguing about what to do next. It seemed the search for the antimatter had found nothing. Either the guards had missed the canister, or the intruder had gotten further into the Vatican than Commander Olivetti wanted to believe.

Langdon dried his hands and face. Then he turned to find a urinal. There wasn't one. Just a toilet. He lifted the lid.

As he stood there, feeling the tension leave his body, a dizzying wave of exhaustion washed over him. The feelings in his chest were many and confusing. He was tired, running on no food or sleep, following the Path of Illumination, and shocked by two violent murders. Langdon felt a growing fear about how this would all end.

Think, he told himself. His mind was empty.

As he flushed, he suddenly realized something. This is the Pope’s toilet, he thought. I just peed in the Pope’s toilet. He had to laugh. The Holy Throne.

In London, a BBC technician took a video cassette out of a satellite receiver and rushed across the control room. She ran into the editor-in-chief's office, shoved the video into his VCR, and hit play.

As the tape played, she told him about her conversation with Gunther Glick in Vatican City. Also, the BBC photo archives had just confirmed the identity of the victim in St. Peter’s Square.

When the editor-in-chief came out of his office, he was ringing a cowbell. Everything in editorial stopped.

“Live in five!” he shouted. “On-air talent, get ready! Media coordinators, get your contacts ready! We’ve got a story to sell! And we’ve got video!”

The market coordinators grabbed their Rolodexes.

“Video details!” one of them yelled.

“Thirty-second clip,” the chief replied.

“What’s in it?”

“Live murder.”

The coordinators looked excited. “Price for use and rights?”

“A million U.S. each.”

Heads shot up. “What!”

“You heard me! I want the best coverage. CNN, MSNBC, then the big three! Offer a preview they can call in to hear. Give them five minutes to use it before BBC runs it.”

“What the hell happened?” someone demanded. “Did the prime minister get ripped to shreds?”

The chief shook his head. “Better.”

At that exact instant, somewhere in Rome, the Hassassin enjoyed a brief moment of rest in a comfortable chair. He admired the famous room around him. I am sitting in the Church of Illumination , he thought. The Illuminati hideout. He could not believe it was still here after so many centuries.

He dialed the BBC reporter he had spoken to earlier. It was time. The world was about to hear the most shocking news of all.
