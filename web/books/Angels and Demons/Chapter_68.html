Angels and Demons

Chapter 68

Despite the soft glow of candlelight in the Sistine Chapel, Cardinal Mortati was on edge. The Conclave had officially begun. And it had begun in a most unlucky way.

Half an hour ago, at the set time, Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca had entered the chapel. He walked to the front altar and gave the opening prayer. Then, he unfolded his hands and spoke to them in a tone as direct as anything Mortati had ever heard from the altar of the Sistine.

“You are well aware,” the camerlegno said, “that our four favorites are not here at this moment. I ask, in the name of his late Holiness, that you proceed as you must… with faith and purpose. May you have only God before your eyes.” Then he turned to leave.

“But,” one cardinal blurted out, “where are they?”

The camerlegno paused. “That I cannot honestly say.”

“When will they return?”

“That I cannot honestly say.”

“Are they okay?”

“That I cannot honestly say.”

"Will they come back?"

There was a long pause.

"Have faith," the camerlegno said. Then he walked out of the room.

The doors to the Sistine Chapel had been sealed, as was the custom, with two heavy chains on the outside. Four Swiss Guards stood watch in the hallway beyond. Mortati knew the only way the doors could now be opened, before a Pope was chosen, was if someone inside became gravely ill, or if the chosen ones arrived. Mortati prayed it would be the latter, though the knot in his stomach made him unsure.

We must go on, Mortati decided, taking courage from the camerlegno's firm voice. So he had called for a vote. What else could he do?

It had taken thirty minutes to complete the rituals before this first vote. Mortati had waited patiently at the main altar as each cardinal, in order of seniority, had approached and performed the specific voting steps.

Now, at last, the final cardinal had arrived at the altar and was kneeling before him.

"I call as my witness," the cardinal declared, just like those before him, "Christ the Lord, who will judge me, that my vote is given to the one who I believe should be elected before God."

The cardinal stood up. He held his ballot high over his head for everyone to see. Then he lowered the ballot to the altar, where a plate sat on top of a large chalice. He placed the ballot on the plate. Next, he picked up the plate and used it to drop the ballot into the chalice. The plate was used to make sure no one secretly dropped in more than one ballot.

After he had submitted his ballot, he replaced the plate over the chalice, bowed to the cross, and returned to his seat.

The final vote had been taken.

Now it was time for Mortati to begin.

Leaving the plate on the chalice, Mortati shook the ballots to mix them. Then he took off the plate and pulled out a ballot randomly. He unfolded it. The ballot was exactly two inches wide. He read it aloud for everyone to hear.

“Eligo in summum pontificem …” he said, reading the words printed at the top of every ballot. I elect as Supreme Pontiff … Then he announced the name written beneath it. After reading the name, he raised a needle and poked it through the word Eligo on the ballot, carefully sliding the ballot onto the thread. Then he wrote down the vote in a logbook.

Next, he did the same thing again. He chose a ballot from the chalice, read it aloud, threaded it onto the line, and noted it in his log. Almost right away, Mortati knew this first vote would fail. No agreement. After only seven ballots, seven different cardinals had already been named. As usual, the handwriting on each ballot was hidden by block printing or fancy writing. The hiding was funny in this case because the cardinals were clearly voting for themselves. This obvious vanity, Mortati knew, wasn't about selfish ambition. It was a way to wait. A way to protect themselves. A way to delay so no cardinal got enough votes to win… and another vote would be needed.

The cardinals were waiting for their preferiti …

When the last of the ballots had been counted, Mortati said the vote had “failed.”

He took the thread with all the ballots and tied the ends together to make a ring. Then he put the ring of ballots on a silver tray. He added the right chemicals and took the tray to a small chimney behind him. There, he lit the ballots. As they burned, the chemicals he added made black smoke. The smoke went up a pipe to a hole in the roof where it rose above the chapel for everyone to see. Cardinal Mortati had just sent his first message to the outside world.

One vote. No Pope.
