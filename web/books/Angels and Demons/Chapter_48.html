Angels and Demons

Chapter 48

BBC journalist Gunther Glick sat sweating in the BBC van parked on the east side of St. Peter’s Square and cursed his editor. Although Glick’s first review had been full of praise—resourceful, sharp, dependable—here he was in Vatican City on “Pope-Watch.” He reminded himself that reporting for the BBC was much more respected than writing stories for the British Tattler, but still, this was not his idea of reporting.

Glick’s job was simple. Insultingly simple. He was to sit here waiting for a bunch of old men to elect their next top old man, then he was to step outside and record a fifteen-second “live” report with the Vatican behind him.

Brilliant.

Glick couldn’t believe the BBC still sent reporters to cover this nonsense. You don’t see the American networks here tonight. Hell no! That was because the big networks did it right. They watched CNN, summarized it, and then filmed their “live” report in front of a blue screen, using stock video for a realistic backdrop. MSNBC even used wind and rain machines in the studio to make it seem real. Viewers didn’t want truth anymore; they wanted entertainment.

Glick gazed out through the windshield and felt more and more depressed. The huge Vatican City rose before him as a sad reminder of what people could do when they tried.

“What have I done in my life?” he wondered aloud. “Nothing.”

“So give up,” a woman’s voice said from behind him.

Glick jumped. He had almost forgotten he was not alone. He turned to the back seat, where his camerawoman, Chinita Macri, sat silently cleaning her glasses. She was always cleaning her glasses. Chinita was black, although she preferred African American, a little heavy, and very smart. She wouldn’t let you forget it either. She was an odd person, but Glick liked her. And Glick could really use the company.

“What’s the problem, Gunth?” Chinita asked.

“What are we doing here?”

She kept polishing. “Watching something interesting.”

“Old men locked in the dark is interesting?”

“You know you’re going to hell, right?”

“Already there.”

“Talk to me.” She sounded like his mother.

“I just want to do something that matters.”

“You wrote for the British Tattler.”

“Yeah, but nothing that really stuck with people.”

“Oh, come on, I heard you wrote a huge story about the queen secretly having sex with aliens.”

“Thanks.”

“Hey, things are getting better. Tonight, you'll be on TV for the first time.”

Glick groaned. He could already hear the news anchor. “Thanks Gunther, great report.” Then the anchor would roll his eyes and move on to the weather. “I should have tried to be an anchor.”

Macri laughed. “With no experience? And that beard? No way.”

Glick ran his hands through the reddish clump of hair on his chin. “I think it makes me look smart.”

The van’s cell phone rang, thankfully stopping another one of Glick’s failures. “Maybe that’s the news desk,” he said, suddenly hopeful. “You think they want a live update?”

“On this story?” Macri laughed. “Keep dreaming.”

Glick answered the phone in his best anchorman voice. “Gunther Glick, BBC, Live in Vatican City.”

The man on the line had a thick Arabic accent. “Listen carefully,” he said. “I am about to change your life.”
