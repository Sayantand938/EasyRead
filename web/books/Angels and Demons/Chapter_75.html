Angels and Demons

Chapter 75

Langdon was the first to reach the screaming girl.

The terrified girl stood still, pointing at the bottom of the obelisk where a dirty, old drunk sat slumped on the stairs. The man looked awful… clearly one of Rome’s homeless. His gray hair hung in greasy strands, and his whole body was covered in some kind of dirty cloth. The girl kept screaming as she ran away into the crowd.

Langdon felt a surge of fear as he ran toward the injured man. A dark stain was spreading across the man’s rags. Fresh, flowing blood.

Then, everything seemed to happen at once.

The old man seemed to collapse, falling forward. Langdon reached out, but he was too late. The man fell off the stairs and hit the ground face first. He didn't move.

Langdon knelt down. Vittoria came beside him. A crowd was forming.

Vittoria put her fingers on the man’s throat from behind. “He has a pulse,” she said. “Roll him over.”

Langdon was already moving. He grabbed the man’s shoulders and rolled him over. As he did, the loose rags seemed to fall away like dead skin. The man fell onto his back. Right in the middle of his bare chest was a large area of burned skin.

Vittoria gasped and stepped back.

Langdon felt frozen, caught between sickness and wonder. The symbol was frighteningly simple.

“Air,” Vittoria gasped. “It’s… him.”

Swiss Guards seemed to materialize, yelling commands, chasing after an invisible killer.

Close by, a tourist mentioned that just moments before, a dark-skinned man had kindly assisted this frail, gasping, homeless man across the square… even sitting with him on the steps for a moment before vanishing into the crowd.

Vittoria tore the remaining rags from the man’s stomach. He had two deep holes, one on each side of the mark, just under his ribs. She tilted the man’s head back and began giving him mouth-to-mouth resuscitation. Langdon was not ready for what came next. As Vittoria breathed, the wounds on either side of the man’s torso hissed and shot blood into the air like a whale’s blowholes. The salty liquid splattered on Langdon’s face.

Vittoria paused, looking terrified. “His lungs…” she stuttered. “They’re… punctured.”

Langdon wiped his eyes as he looked at the two holes. They gurgled. The cardinal’s lungs were ruined. He was dead.

Vittoria covered the body as the Swiss Guards approached.

Langdon stood, confused. As he did, he noticed her. The woman who had been trailing them earlier was crouched nearby. Her BBC video camera was on her shoulder, pointed, and recording. She and Langdon made eye contact, and he knew she had captured everything. Then, like a cat, she darted away.
