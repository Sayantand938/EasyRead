Angels and Demons

Chapter 6

Sixty-four minutes had gone by when Robert Langdon, feeling queasy and unable to believe what was happening, walked off the plane onto the sunny runway. A cool breeze moved the edges of his tweed jacket. The open space felt great. He looked at the green valley that rose to snowy mountains all around them.

I’m dreaming, he thought. I’ll wake up any second.

“Welcome to Switzerland,” the pilot said, shouting over the loud noise of the X-33’s engines as they slowed down behind them.

Langdon looked at his watch. It said 7:07 A.M.

“You just went across six time zones,” the pilot said. “It’s a little after 1 P.M. here.”

Langdon changed the time on his watch.

“How do you feel?”

He rubbed his stomach. “Like I’ve been eating Styrofoam.”

The pilot nodded. “Altitude sickness. We were sixty thousand feet up. You weigh less up there. It’s a good thing we only went a short distance. If we’d gone to Tokyo, I’d have taken the plane all the way up—a hundred miles. That would really make your stomach turn.”

Langdon nodded weakly, feeling fortunate. Considering everything, the flight had been surprisingly normal. Besides the intense pressure during takeoff, the plane's movement had been fairly standard—some light turbulence, slight pressure changes as they gained altitude, but nothing to indicate they were flying through space at the incredible speed of 11,000 miles per hour.

A few technicians hurried onto the runway to service the X-33. The pilot led Langdon to a black Peugeot sedan in a parking area near the control tower. Soon they were speeding down a paved road that ran across the valley floor. A small group of buildings appeared in the distance. Outside, the grassy plains rushed by.

Langdon was amazed as the pilot increased the speedometer to around 170 kilometers an hour—over 100 miles per hour. Why is this guy so obsessed with speed? he wondered.

"Five kilometers to the lab," the pilot said. "I'll get you there in two minutes."

Langdon looked around for a seat belt but couldn't find one. Why not take three minutes and get us there safely?

The car sped on.

"Do you like Reba?" the pilot asked, putting a cassette into the tape player.

A woman began to sing. "It's just the fear of being alone…"

No fear here, Langdon thought distractedly. His female colleagues often teased him that his collection of valuable artifacts was just an obvious attempt to fill a lonely home, a home they believed would be better with a woman. Langdon always brushed it off, reminding them he already had three loves—symbology, water polo, and being single—the last of which allowed him to travel the world, sleep in, and enjoy peaceful nights at home with a brandy and a good book.

"We're like a small city," the pilot said, bringing Langdon back to reality. "Not just labs. We have supermarkets, a hospital, even a cinema."

Langdon nodded, looking blankly at the many buildings in front of them.

“Actually,” the pilot added, “we have the biggest machine on earth.”

“Really?” Langdon looked around at the area.

“You can’t see it out there, sir.” The pilot smiled. “It’s buried six floors underground.”

Langdon didn’t get to ask anything. The pilot suddenly slammed on the brakes. The car stopped quickly outside a strong guard station.

Langdon read the sign in front of him. SECURITE. ARRETEZ. He suddenly felt panic, understanding where he was. “My God! I don’t have my passport!”

“Passports aren't needed,” the driver said. “We have an agreement with the Swiss government.”

Langdon watched in shock as his driver gave the guard an ID. The guard scanned it with an electronic reader. The machine showed a green light.

“Passenger name?”

“Robert Langdon,” the driver replied.

“Guest of?”

“The director.”

The guard raised his eyebrows. He turned and checked a computer printout, comparing it to the data on his screen. Then he turned back to the window. “Enjoy your stay, Mr. Langdon.”

The car sped off again, accelerating another 200 yards around a wide roundabout that led to the facility’s main entrance. A rectangular, very modern building of glass and steel stood before them. Langdon was amazed by the building’s impressive transparent design. He had always loved architecture.

“The Glass Cathedral,” the escort said.

“A church?”

“No way. A church is the one thing we don’t have. Physics is the religion here. Use the Lord’s name all you like,” he laughed, “just don’t insult any quarks or mesons.”

Langdon sat confused as the driver turned the car around and stopped in front of the glass building. Quarks and mesons? No border control? Mach 15 jets? Who ARE these people? The engraved granite slab in front of the building held the answer:

(CERN)Conseil Europ* _й_* en pour la Recherche Nucl* _й_* aire

“Nuclear Research?” Langdon asked, pretty sure his translation was right.

The driver didn't answer. He leaned forward, busy with the car's cassette player. "This is your stop. The director will meet you at this entrance."

Langdon saw a man in a wheelchair leaving the building. He looked to be in his early sixties, thin and completely bald, with a firm jaw. He wore a white lab coat and dress shoes resting on the wheelchair's footrest. Even from far away, his eyes looked lifeless, like two gray stones.

"Is that him?" Langdon asked.

The driver looked up. "Well, I'll be." He turned and gave Langdon a strange smile. "Talk of the devil."

Not knowing what to expect, Langdon got out of the car.

The man in the wheelchair sped toward Langdon and offered a cold, sweaty hand. "Mr. Langdon? We spoke on the phone. My name is Maximilian Kohler."
