Angels and Demons

Chapter 107

Robert Langdon hurried around the castle's outer wall, thankful for the bright lights. As he went around, the courtyard below looked like a museum of old weapons—catapults, piles of stone cannonballs, and a collection of scary devices. Tourists could visit parts of the castle during the day, and the courtyard had been partly returned to how it originally looked.

Langdon's eyes moved across the courtyard to the fortress's central part. The round citadel rose straight up, 107 feet to the bronze angel on top. The balcony at the top still glowed from inside. Langdon wanted to shout, but he knew better. He had to find a way in.

He checked his watch.

11:12 P.M.

Running down the stone ramp that followed the inside of the wall, Langdon went down to the courtyard. Back on the ground, he ran through shadows, circling the fort clockwise. He passed three archways, but they were all permanently closed. How did the Hassassin get in? Langdon kept going. He passed two modern entrances, but they were locked from the outside. Not here. He kept running.

Langdon had almost circled the entire building when he saw a gravel path crossing the courtyard in front of him. At one end, on the castle's outer wall, he saw the back of the gated drawbridge leading back outside. At the other end, the path disappeared into the fortress. The path seemed to enter a kind of tunnel—a wide opening in the central part. Il traforo! Langdon had read about this castle's traforo, a giant spiral ramp inside the fort, used by commanders on horseback to quickly ride from top to bottom. The Hassassin drove up! The gate blocking the tunnel was raised, inviting Langdon in. He felt almost joyful as he ran toward the tunnel. But as he reached the opening, his excitement vanished.

The tunnel spiraled down.

The wrong way. This section of the traforo apparently went down to the dungeons, not to the top.

Standing at the entrance of a dark hole that seemed to twist endlessly deeper into the earth, Langdon hesitated, looking up again at the balcony. He could have sworn he saw movement up there. Decide! With no other choices, he dashed down into the tunnel.

High overhead, the Hassassin stood over his prey. He ran a hand across her arm. Her skin was like cream. The thought of exploring her body was intoxicating. How many ways could he violate her?

The Hassassin felt he deserved this woman. He had served Janus well. She was a spoil of war, and when he was finished with her, he would pull her from the divan and force her to her knees. She would serve him again. The ultimate submission. Then, at the moment of his own climax, he would slit her throat.

Ghayat assa’adah , they called it. The ultimate pleasure.

Afterward, basking in his glory, he would stand on the balcony and savor the culmination of the Illuminati triumph… a revenge desired by so many for so long.

The tunnel grew darker. Langdon descended.

After one complete turn into the earth, the light was almost gone. The tunnel leveled out, and Langdon slowed, sensing by the echo of his footfalls that he had just entered a larger chamber. Before him in the gloom, he thought he saw glimmers of light… fuzzy reflections in the dim glow. He moved forward, reaching out his hand. He found smooth surfaces. Chrome and glass. It was a vehicle. He groped the surface, found a door, and opened it.

The vehicle’s interior dome-light flashed on. He stepped back and recognized the black van immediately. Feeling a surge of loathing, he stared a moment, then he dove in, searching around in hopes of finding a weapon to replace the one he’d lost in the fountain. He found none. He did, however, find Vittoria’s cell phone. It was shattered and useless. The sight of it filled Langdon with fear. He prayed he was not too late.

He reached up and turned on the van’s headlights. The room around him blazed into existence, harsh shadows in a simple chamber. Langdon guessed the room was once used for horses and ammunition. It was also a dead end.

No exit. I came the wrong way!

At the end of his rope, Langdon jumped from the van and scanned the walls around him. No doorways. No gates. He thought of the angel over the tunnel entrance and wondered if it had been a coincidence. No! He thought of the killer’s words at the fountain. She is in the Church of Illumination… awaiting my return. Langdon had come too far to fail now. His heart was pounding. Frustration and hatred were starting to cripple his senses.

When he saw the blood on the floor, Langdon’s first thought was for Vittoria. But as his eyes followed the stains, he realized they were bloody footprints. The steps were long. The blood was only on the left foot. The Hassassin!

Langdon followed the footprints toward the corner of the room, his large shadow growing fainter. He felt more and more puzzled with every step. The bloody prints looked as though they walked directly into the corner of the room and then disappeared.

When Langdon arrived in the corner, he could not believe his eyes. The granite block in the floor here was not a square like the others. He was looking at another signpost. The block was carved into a perfect pentagram, with the tip pointing into the corner. Hidden by overlapping walls, a narrow slit in the stone served as an exit. Langdon slid through. He was in a passage. In front of him were the remains of a wooden barrier that had once blocked this tunnel.

Beyond it there was light.

Langdon was running now. He climbed over the wood and headed for the light. The passage quickly opened into another, larger chamber. Here a single torch flickered on the wall. Langdon was in a part of the castle that had no electricity… a part no tourists would ever see. The room would have been scary in daylight, but the torch made it even more gruesome.

Il prigione.

There were a dozen tiny jail cells, the iron bars on most rusted away. One of the larger cells, however, remained intact, and on the floor Langdon saw something that almost stopped his heart. Black robes and red sashes on the floor. This is where he held the cardinals!

Near the cell was an iron doorway in the wall. The door was ajar and beyond it Langdon could see some sort of passage. He ran toward it. But Langdon stopped before he got there. The trail of blood did not enter the passage. When Langdon saw the words carved over the archway, he knew why.

Il Passetto.

He was stunned. He had heard of this tunnel many times, never knowing exactly where the entrance was. Il Passetto -The Little Passage-was a narrow, three-quarter-mile tunnel built between Castle St. Angelo and the Vatican. It had been used by various Popes to escape to safety during sieges of the Vatican… as well as by a few less pious Popes to secretly visit mistresses or watch the torture of their enemies. Nowadays both ends of the tunnel were supposedly sealed with strong locks whose keys were kept in some Vatican vault. Langdon suddenly feared he knew how the Illuminati had been moving in and out of the Vatican. He wondered who on the inside had betrayed the church and given up the keys. Olivetti? One of the Swiss Guard? None of it mattered anymore.

The blood on the floor led to the other side of the prison. Langdon followed. There, a rusty gate hung with chains. The lock was gone, and the gate was slightly open. Beyond it, a steep spiral staircase climbed upward. The floor here also had a pentagram-shaped block. Langdon stared at it, trembling, wondering if Bernini himself had shaped these stones. Above, a small carved cherub decorated the archway. This was it.

The trail of blood curved up the stairs.

Before going up, Langdon knew he needed a weapon. He found a four-foot piece of iron bar near a cell. It had a sharp, broken end. It was very heavy, but it was the best he could find. He hoped the surprise, along with the Hassassin’s injury, would be enough to give him an advantage. Most of all, he hoped he wasn't too late.

The spiral steps were worn and twisted sharply upward. Langdon climbed, listening for sounds. There were none. As he climbed, the light from the prison faded. He climbed into complete darkness, keeping one hand on the wall. Higher. In the darkness, Langdon felt the presence of Galileo, climbing these same stairs, wanting to share his visions of heaven with other scientists and believers.

Langdon was still shocked by the location of the lair. The Illuminati meeting hall was in a building owned by the Vatican. While the Vatican guards were searching basements and homes of famous scientists, the Illuminati were meeting here … right under the Vatican's nose. It seemed so perfect. Bernini, as the main architect of renovations here, would have had unlimited access to this building… remodeling it as he wanted without anyone questioning him. How many secret entrances had Bernini added? How many hidden clues pointing the way?

The Church of Illumination. Langdon knew he was close.

As the stairs narrowed, Langdon felt the passage closing in around him. The shadows of history whispered in the dark, but he kept going. When he saw a line of light ahead, he realized he was a few steps below a landing, where torchlight shone under a door. Silently, he moved up.

Langdon had no idea where in the castle he was, but he knew he had climbed high enough to be near the top. He imagined the huge angel statue on the castle roof and thought it was directly above him.

Watch over me, angel, he thought, gripping the bar. Then, silently, he reached for the door.

On the divan, Vittoria's arms ached. When she woke up and found them tied behind her back, she thought she could relax and free her hands. But time had run out. The beast had returned. Now he stood over her, his chest bare and strong, scarred from battles. His eyes were like black slits as he stared down at her body. Vittoria felt he was imagining what he was about to do. Slowly, as if to tease her, the Hassassin took off his wet belt and dropped it on the floor.

Vittoria felt a sickening dread. She closed her eyes. When she opened them again, the Hassassin had taken out a switchblade knife. He flicked it open right in front of her face.

Vittoria saw her own scared face in the steel.

The Hassassin turned the blade over and ran the back of it across her stomach. The cold metal gave her chills. With a hateful look, he slid the blade under the waistband of her shorts. She gasped. He moved back and forth, slowly, threateningly… lower. Then he leaned forward, his hot breath whispering in her ear.

“This blade cut out your father’s eye.”

Vittoria knew right then that she could kill.

The Hassassin turned the blade again and began cutting upward through the material of her khaki shorts. Suddenly, he stopped, looking up. Someone was in the room.

“Get away from her,” a deep voice growled from the doorway.

Vittoria could not see who had spoken, but she knew the voice. Robert! He’s alive!

The Hassassin looked shocked. “Mr. Langdon, you must have a guardian angel.”
