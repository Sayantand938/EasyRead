Angels and Demons

Chapter 104

Robert Langdon lay on a bed of coins at the bottom of the Fountain of the Four Rivers. His mouth was still around the plastic hose. The air being pumped through the spumanti tube to make the fountain bubble had been polluted by the pump, and his throat burned. He wasn't complaining, though. He was alive.

He wasn't sure how good his acting as a drowning man had been, but having been around water his whole life, Langdon had heard stories. He had done his best. Near the end, he had even blown all the air from his lungs and stopped breathing so his body would sink to the bottom.

Thankfully, the Hassassin had fallen for it and let go.

Now, resting on the bottom of the fountain, Langdon waited as long as he could. He was about to choke. He wondered if the Hassassin was still out there. Taking a bitter breath from the tube, Langdon let go and swam across the bottom of the fountain until he found the smooth curve of the central core. Silently, he followed it upward, surfacing out of sight, in the shadows under the huge marble figures.

The van was gone.

That was all Langdon needed to see. Taking a long breath of fresh air, he scrambled back toward where Cardinal Baggia had gone down. Langdon knew the man would be unconscious now, and his chances of revival were low, but he had to try. When Langdon found the body, he stood with his feet on either side, reached down, and grabbed the chains around the cardinal. Then Langdon pulled. When the cardinal came out of the water, Langdon could see his eyes were already rolled back, bulging. Not a good sign. There was no breath or pulse.

Knowing he could never get the body up and over the fountain's edge, Langdon dragged Cardinal Baggia through the water and into the hollow under the central mound of marble. Here the water became shallow, and there was a sloping ledge. Langdon dragged the naked body up onto the ledge as far as he could. Not far.

Then he went to work. Pushing on the cardinal’s chained chest, Langdon pumped the water from his lungs. Then he began CPR. Counting carefully. Deliberately. Fighting the urge to blow too hard and too fast. For three minutes Langdon tried to revive the old man. After five minutes, Langdon knew it was over.

Il preferito. The man who would be Pope. Lying dead before him.

Somehow, even now, lying in the shadows on the wet ledge, Cardinal Baggia still seemed dignified. The water softly touched his chest, almost sadly, as if asking to be forgiven for killing him, as if trying to clean the burned wound that proved it.

Gently, Langdon smoothed the man’s face and closed his open eyes. As he did, he felt a sudden urge to cry. It surprised him. Then, for the first time in years, Langdon cried.
