Angels and Demons

Chapter 116

It was 11:39 P.M. when Langdon and the others left St. Peter’s Basilica. The bright light was blinding. The media lights reflected off the white marble like sunlight on snow. Langdon squinted, trying to hide behind the huge columns, but the light came from everywhere. In front of him, large video screens towered above the crowd.

Standing on the grand stairs leading down to the piazza, Langdon felt like an unwilling actor on the world's biggest stage. Beyond the bright lights, Langdon heard a helicopter and the roar of thousands of voices. To their left, cardinals were leaving for the square. They stopped, looking worried by the scene on the staircase.

"Careful," Chartrand said, sounding serious as the group started down the stairs toward the helicopter.

Langdon felt like he was moving in slow motion. His arms hurt from carrying the camerlegno and the table. He wondered if things could get any worse. Then he saw the answer. Two BBC reporters had been crossing the square to get back to the press area. But now, hearing the crowd, they had turned around. Glick and Macri were running back toward them. Macri's camera was up and filming. Here come the vultures, Langdon thought.

“Alt!” Chartrand shouted. “Get back!”

But the reporters kept coming. Langdon guessed it would take other networks about six seconds to show this live BBC feed. He was wrong. It took two. As if everyone was thinking the same thing, every media screen in the piazza stopped showing their countdown clocks and Vatican experts and started showing the same picture—shaky footage zooming up the Vatican stairs. Now, everywhere Langdon looked, he saw the camerlegno’s limp body in a close-up.

This is wrong! Langdon thought. He wanted to run down the stairs and stop them, but he couldn't. It wouldn’t have helped anyway. Langdon didn't know if it was the crowd or the cool air, but at that moment, the unbelievable happened.

Like a man waking from a nightmare, the camerlegno’s eyes flew open, and he sat straight up. Surprised, Langdon and the others struggled with the shifting weight. The front of the table tilted. The camerlegno started to slide. They tried to save him by putting the table down, but it was too late. The camerlegno slid off. Amazingly, he didn't fall. His feet hit the marble, and he stood up straight. He stood for a moment, looking confused, and then, before anyone could stop him, he stumbled forward, staggering down the stairs toward Macri.

“No!” Langdon screamed.

Chartrand hurried forward, trying to stop the camerlegno. But the camerlegno turned on him, eyes wide and frantic. “Leave me!”

Chartrand jumped back.

The scene worsened. The camerlegno’s torn cassock, which Chartrand had only placed over his chest, started to slip down. For a moment, Langdon thought it might stay in place, but the moment passed. The cassock fell, sliding off his shoulders to his waist.

The crowd gasped, the sound seeming to travel around the world and back in an instant. Cameras flashed, taking photos. On screens everywhere, the image of the camerlegno’s branded chest was shown, large and in horrifying detail. Some screens even froze the image and turned it upside down.

The ultimate Illuminati victory.

Langdon stared at the brand on the screens. Although it was the imprint of the square brand he had held earlier, the symbol now made sense. Perfect sense. The marking’s incredible power struck Langdon hard.

Orientation. Langdon had forgotten the first rule of symbols. When is a square not a square? He had also forgotten that iron brands, like rubber stamps, never look like their imprints. They are reversed. Langdon had been looking at the brand’s negative!

As the chaos grew, an old Illuminati saying echoed with new meaning: “A flawless diamond, born of the ancient elements with such perfection that all those who saw it could only stare in wonder.”

Langdon now knew the myth was true.

Earth, Air, Fire, Water.

The Illuminati Diamond.
