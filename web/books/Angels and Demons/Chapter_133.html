Angels and Demons

Chapter 133

The Pope had a child.

Inside the Sistine Chapel, the camerlegno stood firm as he spoke. Five simple words of shocking news. Everyone there seemed to pull back together. The cardinals' angry looks turned to stunned stares, as if everyone in the room was praying the camerlegno was wrong.

The Pope had a child.

Langdon felt the shock hit him too. Vittoria’s hand, tight in his, jumped, while Langdon’s mind, already blank with unanswered questions, struggled to find balance.

The camerlegno’s words seemed like they would hang forever in the air above them. Even in the camerlegno’s wild eyes, Langdon could see real belief. Langdon wanted to pull away, tell himself he was stuck in some awful nightmare, soon to wake up in a world that made sense.

“This must be a lie!” one of the cardinals yelled.

“I will not believe it!” another argued. “His Holiness was as faithful a man as ever lived!”

Mortati spoke next, his voice weak with sadness. “My friends. What the camerlegno says is true.” Every cardinal in the chapel turned sharply as if Mortati had just shouted something rude. “The Pope did have a child.”

The cardinals turned pale with fear.

The camerlegno looked stunned. “You knew? But… how could you possibly know this?”

Mortati sighed. “When His Holiness was elected… I was the Devil’s Advocate.”

There was a communal gasp.

Langdon understood. This meant the information was probably true. The infamous “Devil’s Advocate” was the expert when it came to scandalous information inside the Vatican. Secrets in a Pope’s past were dangerous, and before elections, secret investigations into a candidate’s background were done by a single cardinal who was the “Devil’s Advocate”-that person responsible for finding reasons why the eligible cardinals should not become Pope. The Devil’s Advocate was chosen ahead of time by the current Pope to prepare for his own death. The Devil’s Advocate was never supposed to reveal who he was. Ever.

“I was the Devil’s Advocate,” Mortati repeated. “That is how I found out.”

Mouths dropped. It seemed tonight was a night when all the rules were being broken.

The camerlegno felt his heart filling with rage. “And you… told no one?”

“I confronted His Holiness,” Mortati said. “And he confessed. He explained the whole story and only asked that I let my heart decide whether or not to reveal his secret.”

“And your heart told you to hide the information?”

“He was expected to win the papacy. People loved him. The scandal would have deeply hurt the church.”

“But he had a child! He broke his sacred promise of celibacy!” The camerlegno was screaming now. He could hear his mother’s voice. A promise to God is the most important promise of all. Never break a promise to God. “The Pope broke his promise!”

Mortati looked distraught with worry. “Carlo, his love… was pure. He broke no promise. Didn’t he explain it to you?”

“Explain what?” The camerlegno remembered running out of the Pope’s office while the Pope was calling to him. Let me explain!

Slowly, sadly, Mortati told the story. Many years ago, the Pope, when he was still just a priest, had fallen in love with a young nun. Both had made promises of celibacy and never even thought of breaking their agreement with God. Still, as they fell more deeply in love, though they could resist physical temptation, they both longed for something they never expected—to share in God’s greatest miracle of creation—a child. Their child. The longing, especially in her, became very strong. Still, God came first. A year later, when the frustration was almost unbearable, she came to him, excited. She had just read about a new miracle of science—a way for two people to have a child without sexual relations. She felt it was a sign from God. The priest saw the happiness in her eyes and agreed. A year later, she had a child through artificial insemination…

“This can’t… be true,” the camerlegno said, panicked, hoping it was the morphine affecting his senses. He must be hearing things.

Mortati now had tears in his eyes. “Carlo, that’s why His Holiness always cared about science. He felt he owed something to science. Science allowed him to be a father without breaking his promise of celibacy. His Holiness told me he only regretted one thing—that his high position in the church kept him from being with the woman he loved and watching his child grow up.”

Camerlegno Carlo Ventresca felt the madness returning. He wanted to tear at his skin. How could I have known?

“The Pope didn’t sin, Carlo. He was pure.”

“But…” The camerlegno searched his troubled mind for any reason. “Think of the danger… of his actions.” His voice felt weak. “What if this woman came forward? Or, God forbid, his child? Imagine the shame the church would suffer.”

Mortati’s voice trembled. “The child has already come forward.”

Everything stopped.

“Carlo… ?” Mortati broke down. “His Holiness’s child… is you.”

At that moment, the camerlegno felt his faith begin to die. He stood trembling on the altar, under Michelangelo’s huge Last Judgment. He knew he had just seen hell. He tried to speak, but his lips trembled, and no sound came out.

“Don’t you see?” Mortati gasped. “That is why His Holiness visited you in the hospital in Palermo when you were a boy. That is why he took you in and raised you. The nun he loved was Maria… your mother. She left the nunnery to raise you, but she never stopped being devoted to God. When the Pope heard she had died in an explosion and that you, his son, had miraculously survived… he promised God he would never leave you alone again. Carlo, your parents were both virgins. They kept their promises to God. And yet they had you. You were their miracle.”

The camerlegno covered his ears, trying to shut out the words. He stood frozen on the altar. Then, as if the ground had vanished beneath him, he fell hard to his knees and cried out in pain.

Seconds. Minutes. Hours.

Time seemed meaningless inside the chapel. Vittoria felt herself slowly waking up from the paralysis that had gripped them all. She let go of Langdon’s hand and started to move through the crowd of cardinals. The chapel door seemed far away, and she felt like she was moving underwater… in slow motion.

As she moved through the robes, her movement seemed to wake others up. Some of the cardinals began to pray. Others cried. Some turned to watch her go, their blank faces slowly changing to a look of dread as she moved toward the door. She had almost reached the back of the crowd when a hand grabbed her arm. The touch was weak but firm. She turned to face an old, wrinkled cardinal. His face was filled with fear.

“No,” the man whispered. “You can’t.”

Vittoria stared, unable to believe what she was hearing.

Another cardinal was now at her side. “We must think before we act.”

And another. “The pain this could cause…”

Vittoria was surrounded. She looked at them all, stunned. “But these acts here today, tonight… surely the world should know the truth.”

“My heart agrees,” the aged cardinal said, still holding her arm, “yet this is a path from which we can’t turn back. We must think about the ruined hopes. The distrust. How could people ever trust again?”

Suddenly, more cardinals seemed to block her way. A wall of black robes stood before her. “Listen to the people in the square,” one said. “What will this do to them? We must be careful.”

“We need time to think and pray,” another said. “We must act wisely. The effects of this…”

“He killed my father!” Vittoria said. “He killed his own father!”

“I’m sure he will pay for his sins,” the cardinal holding her arm said sadly.

Vittoria was sure too, and she planned to make sure he paid. She tried to push toward the door again, but the cardinals moved closer, their faces frightened.

“What are you going to do?” she exclaimed. “Kill me?”

The old men paled, and Vittoria instantly wished she could take back her words. She could tell these men were kind. They had witnessed enough violence tonight. They were not a threat. They were simply caught. Afraid. Trying to understand what was happening.

“I want…” the aged cardinal said, “… to do what is right.”

“Then you will let her out,” a deep voice stated from behind her. The words were calm but firm. Robert Langdon came to her side, and she felt his hand take hers. “Ms. Vetra and I are leaving this chapel. Immediately.”

Stumbling, unsure, the cardinals began to move out of the way.

“Wait!” It was Mortati. He walked toward them now, down the main aisle, leaving the camerlegno alone and beaten on the altar. Mortati looked older all at once, tired beyond his age. His movement was heavy with shame. He arrived, placing a hand on Langdon’s shoulder and one on Vittoria’s as well. Vittoria felt truth in his touch. The man’s eyes were more filled with tears now.

“ Of course you are free to go,” Mortati said. “Of course.” The man paused, his sadness almost able to be touched. “I ask only this…” He looked down at his feet for a long moment then back up at Vittoria and Langdon. “Let me do it. I will go into the square right now and find a way. I will tell them. I don’t know how… but I will find a way. The church’s admission should come from us. We should reveal our own mistakes.”

Mortati turned sadly back toward the altar. “Carlo, you have brought this church to a terrible crisis.” He paused, looking around. The altar was empty.

There was a soft noise of fabric down the side aisle, and the door clicked shut.

The camerlegno was gone.
