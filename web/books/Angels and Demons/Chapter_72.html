Angels and Demons

Chapter 72

Langdon climbed down from the scaffolding and landed on the ground. He dusted off his clothes. Vittoria was there to meet him.

“No luck?” she asked.

He shook his head.

“They put the cardinal in the trunk.”

Langdon glanced at the parked car where Olivetti and some soldiers were studying a map on the hood. “Are they looking southwest?”

She nodded. “No churches. From here, the closest one is St. Peter’s.”

Langdon grunted. At least they agreed. He walked toward Olivetti. The soldiers stepped aside to let him pass.

Olivetti looked up. “Nothing. But this doesn’t show all the churches, just the main ones. About fifty.”

“Where are we?” Langdon asked.

Olivetti pointed to Piazza del Popolo and drew a straight line southwest. The line missed the group of black squares showing Rome’s main churches by quite a bit. Sadly, Rome’s main churches were also its oldest... the ones from the 1600s.

“I need to decide something,” Olivetti said. “Are you sure about the direction?”

Langdon imagined the angel’s pointing finger, feeling the urgency grow again. “Yes, sir. I’m sure.”

Olivetti shrugged and drew the straight line again. It crossed the Margherita Bridge, Via Cola di Riezo, and went through Piazza del Risorgimento, not hitting any churches until it stopped at the center of St. Peter’s Square.

“What’s wrong with St. Peter’s?” one of the soldiers asked. He had a deep scar under his left eye. “It’s a church.”

Langdon shook his head. “It needs to be a public place. It doesn't feel very public right now.”

“But the line goes through St. Peter’s Square,” Vittoria added, looking over Langdon’s shoulder. “The square is public.”

Langdon had already thought about it. “But there are no statues.”

“Isn’t there a tall stone pillar in the middle?”

She was right. There was an Egyptian pillar in St. Peter’s Square. Langdon looked at the pillar in the piazza in front of them. The lofty pyramid. A strange coincidence, he thought. He ignored it. “The Vatican’s pillar isn’t by Bernini. Caligula brought it here. And it has nothing to do with Air.” There was another problem too. “Besides, the poem says the elements are spread across Rome. St. Peter’s Square is in Vatican City. Not Rome.”

“Depends on who you ask,” a guard said.

Langdon looked up. “What?”

“It’s always debated. Most maps include St. Peter’s Square as part of Vatican City, but because it’s outside the walled city, Rome has claimed it as part of their city for centuries.”

“You’re kidding,” Langdon said. He had never known that.

“I only bring it up,” the guard continued, “because Commander Olivetti and Ms. Vetra were asking about a sculpture related to Air.”

Langdon was surprised. “And you know of one in St. Peter’s Square?”

“Not really. It’s not exactly a sculpture. Probably not important.”

“Tell us anyway,” Olivetti urged.

The guard shrugged. “I only know about it because I usually work on the square. I know every part of St. Peter’s Square.”

“The sculpture,” Langdon pressed. “What does it look like?” Langdon was starting to wonder if the Illuminati could have been bold enough to place their second marker right outside St. Peter’s Church.

“I walk past it every day,” the guard said. “It’s in the middle, right where that line points. That’s what made me think of it. Like I said, it’s not really a sculpture. It’s more of a… block.”

Olivetti looked angry. “A block?”

“Yes, sir. A marble block set in the square. At the bottom of the monolith. But the block isn’t a rectangle. It’s an ellipse. And the block has a carving of a gust of wind.” He stopped. “Air, I guess, if you want to be exact about it.”

Langdon stared at the young soldier, surprised. “A relief!” he said quickly.

Everyone looked at him.

“Relief,” Langdon said, “is the other part of sculpture!” Sculpture is shaping figures as full forms and as reliefs. He had written that definition on chalkboards for years. Reliefs were like flat sculptures, like Abraham Lincoln’s face on the penny. Bernini’s Chigi Chapel medallions were another great example.

“Bassorelievo?” the guard asked, using the Italian art word.

“Yes! Bas-relief!” Langdon tapped the hood. “I wasn’t thinking about it that way! That tile you’re talking about in St. Peter’s Square is called the West Ponente - the West Wind. It’s also known as Respiro di Dio.”

“Breath of God?”

“Yes! Air! And it was carved and placed there by the first architect!”

Vittoria looked confused. “But I thought Michelangelo designed St. Peter’s.”

“Yes, the basilica!” Langdon exclaimed, triumph in his voice. “But St. Peter’s Square was designed by Bernini!”

As the line of Alpha Romeos sped out of Piazza del Popolo, everyone was in too much of a hurry to notice the BBC van pulling out behind them.
