Angels and Demons

Chapter 112

The narrow tunnel, Il Passetto, stretched before Langdon and Vittoria as they rushed toward Vatican City. The torch in Langdon’s hand lit only a few feet ahead. The walls were close, and the ceiling low. The air smelled musty. Langdon raced into the darkness with Vittoria close behind.

The tunnel sloped up as it left the Castel Sant'Angelo, rising into the base of a stone structure like a Roman aqueduct. There, the tunnel leveled out and began its hidden path toward Vatican City.

As Langdon ran, his thoughts swirled with confusing images—Kohler, Janus, the Hassassin, Rocher… a sixth brand? I’m sure you’ve heard about the sixth brand, the killer had said. The most brilliant of all. Langdon was sure he had not. Even in conspiracy lore, Langdon knew of no sixth brand. Real or imagined. There were rumors of gold and a perfect Illuminati Diamond but never a sixth brand.

“Kohler can’t be Janus!” Vittoria said as they ran through the tunnel. “It’s impossible!”

Impossible was a word Langdon had stopped using tonight. “I don’t know,” Langdon yelled as they ran. “Kohler has a serious grudge, and he has a lot of influence.”

“This crisis makes CERN look like monsters! Max would never hurt CERN’s reputation!”

Langdon knew CERN had been publicly criticized tonight, because the Illuminati wanted a public spectacle. And yet, he wondered how much CERN had really been hurt. The church often criticized CERN. In fact, the more Langdon thought about it, the more he wondered if this crisis might help CERN. If publicity was the goal, then antimatter had won tonight. The whole world was talking about it.

“You know what P. T. Barnum said,” Langdon called. “ ‘I don’t care what you say about me, just spell my name right!’ I bet people are already planning to use antimatter technology. And after they see its power at midnight tonight…”

“Illogical,” Vittoria said. “Publicizing scientific discoveries isn't about showing destructive power! This is terrible for antimatter, trust me!”

Langdon’s torch was fading now. “Then maybe it’s all much simpler. Maybe Kohler bet that the Vatican would keep the antimatter a secret, not wanting to make the Illuminati stronger by admitting the weapon existed. Kohler thought the Vatican would stay quiet about the threat, but the camerlegno changed things.”

Vittoria was silent as they dashed down the tunnel.

Suddenly, the situation made more sense to Langdon. “Yes! Kohler didn't expect the camerlegno's reaction. The camerlegno broke the Vatican's tradition of secrecy and told everyone about the crisis. He was completely honest. He put the antimatter on TV. It was a smart move, and Kohler never saw it coming. The irony is that the Illuminati attack failed. It accidentally created a new church leader in the camerlegno. And now Kohler is coming to kill him!”

“Max is a jerk,” Vittoria said, “but he is not a murderer. And he would never have been involved in my father’s murder.”

In Langdon’s mind, it was Kohler’s voice that answered. Many at CERN thought Leonardo was dangerous because mixing science and God is the worst scientific sin. “Maybe Kohler found out about the antimatter project weeks ago and didn’t like the religious meaning.”

“So he killed my father over it? That’s crazy! Besides, Max Kohler would never have known the project existed.”

“While you were gone, maybe your father broke down and asked Kohler for advice. You said your father was worried about the moral issues of creating such a deadly thing.”

“Ask Maximilian Kohler for moral advice?” Vittoria scoffed. “I don’t think so!”

The tunnel turned slightly westward. The faster they ran, the dimmer Langdon’s torch became. He began to fear what the place would look like if the light went out. Black.

“Besides,” Vittoria argued, “if he is behind everything, why would Kohler have called you this morning and asked for help?”

Langdon had already thought of it. “By calling me, Kohler played it safe. He made sure no one would say he did nothing when things went wrong. He probably never thought we’d get this far.”

The thought of being used by Kohler made Langdon angry. Langdon’s involvement had made the Illuminati seem more real. The media had quoted his qualifications and books all night. As silly as it was, a Harvard professor being in Vatican City had somehow made the whole emergency seem more than just a crazy idea. It had convinced doubters worldwide that the Illuminati were not just a thing of the past but a force to be reckoned with.

“That BBC reporter,” Langdon said, “thinks CERN is the new Illuminati hideout.”

“What!” Vittoria tripped behind him. She caught herself and kept running. “He said that!?”

“On TV. He compared CERN to the Masonic lodges—an innocent group unknowingly hiding the Illuminati inside.”

“My God, this will ruin CERN.”

Langdon wasn't so sure. Either way, the idea suddenly seemed more possible. CERN was the perfect place for science. It was home to scientists from many countries. They seemed to have plenty of secret funding. And Maximilian Kohler was their leader.

Kohler is two-faced.

“If Kohler’s not involved,” Langdon argued, “then why is he here?”

“Probably trying to stop this craziness. To show he cares. Maybe he really is trying to help! He could have found out who knew about the antimatter project and come to share what he knows.”

“The killer said he was coming to brand the camerlegno.”

“Listen to yourself! It would be suicide. Max would never make it out alive.”

Langdon thought about it. Maybe that was the point.

The shape of a steel gate appeared ahead, blocking the tunnel. Langdon’s heart almost stopped. But when they got closer, they saw the old lock was open. The gate swung open easily.

Langdon sighed in relief, realizing the tunnel was in use, just as he thought. Recently. Like today. He was now pretty sure the four scared cardinals had been taken through here earlier.

They ran on. Langdon could now hear the sounds of chaos to his left. It was St. Peter’s Square. They were getting close.

They came to another gate, this one heavier. It was also unlocked. The sound of St. Peter’s Square faded behind them now, and Langdon felt they had passed through the outer wall of Vatican City. He wondered where inside the Vatican this old passage would end. In the gardens? In the basilica? In the papal residence?

Then, without warning, the tunnel stopped.

The heavy door blocking their way was a thick wall of iron with rivets. Even in the last flickers of his torch, Langdon could see the door was completely smooth—no handles, no knobs, no keyholes, no hinges. No way in.

He felt a surge of panic. Architects called this kind of rare door a senza chiave —a one-way door, used for security, that could only be opened from one side—the other side. Langdon’s hope vanished… along with the torch in his hand.

He looked at his watch. Mickey glowed.

11:29 P.M.

With a frustrated scream, Langdon swung the torch and started banging on the door.
