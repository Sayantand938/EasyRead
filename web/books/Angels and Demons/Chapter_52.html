Angels and Demons

Chapter 52

The layout inside Archival Vault 10 wasn't as easy to understand as Langdon had hoped, and the Diagramma manuscript didn't seem to be with other similar books by Galileo. Without the Biblion computer system and a way to find things, Langdon and Vittoria were stuck.

“Are you sure Diagramma is in here?” Vittoria asked.

“Yes. It's listed for sure in both the Uficcio della Propaganda delle Fede -”

“Okay. Just checking.” She went left, and he went right.

Langdon started looking by hand. He had to really hold back from stopping to read every amazing thing he saw. The collection was incredible. The Assayer … The Starry Messenger … The Sunspot Letters … Letter to the Grand Duchess Christina … Apologia pro Galileo … It went on and on.

Vittoria was the one who finally found it in the back of the vault. She called out in her deep voice, “Diagramma della Veritа!”

Langdon hurried through the red light to get to her. “Where?”

Vittoria pointed, and Langdon quickly understood why they hadn't found it before. The manuscript was in a box for large papers, not on the shelves. These boxes were often used for loose pages. The label on the front clearly showed what was inside.

DIAGRAMMA DELLA VERITA

Galileo Galilei, 1639

Langdon dropped to his knees, his heart pounding. “Diagramma.” He grinned at her. “Good job. Help me pull out this bin.”

Vittoria knelt beside him, and they pulled hard. The metal tray holding the bin rolled toward them on wheels, showing the top of the container.

“No lock?” Vittoria asked, sounding surprised at the simple latch.

“Never. Documents sometimes need to be taken out fast. Like in floods or fires.”

“So open it.”

Langdon didn’t need to be told twice. With his biggest academic dream right in front of him and the thin air in the room, he didn't want to waste time. He unsnapped the latch and lifted the lid. Inside, flat on the bottom of the bin, was a black, duck-cloth bag. The cloth let air in to help keep what was inside safe. Reaching in with both hands and keeping the bag flat, Langdon lifted it out of the bin.

“I thought it would be a treasure chest,” Vittoria said. “Looks more like a pillowcase.”

“Follow me,” he said. Holding the bag in front of him like something holy, Langdon walked to the center of the vault where he saw the usual glass-topped table for looking at old documents. Even though the center spot was meant to keep documents from being moved around too much, researchers liked that the surrounding shelves gave them privacy. Important discoveries were made in these vaults all over the world, and most professors didn’t want others watching them work.

Langdon put the bag on the table and unbuttoned it. Vittoria stood close by. Looking through a tray of tools, Langdon found the felt-pad pincers archivists called finger cymbals - large tweezers with flat disks on each arm. As he got more excited, Langdon worried he might wake up back in Cambridge with papers to grade. He took a deep breath and opened the bag. His cotton-gloved fingers trembling, he reached in with his tongs.

“Relax,” Vittoria said. “It’s paper, not plutonium.”

Langdon carefully slid the tongs around the stack of documents, making sure to apply even pressure. Then, instead of pulling the documents out, he held them in place while he slid the bag off—an archivist’s way of reducing stress on the artifact. He didn’t breathe easily again until the bag was off and he had turned on the exam light under the table.

Vittoria looked ghostly, lit from below by the lamp under the glass. “Small sheets,” she said, her voice full of awe.

Langdon nodded. The stack of pages looked like loose pages from a small paperback book. Langdon saw that the top sheet was an elaborate pen and ink cover with the title, the date, and Galileo’s signature.

In that moment, Langdon forgot the small space, his tiredness, and the terrible situation that had brought him there. He simply stared in wonder. Being close to history always filled Langdon with reverence… like seeing the brushstrokes on the Mona Lisa.

The faded, yellow papyrus clearly showed its age and authenticity, but aside from some expected fading, the document was in great condition. Slight fading of the color. Minor splitting and sticking of the papyrus. But overall … in really good shape. He studied the fancy etching on the cover, his vision blurring in the dry air. Vittoria was silent.

“Hand me a spatula, please.” Langdon pointed to a tray of stainless-steel tools beside Vittoria. She gave it to him. Langdon took the tool. It was a good one. He ran his fingers over the surface to remove any static, and then carefully slid the blade under the cover. He lifted the spatula and turned the cover sheet over.

The first page was handwritten in tiny, fancy writing that was hard to read. Langdon immediately noticed there were no diagrams or numbers. It was an essay.

“Heliocentricity,” Vittoria said, translating the heading on the first page. She scanned the text. “Looks like Galileo is finally rejecting the geocentric model. It’s old Italian, though, so I can’t promise a perfect translation.”

“Forget it,” Langdon said. “We’re looking for math. The universal language.” He used the spatula to turn the next page. Another essay. No math or diagrams. Langdon’s hands began to sweat inside his gloves.

“Movement of the Planets,” Vittoria said, translating the title.

Langdon frowned. On any other day, he would have been eager to read it. Amazingly, NASA's current model of planetary orbits, seen through powerful telescopes, was said to be almost the same as Galileo's first guesses.

“No math,” Vittoria said. “He’s talking about backward movements and oval-shaped orbits or something.”

Oval-shaped orbits. Langdon remembered that much of Galileo's legal trouble had started when he described planets moving in ovals. The Vatican praised the perfection of the circle and said that heavenly motion had to be circular. Galileo’s Illuminati, though, saw perfection in the oval too, admiring the mathematical balance of its two focal points. The Illuminati’s ellipse was still visible today in modern Masonic drawings and floor designs.

“Next,” Vittoria said.

Langdon turned the page.

“Lunar phases and tidal motion,” she said. “No numbers. No drawings.”

Langdon flipped again. Nothing. He kept flipping through a dozen or so pages. Nothing. Nothing. Nothing.

“I thought this guy was a mathematician,” Vittoria said. “This is all text.”

Langdon felt like he was running out of air. His hopes were fading too. The pile was getting smaller.

“Nothing here,” Vittoria said. “No math. A few dates, some normal numbers, but nothing that looks like a clue.”

Langdon flipped over the last page and sighed. It was another essay.

“Short book,” Vittoria said, frowning.

Langdon nodded.

“Merda, as we say in Rome.”

Shit is right, Langdon thought. His reflection in the glass seemed to mock him, like the image staring back at him this morning from his bay window. An aging ghost. “There has to be something,” he said, the rough desperation in his voice surprising him. “The segno is here somewhere. I know it!”

“Maybe you were wrong about DIII?”

Langdon turned and stared at her.

“Okay,” she agreed, “DIII makes perfect sense. But maybe the clue isn’t about math?”

“Lingua pura. What else could it be?”

“Art?”

“Except there are no charts or images in the book.”

“All I know is that lingua pura means something other than Italian. Math just seems logical.”

“I agree.”

Langdon didn't want to give up so easily. “The numbers must be written out. The math must be in words, not equations.”

“It'll take a while to read all the pages.”

“We don't have much time. We'll have to divide the work.” Langdon flipped the stack back to the start. “I know enough Italian to find numbers.” Using his spatula, he cut the stack like a deck of cards and put the first few pages in front of Vittoria. “It’s in here somewhere. I’m sure of it.”

Vittoria reached down and turned her first page.

“Spatula!” Langdon said, grabbing her another tool from the tray. “Use the spatula.”

“I’m wearing gloves,” she complained. “How much damage could I do?”

“Just use it.”

Vittoria picked up the spatula. “You feeling what I’m feeling?”

“Tense?”

“No. Short of breath.”

Langdon was definitely starting to feel it too. The air was getting thin faster than he had thought it would. He knew they had to hurry. He had solved old puzzles before, but usually he had more time. Without another word, Langdon bowed his head and began translating the first page in his stack.

Show yourself, damn it! Show yourself!
