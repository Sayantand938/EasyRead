Angels and Demons

Chapter 39

The Apostolic Palace is a group of buildings near the Sistine Chapel in the northeast part of Vatican City. It overlooks St. Peter’s Square and contains both the Pope’s living quarters and his office.

Vittoria and Langdon walked quietly behind Commander Olivetti down a long, ornate hallway, his neck muscles tight with anger. After going up three flights of stairs, they entered a wide, dimly lit hall.

Langdon was amazed by the artwork on the walls—perfectly preserved busts, tapestries, and friezes worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. About two-thirds of the way down the hall, they passed an alabaster fountain. Olivetti turned left into a small recess and walked to one of the biggest doors Langdon had ever seen.

“Ufficio di Papa,” the commander announced, glaring angrily at Vittoria. Vittoria didn’t react. She reached past Olivetti and knocked loudly on the door.

Office of the Pope, Langdon thought, still finding it hard to believe he was standing outside one of the most sacred rooms in the world's religions.

“Avanti!” someone called from inside.

When the door opened, Langdon had to shield his eyes. The sunlight was dazzling. Slowly, he could see what was in front of him.

The Office of the Pope felt more like a ballroom than an office. Red marble floors stretched out to walls covered with bright frescoes. A huge chandelier hung from the ceiling, and beyond it, arched windows provided a beautiful view of the sunny St. Peter’s Square.

My God, Langdon thought. This room has an incredible view.

At the far end of the hall, at a carved desk, a man sat writing quickly. “Avanti,” he called out again, setting down his pen and waving them over.

Olivetti led the way, walking like a soldier. “Signore,” he said apologetically. “No ho potuto-”

The man cut him off. He stood and looked at his two visitors.

The camerlegno was nothing like the frail, blessed old men Langdon usually pictured in the Vatican. He wore no rosary beads or pendants. No heavy robes. He was dressed in a simple black cassock that made his large frame seem even bigger. He looked to be in his late thirties, very young by Vatican standards. He had a surprisingly handsome face, thick brown hair, and bright green eyes that seemed to shine with the secrets of the universe. As the man got closer, though, Langdon saw a deep tiredness in his eyes, like someone who had been through the hardest two weeks of his life.

“I am Carlo Ventresca,” he said, his English perfect. “The late Pope’s camerlegno.” His voice was simple and kind, with only a slight Italian accent.

“Vittoria Vetra,” she said, stepping forward and offering her hand. “Thank you for seeing us.”

Olivetti flinched as the camerlegno shook Vittoria’s hand.

“This is Robert Langdon,” Vittoria said. “A religious historian from Harvard University.”

“Padre,” Langdon said, in his best Italian accent. He bowed his head as he held out his hand.

“No, no,” the camerlegno insisted, lifting Langdon back up. “Being in His Holiness’s office does not make me holy. I am just a priest—a chamberlain helping in a time of need.”

Langdon stood up straight.

“Please,” the camerlegno said, “everyone sit.” He arranged some chairs around his desk. Langdon and Vittoria sat. Olivetti seemed to prefer to stand.

The camerlegno sat at the desk, folded his hands, sighed, and looked at his visitors.

“Sir,” Olivetti said. “The woman’s clothing is my fault. I-”

“Her clothing is not what I’m worried about,” the camerlegno replied, sounding too tired to care. “When the Vatican operator calls me half an hour before I start the conclave to tell me a woman is calling from your private office to warn me about some major security problem I haven’t been told about, that’s what worries me.”

Olivetti stood stiffly, his back straight like a soldier being inspected.

Langdon felt drawn in by the camerlegno’s presence. Young and tired as he was, the priest seemed like a mythical hero, giving off charisma and authority.

“Sir,” Olivetti said, sounding sorry but still firm. “You shouldn’t worry about security matters. You have other duties.”

“I know my other duties very well. I also know that as interim director, I’m responsible for the safety of everyone at this conclave. What is happening here?”

“I have the situation handled.”

“Apparently not.”

“Father,” Langdon interrupted, taking out the crumpled fax and handing it to the camerlegno, “please.”

Commander Olivetti stepped forward, trying to interfere. “Father, please do not concern yourself with-”

The camerlegno took the fax, ignoring Olivetti for a long moment. He looked at the picture of the murdered Leonardo Vetra and gasped. “What is this?”

“That is my father,” Vittoria said, her voice shaking. “He was a priest and a scientist. He was murdered last night.”

The camerlegno’s face softened at once. He looked up at her. “My dear child. I am so sorry.” He crossed himself and looked again at the fax, his eyes seeming to fill with waves of disgust. “Who would… and this burn on his…” The camerlegno paused, looking closely at the picture.

“It says Illuminati,” Langdon said. “I’m sure you know the name.”

A strange look came across the camerlegno’s face. “I have heard the name, yes, but…”

“The Illuminati murdered Leonardo Vetra to steal a new technology he was-”

“Signore,” Olivetti interrupted. “This is ridiculous. The Illuminati? This is obviously some kind of trick.”

The camerlegno seemed to think about Olivetti’s words. Then he turned and looked at Langdon so intently that Langdon felt breathless. “Mr. Langdon, I have spent my life in the Catholic Church. I know about the Illuminati stories… and the legend of the brandings. But I must warn you, I live in the real world. Christianity has enough real enemies without bringing back old legends.”

“The symbol is real,” Langdon said, perhaps too defensively. He reached over and turned the fax for the camerlegno.

The camerlegno was silent when he saw the symmetry.

“Even today’s computers,” Langdon added, “cannot create a symmetrical ambigram of this word.”

The camerlegno folded his hands and said nothing for a long time. “The Illuminati are dead,” he finally said. “A long time ago. That is a historical fact.”

Langdon nodded. “Yesterday, I would have agreed with you.”

“Yesterday?”

“Before what happened today. I believe the Illuminati have returned to fulfill an old agreement.”

“Forgive me. My history is a bit weak. What old agreement is this?”

Langdon took a deep breath. “The destruction of Vatican City.”

"Destroy Vatican City?" The camerlegno looked more puzzled than scared. "That can't be done."

Vittoria shook her head. "I'm afraid there's more bad news."
