Angels and Demons

Chapter 85

The Holy Vatican Grottoes are located under the main floor of St. Peter’s Basilica. They are where Popes are buried after they die.

Vittoria reached the bottom of the spiral staircase and entered the grotto. The dark tunnel reminded her of CERN’s Large Hadron Collider—black and cold. Lit only by the flashlights of the Swiss Guards, the tunnel felt strangely unreal. On both sides, empty spaces lined the walls. Set back in these spaces, as far as the lights allowed them to see, were the large, shadowy shapes of coffins.

A chill ran down her spine. It’s the cold, she told herself, knowing that was only partly true. She felt like they were being watched, not by living people, but by ghosts in the dark. On top of each tomb, dressed in full papal robes, lay life-sized figures of each Pope, shown in death, with their arms crossed over their chests. The still bodies seemed to rise from inside the tombs, pushing upward against the marble lids as if trying to break free. The line of flashlights moved on, and the papal shapes rose and fell against the walls, stretching and disappearing in a strange, shadowy dance.

A silence had fallen over the group, and Vittoria couldn’t tell if it was out of respect or fear. She felt both. The camerlegno moved with his eyes closed, as if he knew every step. Vittoria guessed he had taken this eerie walk many times since the Pope’s death… maybe to pray at his tomb for guidance.

I worked with the cardinal for many years, the camerlegno had said. He was like a father to me. Vittoria remembered the camerlegno saying this about the cardinal who had “saved” him from the army. Now, however, Vittoria understood the rest of the story. The cardinal who had taken the camerlegno in had later become Pope and brought his young helper with him to serve as chamberlain.

That explains a lot, Vittoria thought. She had always been good at sensing other people’s feelings, and something about the camerlegno had bothered her all day. Since meeting him, she had felt a deep, personal sadness that was more than the huge crisis he now faced. Behind his calm, religious face, she saw a man suffering from his own problems. Now she knew she was right. He was not only dealing with the biggest threat in Vatican history, but he was doing it without his mentor and friend… all alone.

The guards slowed down, as if unsure exactly where the most recent Pope was buried in the darkness. The camerlegno kept going confidently and stopped before a marble tomb that seemed to shine brighter than the others. On top was a carved figure of the late Pope. When Vittoria recognized his face from television, she felt a jolt of fear. What are we doing?

“I know we don’t have much time,” the camerlegno said. “But I still ask that we take a moment to pray.”

The Swiss Guard all bowed their heads where they were standing. Vittoria did the same, her heart pounding in the silence. The camerlegno knelt before the tomb and prayed in Italian. As Vittoria listened to his words, a sudden sadness came over her, and she cried… tears for her own mentor… her own father figure. The camerlegno’s words seemed as fitting for her father as they did for the Pope.

“Great father, guide, friend.” The camerlegno’s voice echoed softly in the room. “You told me when I was young that the voice in my heart was God’s. You told me I must listen to it, no matter how hard it gets. I hear that voice now, asking me to do things that seem impossible. Give me strength. Forgive me. What I do… I do for everything you believe in. Amen.”

“Amen,” the guards whispered.

Amen, Father. Vittoria wiped her eyes.

The camerlegno stood slowly and moved away from the tomb. “Move the cover aside.”

The Swiss Guards hesitated. “Signore,” one said, “by law we must obey you.” He paused. “We will do what you say…”

The camerlegno seemed to know what the young man was thinking. “Someday I will ask you to forgive me for putting you in this situation. Today I ask you to obey me. Vatican laws are made to protect this church. It is to protect the church that I command you to break them now.”

There was a moment of silence, and then the lead guard gave the order. The three men put their flashlights on the floor, and their shadows jumped above them. Now lit from below, the men walked toward the tomb. Putting their hands against the marble cover near the head of the tomb, they braced their feet and got ready to push. When they pushed, straining against the huge slab, the lid did not move at all. Vittoria almost hoped it was too heavy. She was suddenly afraid of what they would find inside.

The men pushed harder, but the stone still did not move.

“Ancora,” the camerlegno said, rolling up the sleeves of his robe and getting ready to push with them. “Ora!” Everyone pushed hard.

Vittoria was about to offer to help, but just then, the lid began to slide. The men pushed again, and with a deep, rough sound of stone on stone, the lid turned off the top of the tomb and rested at an angle—the Pope’s carved head now pushed back into the wall and his feet sticking out into the hallway.

Everyone stepped back.

Carefully, a guard bent and picked up his flashlight. Then he pointed it into the tomb. The light seemed to shake for a moment, and then the guard held it still. The other guards gathered one by one. Even in the darkness Vittoria felt them pull back. One after another, they crossed themselves.

The camerlegno shivered when he looked into the tomb, his shoulders slumping. He stood there for a long moment before turning away.

Vittoria had worried the corpse’s mouth might be frozen shut and that she would have to suggest breaking the jaw to see the tongue. She now saw it wasn't needed. The cheeks had sunk in, and the Pope’s mouth was wide open.

His tongue was black as death.
