Angels and Demons

Chapter 38

The Vatican switchboard is in the Communications Office, behind the Vatican post office. It's a small room with an eight-line Corelco 141 switchboard. The office handles over 2,000 calls a day, mostly sent automatically to the recording system.

Tonight, the only operator on duty quietly sipped tea. He felt proud to be one of the few still allowed inside Vatican City. Of course, the honor was lessened by the Swiss Guards outside his door. An escort to the bathroom, he thought. Ah, the things we put up with for the Holy Conclave.

Luckily, it had been a quiet evening. Or maybe not so lucky, he thought. World interest in Vatican events seemed to have fallen off in recent years. Fewer press calls came in, and even the crazies weren’t calling as often. The press office had hoped tonight would be more exciting. Sadly, though, despite St. Peter’s Square being full of press trucks, most seemed to be standard Italian and European press. Only a few global networks were there… probably having sent their second-string journalists.

The operator gripped his mug, wondering how long tonight would last. Midnight or so, he guessed. These days, most insiders knew who was likely to become Pope before the conclave even started, so it was more of a three- or four-hour ritual than a real election. Of course, last-minute disagreements could drag it out through dawn… or longer. The conclave of 1831 had lasted fifty-four days. Not tonight, he told himself; rumor was this conclave would be a “quick vote.”

The operator’s thoughts vanished with the buzz of an inside line. He looked at the blinking red light and frowned. That’s odd, he thought. The zero-line. Who inside would be calling operator information tonight? Who is even inside?

“ Vatican City, hello?” he said, picking up the phone.

The voice spoke quickly in Italian. The operator vaguely recognized the accent as that of Swiss Guards—Italian mixed with French-Swiss influence. But this caller was definitely not Swiss Guard.

Hearing the woman’s voice, the operator stood suddenly, almost spilling his tea. He looked back at the line. He hadn't been wrong. An internal extension. The call was from inside. There must be some mistake! he thought. A woman inside Vatican City? Tonight?

The woman was speaking fast and urgently. The operator had been on the phones long enough to know when he was dealing with a nut. This woman didn't sound crazy. She was urgent but made sense. Calm and efficient. He listened to her request, bewildered.

“Il camerlegno?” the operator said, still trying to figure out where the call was coming from. “I can’t connect you… yes, I know he’s in the Pope’s office but… who are you again?… and you want to warn him about…” He listened, getting more and more worried. Everyone is in danger? How? And where are you calling from? “Maybe I should call the Swiss…” The operator stopped. “You say you’re where? Where?”

He listened in shock, then decided what to do. “Hold, please,” he said, putting the woman on hold before she could reply. Then he called Commander Olivetti’s direct line. There’s no way that woman is really -

The line picked up right away.

“Per l’amore di Dio!” a familiar woman’s voice shouted. “Make the damn call!”

The door of the Swiss Guards’ security center slid open. The guards stepped aside as Commander Olivetti rushed into the room. Turning the corner to his office, Olivetti saw that Vittoria Vetra was standing at his desk talking on his private phone, just like his guard had said on the walkie-talkie.

Che coglioni che ha questa! he thought. She's got some nerve!

Furious, he went to the door and jammed the key into the lock. He pulled open the door and demanded, “What are you doing!”

Vittoria ignored him. “Yes,” she was saying into the phone. “And I must warn-”

Olivetti grabbed the receiver from her hand and held it to his ear. “Who the hell is this!”

For a split second, Olivetti’s stiff posture softened. “Yes, camerlegno…” he said. “That’s right, signore… but security questions require… of course not… I’m holding her here for… certainly, but…” He listened. “Yes, sir,” he said finally. “I’ll bring them up right away.”
