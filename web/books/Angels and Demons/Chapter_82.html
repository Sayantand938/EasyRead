Angels and Demons

Chapter 82

At CERN, secretary Sylvie Baudeloque was hungry and wishing she could go home. To her dismay, Kohler had apparently survived his trip to the infirmary; he had phoned and demanded -not asked, demanded-that Sylvie stay late this evening. No explanation.

Over the years, Sylvie had learned to ignore Kohler’s strange mood swings and odd habits-his silent treatments, his unnerving habit of secretly filming meetings with his wheelchair’s camera. She secretly hoped one day he would shoot himself during his weekly visit to CERN’s pistol range, but apparently he was a pretty good shot.

Now, sitting alone at her desk, Sylvie heard her stomach growling. Kohler had not yet returned, nor had he given her any more work for the evening. To hell with sitting here bored and starving , she decided. She left Kohler a note and headed for the staff dining hall to grab a quick bite.

She never made it.

As she passed CERN’s recreational “suites de loisir ”- a long hallway of lounges with televisions-she noticed the rooms were full of employees who had apparently skipped dinner to watch the news. Something big was going on. Sylvie entered the first suite. It was packed with young computer programmers. When she saw the headlines on the TV, she gasped.

TERROR AT THE VATICAN

Sylvie listened to the report, unable to believe what she was hearing. Some ancient brotherhood killing cardinals? What did that prove? Their hatred? Their power? Their ignorance?

And yet, incredibly, the mood in this suite seemed anything but sad.

Two young techies ran by waving T-shirts that bore a picture of Bill Gates and the message: AND THE GEEK SHALL INHERIT THE EARTH!

“Illuminati!” one shouted. “I knew it! These guys were real!”

“Wow! I thought it was just a joke!”

“They killed the Pope! The Pope!”

“Man! How many points do you get for that?”

They ran off laughing.

Sylvie was stunned. As a Catholic working with scientists, she sometimes heard anti-religious comments, but these kids seemed overjoyed at the church's loss. How could they be so heartless? Why the hate?

For Sylvie, the church had always been harmless… a place to connect and reflect… sometimes just a place to sing without being stared at. The church marked the important events in her life—funerals, weddings, baptisms, holidays—and asked for nothing back. Even the donations were optional. Her children came out of Sunday School happy, full of ideas about helping others and being kind. What was wrong with that?

It always surprised her that so many of CERN’s “smartest people” didn’t understand why the church mattered. Did they really think quarks and mesons inspired people? Or that equations could replace someone’s need to believe in God?

Confused, Sylvie walked down the hall past the other lounges. All the TV rooms were full. She now wondered about the call Kohler had gotten from the Vatican earlier. Coincidence? Maybe. The Vatican sometimes called CERN as a “courtesy” before criticizing CERN’s research—most recently for CERN’s advances in nanotechnology, which the church opposed because of its impact on genetic engineering. CERN never cared. Usually, right after the Vatican spoke out, Kohler’s phone would be flooded with calls from tech companies wanting to use the new discovery. “There’s no such thing as bad publicity,” Kohler always said.

Sylvie wondered if she should call Kohler, wherever he was, and tell him to watch the news. Did he care? Had he heard? Of course, he’d heard. He was probably recording the whole thing with his weird little camcorder, smiling for the first time in a year.

As Sylvie continued down the hall, she finally found a lounge where the mood was quiet… almost sad. Here the scientists watching the report were some of CERN’s oldest and most admired. They did not even look up as Sylvie slipped in and took a seat.

On the other side of CERN, in Leonardo Vetra’s cold apartment, Maximilian Kohler had finished reading the leather journal he’d taken from Vetra’s bedside table. Now he was watching the television reports. After a few minutes, he put Vetra’s journal back, turned off the television, and left the apartment.

Far away, in Vatican City, Cardinal Mortati carried another tray of ballots to the Sistine Chapel chimney. He burned them, and the smoke was black.

Two ballot counts. No Pope.
