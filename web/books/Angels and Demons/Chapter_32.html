Angels and Demons

Chapter 32

Langdon held his breath as the X-33 spiraled into Rome’s Leonardo da Vinci International Airport. Vittoria sat across from him, eyes closed as if trying to will the situation into control. The craft touched down and taxied to a private hangar.

“Sorry for the slow flight,” the pilot apologized, coming out of the cockpit. “Had to slow down. Noise rules over populated areas.”

Langdon checked his watch. They had been in the air thirty-seven minutes.

The pilot opened the outer door. “Anybody want to tell me what’s going on?”

Neither Vittoria nor Langdon responded.

“Fine,” he said, stretching. “I’ll be in the cockpit with the air-conditioning and my music. Just me and Garth.”

The late-afternoon sun blazed outside the hangar. Langdon carried his tweed jacket over his shoulder. Vittoria turned her face skyward and inhaled deeply, as if the sun’s rays somehow gave her some mystical energy.

Mediterraneans, Langdon thought, already sweating.

“A little old for cartoons, aren’t you?” Vittoria asked, without opening her eyes.

“I’m sorry?”

“Your wristwatch. I saw it on the plane.”

Langdon blushed a little. He was used to having to explain his watch. The special Mickey Mouse watch was a gift from his parents when he was a kid. Even though Mickey’s arms looked silly pointing to the time, it was the only watch Langdon had ever worn. It was waterproof and glowed in the dark, so it was great for swimming or walking on dark college paths at night. When Langdon’s students made fun of his style, he told them he wore Mickey to remind himself to stay young.

“It’s six o’clock,” he said.

Vittoria nodded, still with her eyes closed. “I think our ride is here.”

Langdon heard a faraway whirring sound, looked up, and felt uneasy. A helicopter was coming from the north, flying low over the runway. Langdon had been on a helicopter once in the Andean Palpa Valley to see the Nazca sand drawings and had hated it. A flying shoebox. After flying in space planes all morning, Langdon had hoped the Vatican would send a car.

Apparently not.

The helicopter slowed down above them, paused, and landed on the runway in front of them. It was white with a coat of arms on the side—two skeleton keys crossing a shield and a papal crown. He knew the symbol well. It was the usual symbol of the Vatican—the sacred symbol of the Holy See or “holy seat” of government, the seat being the actual ancient throne of St. Peter.

The Holy Chopper, Langdon sighed, watching it land. He had forgotten the Vatican had one of these, used to take the Pope to the airport, to meetings, or to his summer home in Gandolfo. Langdon would have much preferred a car.

The pilot got out of the helicopter and walked toward them across the runway.

Now it was Vittoria who looked uneasy. “That’s our pilot?”

Langdon shared her concern. “To fly, or not to fly. That is the question.”

The pilot looked like he was dressed for a Shakespearean play. His puffy tunic had bright blue and gold vertical stripes. He wore matching pantaloons and spats. On his feet were black flats that looked like slippers. On top of it all, he wore a black felt beret.

“Traditional Swiss Guard uniforms,” Langdon explained. “Designed by Michelangelo himself.” As the man drew closer, Langdon grimaced. “I admit, not one of Michelangelo’s better efforts.”

Despite the man’s flashy clothes, Langdon could tell the pilot was serious. He moved toward them with the strictness and poise of a U.S. Marine. Langdon had often read about the tough requirements to become one of the elite Swiss Guard. Recruited from Switzerland’s four Catholic regions, applicants had to be Swiss men between nineteen and thirty years old, at least 5 feet 6 inches tall, trained by the Swiss Army, and unmarried. This elite group was admired by world governments as the most loyal and lethal security force in the world.

“You are from CERN?” the guard asked, arriving before them. His voice was firm.

“Yes, sir,” Langdon replied.

“You arrived quickly,” he said, staring at the X-33 in wonder. He turned to Vittoria. “Ma’am, do you have other clothes?”

“I’m sorry?”

He pointed to her legs. “Short pants are not allowed inside Vatican City.”

Langdon looked at Vittoria’s legs and frowned. He had forgotten. Vatican City didn't allow bare legs above the knee, for men or women. It was a way of respecting the holiness of God’s city.

“This is all I have,” she said. “We came quickly.”

The guard nodded, looking unhappy. He turned to Langdon. “Are you carrying any weapons?”

Weapons? Langdon thought. I don’t even have extra underwear! He shook his head.

The officer knelt and began patting Langdon down, starting at his socks. He trusts me, Langdon thought. The guard’s strong hands moved up Langdon’s legs, getting close to his groin. Then they moved up to his chest and shoulders. Seeming sure Langdon was clean, the guard turned to Vittoria. He looked up and down her legs and body.

Vittoria glared. “Don’t even think about it.”

The guard stared at Vittoria, trying to scare her. Vittoria didn’t move.

“What’s that?” the guard said, pointing to a small square bump in her shorts pocket.

Vittoria took out a very thin cell phone. The guard took it, turned it on, waited for a dial tone, and then, seeming sure it was just a phone, gave it back. Vittoria put it back in her pocket.

“Turn around, please,” the guard said.

Vittoria agreed, holding her arms out and turning all the way around.

The guard looked her over carefully. Langdon had already decided that Vittoria’s tight shorts and shirt weren't hiding anything. It seemed the guard agreed.

“Thank you. This way, please.”

The Swiss Guard helicopter idled as Langdon and Vittoria walked up. Vittoria got on first, like she knew what she was doing, barely bending as she walked under the moving blades. Langdon waited a second.

“Couldn’t we take a car?” he shouted, half kidding to the Swiss Guard, who was getting into the pilot’s seat.

The man didn't answer.

Langdon knew that with Rome's crazy drivers, flying was probably safer anyway. He took a deep breath and got on, bending low as he went under the spinning blades.

As the guard started the engines, Vittoria asked, “Have you found the canister?”

The guard looked back, confused. “The what?”

“The canister. You called CERN about a canister?”

The man shrugged. “No idea what you’re talking about. We’ve been very busy today. My commander told me to pick you up. That’s all I know.”

Vittoria gave Langdon a worried look.

“Buckle up, please,” the pilot said as the engine sped up.

Langdon reached for his seat belt and strapped himself in. The small body of the plane seemed to shrink around him. Then with a roar, the craft shot up and turned sharply north toward Rome.

Rome … the center of the world, where Caesar once ruled, where St. Peter was crucified. The birthplace of modern civilization. And at its core… a ticking bomb.
