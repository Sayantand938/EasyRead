Angels and Demons

Chapter 87

Langdon’s watch, now smeared with blood, read 9:41 P.M. as he ran across the Courtyard of the Belvedere and reached the fountain outside the Swiss Guard security center. His hand had stopped bleeding and now felt worse than it looked. As he arrived, everyone seemed to gather at once—Olivetti, Rocher, the camerlegno, Vittoria, and some guards.

Vittoria hurried toward him immediately. “Robert, you’re hurt.”

Before Langdon could answer, Olivetti was before him. “Mr. Langdon, I’m glad you’re okay. I’m sorry about the mix-up in the archives.”

“Mix-up?” Langdon demanded. “You knew damn well-”

“It was my fault,” Rocher said, stepping forward, sounding sorry. “I had no idea you were in the archives. Parts of our restricted areas are connected to that building. We were expanding our search. I’m the one who cut the power. If I had known…”

“Robert,” Vittoria said, taking his wounded hand in hers and looking it over, “the Pope was poisoned. The Illuminati killed him.”

Langdon heard the words, but they barely registered. He was overwhelmed. All he could feel was the warmth of Vittoria’s hands.

The camerlegno pulled a silk handkerchief from his cassock and handed it to Langdon so he could clean himself. The man said nothing. His green eyes seemed filled with a new fire.

“Robert,” Vittoria pressed, “you said you found where the next cardinal is going to be killed?”

Langdon felt dizzy. “I do, it’s at the-”

“No,” Olivetti interrupted. “Mr. Langdon, there was a reason I told you not to say another word on the walkie-talkie.” He turned to the few Swiss Guards standing there. “Excuse us, gentlemen.”

The soldiers disappeared into the security center. No shame. Just obedience.

Olivetti turned back to the group. “It pains me to say this, but the murder of our Pope could only have happened with inside help. For everyone’s sake, we can’t trust anyone. Not even our guards.” He seemed to be in pain as he said it.

Rocher looked worried. “Inside help means-”

“Yes,” Olivetti said. “Your search is no longer trustworthy. But we have to risk it. Keep looking.”

Rocher looked like he wanted to say something, changed his mind, and left.

The camerlegno took a deep breath. He hadn’t said anything yet, and Langdon sensed a new firmness in him, like things had reached a turning point.

“Commander?” The camerlegno’s voice was firm. “I am going to break conclave.”

Olivetti frowned, looking grim. “I advise against it. We still have two hours and twenty minutes.”

“A heartbeat.”

Olivetti’s tone was now challenging. “What do you plan to do? Evacuate the cardinals by yourself?”

“I plan to save this church with the strength God has given me. What I do next is not your concern.”

Olivetti stood up straighter. “Whatever you plan to do…” He paused. “I don’t have the power to stop you, especially since I’ve clearly failed as head of security. I only ask that you wait. Wait twenty minutes… until after ten o’clock. If Mr. Langdon is right, I might still be able to catch this killer. There’s still a chance to keep things proper and respectful.”

“Respectful?” The camerlegno chuckled. “We’re far beyond what’s proper, commander. If you haven’t noticed, this is war.”

A guard came out of the security center and called to the camerlegno, “Sir, I just heard we’ve caught the BBC reporter, Mr. Glick.”

The camerlegno nodded. “Have him and his camerawoman meet me outside the Sistine Chapel.”

Olivetti’s eyes widened. “What are you doing?”

“Twenty minutes, commander. That’s all I’m giving you.” Then he left.

When Olivetti’s Alpha Romeo sped out of Vatican City, this time no unmarked cars followed him. In the back seat, Vittoria bandaged Langdon’s hand with a first-aid kit she found in the glove box.

Olivetti stared straight ahead. “Okay, Mr. Langdon. Where to?”
