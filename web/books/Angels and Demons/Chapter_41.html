Angels and Demons

Chapter 41

The voice from the camerlegno's speaker was robotic and cold, full of pride. Everyone in the room listened.

Langdon tried to identify the accent. Maybe Middle Eastern?

"I am a messenger from an old group," the voice said in a strange rhythm. "A group you have hurt for centuries. I am a messenger of the Illuminati."

Langdon felt his muscles tense, his last doubts disappearing. For a moment, he felt the familiar mix of excitement, honor, and deep fear he had felt when he first saw the ambigram this morning.

"What do you want?" the camerlegno asked.

"I speak for men of science. Men who, like you, are seeking answers. Answers to man's fate, his purpose, his creator."

"Whoever you are," the camerlegno said, "I-"

"Silenzio. It would be wise to listen. For two thousand years, your church has controlled the search for truth. You have destroyed your enemies with lies and warnings of disaster. You have twisted the truth to suit your needs, killing those whose discoveries went against your politics. Are you surprised that smart men from around the world are targeting you?"

"Smart men don't use blackmail to advance their goals."

“Blackmail?” The caller laughed. “This isn't blackmail. We have no demands. Getting rid of the Vatican forever is the only option. We've waited four hundred years for this day. At midnight, your city will be destroyed. There's nothing you can do.”

Olivetti stormed toward the speakerphone. “Getting into this city is impossible! You couldn't have planted explosives here!”

“You speak with the blind faith of a Swiss Guard. Maybe even an officer? Surely you know that for centuries the Illuminati have secretly joined powerful groups all over the world. Do you really think the Vatican is safe?”

Jesus, Langdon thought, they've got someone on the inside. Everyone knew that secretly joining groups was how the Illuminati gained power. They had secretly joined the Masons, big banks, and governments. In fact, Churchill once told reporters that if English spies had secretly joined the Nazis as much as the Illuminati had secretly joined English Parliament, the war would have been over in a month.

“A clear lie,” Olivetti snapped. “You can't have that much power.”

“Why? Because your Swiss Guards are alert? Because they watch every part of your private world? What about the Swiss Guards themselves? Aren't they just men? Do you really think they'd risk their lives for a story about a man who walks on water? Ask yourself how else the container could have entered your city. Or how four of your most important things could have disappeared this afternoon.”

“Our things?” Olivetti scowled. “What do you mean?”

“One, two, three, four. Haven't you noticed they're missing yet?”

“What the hell are you tal-” Olivetti stopped, his eyes widening as if he'd been punched.

“Now you understand,” the caller said. “Should I say their names?”

“What’s going on?” the camerlegno asked, looking confused.

The caller laughed. “Hasn’t your officer told you yet? How awful. Not surprising. Such arrogance. I guess he’s ashamed to tell you the truth… that four cardinals he promised to protect seem to have vanished…”

Olivetti exploded. “Where did you hear this!”

“Camerlegno,” the caller bragged, “ask your commander if all your cardinals are in the Sistine Chapel.”

The camerlegno turned to Olivetti, his green eyes demanding an answer.

“Signore,” Olivetti whispered in the camerlegno’s ear, “it’s true that four of our cardinals haven’t arrived at the Sistine Chapel yet, but there’s no need to worry. They all checked into the residence hall this morning, so we know they’re safe inside Vatican City. You had tea with them yourself just hours ago. They’re just late for the gathering before the conclave. We’re searching, but I’m sure they just lost track of time and are still out enjoying the gardens.”

“Enjoying the gardens?” The camerlegno’s voice lost its calmness. “They should have been in the chapel over an hour ago!”

Langdon looked at Vittoria, amazed. Missing cardinals? So that’s what they were looking for downstairs?

“Our list,” the caller said, “you’ll find it very convincing. There’s Cardinal Lamassé from Paris, Cardinal Guidera from Barcelona, Cardinal Ebner from Frankfurt…”

Olivetti seemed to get smaller and smaller with each name.

The caller paused, as though enjoying the last name. “And from Italy… Cardinal Baggia.”

The camerlegno relaxed like a tall ship suddenly hitting calm waters. His robe puffed out, and he fell into his chair. “I preferiti,” he whispered. “The four favorites… including Baggia… the most likely to be the next Pope… how is this possible?”

Langdon had read enough about modern papal elections to understand the desperate look on the camerlegno’s face. Although technically any cardinal under eighty could become Pope, only a few had the respect to get a two-thirds majority in the fiercely divided voting. They were known as the preferiti. And they were all gone.

Sweat ran down the camerlegno’s forehead. “What are you going to do with these men?”

“What do you think I’m going to do? I am a descendant of the Hassassin.”

Langdon shivered. He knew the name well. The church had made some deadly enemies over the years-the Hassassin, the Knights Templar, armies that the Vatican had either hunted or betrayed.

“Let the cardinals go,” the camerlegno said. “Isn’t threatening to destroy the City of God enough?”

“Forget your four cardinals. They are lost to you. But their deaths will be remembered… by millions. Every martyr’s dream. I will make them famous in the media. One by one. By midnight the Illuminati will have everyone’s attention. Why change the world if no one is watching? Public killings are horribly fascinating, aren’t they? You proved that long ago… the inquisition, the torture of the Knights Templar, the Crusades.” He paused. “And of course, la purga.”

The camerlegno was silent.

“Don’t you remember la purga?” the caller asked. “Of course not, you are a child. Priests aren’t good at history, anyway. Maybe because their history makes them ashamed?”

“The purge,” Langdon heard himself say. “Sixteen sixty-eight. The church marked four Illuminati scientists with the symbol of the cross. To wash away their sins.”

“Who is speaking?” the voice asked, sounding more curious than worried. “Who else is there?”

Langdon felt shaky. “My name is not important,” he said, trying to keep his voice steady. Talking to a real Illuminatus was strange for him… like talking to George Washington. “I am a teacher who has studied the history of your group.”

“Excellent,” the voice replied. “I am glad there are still people who remember the wrongs done to us.”

“Most of us think you are dead.”

“A lie the brotherhood has worked hard to spread. What else do you know about the purge?”

Langdon paused. What else do I know? That this whole thing is crazy, that’s what I know! “After the markings, the scientists were killed, and their bodies were left in public places around Rome as a warning to other scientists not to join the Illuminati.”

“Yes. So we will do the same. A fair exchange. Think of it as payback for our murdered brothers. Your four cardinals will die, one every hour starting at eight. By midnight the whole world will be fascinated.”

Langdon moved toward the phone. “You really plan to mark and kill these four men?”

“History repeats itself, doesn’t it? Of course, we will be more stylish and daring than the church was. They killed secretly, leaving bodies when no one was watching. It seems so cowardly.”

“What are you saying?” Langdon asked. “That you are going to brand and kill these men in public?”

“Very good. Though it depends on what you call public. I know not many people go to church these days.”

Langdon looked surprised. “You’re going to kill them in churches?”

“A kind act. Helping God quickly take their souls to heaven. It seems fair. And the press will love it, I think.”

“You’re bluffing,” Olivetti said, sounding calm again. “You can't kill a man in a church and think you'll get away with it.”

“Bluffing? We move among your Swiss Guard like ghosts, take four of your cardinals from inside your walls, put a deadly bomb at your most holy place, and you think this is a bluff? When the killings happen and the victims are found, the media will be everywhere. By midnight the world will know the Illuminati's purpose.”

“And if we put guards in every church?” Olivetti said.

The caller laughed. “I think your religion has too many churches for that to be easy. Have you counted recently? There are over four hundred Catholic churches in Rome. Cathedrals, chapels, tabernacles, abbeys, monasteries, convents, schools…”

Olivetti’s face stayed serious.

“It starts in ninety minutes,” the caller said, sounding final. “One an hour. A mathematical series of deaths. Now I have to go.”

“Wait!” Langdon demanded. “Tell me about the marks you’re going to put on these men.”

The killer sounded amused. “I think you already know what the marks will be. Or do you doubt it? You’ll see them soon enough. Proof the old stories are true.”

Langdon felt dizzy. He knew exactly what the man was saying. Langdon pictured the mark on Leonardo Vetra’s chest. Illuminati stories mentioned five marks in total. Four marks are left, Langdon thought, and four missing cardinals.

“I promised,” the camerlegno said, “to bring a new Pope tonight. I promised by God.”

“Camerlegno,” the caller said, “the world doesn’t need a new Pope. After midnight, he’ll only have rubble to rule. The Catholic Church is finished. Your time on earth is over.”

Silence hung.

The camerlegno looked truly sad. “You’re wrong. A church is more than just buildings. You can’t erase two thousand years of faith… any faith. You can’t destroy faith by destroying its buildings. The Catholic Church will continue with or without Vatican City.”

“A grand lie. But a lie nonetheless. We both know the truth. Tell me, why is Vatican City a walled fortress?”

“Men of God live in a dangerous world,” the camerlegno said.

“How naive are you? The Vatican is a fortress because the Catholic Church keeps half its wealth inside its walls—rare paintings, sculptures, cheap jewels, priceless books… and the gold and property deeds inside the Vatican Bank vaults. Some say the value of Vatican City is about 48.5 billion dollars. Quite a stash you’re sitting on. Tomorrow it will be ash. Turned into cash, so to speak. You’ll be broke. Not even priests can work for free.”

Olivetti and the camerlegno looked stunned, as if the statement was true. Langdon was amazed that the Catholic Church had that much money, and that the Illuminati knew about it.

The camerlegno sighed. “Faith, not money, is what keeps this church strong.”

“More lies,” the caller said. “Last year, you spent 183 million dollars to help struggling churches around the world. Church attendance is the lowest it’s ever been—down forty-six percent in the last ten years. Donations are half of what they were seven years ago. Fewer men are becoming priests. Your church is dying, even if you won’t admit it. Think of this as a chance to go out with a bang.”

Olivetti stepped forward, seeming less aggressive, as if he realized the truth. He looked like a man searching for a way out. Any way out. “What if some of that money went to fund your cause?”

“Don’t insult us both.”

“We have money.”

“So do we. More than you can imagine.”

Langdon thought of the rumored Illuminati wealth, the old money of the Bavarian stone masons, the Rothschilds, the Bilderbergers, the famous Illuminati Diamond.

“I preferiti,” the camerlegno said, changing the subject. He sounded like he was begging. “Spare them. They are old. They-”

“They are virgin sacrifices.” The caller laughed. “Do you think they are really virgins? Will the little lambs cry when they die? Sacrifici vergini nell‘ altare di scienza.”

The camerlegno was silent for a long time. “They are men of faith,” he finally said. “They do not fear death.”

The caller sneered. “Leonardo Vetra was a man of faith, and yet I saw fear in his eyes last night. A fear I ended.”

Vittoria, who had been silent, suddenly jumped up, her body tight with hatred. “Asino! He was my father!”

A cackle echoed from the speaker. “Your father? What is this? Vetra has a daughter? You should know your father whimpered like a child at the end. Truly sad. A pathetic man.”

Vittoria staggered as if pushed back by the words. Langdon reached for her, but she regained her balance and fixed her dark eyes on the phone. “I swear on my life, before this night is over, I will find you.” Her voice sharpened like a laser. “And when I do…”

The caller laughed roughly. “A woman of spirit. I am excited. Perhaps before this night is over, I will find you. And when I do…”

The words hung like a blade. Then he was gone.
