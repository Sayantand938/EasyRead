Angels and Demons

Chapter 106

It was 11:07 P.M. Langdon’s car sped through the Roman night. Racing down Lungotevere Tor Di Nona, alongside the river, Langdon could now see his destination rising like a mountain to his right.

Castel Sant‘ Angelo. Castle of the Angel.

Suddenly, the turnoff to the narrow Bridge of Angels—Ponte Sant‘ Angelo—appeared. Langdon slammed on his brakes and swerved. He turned in time, but the bridge was blocked. He skidded ten feet and crashed into a series of short cement pillars. Langdon jerked forward as the car stalled, sputtering and shaking. He had forgotten that the Bridge of Angels was now for pedestrians only, to protect it.

Shaken, Langdon climbed out of the damaged car, wishing he had taken another route. He felt cold, shivering from the fountain. He put his Harris tweed jacket over his damp shirt, thankful for Harris’s thick lining. The Diagramma folio would stay dry. Before him, across the bridge, the stone fortress rose like a mountain. Aching and exhausted, Langdon started to jog.

On both sides of him now, like a line of guards, a series of Bernini angels rushed past, guiding him toward his final destination. Let angels guide you on your lofty quest. The castle seemed to grow as he moved forward, an impossible peak, even more daunting than St. Peter’s. He sprinted toward the stronghold, running on empty, looking up at the citadel’s round center as it soared upward to a giant, sword-wielding angel.

The castle seemed empty.

Langdon knew that over the centuries the Vatican had used the building as a tomb, a fortress, a papal shelter, a prison for enemies of the church, and a museum. Apparently, the castle had other occupants as well—the Illuminati. Somehow it made strange sense. Although the castle belonged to the Vatican, it was used only now and then, and Bernini had made many changes to it over the years. The building was now rumored to be full of secret entrances, passages, and hidden rooms. Langdon had little doubt that the angel and surrounding pentagon-shaped park were Bernini’s work as well.

Reaching the castle’s massive double doors, Langdon pushed them hard. Not surprisingly, they didn’t move. Two iron knockers hung at eye level. Langdon didn’t bother. He stepped back, his eyes scanning the steep outer wall. These walls had defended against armies of Berbers, pagans, and Moors. He felt his chances of breaking in were slim.

Vittoria, Langdon thought. Are you in there?

Langdon hurried around the outer wall. There must be another entrance!

Rounding the second wall to the west, Langdon arrived breathless in a small parking area off Lungotere Angelo. On this wall he found a second castle entrance, a drawbridge, raised and sealed shut. Langdon gazed upward again.

The only lights on the castle were exterior floods lighting up the front. All the tiny windows inside seemed black. Langdon’s eyes climbed higher. At the very peak of the central tower, a hundred feet above, directly beneath the angel’s sword, a single balcony stuck out. The marble railing seemed to shimmer slightly, as if the room beyond it were lit by torchlight. Langdon paused, his soaked body shivering suddenly. A shadow? He waited, straining. Then he saw it again. His spine prickled. Someone is up there!

“Vittoria!” he called out, unable to help himself, but his voice was swallowed by the raging Tiber behind him. He wheeled in circles, wondering where the hell the Swiss Guard were. Had they even heard his message?

Across the lot a large media truck was parked. Langdon ran toward it. A paunchy man in headphones sat in the cabin adjusting levers. Langdon rapped on the side of the truck. The man jumped, saw Langdon’s dripping clothes, and yanked off his headset.

“What’s the worry, mate?” His accent was Australian.

“I need your phone.” Langdon was frantic.

The man shrugged. “No dial tone. Been trying all night. Lines are busy.”

Langdon swore aloud. “Have you seen anyone go in there?” He pointed to the drawbridge.

“Actually, yeah. A black van’s been going in and out all night.”

Langdon felt a knot form in his stomach.

“Lucky guy,” the Aussie said, looking up at the tower, then frowning at his blocked view of the Vatican. “I bet the view from up there is amazing. I couldn’t get through the traffic in St. Peter’s, so I’m shooting from here.”

Langdon wasn’t listening. He was looking for possibilities.

“What do you think?” the Australian said. “Is this 11th Hour Samaritan for real?”

Langdon turned. “The what?”

“You didn’t hear? The Captain of the Swiss Guard got a call from someone who says they have some top-notch info. The guy’s flying in right now. All I know is if he saves the day… there go the ratings!” The man laughed.

Langdon was suddenly confused. A good Samaritan flying in to help? Did the person somehow know where the antimatter was? Then why didn’t he just tell the Swiss Guard? Why was he coming in person? Something was strange, but Langdon didn’t have time to figure it out.

“Hey,” the Aussie said, looking at Langdon more closely. “Aren’t you that guy I saw on TV? Trying to save that cardinal in St. Peter’s Square?”

Langdon did not answer. His eyes had suddenly locked on a device attached to the top of the truck-a satellite dish on a folding arm. Langdon looked at the castle again. The outer wall was fifty feet tall. The inner fortress went up even higher. A layered defense. The top was impossibly high from here, but maybe if he could get over the first wall…

Langdon spun to the newsman and pointed to the satellite arm. “How high does that go?”

“Huh?” The man looked confused. “Fifteen meters. Why?”

“Move the truck. Park next to the wall. I need help.”

“What are you talking about?”

Langdon explained.

The Aussie’s eyes went wide. “Are you insane? That’s a two-hundred-thousand-dollar telescoping extension. Not a ladder!“

“You want ratings? I’ve got information that will make your day.” Langdon was desperate.

“Information worth two hundred grand?”

Langdon told him what he would reveal in exchange for the favor.

Ninety seconds later, Robert Langdon was gripping the top of the satellite arm, swaying in the breeze fifty feet off the ground. Leaning out, he grabbed the top of the first wall, pulled himself onto it, and dropped onto the castle’s lower defense.

“Now keep your promise!” the Aussie called up. “Where is he?”

Langdon felt bad for telling them, but a deal was a deal. Besides, the Hassassin would probably call the press anyway. “Piazza Navona,” Langdon shouted. “He’s in the fountain.”

The Aussie lowered his satellite dish and sped off after the story of his career.

In a stone room high above the city, the Hassassin took off his wet boots and bandaged his hurt toe. There was pain, but not enough to ruin his fun.

He turned to his prize.

She was in the corner of the room, on her back on a simple couch, hands tied behind her, mouth covered. The Hassassin moved toward her. She was awake now. This pleased him. Surprisingly, in her eyes, he saw anger instead of fear.

The fear will come.
