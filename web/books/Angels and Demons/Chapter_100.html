Angels and Demons

Chapter 100

Robert Langdon had no idea where he was or how long he had been out when he opened his eyes and saw the bottom of a fancy, painted dome. Smoke floated above him. Something covered his mouth. An oxygen mask. He pulled it off. There was a horrible smell in the room—like burning flesh.

Langdon groaned as his head throbbed. He tried to sit up. A man in white knelt beside him.

“Riposati!” the man said, gently pushing Langdon back down. “Sono il paramйdico.”

Langdon gave in, his head spinning like the smoke above. What the hell happened? Vague feelings of panic stirred in his mind.

“Sуrcio salvatore,” the paramedic said. “Mouse… savior.”

Langdon felt even more confused. Mouse savior?

The man pointed to the Mickey Mouse watch on Langdon’s wrist. Langdon’s thoughts started to become clear. He remembered setting the alarm. As he stared blankly at the watch face, Langdon also saw the time. 10:28 P.M.

He sat up quickly.

Then, it all came back.

Langdon stood near the main altar with the fire chief and some of his men. They had been peppering him with questions. Langdon wasn’t listening. He had his own questions. His whole body ached, but he knew he had to act fast.

A pompiero came up to Langdon in the church. “I checked again, sir. The only bodies we found are Cardinal Guidera and the Swiss Guard commander. There’s no sign of a woman here.”

“Grazie,” Langdon said, unsure if he was relieved or horrified. He knew he had seen Vittoria passed out on the floor. Now she was gone. The only reason he could think of was not good. The killer hadn’t been subtle on the phone. A woman of spirit. I am aroused. Perhaps before this night is over, I will find you. And when I do…“

Langdon looked around. “Where are the Swiss Guard?”

“Still no contact. The Vatican lines are busy.”

Langdon felt lost and alone. Olivetti was dead. The cardinal was dead. Vittoria was missing. Half an hour of his life had vanished in a flash.

Outside, Langdon could hear the press gathering. He figured footage of the third cardinal’s awful death would air soon, if it hadn’t already. Langdon hoped the camerlegno had already assumed the worst and acted. Get everyone out of the Vatican! Enough games! We lose!

Langdon suddenly realized that everything driving him—helping save Vatican City, rescuing the four cardinals, facing the brotherhood he had studied for years—had disappeared from his mind. The war was lost. A new urge had taken over. It was simple. Clear. Basic.

Find Vittoria.

He felt a strange emptiness inside. Langdon had often heard that intense situations could unite two people more than years together could. He now believed it. Without Vittoria, he felt something he hadn’t felt in years. Loneliness. The pain gave him strength.

Pushing everything else aside, Langdon focused. He hoped the Hassassin would handle his task before enjoying himself. Otherwise, Langdon knew he was already too late. No, he told himself, you have time. Vittoria’s captor still had work to do. He had to appear one last time before vanishing forever.

The last altar of science, Langdon thought. The killer had one final task. Earth. Air. Fire. Water.

He looked at his watch. Thirty minutes. Langdon moved past the firemen toward Bernini’s Ecstasy of St. Teresa. This time, as he stared at Bernini’s marker, Langdon knew exactly what he was looking for.

Let angels guide you on your lofty quest…

Directly above the reclining saint, against a background of golden flame, hovered Bernini’s angel. The angel held a pointed spear of fire. Langdon’s eyes followed the direction of the spear, curving toward the right side of the church. His eyes reached the wall. He scanned the spot where the spear was pointing. There was nothing there. Langdon knew the spear was pointing far beyond the wall, into the night, somewhere across Rome.

“What direction is that?” Langdon asked, turning to the chief with a new determination.

“Direction?” The chief glanced where Langdon was pointing. He seemed confused. “I don’t know… west, I think.”

“What churches are in that direction?”

The chief seemed even more puzzled. “Dozens. Why?”

Langdon frowned. Of course, there were dozens. “I need a city map. Right away.”

The chief sent someone running to the fire truck for a map. Langdon turned back to the statue. Earth… Air… Fire… VITTORIA.

The final marker is Water, he told himself. Bernini’s Water. It was in a church somewhere out there. A needle in a haystack. He pushed his mind to remember all the Bernini works he could. I need a tribute to Water!

Langdon thought of Bernini’s statue of Triton -the Greek God of the sea. Then he realized it was in the square outside this very church, in the wrong direction. He forced himself to think. What figure would Bernini have carved to celebrate water? Neptune and Apollo? But that statue was in London’s Victoria & Albert Museum.

“Signore?” A fireman ran in with a map.

Langdon thanked him and spread it out on the altar. He knew he had asked the right people; the fire department’s map of Rome was as detailed as any Langdon had seen. “Where are we now?”

The man pointed. “Next to Piazza Barberini.”

Langdon looked at the angel’s spear again to get his bearings. The chief's guess was right. According to the map, the spear was pointing west. Langdon traced a line west from where he was. His hopes began to drop quickly. It seemed that with every inch his finger moved, he passed another building marked by a tiny black cross. Churches. The city was full of them. Finally, Langdon’s finger ran out of churches and into the suburbs of Rome. He sighed and stepped back from the map. Damn.

Looking at the whole of Rome, Langdon’s eyes landed on the three churches where the first three cardinals had been killed. The Chigi Chapel… St. Peter’s… here…

Seeing them all in front of him, Langdon noticed something strange about where they were. He had thought the churches would be scattered randomly across Rome. But they definitely were not. Strangely, the three churches seemed to be separated in a systematic way, in a huge triangle across the city. Langdon checked again. He wasn't imagining things. “Penna,” he said suddenly, without looking up.

Someone handed him a ballpoint pen.

Langdon circled the three churches. His pulse quickened. He checked his markings again. A perfect triangle!

Langdon thought first of the Great Seal on the dollar bill—the triangle with the all-seeing eye. But it didn’t fit. He had marked only three points. There should be four in all.

So where is Water? Langdon knew that any place he put the fourth point would ruin the triangle. To keep the symmetry, he had to put the fourth marker inside the triangle, at the center. He looked at that spot on the map. Nothing. He didn't like the idea anyway. The four elements of science were equal. Water wasn't special; it wouldn't be at the center of the others.

Still, he felt the careful layout couldn't be an accident. I don’t see the whole picture yet. There was only one other way to look at it. The four points didn't make a triangle; they made some other shape.

Langdon looked at the map. A square, maybe? A square didn't make symbolic sense, but at least it was symmetrical. Langdon put his finger on the map at a spot that would turn the triangle into a square. He quickly saw that a perfect square was impossible. The angles of the original triangle were uneven and made a crooked four-sided shape.

As he looked at other possible points around the triangle, something strange happened. He saw that the line he'd drawn earlier to show the direction of the angel’s spear went right through one of the possible points. Stunned, Langdon circled that point. Now he had four ink marks on the map, in a sort of awkward, kite-like diamond shape.

He frowned. Diamonds weren't an Illuminati symbol either. He paused. Then again…

For a moment, Langdon thought of the famous Illuminati Diamond. But it was silly. He pushed the thought away. Besides, this diamond was long and thin—like a kite—not the perfect symmetry the Illuminati Diamond was known for.

When he leaned in to see where he had put the final mark, Langdon was surprised to find that the fourth point was right in the middle of Rome’s famous Piazza Navona. He knew the piazza had a big church, but he had already checked that piazza and thought about the church there. As far as he knew, it had no Bernini works. The church was called Saint Agnes in Agony, named for St. Agnes, a beautiful young virgin who was forced into sexual slavery for refusing to give up her faith.

There must be something in that church! Langdon tried to remember the inside of the church. He couldn't think of any Bernini works there, let alone anything about water. The layout on the map bothered him too. A diamond. It was too close to be a coincidence, but not close enough to make sense. A kite? Langdon wondered if he had picked the wrong point. What am I missing!

The answer took another thirty seconds to hit him, but when it did, Langdon felt a thrill unlike anything he had ever felt in his academic life.

The Illuminati genius, it seemed, would never stop.

The shape he was looking at was not meant to be a diamond at all. The four points only made a diamond because Langdon had connected nearby points. The Illuminati believe in opposites! Connecting opposite corners with his pen, Langdon’s fingers trembled. There before him on the map was a huge cross. It’s a cross! The four elements of science appeared before his eyes… spread across Rome in an enormous, city-wide cross.

As he stared in wonder, a line of poetry popped into his head… like an old friend with a new face.

‘Cross Rome the mystic elements unfold…

‘Cross Rome…

The fog began to lift. Langdon saw that the answer had been right in front of him all night! The Illuminati poem had been telling him how the altars were arranged. A cross!

‘Cross Rome the mystic elements unfold!

It was clever wordplay. Langdon had first read the word ‘Cross as a shortened form of Across. He thought it was poetic freedom to keep the poem’s rhythm. But it was much more than that! Another hidden clue.

The cross on the map, Langdon realized, was the ultimate Illuminati paradox. It was a religious symbol made of elements of science. Galileo’s path of Illumination honored both science and God!

The rest of the puzzle clicked into place almost instantly.

Piazza Navona.

Right in the middle of Piazza Navona, outside the church of St. Agnes in Agony, Bernini had created one of his most famous sculptures. Everyone who visited Rome went to see it.

The Fountain of the Four Rivers!

A perfect celebration of water, Bernini’s Fountain of the Four Rivers honored the four main rivers of the Old World—The Nile, Ganges, Danube, and Rio Plata.

Water, Langdon thought. The last marker. It was perfect.

And even better, Langdon realized, the best part, was that a tall obelisk stood on top of Bernini’s fountain.

Leaving confused firemen behind, Langdon ran across the church toward Olivetti’s body.

10:31 P.M., he thought. Enough time. It was the first time all day that Langdon felt in control.

Kneeling next to Olivetti, hidden behind some pews, Langdon quietly took the commander’s gun and walkie-talkie. Langdon knew he would call for help, but not here. The final altar of science needed to stay secret for now. The media and fire department rushing to Piazza Navona with sirens would only make things worse.

Without a word, Langdon slipped out the door and dodged the crowd, who were now entering the church in droves. He crossed Piazza Barberini. In the shadows he turned on the walkie-talkie. He tried to reach Vatican City but heard only static. He was either out of range or the transmitter needed some kind of authorization code. Langdon adjusted the dials and buttons without success. Suddenly, he realized his plan to get help was not going to work. He spun, looking for a pay phone. None. Vatican circuits were jammed anyway.

He was alone.

Feeling his initial confidence fade, Langdon stood a moment and assessed his sad state-covered in bone dust, cut, deliriously exhausted, and hungry.

Langdon glanced back at the church. Smoke rose over the dome, lit by the media lights and fire trucks. He wondered if he should go back and get help. Instinct warned him however that extra help, especially untrained help, would only be a burden. If the Hassassin sees us coming… He thought of Vittoria and knew this would be his last chance to face her captor.

Piazza Navona, he thought, knowing he could get there in plenty of time and watch it. He scanned the area for a taxi, but the streets were almost entirely deserted. Even the taxi drivers, it seemed, had dropped everything to find a television. Piazza Navona was only about a mile away, but Langdon had no intention of wasting precious energy walking. He glanced back at the church, wondering if he could borrow a vehicle from someone.

A fire truck? A press van? Be serious.

Sensing options and minutes slipping away, Langdon made his decision. Pulling the gun from his pocket, he did something so unlike himself that he suspected his soul must now be possessed. Running over to a lone Citroлn sedan idling at a stoplight, Langdon pointed the weapon through the driver’s open window. “Fuori!” he yelled.

The trembling man got out.

Langdon jumped behind the wheel and hit the gas.
