Angels and Demons

Chapter 91

Langdon and Vittoria rushed to the main entrance of the church of Santa Maria della Vittoria and found the wooden door locked. Vittoria fired three shots from Olivetti’s semi-automatic into the old lock, and it broke apart.

The church had no entryway, so the entire sanctuary opened up in one sweeping view as Langdon and Vittoria threw open the main door. The scene before them was so unexpected, so strange, that Langdon had to close and reopen his eyes before he could take it all in.

The church was richly baroque… with gilded walls and altars. Right in the center of the sanctuary, under the main dome, wooden pews had been stacked high and were now burning in some kind of huge funeral fire. A bonfire reaching high into the dome. As Langdon’s eyes followed the fire upward, the true horror of the scene struck him.

High overhead, from the left and right sides of the ceiling, hung two censer cables—ropes used for swinging incense burners above the congregation. These ropes, however, held no censers now. Nor were they swinging. They had been used for something else…

Hanging from the cables was a human being. A naked man. Each wrist had been tied to a cable on the opposite side, and he had been lifted almost to the point of being torn apart. His arms were stretched out wide as if he were nailed to some invisible cross in the house of God.

Langdon felt frozen as he stared upward. A moment later, he saw the final horror. The old man was alive, and he raised his head. A pair of terrified eyes looked down in a silent plea for help. On the man’s chest was a burned symbol. He had been branded. Langdon could not see it clearly, but he had little doubt what the marking said. As the flames climbed higher, licking at the man’s feet, the victim cried out in pain, his body shaking.

As if sparked by some unseen force, Langdon felt his body suddenly move, rushing down the main aisle toward the fire. His lungs filled with smoke as he got closer. Ten feet from the fire, at a full sprint, Langdon hit a wall of heat. The skin on his face burned, and he fell back, shielding his eyes and landing hard on the marble floor. Staggering up, he pushed forward again, hands raised to protect himself.

Instantly he knew. The fire was far too hot.

Moving back again, he scanned the chapel walls. A heavy tapestry, he thought. If I can somehow smother the… But he knew a tapestry was not to be found. This is a baroque chapel, Robert, not some damn German castle! Think! He forced his eyes back to the hanging man.

Smoke and flames spun in the dome high above. The cables from the man’s wrists stretched up to the ceiling, through pulleys, and down to metal clamps on the church walls. Langdon glanced at a clamp. It was high up, but he knew if he could reach it and loosen a line, the tension would ease, and the man would swing away from the fire.

Suddenly, flames burst higher, and Langdon heard a scream from above. The man’s feet were blistering. The cardinal was being burned alive. Langdon focused on the clamp and ran.

In the back of the church, Vittoria gripped a pew, trying to clear her head. The scene above was awful. She looked away. Do something! Where was Olivetti? Had he seen the killer? Had he caught him? Where were they? Vittoria moved to help Langdon, but a sound stopped her.

The flames crackled louder, but another sound cut through the air. A metallic buzz. Close by. The pulsing seemed to come from the end of the pews to her left. It was a harsh rattle, like a phone ringing, but hard and stony. She gripped her gun and moved down the pews. The sound grew louder. On. Off. A repeating buzz.

As she neared the end of the aisle, she felt the sound came from the floor around the corner at the end of the pews. Moving forward, gun out, she realized she held something in her left hand—her cell phone. In her panic, she’d forgotten she had used it to call the commander, setting off his phone’s silent vibrate as a warning. Vittoria raised her phone to her ear. It still rang. The commander hadn't answered. Suddenly, fear rising, Vittoria knew the sound's source. She stepped forward, trembling.

The church seemed to drop beneath her as she saw the body on the floor. No blood flowed. No wounds marked the skin. Only the commander's head, twisted backward 180 degrees, filled her with dread. Vittoria fought back memories of her father's body.

The phone on the commander’s belt vibrated against the cold marble. Vittoria hung up her phone, and the ringing stopped. In the silence, she heard a new sound. Breathing in the dark behind her.

She spun, gun up, but knew she was too late. Heat seared from her skull to her feet as the killer’s elbow struck the back of her neck.

“Now you are mine,” a voice said.

Then, darkness.

Across the sanctuary, on the left side wall, Langdon balanced on a pew and reached up the wall, trying to get to the cleat. The cable was still six feet above him. Cleats like these were common in churches and were placed high to prevent anyone from messing with them. Langdon knew priests used wooden ladders called piuт li to reach the cleats. The killer had clearly used the church’s ladder to lift his victim. So where the hell is the ladder now! Langdon looked down, searching the floor around him. He vaguely remembered seeing a ladder in here somewhere. But where? A moment later, his heart sank. He knew where he had seen it. He turned toward the raging fire. Sure enough, the ladder was high above the blaze, engulfed in flames.

Now filled with desperation, Langdon scanned the entire church from where he stood, looking for anything that could help him reach the cleat. As he looked around the church, he suddenly realized something.

Where the hell is Vittoria? She had disappeared. Did she go for help? Langdon screamed her name, but there was no answer. And where is Olivetti!

There was a howl of pain from above, and Langdon felt he was already too late. As he looked up again and saw the victim slowly roasting, Langdon thought of only one thing. Water. Lots of it. Put out the fire. At least lower the flames. “I need water, damn it!” he yelled.

“That’s next,” a voice growled from the back of the church.

Langdon spun around, almost falling off the pews.

A dark, monstrous man was walking up the side aisle directly toward him. Even in the firelight, his eyes burned black. Langdon recognized the gun in his hand as the one from his own jacket pocket… the one Vittoria had been carrying when they came in.

The sudden panic that rose in Langdon was a mix of confused fears. His first thought was for Vittoria. What had this animal done to her? Was she hurt? Or worse? At the same moment, Langdon realized the man overhead was screaming louder. The cardinal would die. Helping him now was impossible. Then, as the Hassassin pointed the gun at Langdon’s chest, Langdon’s panic turned inward, his senses overloaded. He reacted without thinking as the shot went off. Jumping off the bench, Langdon dove headfirst over the row of church pews.

When he landed on the pews, he hit harder than he expected and immediately rolled to the floor. The marble cushioned his fall with the grace of cold steel. Footsteps came closer to his right. Langdon turned toward the front of the church and began scrambling for his life under the pews.

High above the chapel floor, Cardinal Guidera endured his last painful moments of consciousness. Looking down at his naked body, he saw the skin on his legs begin to blister and peel. I am in hell, he thought. God, why have you abandoned me? He knew this must be hell because he was looking at the brand on his chest upside down… and yet, as if by magic, the word made perfect sense.

The old house stood on a hill, a silent witness to the passage of time. Its weathered walls and peeling paint whispered stories of bygone eras.

Sunlight filtered through the aged windowpanes, casting elongated shadows across the dusty floorboards. The air was thick with the scent of dried lavender and forgotten memories.

In the attic, cobwebs clung to antique furniture like ghostly shrouds. Each object held a fragment of the past, waiting to be rediscovered.

A gentle breeze stirred the curtains, carrying with it the faint echo of laughter and whispered secrets. The house seemed to breathe, alive with the echoes of those who once called it home.
