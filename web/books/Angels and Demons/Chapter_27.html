Angels and Demons

Chapter 27

"Vittoria, I won't let it happen!" Kohler's breath was heavy and worsening as the Haz-Mat elevator went up.

Vittoria ignored him. She longed for a safe place, something familiar in this place that no longer felt like home. She knew it wasn't possible. Right now, she had to ignore the pain and act. Find a phone.

Robert Langdon was next to her, quiet as always. Vittoria had stopped wondering who he was. A specialist? Could Kohler be any less clear? Mr. Langdon can help us find your father’s killer. Langdon wasn't helping at all. His warmth and kindness seemed real, but he was clearly hiding something. They both were.

Kohler spoke to her again. "As director of CERN, I must protect the future of science. If you turn this into a global issue and CERN suffers-"

"Future of science?" Vittoria faced him. "Do you really plan to avoid blame by never admitting this antimatter came from CERN? Do you plan to ignore the lives we've risked?"

"Not we," Kohler replied. "You. You and your father."

Vittoria looked away.

"And as for risking lives," Kohler said, "life is what this is about. You know antimatter technology has huge effects on life on this planet. If CERN goes broke, destroyed by scandal, everyone loses. Humanity's future depends on places like CERN, scientists like you and your father, working to solve tomorrow's problems."

Vittoria had heard Kohler's Science-as-God talk before, and she never believed it. Science itself caused half the problems it was trying to solve. "Progress" was the Earth's biggest threat.

“Scientific progress carries risk,” Kohler argued. “It always has. Space programs, genetic research, medicine—they all make mistakes. Science needs to survive its own mistakes, at any cost. For everyone’s sake.”

Vittoria was amazed at Kohler’s ability to consider moral issues without emotion. His intellect seemed to be completely separate from his feelings. “You think CERN is so important to the earth’s future that we shouldn’t be held responsible?”

“Don’t talk to me about morals. You went too far when you made that sample, and you’ve put this whole facility at risk. I’m trying to protect not only the jobs of the three thousand scientists who work here, but also your father’s reputation. Think about him. A man like your father doesn’t deserve to be remembered as the creator of a weapon of mass destruction.”

Vittoria felt his words hit home. I am the one who convinced my father to create that sample. This is my fault!

When the door opened, Kohler was still talking. Vittoria stepped out of the elevator, pulled out her phone, and tried again.

Still no dial tone. Damn! She headed for the door.

“Vittoria, stop.” The director sounded like he was having trouble breathing, as he hurried after her. “Slow down. We need to talk.”

“Basta di parlare!”

“Think of your father,” Kohler urged. “What would he do?”

She kept going.

“Vittoria, I haven’t been completely honest with you.”

Vittoria felt her legs slow down.

“I don’t know what I was thinking,” Kohler said. “I was just trying to keep you safe. Just tell me what you want. We have to work together here.”

Vittoria stopped completely in the middle of the lab, but she didn’t turn around. “I want to find the antimatter. And I want to know who killed my father.” She waited.

Kohler sighed. “Vittoria, we already know who killed your father. I’m sorry.”

Now Vittoria turned around. “You what?”

“I didn’t know how to tell you. It’s a hard-”

“You know who killed my father?”

“We have a very good idea, yes. The killer left a kind of signature. That’s why I called Mr. Langdon. The group that says they did it is his area of expertise.”

“The group? A terrorist group?”

“Vittoria, they stole a quarter gram of antimatter.”

Vittoria looked at Robert Langdon across the room. Everything clicked. That's why the secrecy. She was amazed she hadn’t realized it sooner. Kohler had called the authorities. The authorities. It seemed clear now. Robert Langdon was American, neat, serious, obviously very smart. Who else could it be? Vittoria should have known from the start. She felt hopeful as she turned to him.

“Mr. Langdon, I want to know who killed my father. And I want to know if your people can find the antimatter.”

Langdon seemed confused. “My people?”

“You’re with U.S. Intelligence, I assume.”

“Actually… no.”

Kohler stepped in. “Mr. Langdon is an art history professor at Harvard University.”

Vittoria felt like she had been shocked. “An art teacher?”

“He studies religious symbols.” Kohler sighed. “Vittoria, we think your father was killed by a satanic cult.”

Vittoria heard the words, but she couldn't understand them. A satanic cult.

"The group claiming they're responsible calls themselves the Illuminati."

Vittoria looked at Kohler and then at Langdon, wondering if this was some kind of sick joke. "The Illuminati?" she asked. "Like the Bavarian Illuminati?"

Kohler seemed shocked. "You've heard of them?"

Vittoria felt tears of frustration rising. "Bavarian Illuminati: New World Order. Steve Jackson computer games. Half the tech people here play it online." Her voice broke. "But I don't understand..."

Kohler looked at Langdon, confused.

Langdon nodded. "Popular game. Old group tries to take over the world. Based on some history. I didn't know it was popular in Europe too."

Vittoria was puzzled. "What are you talking about? The Illuminati? It's a computer game!"

"Vittoria," Kohler said, "the Illuminati is the group saying they're responsible for your father's death."

Vittoria gathered all her courage to fight back the tears. She forced herself to stay calm and think clearly. But the more she tried to focus, the less she understood. Her father had been killed. CERN had a big security problem. There was a bomb somewhere that she was responsible for. And the director had asked an art teacher to help them find a made-up group of devil worshipers.

Vittoria suddenly felt very alone. She turned to leave, but Kohler stopped her. He reached into his pocket. He took out a crumpled piece of fax paper and gave it to her.

Vittoria recoiled in horror as she saw the image.

“They branded him,” Kohler said. “They branded his goddamn chest.”
