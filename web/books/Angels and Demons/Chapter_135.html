Angels and Demons

Chapter 135

Dawn arrived late in Rome.

An early rainstorm had driven the crowds from St. Peter’s Square. The media stayed, huddled under umbrellas and in vans, commenting on the evening’s events. Across the world, churches were full. It was a time of thought and talk… in all religions. Questions were many, but the answers only seemed to lead to more questions. So far, the Vatican had been silent, making no statement at all.

Deep in the Vatican Grottoes, Cardinal Mortati knelt alone before the open coffin. He reached in and closed the old man’s blackened mouth. His Holiness looked peaceful now. Quietly resting for eternity.

At Mortati’s feet was a golden jar, full of ashes. Mortati had gathered the ashes himself and brought them here. “A chance to be forgiven,” he said to His Holiness, placing the jar inside the coffin next to the Pope. “No love is greater than a father’s for his son.” Mortati hid the jar under the papal robes. He knew this sacred grotto was only for the remains of Popes, but Mortati felt this was right.

“Signore?” someone said, entering the grottoes. It was Lieutenant Chartrand. He was with three Swiss Guards. “They are ready for you in the meeting.”

Mortati nodded. “In a moment.” He looked one last time into the coffin before him, and then stood up. He turned to the guards. “It is time for His Holiness to have the peace he has earned.”

The guards came forward and with great effort slid the lid of the Pope’s coffin back into place. It closed with a final, loud thud.

Mortati was alone as he walked across the Borgia Courtyard toward the Sistine Chapel. A wet breeze moved his robe. Another cardinal came out of the Apostolic Palace and walked beside him.

“May I walk you to the meeting, signore?”

“The honor is mine.”

“Signore,” the cardinal said, looking troubled. “The college owes you an apology for last night. We were blinded by-”

“Please,” Mortati replied. “We sometimes see what we want to believe.”

The cardinal was silent a long time. Finally he spoke. “Have you been told? You are no longer our Great Elector.”

Mortati smiled. “Yes. I thank God for little things.”

“The college insisted you could be chosen.”

“It seems kindness still exists in the church.”

“You are a wise man. You would guide us well.”

“I am an old man. I would guide you for a short time.”

They both laughed.

As they reached the end of the Borgia Courtyard, the cardinal hesitated. He turned to Mortati, looking troubled and confused, as if the uneasy awe of the night before had returned.

“Did you know,” the cardinal whispered, “that we found no remains on the balcony?”

Mortati smiled. “Maybe the rain washed them away.”

The man looked at the stormy sky. “Yes, maybe…”
