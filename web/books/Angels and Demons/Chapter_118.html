Angels and Demons

Chapter 118

Eleven-forty-two P.M.

The wild ride back into the basilica to save the camerlegno was something Langdon never thought he'd be part of… let alone leading. But he was nearest the door and had acted without thinking.

He’ll die in here , Langdon thought, rushing over the entrance into the dark space. “Camerlegno! Stop!”

The blackness that surrounded Langdon was complete. His eyes were adjusted to the light outside, and he could only see a few feet in front of him. He stopped quickly. Somewhere ahead, he heard the camerlegno’s robe brush as the priest ran without seeing into the darkness.

Vittoria and the guards arrived right away. Flashlights turned on, but the lights were almost out and barely lit up the basilica. The beams moved back and forth, showing only columns and floor. The camerlegno was nowhere in sight.

“Camerlegno!” Chartrand yelled, scared. “Wait! Signore!”

A noise in the doorway made everyone turn. Chinita Macri’s large body stumbled through the entrance. Her camera was on her shoulder, and the red light showed it was still recording. Glick ran behind her, holding a microphone, telling her to slow down.

Langdon couldn't believe they were here. This is not the time!

“Out!” Chartrand shouted. “This is not for you to see!”

But Macri and Glick kept moving forward.

“Chinita!” Glick sounded scared now. “This is crazy! I’m not going!”

Macri didn't listen. She flipped a switch on her camera. The bright light on top flashed on, blinding everyone.

Langdon covered his face and turned away, hurt. Damn it! But when he looked up, the church around them was lit up for thirty yards.

Just then, the camerlegno’s voice echoed from somewhere far away. “Upon this rock I will build my church!”

Macri swung her camera toward the sound. In the distance, in the gray area at the edge of the light, black cloth waved, showing a familiar figure running down the center of the basilica.

For a quick moment, everyone hesitated, taking in the strange sight. Then everything changed. Chartrand shoved past Langdon and ran after the camerlegno. Langdon followed. Then the guards and Vittoria.

Macri came last, lighting the way and sending the grim chase out to the world. Glick reluctantly followed, cursing and giving a terrified, rushed commentary.

Lieutenant Chartrand had once figured out that the main aisle of St. Peter’s Basilica was longer than an Olympic soccer field. But tonight, it felt twice as long. As the guard ran after the camerlegno, he wondered where he was going. The camerlegno was clearly in shock, probably delirious from his injury and seeing the horrible massacre in the Pope’s office.

Up ahead, beyond the reach of the BBC light, the camerlegno’s voice rang out happily. “Upon this rock I will build my church!”

Chartrand knew the man was shouting Scripture—Matthew 16:18, if Chartrand remembered correctly. Upon this rock I will build my church. It was a cruelly inappropriate inspiration—the church was about to be destroyed. Surely the camerlegno had gone crazy.

Or had he?

For a brief moment, Chartrand’s soul wavered. Holy visions and divine messages had always seemed like wishful fantasies to him—the product of overly enthusiastic minds hearing what they wanted to hear—God did not interact directly!

But then, as if the Holy Spirit Himself had come down to convince Chartrand of His power, Chartrand had a vision.

Fifty yards ahead, in the center of the church, a ghost appeared… a see-through, glowing outline. The pale shape was that of the half-naked camerlegno. The specter seemed transparent, radiating light. Chartrand stopped suddenly, feeling a knot tighten in his chest. The camerlegno is glowing! The body seemed to shine brighter now. Then, it began to sink… deeper and deeper, until it disappeared as if by magic into the blackness of the floor.

Langdon had seen the phantom too. For a moment, he also thought he had witnessed a magical vision. But as he passed the stunned Chartrand and ran toward the spot where the camerlegno had disappeared, he realized what had just happened. The camerlegno had arrived at the Niche of the Palliums—the sunken chamber lit by ninety-nine oil lamps. The lamps in the niche shone up from below, lighting him like a ghost. Then, as the camerlegno descended the stairs into the light, he had seemed to disappear beneath the floor.

Langdon arrived breathless at the edge overlooking the sunken room. He peered down the stairs. At the bottom, lit by the golden glow of oil lamps, the camerlegno dashed across the marble chamber toward the set of glass doors that led to the room holding the famous golden box.

What is he doing? Langdon wondered. Surely he can’t think the golden box —

The camerlegno yanked open the doors and ran inside. Oddly though, he completely ignored the golden box, rushing right past it. Five feet beyond the box, he dropped to his knees and began struggling to lift an iron grate embedded in the floor.

Langdon watched in horror, now realizing where the camerlegno was headed. Good God, no! He dashed down the stairs after him. “Father! Don’t!”

As Langdon opened the glass doors and ran toward the camerlegno, he saw the camerlegno struggle with the grate. The hinged, iron door fell open with a loud crash, showing a narrow shaft and a steep stairway that went down into darkness. As the camerlegno moved toward the hole, Langdon grabbed his bare shoulders and pulled him back. The man’s skin was slippery with sweat, but Langdon held on.

The camerlegno turned, clearly surprised. “What are you doing!”

Langdon was surprised when they looked at each other. The camerlegno no longer looked like he was in a trance. His eyes were sharp, shining with a clear determination. The burn on his chest looked very painful.

“Father,” Langdon said, as calmly as he could, “you can’t go down there. We need to leave.”

“My son,” the camerlegno said, his voice strangely normal. “I have just received a message. I know-”

“Camerlegno!” It was Chartrand and the others. They came running down the stairs into the room, lit by Macri’s camera.

When Chartrand saw the open grate in the floor, he looked terrified. He crossed himself and gave Langdon a thankful look for stopping the camerlegno. Langdon understood; he had read enough about Vatican architecture to know what was below that grate. It was the most sacred place in all of Christendom. Terra Santa. Holy Ground. Some called it the Necropolis. Some called it the Catacombs. According to stories from the few clergy who had gone down there over the years, the Necropolis was a dark maze of underground tombs that could trap a visitor forever if he got lost. It was not a place where they wanted to be chasing the camerlegno.

“Signore,” Chartrand begged. “You’re in shock. We need to leave this place. You can’t go down there. It’s suicide.”

The camerlegno suddenly seemed calm. He reached out and put a gentle hand on Chartrand’s shoulder. “Thank you for your concern and help. I can’t explain how much. I can’t explain that I understand. But I have had a revelation. I know where the antimatter is.”

Everyone stared.

The camerlegno turned to the group. “Upon this rock I will build my church. That was the message. The meaning is clear.”

Langdon still couldn't grasp the camerlegno’s belief that he had spoken to God, let alone that he had understood the message. Upon this rock I will build my church? They were the words Jesus spoke when he chose Peter as his first apostle. What did they have to do with any of this?

Macri moved closer to film. Glick was silent, as if stunned.

The camerlegno spoke fast now. “The Illuminati have placed their weapon of destruction on the very cornerstone of this church. At the foundation.” He pointed down the stairs. “On the very rock upon which this church was built. And I know where that rock is.”

Langdon felt it was time to grab the camerlegno and take him away. Even though he seemed clear-headed, the priest was talking nonsense. A rock? The cornerstone in the foundation? The stairway in front of them didn’t lead to the foundation; it led to the necropolis! “The quote is a figure of speech, Father! There is no actual rock!”

The camerlegno looked strangely sad. “There is a rock, my son.” He pointed into the hole. “Pietro и la pietra.”

Langdon froze. Suddenly, he understood.

The stark simplicity of it gave him chills. As Langdon stood there with the others, staring down the long staircase, he realized that there was indeed a rock buried in the darkness beneath this church.

Pietro и la pietra. Peter is the rock.

Peter’s faith in God was so strong that Jesus called Peter “the rock”—the unwavering disciple on whose shoulders Jesus would build his church. On this very spot, Langdon realized—Vatican Hill—Peter had been crucified and buried. The first Christians built a small shrine over his tomb. As Christianity grew, the shrine got bigger, layer upon layer, ending in this huge basilica. The entire Catholic faith had been built, literally, upon St. Peter. The rock.

“The antimatter is on St. Peter’s tomb,” the camerlegno said, his voice clear.

Despite the seemingly impossible source of the information, Langdon sensed a clear logic in it. Placing the antimatter on St. Peter’s tomb seemed obvious now. The Illuminati, in an act of symbolic defiance, had put the antimatter at the heart of Christianity, both literally and figuratively. The ultimate infiltration.

“And if you all need proof from this world,” the camerlegno said, sounding impatient now, “I just found that grate unlocked.” He pointed to the open hatch in the floor. “It is never unlocked. Someone has been down there… recently.”

Everyone stared into the hole.

An instant later, with surprising agility, the camerlegno spun, grabbed an oil lamp, and headed for the opening.
