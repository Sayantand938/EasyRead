Angels and Demons

Chapter 86

No light. No sound.

The Secret Archives were black.

Langdon now knew that fear was a strong pusher. Gasping, he felt his way through the dark toward the revolving door. He found the button on the wall and slammed his hand against it. Nothing. He tried again. The door was dead.

Turning around blindly, he shouted, but his voice came out weak. He suddenly felt trapped and in danger. His lungs fought for air as the adrenaline made his heart race. He felt like he'd been punched in the stomach.

When he threw himself against the door, he thought for a second that he felt the door start to turn. He pushed again, seeing spots. Now he realized the whole room was turning, not the door. Stumbling back, Langdon tripped over the bottom of a rolling ladder and fell hard. He scraped his knee on the edge of a bookshelf. Cursing, he got up and reached for the ladder.

He found it. He had hoped it would be heavy wood or iron, but it was aluminum. He grabbed the ladder and held it like a weapon. Then he ran through the dark at the glass wall. It was closer than he thought. The ladder hit straight on and bounced back. From the weak sound of the hit, Langdon knew he would need something much stronger than an aluminum ladder to break this glass.

When he thought about the gun, he felt a surge of hope that quickly disappeared. The gun was gone. Olivetti had taken it from him in the Pope’s office, saying he didn't want loaded guns around while the camerlegno was there. It seemed reasonable at the time.

Langdon shouted again, making even less sound than before.

Then he remembered the walkie-talkie the guard had left on the table outside the vault. Why didn’t I bring it in! As he started seeing purple spots, Langdon forced himself to think. You’ve been trapped before , he told himself. You survived worse. You were just a kid and you figured it out. The heavy darkness came crashing in. Think!

Langdon lowered himself onto the floor. He rolled onto his back and put his hands at his sides. The first step was to get control.

Relax. Conserve.

No longer fighting gravity to pump blood, Langdon’s heart began to slow. It was a trick swimmers used to re-oxygenate their blood between races.

There is plenty of air in here, he told himself. Plenty. Now think. He waited, thinking the lights might come back on any second. They did not. As he lay there, able to breathe easier, a strange acceptance came over him. He felt calm. He fought it.

You will move, damn it! But where …

On Langdon’s wrist, Mickey Mouse glowed as if enjoying the dark: 9:33 P.M. Half an hour until Fire. Langdon felt it was much later. Instead of planning an escape, his mind wanted an explanation. Who turned off the power? Was Rocher searching more widely? Wouldn’t Olivetti have told Rocher I’m in here! Langdon knew it didn't matter at this point.

Opening his mouth wide and tilting back his head, Langdon took the deepest breaths he could. Each breath burned a little less than the last. His head cleared. He pulled his thoughts together and forced himself to think.

Glass walls, he told himself. But very thick glass.

He wondered if any of the books were stored in heavy, steel, fireproof file cabinets. Langdon had seen them sometimes in other archives but had seen none here. Besides, finding one in the dark would take time. He couldn’t lift one anyway, especially now.

What about the examination table? Langdon knew this vault, like the others, had an examination table in the center. So what! He knew he couldn’t lift it. And even if he could drag it, he wouldn’t get it far. The shelves were close together, the aisles too narrow.

The aisles are too narrow …

Suddenly, Langdon knew.

With a burst of confidence, he jumped up too quickly. Swaying from a head rush, he reached out in the dark for support. His hand found a stack. He paused, forcing himself to conserve energy. He would need all his strength for this.

Positioning himself against the book stack like a football player against a sled, he planted his feet and pushed. If I can somehow tip the shelf. But it barely moved. He realigned and pushed again. His feet slipped backward on the floor. The stack creaked but didn’t move.

He needed leverage.

Finding the glass wall again, he placed one hand on it to guide him as he raced in the dark toward the far end of the vault. The back wall appeared suddenly, and he collided with it, hitting his shoulder hard. Cursing, Langdon circled the shelf and grabbed the stack at about eye level. Then, propping one leg on the glass behind him and another on the lower shelves, he started to climb. Books fell around him, fluttering into the darkness. He didn’t care. Survival had long since become more important than archival rules. He felt his balance was off in the total darkness and closed his eyes, telling his brain to ignore what he couldn't see. He moved faster now. The air felt thinner the higher he went. He scrambled toward the upper shelves, stepping on books, trying to get a grip, pulling himself upward. Then, like a rock climber reaching the top, Langdon grasped the top shelf. Stretching his legs out behind him, he walked his feet up the glass wall until he was almost horizontal.

Now or never, Robert , a voice urged. Just like the leg press in the Harvard gym.

With dizzying effort, he planted his feet against the wall behind him, braced his arms and chest against the stack, and pushed. Nothing happened.

Gasping for air, he repositioned and tried again, extending his legs. Ever so slightly, the stack moved. He pushed again, and the stack rocked forward an inch or so and then back. Langdon used the motion, inhaling what felt like an oxygenless breath and heaving again. The shelf rocked farther.

Like a swing set , he told himself. Keep the rhythm. A little more.

Langdon rocked the shelf, pushing harder with each swing. His thighs burned, but he ignored the pain. The pendulum was moving. Three more pushes, he told himself.

It only took two.

For a moment, he felt weightless. Then, with a loud crash of books falling, Langdon and the shelf were tipping forward.

Halfway down, the shelf hit the next stack. Langdon held on, leaning forward, trying to knock the second shelf over. There was a moment of still panic, and then, groaning under the weight, the second stack began to fall. Langdon was falling again.

Like huge dominoes, the stacks began to fall, one after another. Metal against metal, books flying everywhere. Langdon held tight as his tilting stack bounced down like a gear on a lift. He wondered how many stacks there were in total. How much would they weigh? The glass at the end was thick…

Langdon’s stack was almost flat when he heard what he was waiting for—a different kind of crash. Far away. At the end of the vault. The sharp sound of metal hitting glass. The vault around him shook, and Langdon knew the last stack, weighed down by the others, had hit the glass hard. The sound that followed was the worst sound Langdon had ever heard.

Silence.

There was no shattering of glass, only the deep thud as the wall took the weight of the stacks now leaning against it. He lay wide-eyed on the pile of books. Somewhere far off, there was a creak. Langdon would have held his breath to listen, but he had none left.

One second. Two…

Then, as he was about to pass out, Langdon heard a distant cracking… a spreading web through the glass. Suddenly, like a cannon, the glass burst. The stack beneath Langdon crashed to the floor.

Like welcome rain on a desert, bits of glass chimed down in the dark. With a great sucking hiss, the air rushed in.

Thirty seconds later, in the Vatican Grottoes, Vittoria stood before a corpse when the walkie-talkie's electronic squawk broke the silence. The voice blaring out sounded winded. “This is Robert Langdon! Can anyone hear me?”

Vittoria looked up. Robert! She couldn’t believe how much she suddenly wished he were there.

The guards exchanged puzzled looks. One took a radio off his belt. “Mr. Langdon? You are on channel three. The commander is waiting to hear from you on channel one.”

“I know he’s on channel one, damn it! I don’t want to speak to him. I want the camerlegno. Now! Somebody find him for me.”

In the darkness of the Secret Archives, Langdon stood amidst shattered glass and tried to catch his breath. He felt a warm liquid on his left hand and knew he was bleeding. The camerlegno’s voice spoke at once, startling Langdon.

“This is Camerlegno Ventresca. What’s going on?”

Langdon pressed the button, his heart still pounding. “I think somebody just tried to kill me!”

There was a silence on the line.

Langdon tried to calm himself. “I also know where the next killing is going to be.”

The voice that answered wasn’t the camerlegno’s. It was Commander Olivetti’s: “Mr. Langdon, don’t say another word.”
