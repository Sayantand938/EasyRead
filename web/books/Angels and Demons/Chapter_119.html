Angels and Demons

Chapter 119

The stone steps went down into the earth at a sharp angle.

I’m going to die down here, Vittoria thought, holding tight to the thick rope railing as she hurried down the narrow passage after the others. Langdon had tried to stop the camerlegno from going into the shaft, but Chartrand had stopped him, grabbing Langdon and holding him back. The young guard now seemed sure the camerlegno knew what he was doing.

After a short struggle, Langdon had gotten away and chased after the camerlegno with Chartrand right behind him. Vittoria had instinctively run after them.

Now she was rushing down a steep slope where one wrong step could mean a fatal fall. Far below, she could see the golden light of the camerlegno’s oil lamp. Behind her, Vittoria could hear the BBC reporters struggling to keep up. The camera spotlight cast twisting shadows down the shaft, lighting up Chartrand and Langdon. Vittoria could hardly believe the world was watching this madness. Turn off that damn camera! But she knew the light was the only way any of them could see where they were going.

As the strange chase went on, Vittoria’s thoughts raced. What could the camerlegno possibly do down here? Even if he found the antimatter, there wasn't any time!

Vittoria was surprised to realize she now felt the camerlegno was probably right. Putting the antimatter three stories underground seemed like a surprisingly kind and merciful choice. Deep underground—like in Z-lab—an antimatter explosion would be partly contained. There would be no heat wave, no flying debris to hurt anyone, just a dramatic opening in the earth and a great basilica collapsing into a hole.

Was this Kohler’s one good deed? Saving lives? Vittoria still couldn’t understand the director’s part in it. She could accept his hatred of religion… but this huge plot seemed beyond him. Could Kohler’s hate really be this strong? Destroying the Vatican? Hiring a killer? The murders of her father, the Pope, and four cardinals? It seemed impossible. And how had Kohler managed to do all this within the Vatican walls? Rocher was Kohler’s man on the inside, Vittoria told herself. Rocher was an Illuminatus. Captain Rocher must have had keys to everything—the Pope’s rooms, Il Passetto, the Necropolis, St. Peter’s tomb, all of it. He could have put the antimatter on St. Peter’s tomb—a very restricted place—and then told his guards not to bother searching the Vatican’s restricted areas. Rocher knew no one would ever find the canister.

But Rocher didn’t count on the camerlegno’s message from above.

The message. This was the leap of faith Vittoria was still struggling to accept. Had God really spoken to the camerlegno? Vittoria’s gut said no, yet she worked with entanglement physics—the study of connections. She saw amazing communications every day—twin sea-turtle eggs separated and placed in labs thousands of miles apart hatching at the same time… acres of jellyfish pulsing in perfect rhythm as if they were one being. There are invisible lines of communication everywhere, she thought.

But what about God and people?

Vittoria wished her father were there to give her faith. He had once explained how God speaks to people using science, and he had made her believe. She still remembered the day she saw him praying and asked, “Father, why do you pray? God can’t answer you.”

Leonardo Vetra looked up from his praying with a fatherly smile. “My skeptical daughter. So you don’t think God speaks to people? Let me explain it in a way you understand.” He took a model of the human brain off a shelf and put it in front of her. “As you know, Vittoria, people usually use only a small part of their brainpower. But if they’re in very emotional situations—like a physical injury, great joy or fear, or deep meditation—their brain cells start firing like crazy, which makes their thinking much clearer.”

“So what?” Vittoria said. “Just because you think clearly doesn’t mean you’re talking to God.”

“Aha!” Vetra exclaimed. “But amazing solutions to impossible problems often come during these moments of clear thinking. Gurus call it higher consciousness. Biologists call it altered states. Psychologists call it super-awareness.” He paused. “And Christians call it answered prayer.” Smiling, he added, “Sometimes, divine revelation just means getting your brain to hear what your heart already knows.”

Now, as she ran down, headfirst into the dark, Vittoria thought maybe her father was right. Was it so hard to believe that the camerlegno’s trauma had put his mind in a state where he simply “realized” where the antimatter was?

Each of us is a God , Buddha had said. Each of us knows all. We just need to open our minds to hear our own wisdom.

In that moment of clear thinking, as Vittoria went deeper into the earth, she felt her own mind open… her own wisdom appear. She now knew without a doubt what the camerlegno planned to do. This awareness brought a fear unlike anything she had ever felt.

“Camerlegno, no!” she shouted down the passage. “You don’t understand!” Vittoria pictured the crowds of people around Vatican City, and her blood ran cold. “If you bring the antimatter up… everyone will die!"

Langdon was leaping three steps at a time now, catching up. The passage was narrow, but he wasn’t afraid of being trapped. His earlier fear was overshadowed by a much greater dread.

“Camerlegno!” Langdon felt himself getting closer to the lantern’s glow. “You must leave the antimatter where it is! There’s no other choice!”

Even as Langdon spoke, he could not believe it. He had not only accepted the camerlegno’s divine revelation of where the antimatter was, but he was arguing for the destruction of St. Peter’s Basilica—one of the greatest buildings on earth… and all the art inside.

But the people outside… it’s the only way.

It seemed cruelly ironic that the only way to save the people now was to destroy the church. Langdon imagined the Illuminati were amused by the symbolism.

The air rising from the bottom of the tunnel was cool and damp. Somewhere down here was the sacred necropolis … burial place of St. Peter and countless other early Christians. Langdon felt a chill, hoping this was not a suicide mission.

Suddenly, the camerlegno’s lantern seemed to stop. Langdon quickly caught up to him.

The end of the stairs appeared suddenly from the shadows. A wrought-iron gate with three skulls on it blocked the bottom of the stairs. The camerlegno was there, pulling the gate open. Langdon jumped, pushing the gate shut, blocking the camerlegno’s way. The others came rushing down the stairs, everyone ghostly white in the BBC spotlight… especially Glick , who was looking paler with every step.

Chartrand grabbed Langdon. “Let the camerlegno pass!”

“No!” Vittoria said from above, breathless. “We must evacuate right now! You cannot take the antimatter out of here! If you bring it up, everyone outside will die!”

The camerlegno’s voice was surprisingly calm. “All of you… we must trust. We have little time.”

“You don’t understand,” Vittoria said. “An explosion on the ground will be much worse than one down here!”

The camerlegno looked at her, his green eyes shining with reason. “Who said anything about an explosion on the ground?”

Vittoria stared. “You’re leaving it down here?”

The camerlegno’s confidence was mesmerizing. “There will be no more death tonight.”

“Father, but-”

“Please… some faith.” The camerlegno’s voice dropped to a persuasive whisper. “I am not asking anyone to join me. You are all free to go. All I am asking is that you not stop His will. Let me do what I have been called to do.” The camerlegno’s stare grew stronger. “I am to save this church. And I can. I swear on my life.”

The silence that followed was deafening.
