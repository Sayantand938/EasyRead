Angels and Demons

Chapter 83

Flashlights seemed weak in the huge darkness of St. Peter’s Basilica. The blackness above felt heavy, and Vittoria felt empty, like she was lost at sea. She stayed close as the Swiss Guards and the camerlegno moved forward. High above, a dove made a soft sound and flew away.

As if he knew she was uneasy, the camerlegno stepped back and put his hand on her shoulder. His touch gave her strength, as if he was giving her the calm she needed for what they were about to do.

What are we about to do? she thought. This is crazy!

But Vittoria knew that even though it felt wrong and scary, they had to do it. The camerlegno had big decisions to make, and he needed information… information hidden in a tomb in the Vatican Grottoes. She wondered what they would find. Did the Illuminati kill the Pope? Are they really that powerful? Am I really going to do the first autopsy on a Pope?

Vittoria thought it was strange that she felt more afraid in this dark church than swimming with barracudas at night. She felt safe in nature. She understood nature. But she didn't understand people and faith. Thinking about dangerous fish in the dark made her think about the press waiting outside. TV images of burned bodies reminded her of her father’s body… and the killer's mean laugh. The killer was still out there. Vittoria felt anger take over her fear.

As they walked around a pillar-bigger than any redwood tree she had ever seen-Vittoria saw an orange light ahead. The light seemed to come from under the floor in the middle of the basilica. As they got closer, she realized what it was. It was the famous underground area below the main altar-the beautiful room that held the Vatican’s most important holy objects. When they reached the gate around the space, Vittoria looked down at the golden box surrounded by many glowing oil lamps.

“St. Peter’s bones?” she asked, even though she knew they weren't. Everyone who came to St. Peter’s knew what was in the golden box.

“Actually, no,” the camerlegno said. “That’s a common mistake. It’s not a container for relics. The box holds palliums -woven bands that the Pope gives to new cardinals.”

“But I thought-”

“Like everyone else. The guidebooks call this St. Peter’s tomb, but his real grave is two levels below us, buried in the ground. The Vatican dug it up in the forties. No one can go down there.”

Vittoria was shocked. As they moved from the glowing area back into the darkness, she thought of the stories she’d heard of pilgrims traveling thousands of miles to look at that golden box, thinking they were near St. Peter. “Shouldn’t the Vatican tell people?”

“We all feel better having a connection to something divine… even if it’s just in our minds.”

Vittoria, as a scientist, couldn't argue with that. She had read many studies of the placebo effect—aspirins curing cancer in people who thought they were taking a miracle drug. What was faith , after all?

“Change,” the camerlegno said, “is hard for us in Vatican City. Admitting our past mistakes, modernizing, are things we usually avoid. His Holiness was trying to change that.” He paused. “Connecting to the modern world. Looking for new ways to reach God.”

Vittoria nodded in the dark. “Like science?”

“To be honest, science seems unimportant.”

“Unimportant?” Vittoria could think of many words to describe science, but in today's world “unimportant” didn't seem like one of them.

“Science can heal, or science can kill. It depends on the person using it. I’m interested in the person’s soul.”

“When did you hear your calling?”

“Before I was born.”

Vittoria looked at him.

“I’m sorry, that always sounds like a weird question. I mean, I’ve always known I would serve God. From the first moment I could think. But it wasn’t until I was a young man, in the military, that I really understood what I was meant to do.”

Vittoria was surprised. “You were in the military?”

“Two years. I wouldn’t fire a gun, so they had me fly instead. Medevac helicopters. I still fly now and then, actually.”

Vittoria tried to imagine the young priest flying a helicopter. Strangely, she could picture him perfectly at the controls. Camerlegno Ventresca had a strength that seemed to highlight his belief rather than hide it. “Did you ever fly the Pope?”

“Goodness, no. We left that important job to the experts. His Holiness let me take the helicopter to our retreat in Gandolfo sometimes.” He stopped, looking at her. “Ms. Vetra, thank you for your help today. I’m very sorry about your father. I really am.”

“Thank you.”

“I never knew my father. He died before I was born. I lost my mother when I was ten.”

Vittoria looked up. “You were an orphan?” She felt a sudden connection.

“I survived an accident. An accident that took my mother.”

“Who took care of you?”

“God,” the camerlegno said. “He actually sent me another father. A bishop from Palermo came to my hospital bed and took me in. I wasn't surprised then. I had felt God watching over me since I was a boy. The bishop's appearance just proved what I already thought, that God had somehow picked me to serve him.”

“You believed God chose you?”

“I did. And I do.” The camerlegno didn't sound proud, just thankful. “I worked with the bishop for many years. He later became a cardinal. But he never forgot me. He is the father I remember.” A flashlight shone on the camerlegno’s face, and Vittoria saw sadness in his eyes.

The group reached a huge pillar, and their lights all shone on an opening in the floor. Vittoria looked down at the stairs going into the darkness and suddenly wanted to go back. The guards were already helping the camerlegno onto the stairs. Then they helped her.

“What happened to him?” she asked, going down, trying to keep her voice calm. “The cardinal who took you in?”

“He left the College of Cardinals for another job.”

Vittoria was surprised.

“And then, I’m sorry to say, he died.”

"My condolences," Vittoria said. "Recently?"

The camerlegno turned, shadows making the pain on his face more visible. "Exactly fifteen days ago. We are going to see him right now."
