Angels and Demons

Chapter 129

In the hallway outside the Sistine Chapel, Vittoria Vetra sat numb on a bench at the bottom of the Royal Staircase. When she saw the figure coming through the back door, she wondered if she was seeing another ghost. He was bandaged, limping, and wearing some kind of medical outfit.

She stood, unable to believe what she was seeing. “Ro… bert?”

He didn't answer. He walked straight to her and hugged her tightly. When he kissed her, it was a sudden, heartfelt kiss full of gratitude.

Vittoria felt tears welling up. “Oh, God… oh, thank God…”

He kissed her again, more deeply, and she held him close, losing herself in his embrace. Their bodies clung together, as if they had known each other for years. She forgot the fear and pain. She closed her eyes, feeling weightless in the moment.

“It is God’s will!” someone was shouting, his voice echoing in the Sistine Chapel. “Who but the chosen one could have survived that evil explosion?”

“Me,” a voice echoed from the back of the chapel.

Mortati and the others turned in amazement at the disheveled figure walking up the center aisle. “Mr… Langdon?”

Without a word, Langdon walked slowly to the front of the chapel. Vittoria Vetra went in too. Then two guards hurried in, pushing a cart with a large television on it. Langdon waited while they plugged it in, facing the cardinals. Then Langdon motioned for the guards to leave. They did, closing the door behind them.

Now it was just Langdon, Vittoria, and the cardinals. Langdon connected the Sony RUVI to the television. Then he pressed PLAY.

The television flashed on.

The scene on the screen showed the Pope’s office. The video looked like it had been filmed secretly and clumsily. Off to one side, the camerlegno stood in the dim light, in front of a fire. He seemed to be talking to the camera, but it soon became clear he was talking to someone else—the person filming. Langdon explained that Maximilian Kohler, the director of CERN, had filmed the video. Just an hour before, Kohler had secretly recorded his meeting with the camerlegno using a tiny camera hidden under the arm of his wheelchair.

Mortati and the cardinals watched, confused. The conversation had already started, but Langdon didn't rewind it. Whatever Langdon wanted them to see was coming up soon.

“Leonardo Vetra kept diaries?” the camerlegno was saying. “I guess that’s good for CERN. If the diaries explain how he made antimatter—”

“They don’t,” Kohler said. “You’ll be glad to know that information died with Leonardo. But his diaries mentioned something else. You.”

The camerlegno looked worried. “I don’t understand.”

“They described a meeting Leonardo had last month. With you.”

The camerlegno paused, then looked at the door. “Rocher shouldn’t have let you in without asking me. How did you get in here?”

“Rocher knows the truth. I called him earlier and told him what you’ve done.”

“What I have done? Whatever story you told him, Rocher is a Swiss Guard and too loyal to this church to believe a bitter scientist over his camerlegno.”

“Actually, he is so loyal that he has to believe. He is so faithful that even with proof that one of his own guards had betrayed the church, he refused to accept it. All day he has been looking for another explanation.”

“So you gave him one.”

“The truth. Shocking as it was.”

“If Rocher believed you, he would have arrested me.”

“No. I wouldn’t let him. I offered to keep quiet if he allowed this meeting.”

The camerlegno laughed strangely. “You plan to blackmail the church with a story that no one could believe?”

“I don’t need blackmail. I just want to hear the truth from you. Leonardo Vetra was a friend.”

The camerlegno said nothing, just stared down at Kohler.

“Try this,” Kohler said sharply. “About a month ago, Leonardo Vetra contacted you asking for an urgent meeting with the Pope—a meeting you allowed because the Pope admired Leonardo’s work and because Leonardo said it was an emergency.”

The camerlegno turned to the fire. He said nothing.

“Leonardo came to the Vatican in secret. He was breaking his daughter’s trust by coming here, which bothered him a lot. But he felt he had to. His research had him confused and he needed spiritual help from the church. In a private meeting, he told you and the Pope that he had made a scientific discovery with huge religious importance. He had proved that Genesis was physically possible, and that strong energy sources—what Vetra called God—could copy the moment of Creation.”

Silence.

“The Pope was shocked,” Kohler continued. “He wanted Leonardo to tell everyone. His Holiness thought this discovery could start to close the gap between science and religion—something the Pope had dreamed of his whole life. Then Leonardo explained the bad side to you—the reason he needed the church’s help. It seemed his Creation experiment, just like your Bible says, made everything in pairs. Opposites. Light and dark. Besides creating matter, Vetra also created antimatter. Should I keep going?”

The camerlegno was silent. He bent down and poked the coals.

“After Leonardo Vetra came here,” Kohler said, “you went to CERN to see his work. Leonardo’s diaries said you took a personal trip to his lab.”

The camerlegno looked up.

Kohler went on. “The Pope couldn’t travel without the media noticing, so he sent you. Leonardo gave you a secret tour of his lab. He showed you an antimatter explosion—the Big Bang—the power of Creation. He also showed you a big sample he kept locked up as proof that his new process could make antimatter on a large scale. You were amazed. You went back to Vatican City to tell the Pope what you had seen.”

The camerlegno sighed. “So what’s bothering you? That I would respect Leonardo’s secret by pretending tonight that I knew nothing about antimatter?”

“No! It bothers me that Leonardo Vetra almost proved your God exists, and you had him killed!”

The camerlegno turned, his face unreadable.

The only sound was the crackling fire.

Suddenly, the camera shook, and Kohler’s arm appeared. He leaned forward, seeming to struggle with something under his wheelchair. When he sat back, he held a pistol out. The camera angle was chilling… looking from behind… down the gun… at the camerlegno.

Kohler said, “Confess your sins, Father. Now.”

The camerlegno looked surprised. “You will never leave here alive.”

“Death would be a relief from the pain your faith has caused me since I was a boy.” Kohler held the gun with both hands. “I am giving you a choice. Confess your sins… or die now.”

The camerlegno glanced at the door.

“Rocher is outside,” Kohler said. “He is also ready to kill you.”

“Rocher is sworn to protect the-”

“Rocher let me in here. Armed. He is disgusted by your lies. You have one choice. Confess to me. I must hear it from you.”

The camerlegno hesitated.

Kohler cocked his gun. “Do you really doubt I will kill you?”

“No matter what I tell you,” the camerlegno said, “a man like you will never understand.”

“Try me.”

The camerlegno stood still for a moment, a strong figure in the dim light of the fire. When he spoke, his words echoed with a dignity more suited to telling tales of good deeds than confessing sins.

“Since the beginning of time,” the camerlegno said, “this church has fought the enemies of God. Sometimes with words. Sometimes with swords. And we have always survived.”

The camerlegno radiated conviction.

“But the demons of the past,” he continued, “were demons of fire and evil… they were enemies we could fight—enemies who inspired fear. Yet Satan is clever. As time passed, he traded his devilish appearance for a new face… the face of pure reason. Clear and subtle, but still soulless.” The camerlegno’s voice flashed with sudden anger—an almost crazy change. “Tell me, Mr. Kohler! How can the church condemn what makes logical sense to our minds! How can we denounce what is now the very foundation of our society! Each time the church raises its voice in warning, you shout back, calling us ignorant. Paranoid. Controlling! And so your evil grows. Hidden in a veil of self-righteous intelligence. It spreads like a cancer. Made holy by the miracles of its own technology. Worshiping itself! Until we no longer think you are anything but pure goodness. Science has come to save us from our sickness, hunger, and pain! Behold science—the new God of endless miracles, all-powerful and kind! Ignore the weapons and the chaos. Forget the broken loneliness and endless danger. Science is here!” The camerlegno stepped toward the gun. “But I have seen Satan’s face lurking… I have seen the peril…”

“What are you talking about! Vetra’s science practically proved the existence of your God! He was your ally!”

“Ally? Science and religion are not on the same side! We do not seek the same God, you and I! Who is your God? One of protons, masses, and particle charges? How does your God inspire? How does your God reach into the hearts of man and remind him he is responsible to a greater power! Remind him that he is responsible to his fellow man! Vetra was wrong. His work was not religious, it was sacrilegious! Man cannot put God’s Creation in a test tube and show it off to the world! This does not glorify God, it demeans God!” The camerlegno was clawing at his body now, his voice manic.

“So you had Leonardo Vetra killed!”

“For the church! For all mankind! How insane! Man is not ready to control the power of Creation. God in a test tube? A drop of liquid that can destroy a city? He had to be stopped!” The camerlegno stopped talking suddenly. He looked away, back at the fire. He seemed to be thinking about what to do.

Kohler raised the gun. “You have confessed. You can’t escape.”

The camerlegno laughed sadly. “Don’t you see? Confessing your sins is the escape.” He looked toward the door. “When God is on your side, you have choices a man like you could never understand.” As he said this, the camerlegno grabbed his cassock at the neck and ripped it open, showing his bare chest.

Kohler jumped, clearly surprised. “What are you doing!”

The camerlegno didn’t answer. He stepped back, toward the fireplace, and took something out of the glowing embers.

“Stop!” Kohler shouted, still pointing the gun. “What are you doing!”

When the camerlegno turned, he was holding a red-hot brand. The Illuminati Diamond. His eyes suddenly looked crazy. “I wanted to do this by myself.” His voice was full of wild intensity. “But now… I see God wanted you to be here. You are my salvation.”

Before Kohler could react, the camerlegno closed his eyes, arched his back, and pushed the red-hot brand into the middle of his chest. His flesh sizzled. “Mother Mary! Blessed Mother… Look at your son!” He screamed in pain.

Kohler stumbled forward… standing unsteadily, the gun shaking in front of him.

The camerlegno screamed louder, staggering in shock. He threw the brand at Kohler’s feet. Then the priest collapsed on the floor, twisting in pain.

What happened next was a blur.

There was a sudden commotion onscreen as the Swiss Guard rushed into the room. The soundtrack boomed with gunfire. Kohler clutched his chest, thrown backward, bleeding, falling into his wheelchair.

“No!” Rocher called, trying to stop his guards from shooting Kohler.

The camerlegno, still twisting on the floor, rolled and pointed wildly at Rocher. “Illuminatus!”

“You bastard,” Rocher yelled, running at him. “You self-righteous ba-”

Chartrand shot him down with three bullets. Rocher slid dead across the floor.

Then the guards ran to the wounded camerlegno, gathering around him. As they huddled, the video caught the face of a dazed Robert Langdon, kneeling beside the wheelchair, looking at the brand. Then, the entire picture began shaking wildly. Kohler had woken up and was detaching the tiny camcorder from its holder under the arm of the wheelchair. Then he tried to hand the camcorder to Langdon.

“G-give…” Kohler gasped. “G-give this to the m-media.”

Then the screen went blank.
