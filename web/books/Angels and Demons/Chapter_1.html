Angels and Demons

Chapter 1

High on the steps of the Pyramid of Giza, a young woman laughed and called down to him. “Robert, hurry! I knew I should have married someone younger!” Her smile was enchanting.

He struggled to keep up, but his legs felt like stone. “Wait,” he pleaded. “Please…”

As he climbed, his vision blurred. There was a pounding in his ears. I must reach her! But when he looked up again, the woman was gone. Instead, an old man with decaying teeth stood there. The man stared down, twisting his lips into a sad grimace. Then he screamed in pain, and the sound echoed across the desert.

Robert Langdon woke suddenly from his nightmare. The phone beside his bed was ringing. Confused, he picked up the receiver.

“Hello?”

“I’m looking for Robert Langdon,” a man’s voice said.

Langdon sat up in his empty bed and tried to clear his head. “This is Robert Langdon.” He squinted at his digital clock. It was 5:18 A.M.

“I must see you right away.”

“Who is this?”

“My name is Maximilian Kohler. I study tiny particles in physics.”

“A what? ” Langdon struggled to understand. “Are you sure you have the right Langdon?”

“You’re a professor of religious symbols at Harvard University. You’ve written three books about symbols and-”

“Do you know what time it is?”

“I’m sorry. I have something you need to see. I can’t talk about it on the phone.”

Langdon groaned quietly. This had happened before. One of the downsides of writing books about religious symbols was calls from religious fanatics who wanted him to confirm their latest sign from God. Last month a stripper from Oklahoma had promised Langdon amazing sex if he would come and confirm that a cross shape had appeared on her bed sheets. The Shroud of Tulsa , Langdon had called it.

“How did you get my number?” Langdon tried to be polite, even though it was late.

“On the internet. The website for your book.”

Langdon frowned. He was sure his book’s site didn’t have his home phone number. The man was obviously lying.

“I need to see you,” the caller said firmly. “I’ll pay you well.”

Now Langdon was getting angry. “I’m sorry, but I really-”

“If you leave right away, you can be there by-”

“I’m not going anywhere! It’s five o’clock in the morning!” Langdon hung up and fell back into bed. He closed his eyes and tried to go back to sleep. It was no use. The dream was stuck in his mind. Reluctantly, he put on his robe and went downstairs.

Robert Langdon walked barefoot through his empty Massachusetts Victorian house and drank his usual insomnia cure-a mug of hot Nestlй’s Quik. The April moon shone through the bay windows and lit up the oriental carpets. Langdon’s colleagues often joked that his place looked more like a museum than a home. His shelves were full of religious items from around the world-an ekuaba from Ghana, a gold cross from Spain, a cycladic idol from the Aegean, and even a rare woven boccus from Borneo, a young warrior’s symbol of lasting youth.

As Langdon sat on his brass Maharishi’s chest and enjoyed the warm chocolate, the bay window showed his reflection. The image was unclear and pale… like a ghost. An aging ghost, he thought, sadly reminded that his young spirit was living in an old body.

Although not very handsome in a typical way, the forty-five-year-old Langdon had what his female colleagues called an “intelligent” appeal-some gray hairs in his thick brown hair, sharp blue eyes, a strikingly deep voice, and the strong, easy smile of a college athlete. A skilled diver in high school and college, Langdon still had the body of a swimmer, a fit, six-foot frame that he kept in shape with fifty laps a day in the university pool.

Langdon’s friends had always seen him as a bit of a mystery-a man stuck between centuries. On weekends he could be seen relaxing on the lawn in blue jeans, talking about computer graphics or religious history with students; other times he could be seen in his Harris tweed and paisley vest, photographed in fancy art magazines at museum openings where he had been asked to speak.

Although a tough teacher and strict, Langdon was the first to enjoy what he called the “lost art of good clean fun.” He enjoyed recreation with an enthusiasm that had earned him a friendly acceptance among his students. His campus nickname-“The Dolphin”-was a reference to both his friendly personality and his amazing ability to dive into a pool and beat the entire opposing team in a water polo match.

As Langdon sat alone, staring into the darkness, the silence of his home was broken again, this time by the ring of his fax machine. Too tired to be annoyed, Langdon gave a tired chuckle.

God’s people, he thought. Two thousand years of waiting for their Messiah, and they’re still as persistent as hell.

Wearily, he returned his empty mug to the kitchen and walked slowly to his oak-paneled study. The incoming fax lay in the tray. Sighing, he picked up the paper and looked at it.

Instantly, he felt a wave of nausea.

The image on the page was of a human corpse. The body was naked, and its head twisted completely backward. On the victim’s chest was a terrible burn. The man had been branded… imprinted with a single word. It was a word Langdon knew well. Very well. He stared at the elaborate lettering in disbelief.

“Illuminati,” he stammered, his heart pounding. It can’t be …

Slowly, afraid of what he was about to see, Langdon rotated the fax 180 degrees. He looked at the word upside down.

Instantly, he felt breathless, as if hit by a truck. Hardly believing his eyes, he rotated the fax again, reading the brand right-side up and then upside down.

“Illuminati,” he whispered.

Stunned, Langdon collapsed in a chair. He sat for a moment, completely bewildered. Gradually, his eyes went to the blinking red light on his fax machine. Whoever sent this fax was still on the line… waiting to talk. Langdon gazed at the blinking light for a long time.

Then, trembling, he picked up the receiver.
