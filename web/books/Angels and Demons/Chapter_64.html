Angels and Demons

Chapter 64

Langdon and Vittoria's taxi raced up the wide Via della Scrofa, covering the mile in just over a minute. They screeched to a halt on the south side of the Piazza del Popolo just before eight. Langdon, not having any lire, paid the driver extra with U.S. dollars. He and Vittoria got out quickly. The piazza was calm except for the laughter of a few locals sitting outside the popular Rosati Café—a favorite spot for Italian writers. The air smelled of espresso and pastries.

Langdon was still stunned by his mistake at the Pantheon. But as he quickly looked around this square, he felt a familiar sense. The piazza seemed subtly full of Illuminati meaning. It was not only shaped like a perfect oval, but in the very center stood a tall Egyptian obelisk—a square stone pillar with a pointed top. These obelisks, taken as treasures from Rome's past conquests, were scattered throughout the city and called "Lofty Pyramids" by symbologists—skyward extensions of the sacred pyramid shape.

As Langdon's eyes traveled up the obelisk, he suddenly noticed something else in the distance. Something even more striking.

"We're in the right place," he said quietly, feeling a sudden sense of danger. "Take a look at that." Langdon pointed to the grand Porta del Popolo—the tall stone archway at the far end of the piazza. The arched structure had stood overlooking the piazza for centuries. Right in the center of the archway's highest point was a symbolic carving. "Does that look familiar?"

Vittoria looked up at the large carving. "A bright star above a triangular pile of stones?"

Langdon shook his head. "A source of Illumination over a pyramid."

Vittoria turned, her eyes widening. "Like... the Great Seal of the United States?"

"Exactly. The Masonic symbol on the one-dollar bill."

Vittoria took a deep breath and looked around the piazza. "So where's this damn church?"

The Church of Santa Maria del Popolo stood out like a misplaced battleship, crooked at the base of a hill on the southeast corner of the piazza. The eleventh-century stone building looked even clumsier with the tower of scaffolding covering the front.

Langdon's thoughts were a blur as they hurried toward the building. He stared up at the church in wonder. Could a murder really be about to happen inside? He wished Olivetti would hurry. The gun felt awkward in his pocket.

The church's front stairs were ventaglio - a welcoming, curved fan - ironic in this case because they were blocked with scaffolding, construction equipment, and a sign warning: CONSTRUZIONE. NON ENTRARE.

Langdon realized that a church closed for renovation meant complete privacy for a killer. Not like the Pantheon. No fancy tricks needed here. Just to find a way in.

Vittoria slipped without hesitation between the sawhorses and headed up the staircase.

"Vittoria," Langdon cautioned. "If he's still in there..."

Vittoria didn't seem to hear. She went up the main porch to the church's only wooden door. Langdon hurried up the stairs behind her. Before he could say a word, she had grasped the handle and pulled. Langdon held his breath. The door didn't move.

"There must be another entrance," Vittoria said.

"Probably," Langdon said, exhaling, "but Olivetti will be here in a minute. It's too dangerous to go in. We should watch the church from out here until-"

Vittoria turned, her eyes blazing. "If there's another way in, there's another way out. If this guy disappears, we're fungito."

Langdon knew enough Italian to know she was right.

The alley to the right of the church was narrow and dark, with tall walls on both sides. It smelled like urine—a common smell in a city where bars outnumbered public restrooms twenty to one.

Langdon and Vittoria hurried into the foul darkness. They had gone about fifteen yards when Vittoria tugged Langdon’s arm and pointed.

Langdon saw it too. Up ahead was a plain wooden door with heavy hinges. Langdon recognized it as the usual porta sacra —a private entrance for clergy. Most of these entrances had stopped being used years ago as buildings got closer and limited space pushed side entrances into inconvenient alleyways.

Vittoria hurried to the door. She got there and stared down at where the doorknob should have been, looking confused. Langdon came up behind her and eyed the strange donut-shaped hoop hanging there.

“An annulus,” he whispered. Langdon reached out and quietly lifted the ring. He pulled the ring toward him. The fixture clicked. Vittoria shifted, looking suddenly uneasy. Quietly, Langdon twisted the ring clockwise. It spun loosely all the way around, not catching. Langdon frowned and tried the other direction with the same result.

Vittoria looked down the rest of the alley. “You think there’s another entrance?”

Langdon doubted it. Most Renaissance cathedrals were designed as temporary fortresses if a city was attacked. They had as few entrances as possible. “If there is another way in,” he said, “it’s probably set back in the rear wall—more of an escape route than an entrance.”

Vittoria was already moving.

Langdon followed deeper into the alley. The walls rose high on both sides of him. Somewhere a bell began ringing eight o’clock…

Robert Langdon didn't hear Vittoria the first time she called him. He had paused at a stained-glass window covered with bars, trying to look inside the church.

“Robert!” Her voice was a loud whisper.

Langdon looked up. Vittoria was at the end of the alley, pointing around the back of the church and waving to him. Langdon walked reluctantly toward her. At the base of the back wall, a stone structure stuck out, hiding a narrow cave—a kind of tight passageway cut into the church's foundation.

“An entrance?” Vittoria asked.

Langdon nodded. Actually an exit, but we won’t get specific.

Vittoria knelt and looked into the tunnel. “Let’s check the door. See if it’s open.”

Langdon started to object, but Vittoria took his hand and pulled him into the opening.

“Wait,” Langdon said.

She turned to him, impatient.

Langdon sighed. “I’ll go first.”

Vittoria looked surprised. “More chivalry?”

“Age before beauty.”

“Was that a compliment?”

Langdon smiled and moved past her into the dark. “Careful on the stairs.”

He moved slowly into the darkness, keeping one hand on the wall. The stone felt sharp on his fingertips. For a moment Langdon thought of the old story of Daedelus, how the boy kept one hand on the wall as he moved through the Minotaur’s maze, knowing he would find the end if he never let go of the wall. Langdon moved forward, not sure he wanted to find the end.

The tunnel got a little narrower, and Langdon slowed down. He felt Vittoria close behind him. As the wall curved left, the tunnel opened into a half-circle space. Strangely, there was a little light here. In the dim light Langdon saw the shape of a heavy wooden door.

“Uh oh,” he said.

“Locked?”

“It was.”

“Was?” Vittoria came to his side.

Langdon pointed. Lit by a beam of light from inside, the door was slightly open… its hinges broken by a metal bar still stuck in the wood.

They stood silently for a moment. Then, in the dark, Langdon felt Vittoria’s hands on his chest, searching, sliding under his jacket.

“Relax, professor,” she said. “I’m just getting the gun.”

At that moment, inside the Vatican Museums, a group of Swiss Guards spread out. The museum was dark, and the guards wore infrared goggles like those used by U.S. Marines. The goggles made everything look strangely green. Each guard wore headphones connected to a detector that looked like an antenna, which he waved back and forth. They used these devices twice a week to check for electronic bugs inside the Vatican. They moved carefully, checking behind statues, inside alcoves, closets, and under furniture. The antennae would beep if they found even the smallest magnetic field.

Tonight, however, they weren't getting any signals.
