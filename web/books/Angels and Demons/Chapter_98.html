Angels and Demons

Chapter 98

The six firemen who came to the fire at the Church of Santa Maria Della Vittoria put out the blaze with Halon gas. Water was cheaper, but the steam would have damaged the chapel's frescoes. The Vatican paid the Roman firemen well for quick, careful work in all its buildings.

Firemen, because of their job, saw tragedy often. But the scene in this church was unforgettable. Part crucifixion, part hanging, part burning, it was like something from a nightmare.

The press, as usual, arrived before the fire department. They filmed a lot before the firemen cleared the church. When the firemen finally cut the victim down, they knew who he was.

“Cardinal Guidera,” one whispered. “From Barcelona.”

The victim was naked. His lower body was crimson-black, blood seeping through cracks in his thighs. His shinbones were visible. One fireman vomited. Another went outside for air.

The worst part was the symbol burned on the cardinal’s chest. The squad chief circled the body in shocked fear. The devil's work, he thought. Satan did this himself. He crossed himself for the first time since he was a child.

“Another body!” someone yelled. One of the firemen had found another body.

The chief recognized the second victim right away. The strict commander of the Swiss Guard was not liked by many law enforcement officials. The chief called the Vatican, but the lines were busy. He knew it didn’t matter. The Swiss Guard would see this on TV soon.

As the chief looked around, trying to imagine what had happened, he saw a hole full of bullet marks. A coffin had fallen off its supports during a struggle. It was a mess. That’s for the police and the Vatican to handle, the chief thought, turning away.

As he turned, though, he stopped. He heard a sound coming from the coffin. It was not a sound any fireman ever liked to hear.

“Bomb!” he cried out. “Everyone out!”

When the bomb squad rolled the coffin over, they found what was making the electronic beeping. They stared, confused.

“Medic!” one finally screamed. “Medic!”

"Any word from Olivetti?" the camerlegno asked, looking tired as Rocher led him back from the Sistine Chapel to the Pope's office.

"No, signore. I fear the worst."

When they got to the Pope's office, the camerlegno's voice was sad. "Captain, there is nothing more I can do here tonight. I fear I have already done too much. I am going into this office to pray. I don't want to be disturbed. The rest is in God's hands."

"Yes, signore."

"It's late, Captain. Find that canister."

"Our search goes on." Rocher paused. "The weapon is too well hidden."

The camerlegno winced, as if he couldn't bear to think of it. "Yes. At exactly 11:15 P.M., if the church is still in danger, I want you to evacuate the cardinals. I'm trusting you with their safety. I ask only one thing. Let these men leave this place with honor. Let them go out into St. Peter's Square and stand with everyone else. I don't want the last memory of this church to be scared old men sneaking out a back door."

"Very good, signore. And you? Should I come for you at 11:15 as well?"

"There's no need."

“Signore?”

“I will leave when I feel like it.”

Rocher wondered if the camerlegno planned to stay until the end.

The camerlegno opened the door to the Pope’s office and entered. “Actually…” he said, turning. “There is one thing.”

“Signore?”

“It seems cold in this office tonight. I am shivering.”

“The electric heat is out. Let me start a fire for you.”

The camerlegno smiled wearily. “Thank you. Thank you, very much.”

Rocher left the Pope’s office where he had left the camerlegno praying by firelight in front of a small statue of the Virgin Mary. It was a strange sight. A black shadow kneeling in the flickering light. As Rocher headed down the hall, a guard appeared, running toward him. Even by candlelight Rocher recognized Lieutenant Chartrand. Young, inexperienced, and enthusiastic.

“Captain,” Chartrand called, holding out a cell phone. “I think the camerlegno’s guess may have worked. We’ve got a caller here who says he has information that can help us. He phoned on one of the Vatican’s private lines. I have no idea how he got the number.”

Rocher stopped. “What?”

“He will only speak to the highest-ranking officer.”

“Any word from Olivetti?”

“No, sir.”

He took the receiver. “This is Captain Rocher. I am the highest-ranking officer here.”

“Rocher,” the voice said. “I will tell you who I am. Then I will tell you what you need to do next.”

When the caller stopped talking and hung up, Rocher stood stunned. He now knew who he was taking orders from.

Back at CERN, Sylvie Baudeloque was frantically trying to keep track of all the licensing questions coming in on Kohler’s voice mail. When the private line on the director’s desk began to ring, Sylvie jumped. Nobody had that number. She answered.

“Yes?”

“Ms. Baudeloque? This is Director Kohler. Contact my pilot. My jet needs to be ready in five minutes.”
