Angels and Demons

Chapter 7

Maximilian Kohler, the head of CERN, was secretly called Kцnig - King. The title reflected fear more than respect for the man who ruled his organization from a wheelchair. Few knew him well, but the terrible story of how he became disabled was well-known at CERN, and few blamed him for his anger or his commitment to pure science.

Langdon had only met Kohler briefly but already felt the director was distant. Langdon almost had to run to keep up with Kohler’s electric wheelchair as it sped silently toward the main entrance. The wheelchair was unlike any Langdon had ever seen. It had many electronic devices, including a multiline phone, a paging system, a computer screen, and even a small, removable video camera. It was King Kohler’s mobile command center.

Langdon followed him through a mechanical door into CERN’s large main lobby.

The Glass Cathedral , Langdon thought, looking up.

Above, the bluish glass roof shimmered in the afternoon sun, creating geometric patterns in the air and making the room feel grand. Angular shadows looked like veins across the white tiled walls and onto the marble floors. The air smelled clean and sterile. A few scientists moved quickly, their footsteps echoing in the large space.

“This way, please, Mr. Langdon.” Kohler's voice sounded almost robotic. His accent was stiff and precise, like his stern face. Kohler coughed, wiped his mouth with a white handkerchief, and stared at Langdon with his dull gray eyes. “Please hurry.” His wheelchair seemed to jump across the tiled floor.

Langdon followed him past many hallways branching off the main atrium. Every hallway was busy. The scientists who saw Kohler seemed surprised and looked at Langdon, wondering who he was to be with Kohler.

“I’m embarrassed to say,” Langdon began, trying to start a conversation, “that I’ve never heard of CERN.”

“That’s not surprising,” Kohler replied, his short answer sounding very efficient. “Most Americans don’t think of Europe as the world leader in scientific research. They see us as just a strange shopping area - an odd view considering the nationalities of people like Einstein, Galileo, and Newton.”

Langdon didn't know what to say. He took the fax from his pocket. “This man in the photo, can you-”

Kohler stopped him with a wave. “Please. Not here. I'm taking you to him now.” He held out his hand. “I should probably take that.”

Langdon gave him the fax and walked with him silently.

Kohler turned sharply left into a wide hall covered in awards. A big plaque stood out at the entrance. Langdon slowed down to read the words as they walked by.

ARS ELECTRONICA AWARD

For Cultural Innovation in the Digital Age Awarded to Tim Berners Lee and CERN for the invention of the WORLDWIDE WEB

Wow, Langdon thought, reading the text. He wasn't kidding. Langdon had always thought the Web was American. But, he only knew about his own book's website and looking at the Louvre or El Prado online on his old Macintosh.

“The Web,” Kohler said, coughing and wiping his mouth, “started here as a network of computers inside CERN. It let scientists from different departments share what they found every day. But everyone thinks the Web is American.”

Langdon followed him down the hall. “Why not correct the story?”

Kohler shrugged, not interested. “A small mistake about a small thing. CERN does much more than connect computers. Our scientists do amazing things almost every day.”

Langdon looked at Kohler, puzzled. “Miracles?” The word “miracle” was definitely not used at Harvard’s Fairchild Science Building. Miracles were for the School of Divinity.

“You seem doubtful,” Kohler said. “I thought you studied religious symbols. Don’t you believe in miracles?”

“I’m not sure about miracles,” Langdon said. Especially ones that happen in science labs.

“Maybe miracle isn’t the right word. I was trying to use words you’d understand.”

“My language?” Langdon felt uneasy. “I don’t want to disappoint you, but I study religious symbols - I’m a professor, not a priest.”

Kohler slowed and turned, his eyes softening. “Of course. How simple of me. You don’t need to have cancer to understand its symptoms.”

Langdon had never heard it said like that.

As they walked down the hall, Kohler nodded, accepting what Langdon said. “I think we’ll understand each other well, Mr. Langdon.”

Langdon doubted it.

As they hurried on, Langdon heard a deep rumbling ahead. The noise grew louder with each step, shaking the walls. It seemed to come from the end of the hall.

“What’s that?” Langdon finally asked, having to shout. He felt like they were nearing an active volcano.

“Free Fall Tube,” Kohler replied, his flat voice easily cutting through the air. He didn't explain further.

Langdon didn’t ask. He was tired, and Maximilian Kohler didn’t seem interested in being friendly. Langdon remembered why he was here. Illuminati. He guessed somewhere in this huge place was a body… a body marked with a symbol he had just flown 3,000 miles to see.

As they neared the hall's end, the rumble became almost deafening, vibrating up through Langdon’s feet. They turned the corner, and a viewing area appeared to the right. Four thick windows were set in a curved wall, like those in a submarine. Langdon stopped and looked through one of the holes.

Professor Robert Langdon had seen some strange things, but this was the strangest. He blinked, wondering if he was imagining things. He was staring into a huge round room. Inside the room, floating as if weightless, were people. Three of them. One waved and did a flip in the air.

My God, he thought. I’m in the land of Oz.

The room's floor was a mesh grid, like giant chicken wire. Underneath the grid was the blurry metal of a huge propeller.

“Free fall tube,” Kohler said, stopping to wait. “Indoor skydiving. For stress relief. It’s a vertical wind tunnel.”

Langdon watched in amazement. One of the free fallers, a large woman, moved toward the window. She was being pushed by the air but grinned and gave Langdon a thumbs-up. Langdon smiled slightly and returned it, wondering if she knew it was the old symbol for male power.

The heavy woman, Langdon noticed, was the only one wearing what looked like a tiny parachute. The piece of fabric puffed out over her like a toy. “What’s her little chute for?” Langdon asked Kohler. “It can’t be more than a yard wide.”

"Friction," Kohler said. "It slows her down so the fan can lift her." He started down the corridor again. "One square yard of drag will slow a falling body almost twenty percent."

Langdon nodded blankly.

He never suspected that later that night, in a country hundreds of miles away, the information would save his life.
