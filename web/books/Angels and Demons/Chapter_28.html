Angels and Demons

Chapter 28

Secretary Sylvie Baudeloque was now in a panic. She paced outside the director’s empty office. Where the hell is he? What do I do?

It had been a bizarre day. Of course, any day working for Maximilian Kohler could be strange, but Kohler was unusually strange today.

“Find me Leonardo Vetra!” he had demanded when Sylvie arrived this morning.

Sylvie did as she was told, paging, phoning, and E-mailing Leonardo Vetra.

Nothing.

So Kohler had left in a huff, apparently to find Vetra himself. When he came back a few hours later, Kohler looked definitely not well… not that he ever looked well , but he looked worse than usual. He locked himself in his office, and she could hear him on his modem, his phone, faxing, talking. Then Kohler left again. He hadn’t been back since.

Sylvie had decided to ignore his behavior as just another Kohler drama, but she began to worry when Kohler didn’t return in time for his daily injections. The director’s physical condition required regular treatment, and when he pushed it, the results were never good—respiratory shock, coughing fits, and a mad dash by the infirmary staff. Sometimes Sylvie thought Maximilian Kohler wanted to die.

She thought about paging him to remind him, but she’d learned Kohler’s pride hated charity. Last week, he had become so angry with a visiting scientist who had shown him too much pity that Kohler got to his feet and threw a clipboard at the man’s head. King Kohler could be surprisingly agile when he was pissй.

At the moment, however, Sylvie’s concern for the director’s health was less important, replaced by a more urgent problem. The CERN switchboard had phoned five minutes ago in a frenzy to say they had an urgent call for the director.

“He’s not available,” Sylvie had said.

Then the CERN operator told her who was calling.

Sylvie almost laughed. “You’re joking, right?” She listened, and her face filled with disbelief. “And your caller ID shows-” Sylvie frowned. “I see. Okay. Can you ask what the-” She sighed. “No. That’s fine. Tell him to hold. I’ll find the director right away. Yes, I understand. I’ll hurry.”

But Sylvie had not been able to find the director. She had called his cell phone three times and each time gotten the same message: “The mobile customer you are trying to reach is out of range.” Out of range? How far could he go? So Sylvie had dialed Kohler’s pager. Twice. No response. Very unlike him. She’d even E-mailed his mobile computer. Nothing. It was like the man had vanished.

So what do I do? she now wondered.

Unless she searched the entire CERN complex herself, Sylvie knew there was only one other way to get the director’s attention. He would not be happy, but the man on the phone was not someone the director should keep waiting. Nor did it sound like the caller would accept that the director was unavailable.

Surprised by her own daring, Sylvie made her decision. She walked into Kohler’s office and went to the metal box on the wall behind his desk. She opened the cover, looked at the controls, and found the right button.

Then she took a deep breath and grabbed the microphone.
