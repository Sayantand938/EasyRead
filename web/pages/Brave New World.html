Brave New World

Chapter One

A short, gray building with only thirty-four floors. Above the main entrance were the words, CENTRAL LONDON HATCHERY AND CONDITIONING CENTRE, and, on a shield, the World State's motto, COMMUNITY, IDENTITY, STABILITY.

The large room on the ground floor faced north. It was cold despite the summer outside the windows, and despite the tropical heat inside. A harsh, weak light shone through the windows, searching for some draped mannequin or pale, scholarly figure, but finding only glass, nickel, and the cold, shiny porcelain of a laboratory. The coldness outside matched the coldness inside. The workers wore white overalls, and their hands were covered with pale, corpse-colored rubber gloves. The light seemed frozen, dead, like a ghost. Only the yellow barrels of the microscopes gave it some rich, living substance, like butter spread along the polished tubes, thick streaks fading into the distance down the work tables.

"And this," said the Director, opening the door, "is the Fertilizing Room."

Bent over their instruments, three hundred Fertilizers were deeply absorbed as the Director of Hatcheries and Conditioning entered. The room was almost silent, filled only with the absent-minded humming or whistling of people completely focused on their work. A group of new students, very young, pink, and inexperienced, followed the Director nervously and humbly. Each carried a notebook, in which they frantically scribbled whenever the great man spoke. They were getting information straight from the source, a rare privilege. The D. H. C. for Central London always made a point of personally showing his new students around the various departments.

"Just to give you a general idea," he would explain. Of course, they needed some general idea to do their work intelligently, but as little as possible if they were to be good and happy members of society. Everyone knows that details lead to virtue and happiness, while general ideas are a necessary evil. Society is built on craftsmen and collectors, not philosophers.

"Tomorrow," he would add, smiling with a slightly threatening friendliness, "you'll start serious work. You won't have time for generalities then. But for now…"

For now, it was a privilege to get information straight from the source and write it down. The boys scribbled like crazy.

Tall, somewhat thin, but standing straight, the Director walked into the room. He had a long chin and large, somewhat prominent teeth, which were covered by his full, red lips when he wasn't talking. Old or young? Thirty? Fifty? Fifty-five? It was hard to tell. And it didn't matter anyway; in this year of stability, A. F. 632, no one thought to ask.

"I shall begin at the beginning," said the D.H.C., and the keenest students wrote down his intention: Begin at the beginning. "These," he gestured, "are the incubators." Opening an insulated door, he showed them and explained, "at blood heat; while the male cells," he opened another door, "must be kept at thirty-five instead of thirty-seven. Full blood heat makes them infertile." Rams in warm wraps have no lambs.

Still leaning against the incubators, he gave them a short description of the modern process of fertilization while the pencils scribbled quickly. He spoke first of its surgical start—"the operation done willingly for the good of society, plus a bonus of six months' pay." He then described how they keep the removed ovary alive and growing. He moved on to the best temperature, salt level, and thickness; mentioned the liquid where the separated, ripe eggs were kept. Leading them to the work tables, he showed how this liquid was drawn from the test tubes, dropped onto warmed microscope slides, and how the eggs were checked for problems, counted, and moved to a porous container. He then showed them how this container was put in a warm broth with free-swimming sperm—at least one hundred thousand per cubic centimeter, he stressed. After ten minutes, the container was lifted out and checked again. If any eggs weren't fertilized, it was put back in, maybe more than once. The fertilized eggs went back to the incubators. The Alphas and Betas stayed there until bottled, while the Gammas, Deltas, and Epsilons were taken out after thirty-six hours for Bokanovsky's Process.

"Bokanovsky's Process," the Director repeated, and the students underlined it.

One egg, one embryo, one normal adult. But a bokanovskified egg will bud, grow, divide. From eight to ninety-six buds, each becoming a perfect embryo, and each embryo a full-sized adult. Making ninety-six people grow where only one did before. Progress.

"Basically," the D.H.C. said, "bokanovskification is stopping development at certain points. We slow the normal growth, and the egg buds in response."

Buds in response. The pencils moved fast.

He pointed. A rack of test tubes moved slowly into a large metal box, and another rack came out. Machines hummed softly. It took eight minutes for the tubes to go through, he said. Eight minutes of strong X-rays is about all an egg can take. A few died; the hardiest split into two; most made four buds; some eight. All went back to the incubators, where the buds grew. After two days, they were suddenly chilled and stopped. Two, four, eight, the buds budded in turn. After budding, they were almost killed with alcohol, so they budded again. Once they had budded—bud out of bud—they were left to grow in peace, since more stopping was usually fatal. By then, the original egg could become anywhere from eight to ninety-six embryos—a huge improvement on nature. Identical twins—not just two or three like in the old days when an egg might split by accident, but dozens, scores at a time.

"Scores," the Director repeated, throwing out his arms as if giving gifts. "Scores."

But one student was foolish enough to ask what the advantage was.

"My good boy!" The Director turned on him. "Can't you see? Can't you see?" He raised a hand, looking serious. "Bokanovsky's Process is a key tool for social stability!"

Key tools for keeping society stable.

Typical men and women; all the same. A whole small factory staffed by people from a single divided egg.

"Ninety-six identical twins running ninety-six identical machines!" The voice shook with excitement. "You really know where you stand. For the first time ever." He said the world's motto. "Community, Identity, Stability." Important words. "If we could keep dividing eggs forever, all our problems would be solved."

Solved by standard Gammas, unchanging Deltas, uniform Epsilons. Millions of identical twins. Mass production finally used in biology.

"But, sadly," the Director said, shaking his head, "we can't divide eggs forever."

Ninety-six seemed to be the most; seventy-two was a good average. To make as many identical twins as possible from the same ovary and sperm–that was the best they could do (though not ideal). And even that was hard.

"Because in nature, it takes thirty years for two hundred eggs to mature. But we need to stabilize the population right now. Producing twins slowly over twenty-five years–what good would that do?"

Clearly, no good at all. But Podsnap's Technique had greatly sped up the maturing process. They could be sure of at least a hundred and fifty mature eggs in two years. Fertilize and divide them–multiply by seventy-two–and you get about eleven thousand brothers and sisters in a hundred and fifty groups of identical twins, all born within two years.

"And sometimes we can get over fifteen thousand adults from one ovary."

He waved over a young, fair-haired man who was walking by. "Mr. Foster," he called. The young man came over. "Can you tell us the record for a single ovary, Mr. Foster?"

"Sixteen thousand and twelve in this Centre," Mr. Foster answered quickly. He spoke fast, had bright blue eyes, and clearly loved quoting numbers. "Sixteen thousand and twelve; in one hundred and eighty-nine groups of identicals. But they've done even better," he continued excitedly, "in some of the tropical Centres. Singapore has often produced over sixteen thousand five hundred; and Mombasa has actually reached seventeen thousand. But they have advantages we don't. You should see how a black woman's ovary responds to pituitary! It's amazing, especially if you're used to working with European material. Still," he added, with a laugh (but his eyes showed he was ready to compete, and he raised his chin as if challenging them), "still, we plan to beat them if we can. I'm working on a great Delta-Minus ovary right now. Only eighteen months old. Over twelve thousand seven hundred children already, either born or in development. And it's still going strong. We'll beat them yet."

"That's the attitude I like!" the Director exclaimed, patting Mr. Foster on the shoulder. "Come with us, and share your knowledge with these students."

Mr. Foster smiled modestly. "Gladly." They left.

In the Bottling Room, everything was a smooth and busy operation. Pieces of fresh pig lining, cut to the right size, shot up in small elevators from the Organ Store in the basement. A quick whirr and then, click! the elevator doors popped open; the bottle-liner simply reached out, took the lining, put it in, smoothed it down, and before the lined bottle had moved too far along the endless belt, whirr, click! another piece of lining had shot up from below, ready to be placed into another bottle, the next in the slow, never-ending line on the belt. Next to the Liners were the Matriculators. The line moved forward; one by one, the eggs were moved from their test tubes to the larger containers; the peritoneal lining was carefully slit, the group of cells dropped in, the salt water poured in… and the bottle had already passed, and it was the labellers' turn. Heredity, date of fertilization, Bokanovsky Group number – details were copied from test tube to bottle. No longer unknown, but named, identified, the line moved slowly on; through an opening in the wall, slowly into the Social Predestination Room.

"Eighty-eight cubic metres of card-index," Mr. Foster said with pleasure, as they entered.

"Containing all the important information," the Director added.

"Updated every morning."

"And put in order every afternoon."

"Based on which they make their calculations."

"So many people, of this or that type," Mr. Foster said.

"Given out in these amounts."

"The best Pouring Speed at any moment."

"Any unexpected losses quickly replaced."

"Quickly," Mr. Foster repeated. "If you knew how much extra time I had to work after the last Japanese earthquake!" He laughed kindly and shook his head.

"The Predestinators send their numbers to the Fertilizers."

"Who give them the embryos they request."

"And the bottles come here to be predestined carefully."

"After that, they're sent down to the Embryo Store."

"Where we're going now."

And opening a door, Mr. Foster led the way down a staircase to the basement.

The temperature was still tropical. They went down into a growing darkness. Two doors and a passage with a double turn kept the cellar safe from any daylight.

"Embryos are like photograph film," said Mr. Foster jokingly, as he pushed open the second door. "They can only handle red light."

And indeed, the hot darkness into which the students now followed him was red, like the darkness you see when you close your eyes on a summer afternoon. The bulging sides of rows and rows and levels of bottles sparkled with countless rubies, and among the rubies moved the faint red figures of men and women with purple eyes and signs of lupus. The faint hum and rattle of machinery stirred the air.

"Give them some numbers, Mr. Foster," said the Director, who was tired of talking.

Mr. Foster was very happy to give them some numbers.

Two hundred and twenty metres long, two hundred wide, ten high. He pointed upwards. Like chickens drinking, the students looked up at the distant ceiling. Three levels of racks: ground floor, first level, second level.

The thin steel framework of level above level disappeared into the dark in every direction. Near them, three red figures were busy unloading large bottles from a moving staircase.

The escalator from the Social Predestination Room.

Each bottle could be put on one of fifteen racks, each rack, though you couldn't see it, was a conveyor moving at thirty-three and a third centimetres an hour. Two hundred and sixty-seven days at eight metres a day. Two thousand one hundred and thirty-six metres in total. One trip around the cellar at ground level, one on the first level, half on the second, and on the two hundred and sixty-seventh morning, daylight in the Decanting Room. Independent existence–so called.

"But in that time," Mr. Foster finished, "we've managed to do a lot to them. Oh, a great deal." He laughed knowingly and triumphantly.

"That's the spirit I like," said the Director again. "Let's walk around. You tell them everything, Mr. Foster."

Mr. Foster did as he was asked.

He told them about the growing embryo on its bed of tissue. He let them taste the rich blood substitute it fed on. He explained why it needed to be stimulated with certain chemicals. He told them about the extract from a part of the ovary. He showed them the jets that automatically injected it every twelve meters from zero to 2040. He spoke of the increasing doses of a hormone given during the final ninety-six meters. He described the artificial mother's blood flow in every bottle at Meter 112; showed them the blood substitute tank, the pump that kept the liquid moving over the placenta and through the artificial lung and filter. He mentioned the embryo's tendency to become anemic, and the large doses of animal stomach extract and young horse liver it needed as a result.

He showed them the simple way that all the embryos were shaken to get them used to movement during the last two meters of every eight. He hinted at the seriousness of being "decanted," or taken out of the bottle, and listed the precautions taken to reduce that shock by training the embryo. He told them about the test for sex near Meter 200. He explained the labeling system–a T for males, a circle for females, and a question mark for those made infertile.

"Because," said Mr. Foster, "fertility is mostly a problem. One fertile ovary in twelve hundred would be enough for us. But we want choices. And we need to be safe. So we allow thirty percent of the female embryos to develop normally. The others get a dose of male hormone every twenty-four meters for the rest of the time. As a result, they are born infertile, structurally normal but sterile. Guaranteed sterile. Which brings us," continued Mr. Foster, "out of simply copying nature and into the more interesting world of human invention."

He rubbed his hands. They didn't just hatch embryos; any cow could do that.

"We also decide their fate and train them. We decant our babies as social beings, as Alphas or Epsilons, future sewage workers or future…" He was going to say "future World controllers," but corrected himself and said "future Directors of Hatcheries" instead. The D.H.C. smiled at the compliment.

They were passing Meter 320 on Rack 11. A young Beta-Minus mechanic was working on the blood substitute pump of a passing bottle with a screwdriver and wrench. The hum of the motor deepened as he turned the nuts. Down, down… A final twist, a look at the counter, and he was done. He moved down the line and began on the next pump.

"Reducing the revolutions per minute," Mr. Foster explained. "The substitute goes around slower, so it passes through the lung less often, giving the embryo less oxygen. A lack of oxygen keeps an embryo from reaching its full potential." He rubbed his hands again.

"But why do you want to keep the embryo below par?" asked a naive student.

"Fool!" said the Director, breaking a long silence. "Hasn't it occurred to you that an Epsilon embryo needs an Epsilon environment as well as Epsilon genes?"

It clearly hadn't occurred to him. He looked completely confused.

"The lower the caste," said Mr. Foster, "the less oxygen." The first organ affected was the brain. After that, the skeleton. At seventy per cent of normal oxygen, you got dwarfs. At less than seventy, eyeless monsters.

"Who are no use at all," Mr. Foster concluded.

But (his voice became confidential and eager), if they could discover a way to shorten the time it takes to mature, what a triumph, what a gift to Society!

"Consider the horse."

They considered it.

Mature at six; the elephant at ten. While at thirteen, a man is not yet sexually mature; and is only fully grown at twenty. Hence, of course, that result of delayed development, human intelligence.

"But in Epsilons," said Mr. Foster very fairly, "we don't need human intelligence."

Didn't need it and didn't get it. But though the Epsilon mind was mature at ten, the Epsilon body was not fit to work till eighteen. Long years of unnecessary and wasted immaturity. If physical development could be sped up until it was as quick as a cow's, what an enormous saving to the Community!

"Amazing!" the students whispered. Mr. Foster's excitement was contagious.

He got quite technical, talking about the unusual hormone balance that made people grow so slowly. He suggested a genetic change to explain it. Could this genetic change be reversed? Could an Epsilon embryo be changed back to the normal growth rate of dogs and cows using a special method? That was the question. And they were close to solving it.

Pilkington, in Mombasa, had created people who were sexually mature at four and fully grown at six and a half. A scientific success. But not useful for society. Six-year-old men and women were too unintelligent to even do Epsilon work. And the process was all or nothing: either you changed nothing, or you changed everything. They were still trying to find the perfect balance between adults of twenty and adults of six. So far, they hadn't succeeded. Mr. Foster sighed and shook his head.

Their walk through the red light had taken them near Metre 170 on Rack 9. From this point, Rack 9 was enclosed, and the bottles continued their journey in a tunnel, broken up by openings every few metres.

"Heat conditioning," said Mr. Foster.

Hot tunnels followed cool tunnels. The coolness was combined with discomfort in the form of strong X-rays. By the time they were born, the embryos hated the cold. They were destined to move to the tropics, to be miners, acetate silk spinners, and steel workers. Later, their minds would be taught to agree with their bodies' preferences. "We train them to enjoy the heat," Mr. Foster concluded. "Our colleagues upstairs will teach them to love it."

"And that," the Director added importantly, "is the secret to happiness and good behavior – enjoying what you have to do. All conditioning aims for that: making people like the social role they can't escape."

In a space between two tunnels, a nurse was carefully inserting a long, thin syringe into the jelly-like contents of a passing bottle. The students and their guides watched her silently for a few moments.

"Well, Lenina," said Mr. Foster, when she finally pulled out the syringe and stood up straight.

The girl turned, startled. Despite the skin condition and her purple eyes, she was remarkably pretty.

"Henry!" Her smile flashed at him–a row of bright red teeth.

"Charming, charming," murmured the Director and, patting her a few times, received a respectful smile in return.

"What are you giving them?" asked Mr. Foster, his voice very professional.

"Oh, the usual typhoid and sleeping sickness."

"Tropical workers start getting shots at Meter 150," Mr. Foster told the students. "The embryos still have gills. We protect the fish from the future man's diseases." Then, turning back to Lenina, "Ten to five on the roof this afternoon," he said, "as usual."

"Charming," said the Director again, and, with a final pat, walked away after the others.

On Rack 10, rows of future chemical workers were being trained to handle lead, caustic soda, tar, chlorine. The first of a group of two hundred and fifty young rocket-plane engineers was passing the eleven hundred meter mark on Rack 3. A special device kept their containers turning. "To improve their balance," Mr. Foster explained. "Fixing a rocket outside in mid-air is tricky. We slow the blood flow when they're right side up, so they're hungry, and double the fake blood when they're upside down. They learn to connect being upside down with feeling good; they're only really happy when they're standing on their heads.

"And now," Mr. Foster went on, "I want to show you some interesting training for Alpha Plus Intellectuals. We have a big group of them on Rack 5. First Gallery level," he called to two boys who had started to go down to the ground floor.

"They're around Meter 900," he explained. "You can't really do any useful brain training until the fetuses have lost their tails. Follow me."

But the Director looked at his watch. "Ten to three," he said. "No time for the brainy embryos, I'm afraid. We must go up to the Nurseries before the children wake up from their afternoon nap." Mr. Foster was disappointed. "At least a quick look at the Decanting Room," he asked.

"Very well then." The Director smiled kindly. "Just one look."

Chapter Two

MR. FOSTER was left in the Decanting Room. The D.H.C. and his students entered the closest elevator and went up to the fifth floor.

BABY NURSERIES. NEO-PAVLOVIAN LEARNING ROOMS, the sign read.

The Director opened a door. They were in a large, empty room, very bright and sunny because the entire south wall was a single window. Six nurses, wearing the standard white viscose-linen uniform of pants and jackets, with their hair covered by white caps, were placing bowls of roses in a long line across the floor. Large bowls, filled with blossoms. Thousands of petals, fully open and silky, like the cheeks of countless little angels, but in that bright light, not just pink and Aryan angels, but also glowing Chinese, also Mexican, also red-faced from blowing heavenly trumpets, also pale as death, pale like white marble after death.

The nurses stood up straight when the D.H.C. entered.

"Put out the books," he said briefly.

The nurses silently did as he said. Between the bowls of roses, the books were placed–a line of nursery books open to a brightly colored picture of an animal, fish, or bird.

"Now bring in the children."

They hurried out of the room and returned in a minute or two, each pushing a tall cart with four wire shelves, each shelf holding eight-month-old babies, all identical (obviously a Bokanovsky Group) and all (since they were Delta caste) wearing khaki.

"Put them down on the floor."

The babies were taken out.

"Now turn them so they can see the flowers and books."

Turned, the babies immediately became quiet, then started to crawl towards the bright colors and cheerful shapes on the white pages. As they got closer, the sun came out from behind a cloud for a moment. The roses seemed to burst into flame with a sudden inner passion; the shining pages of the books seemed filled with new and deep meaning. The crawling babies made little squeals of excitement, gurgles and happy sounds.

The Director rubbed his hands. "Excellent!" he said. "It almost looks like it was planned."

The fastest crawlers had already reached their goal. Small hands reached out carefully, touched, grabbed, pulling petals off the bright roses, crumpling the colorful pages of the books. The Director waited until they were all happily busy. Then, "Watch carefully," he said. And, raising his hand, he gave the signal.

The Head Nurse, who was standing by a switchboard at the other end of the room, pressed down a small lever.

There was a loud explosion. A siren shrieked, louder and louder. Alarm bells rang like crazy.

The children jumped, screamed; their faces twisted with terror.

"And now," the Director shouted (because the noise was deafening), "now we'll drive the lesson home with a mild electric shock."

He waved his hand again, and the Head Nurse pressed a second lever. The babies' screaming suddenly changed. There was something desperate, almost insane, about the sharp, jerky yelps they now made. Their little bodies twitched and stiffened; their limbs moved jerkily as if pulled by unseen wires.

"We can electrify that whole strip of floor," the Director shouted to explain. "But that's enough," he signaled to the nurse.

The explosions stopped, the bells stopped ringing, the siren's shriek faded into silence. The stiffly twitching bodies relaxed, and what had been the sob and yelp of infant maniacs broadened out once more into a normal howl of ordinary terror.

"Offer them the flowers and the books again."

The nurses obeyed; but when the roses came near, at the mere sight of those brightly colored pictures of cats, roosters, and black sheep, the infants shrank back in horror, their howling suddenly increased.

"Observe," said the Director triumphantly, "observe."

Books and loud noises, flowers and electric shocks–already in the infant mind these pairs were damagingly linked; and after two hundred repetitions of the same or a similar lesson would be permanently joined. What man has joined, nature cannot separate.

"They'll grow up with what the psychologists used to call an 'instinctive' hatred of books and flowers. Reflexes that can't be changed. They'll be safe from books and botany all their lives." The Director turned to his nurses. "Take them away again."

Still yelling, the khaki babies were loaded onto their dumbwaiters and wheeled out, leaving behind them the smell of sour milk and a welcome silence.

One of the students raised his hand; and though he understood why lower-class people shouldn't waste the Community's time on books, and that there was always the risk of them reading something that might negatively change one of their reflexes, yet … well, he couldn't understand about the flowers. Why bother making it psychologically impossible for Deltas to like flowers?

The D.H.C. explained patiently. Making the children scream at the sight of a rose was based on important economic strategy. Not long ago (about a century), Gammas, Deltas, and even Epsilons were taught to like flowers and nature in general. The goal was to make them want to go to the countryside whenever possible, forcing them to use transportation.

"And didn't they use transportation?" the student asked.

"Quite a bit," the D.H.C. replied. "But nothing else."

He explained that primroses and landscapes have a major flaw: they are free. A love for nature doesn't keep factories busy. So, they decided to get rid of the love of nature, at least among the lower classes, but not the need to use transportation. It was important for them to keep going to the countryside, even if they hated it. The challenge was to find a better economic reason for using transportation than just liking primroses and landscapes. They found it.

"We teach the masses to hate the countryside," the Director concluded. "But we also teach them to love all country sports. At the same time, we make sure that all country sports require a lot of equipment. So, they use manufactured goods as well as transportation. That's why we use electric shocks."

"I see," said the student, quietly admiring.

There was a silence. Then, clearing his throat, "Once upon a time," the Director began, "when Ford was still alive, there was a little boy named Reuben Rabinovitch. Reuben's parents spoke Polish."

The Director stopped himself. "You know what Polish is, right?"

"A dead language."

"Like French and German," another student added, showing off.

"And 'parent'?" the D.H.C. asked.

There was an uneasy silence. Several of the boys blushed. They hadn't yet learned to tell the important but often subtle difference between dirty talk and pure science. Finally, one had the courage to raise a hand.

"Human beings used to be …" he hesitated; his face turned red. "Well, they used to give birth to live babies."

"Quite right." The Director nodded approvingly.

"And when the babies were decanted …"

"'Born,'" came the correction.

"Well, then they were the parents–I mean, not the babies, of course; the other ones." The poor boy was very confused.

"In short," the Director summed up, "the parents were the father and the mother." The dirty talk that was actually science landed heavily in the boys' silent, averted gazes. "Mother," he repeated loudly, emphasizing the science; and, leaning back in his chair, "These," he said seriously, "are unpleasant facts; I know it. But most facts from history are unpleasant."

He returned to Little Reuben–to Little Reuben, in whose room, one evening, by accident, his father and mother (crash, crash!) happened to leave the radio on.

("Remember that in those days of crude live birth, children were always raised by their parents and not in State Conditioning Centers.")

While the child slept, a broadcast from London suddenly came on; and the next morning, to the surprise of his parents (the bolder boys dared to grin at each other), Little Reuben woke up repeating word for word a long lecture by that strange old writer ("one of the few whose works have survived"), George Bernard Shaw, who was, according to a reliable story, talking about his own genius. To Little Reuben's parents, this lecture was, of course, completely meaningless, and thinking their child had suddenly gone crazy, they called a doctor. He, luckily, understood English, recognized the speech as the one Shaw had broadcast the night before, realized what it meant, and wrote a letter to the medical journal about it.

"The idea of sleep-teaching, or hypnopaedia, had been discovered." The D.H.C. paused impressively.

The idea had been discovered, but many years would pass before it was put to good use.

"Little Reuben's case happened only twenty-three years after Ford's first Model T came out." (Here the Director made the sign of the T on his stomach, and all the students respectfully copied him.) "And yet…"

The students scribbled furiously. "Hypnopaedia, first officially used in A.F. 214. Why not sooner? Two reasons. (a)…"

"These early researchers," the D.H.C. said, "were on the wrong path. They believed hypnopaedia could be used for intellectual education…"

(A small boy asleep on his side, his right arm out, his hand hanging over the edge of the bed. Through a round opening in the side of a box, a voice speaks softly.

"The Nile is the longest river in Africa and the second longest in the world. Although it is shorter than the Mississippi-Missouri, the Nile's basin is the largest, stretching across 35 degrees of latitude…"

At breakfast the next morning, "Tommy," someone asks, "do you know which is the longest river in Africa?" He shakes his head. "But don't you remember something that starts: The Nile is the…"

"The - Nile - is - the - longest - river - in - Africa - and - the - second - longest - in - the - world…" The words spill out. "Although - it - is - shorter - than…"

"Well now, which is the longest river in Africa?"

The eyes are blank. "I don't know."

"But the Nile, Tommy."

"The - Nile - is - the - longest - river - in - Africa - and - second …"

"Then which river is the longest, Tommy?"

Tommy burst into tears. "I don't know," he howls.)

That howl, the Director explained, discouraged the first researchers. The experiments stopped. No more attempts were made to teach children the length of the Nile while they slept. And rightly so. You can't learn a science if you don't know what it's about.

"But, if they had only started with moral education," said the Director, heading towards the door. The students followed, writing quickly as they walked and all the way up in the lift. "Moral education, which should never be rational, no matter what."

"Silence, silence," whispered a loudspeaker as they stepped out on the fourteenth floor, and "Silence, silence," the trumpet mouths tirelessly repeated down every corridor. The students and even the Director rose onto their toes without thinking. They were Alphas, of course, but even Alphas had been well trained. "Silence, silence." All the air on the fourteenth floor hissed with the strict command. After tiptoeing for fifty yards, they reached a door that the Director carefully opened. They stepped into the dim light of a darkened dormitory. Eighty cots stood in a row against the wall. There was a sound of light, steady breathing and a constant murmur, like very quiet voices whispering far away.

A nurse stood up as they entered and stood straight before the Director.

"What's the lesson this afternoon?" he asked.

"We had Basic Sex for the first forty minutes," she answered. "But now it's switched over to Basic Class Awareness."

The Director walked slowly down the long line of cots. Rosy and relaxed from sleep, eighty little boys and girls lay softly breathing. There was a whisper under every pillow. The D.H.C. stopped and, bending over one of the little beds, listened closely.

"Basic Class Awareness, did you say? Let's have it repeated a little louder by the trumpet."

At the end of the room a loud speaker stuck out from the wall. The Director walked up to it and pressed a switch.

"… all wear green," said a soft but very clear voice, starting in the middle of a sentence, "and Delta Children wear khaki. Oh no, I don't want to play with Delta children. And Epsilons are even worse. They're too stupid to read or write. Besides they wear black, which is such a horrible colour. I'm so glad I'm a Beta."

They took their seats in the plane and took off. Ten minutes later, they were crossing the border that separated civilization from savagery. Up and down hills, across deserts of salt or sand, through forests, into the deep violet of canyons, over rocks and peaks and flat-topped mesas, the fence stretched endlessly, a straight line, a perfect symbol of human success. And at its base, here and there, a collection of white bones, an unrotted body dark on the tan ground marked the spot where deer or steer, puma or porcupine or coyote, or the vultures drawn by the smell of decay, had been killed as if by fate, had gotten too close to the deadly wires.

"They never learn," said the pilot in green, pointing down at the skeletons below. "And they never will learn," he added, laughing as if he had personally defeated the electrocuted animals.

Bernard laughed too; after two grammes of soma, the joke seemed funny for some reason. He laughed and then quickly fell asleep, and slept as they flew over Taos and Tesuque; over Nambe and Picuris and Pojoaque, over Sia and Cochiti, over Laguna and Acoma and the Enchanted Mesa, over Zuñi and Cibola and Ojo Caliente, and finally woke up to find the plane on the ground, Lenina carrying the suitcases into a small square house, and the light-skinned man in Gamma-green talking in a strange language with a young Indian.

"Malpais," the pilot explained as Bernard got out. "This is the rest-house. And there's a dance this afternoon at the pueblo. He'll take you there." He pointed to the gloomy young savage. "Funny, I bet." He grinned. "Everything they do is funny." With that, he climbed into the plane and started the engines. "Back tomorrow. And remember," he added to Lenina, "they're perfectly safe; savages won't hurt you. They know enough about gas bombs to not cause trouble." Still laughing, he started the helicopter blades, sped up, and left.

Chapter Seven

THE MESA was like a ship stuck in a sea of orange dust. The channel curved between steep banks, and a green strip crossed the valley from one side to the other—the river and its fields. On the edge of that stone ship, a shaped and precise piece of bare rock, stood the village of Malpais. Block on top of block, each level smaller than the one below, the tall houses rose like stepped pyramids into the blue sky. At their base were scattered low buildings, a maze of walls; and on three sides, the cliffs dropped straight down into the plain. A few columns of smoke rose straight up into the still air and disappeared.

"Queer," said Lenina. "Very queer." It was her usual way to say she didn't like something. "I don't like it. And I don't like that man." She pointed to the Indian guide who was going to take them up to the village. He seemed to feel the same way about her; even the way he walked in front of them seemed unfriendly and scornful.

"Besides," she said quietly, "he smells."

Bernard didn't argue. They walked on.

Suddenly, it felt like the air itself was alive, pulsing with the steady beat of blood. Up in Malpais, the drums were playing. Their feet matched the rhythm of that mysterious heart, and they walked faster. The path led them to the bottom of the cliff. The sides of the huge mesa towered over them, three hundred feet to the top.

"I wish we could have brought the plane," said Lenina, looking up at the steep rock face. "I hate walking. And you feel so small when you're on the ground at the bottom of a hill."

They walked along in the shadow of the mesa, went around a corner, and there, in a worn gully, was the way up. They climbed. The path was very steep, zigzagging up the gully. Sometimes the drums were barely audible, and other times they seemed to be just around the corner.

Halfway up, an eagle flew so close that they felt the wind from its wings on their faces. In a crack in the rock, there was a pile of bones. It was all strangely disturbing, and the Indian smelled stronger and stronger. Finally, they came out of the gully into the sunlight. The top of the mesa was a flat stone surface.

"Like the Charing-T Tower," Lenina said. But she didn't get to enjoy this comforting similarity for long. The sound of soft footsteps made them turn around. Naked from the neck to the stomach, their dark brown bodies painted with white lines ("like asphalt tennis courts," Lenina would later say), their faces made strange with red, black, and yellow paint, two Indians came running down the path. Their black hair was braided with fox fur and red cloth. Cloaks of turkey feathers fluttered from their shoulders; huge feather headdresses stuck out around their heads. With every step, their silver bracelets and heavy necklaces of bone and turquoise beads clinked and rattled. They came on without a word, running silently in their deerskin shoes. One of them held a feather brush; the other carried what looked like thick ropes. One of the ropes moved, and Lenina saw that they were snakes.

The men came closer; their dark eyes looked at her, but without any sign they knew her, not even the smallest sign that they had seen her or knew she was there. The limp snake hung still with the others. The men passed. "I don't like it," said Lenina. "I don't like it."

She liked even less what was waiting for her at the entrance to the pueblo, where their guide had left them to get instructions. The dirt, for one thing, the piles of trash, the dust, the dogs, the flies. Her face twisted in disgust. She held her handkerchief to her nose.

"But how can they live like this?" she burst out, her voice full of angry disbelief. (It seemed impossible.)

Bernard shrugged. "Well," he said, "they've been doing it for five or six thousand years. So I guess they're used to it by now."

"But cleanliness is next to Fordliness," she insisted.

"Yes, and civilization is sterilization," Bernard went on, ironically finishing the second sleep-teaching lesson in basic hygiene. "But these people have never heard of Our Ford, and they're not civilized. So there's no point in…"

"Oh!" She grabbed his arm. "Look."

An almost naked Indian was slowly climbing down the ladder from the second-story terrace of a nearby house–one rung at a time, with the shaky care of someone very old. His face was deeply wrinkled and black, like a mask of obsidian. His toothless mouth had sunk in. At the corners of his lips, and on each side of his chin, a few long, almost white hairs shone against his dark skin. His long, loose hair hung down in gray strands around his face. His body was bent and thin, almost skin and bones. Slowly he came down, pausing on each rung before trying the next step.

"What's wrong with him?" whispered Lenina. Her eyes were wide with horror and surprise.

"He's just old," Bernard answered, trying to sound casual. He was also startled, but he tried to seem calm.

"Old?" she asked again. "But the Director's old; lots of people are old; they're not like that."

"That's because we don't let them be like that. We protect them from diseases. We keep their hormones balanced like young people's. We don't let their magnesium-calcium levels drop below what they were at thirty. We give them blood from young people. We keep their bodies working hard all the time. So, of course, they don't look like that. Partly," he added, "because most of them die long before they get as old as this creature. Young until sixty, and then, snap! it's over."

But Lenina wasn't listening. She was watching the old man. Slowly, he came down. His feet touched the ground. He turned. His eyes, deep in their sockets, were still very bright. They looked at her for a long moment without expression, without surprise, as if she wasn't there. Then slowly, with his back bent, the old man walked past them and was gone.

"But it's terrible," Lenina whispered. "It's awful. We shouldn't have come here." She felt in her pocket for her soma–only to find that, somehow, she had left the bottle at the rest-house. Bernard's pockets were also empty.

Lenina had to face the horrors of Malpais alone. They came at her quickly. Seeing two young women breastfeeding their babies made her blush and look away. She had never seen anything so indecent. And what made it worse was that, instead of ignoring it, Bernard started making comments about this disgusting way of giving birth. Ashamed, now that the soma had worn off, of how weak he had been that morning in the hotel, he tried to act strong and different.

"What a wonderfully close relationship," he said, trying to shock her. "And what strong feelings it must create! I often think I've missed something by not having a mother. And maybe you've missed something by not being a mother, Lenina. Imagine sitting there with your own baby. …"

"Bernard! How can you?" An old woman with an eye disease and a skin problem distracted her from her anger.

"Let's go," she begged. "I don't like it."

But just then their guide came back and, waving them to follow, led them down the narrow street between the houses. They turned a corner. A dead dog was lying on a pile of trash; a woman with a growth on her neck was looking for lice in a little girl's hair. Their guide stopped at a ladder, pointed up, then pointed forward. They did what he told them–climbed the ladder and walked through the doorway into a long, narrow room, dark and smelling of smoke and cooked grease and clothes that had been worn and not washed for a long time. At the far end of the room was another doorway, letting in some light and the very loud sound of drums.

They stepped through the doorway and found themselves on a wide terrace. Below them, surrounded by the tall houses, was the village square, full of Indians. Bright blankets, feathers in black hair, shiny turquoise stones, and dark skin glowing with heat. Lenina put her handkerchief to her nose again. In the middle of the square were two round platforms made of stone and dirt–the roofs of underground rooms, it seemed; in the center of each platform was an open hole, with a ladder coming up from the darkness below. The sound of flutes playing underground came up and was almost lost in the steady, constant beat of the drums.

Lenina liked the drums. Shutting her eyes, she lost herself in their soft, repeated thunder, letting it fill her mind more and more, until there was nothing left but that one deep pulse of sound. It reminded her of the comforting synthetic noises at Solidarity Services and Ford's Day celebrations. "Orgy-porgy," she whispered. These drums beat out the same rhythms.

There was a sudden burst of singing–hundreds of men shouting loudly in a harsh, metallic voice. A few long notes and silence, the thunderous silence of the drums; then shrill, in a neighing treble, the women's answer. Then again the drums; and once more the men's deep, wild claim to their manhood.

Strange–yes. The place was strange, so was the music, so were the clothes and the goiters and the skin diseases and the old people. But the performance itself–there seemed to be nothing especially strange about that.

"It reminds me of a lower-class Community Sing," she told Bernard.

But a little later, it reminded her less of that harmless event. Suddenly, a horrible group of monsters swarmed up from those round underground rooms. Hideously masked or painted to look inhuman, they stomped out a strange, limping dance around the square; round and round, singing as they went, round and round–each time a little faster; and the drums changed and quickened their rhythm, so it became like the throbbing of fever in the ears; and the crowd began to sing with the dancers, louder and louder; and first one woman shrieked, and then another and another, as if they were being killed; and then suddenly the leader of the dancers broke from the line, ran to a big wooden chest at one end of the square, raised the lid, and pulled out two black snakes. A great yell went up from the crowd, and all the other dancers ran toward him with outstretched hands. He tossed the snakes to the first ones there, then dipped back into the chest for more. More and more black snakes and brown and spotted–he threw them out. And then the dance began again with a different rhythm. Round and round they went with their snakes, snakelike, with a soft, swaying movement at the knees and hips. Round and round. Then the leader gave a signal, and one by one, all the snakes were thrown down in the middle of the square; an old man came up from underground and sprinkled them with cornmeal, and from the other opening came a woman and sprinkled them with water from a black jar. Then the old man lifted his hand, and suddenly, terrifyingly, there was complete silence. The drums stopped, life seemed to have ended. The old man pointed toward the two openings that led to the lower world. And slowly, raised by unseen hands from below, a painted image of an eagle emerged from one, and from the other, an image of a man, naked, and nailed to a cross. They hung there, seemingly floating, as though watching. The old man clapped his hands. Naked except for a white cotton cloth, a boy of about eighteen stepped out of the crowd and stood before him, his hands crossed over his chest, his head bowed. The old man made the sign of the cross over him and turned away. Slowly, the boy began to walk around the writhing pile of snakes. He had finished the first circle and was halfway through the second when, from among the dancers, a tall man wearing a coyote mask and holding a whip of braided leather, walked toward him. The boy moved on as though unaware of the other's presence. The coyote-man raised his whip, there was a long moment of waiting, then a quick movement, the whistle of the lash, and its loud, flat impact on the flesh. The boy's body trembled, but he made no sound, he walked on at the same slow, steady pace. The coyote struck again, again; and with every blow, first a gasp, and then a deep groan went up from the crowd. The boy walked. Twice, three times, four times around he went. The blood was flowing. Five times around, six times around. Suddenly, Lenina covered her face with her hands and began to sob. "Oh, stop them, stop them!" she begged. But the whip fell and fell without stopping. Seven times around. Then all at once the boy staggered and, still without a sound, fell forward on his face. Bending over him, the old man touched his back with a long white feather, held it up for a moment, crimson, for the people to see, then shook it three times over the snakes. A few drops fell, and suddenly the drums burst out again into a rush of hurried notes; there was a great shout. The dancers rushed forward, picked up the snakes, and ran out of the square. Men, women, children, all the crowd ran after them. A minute later, the square was empty, only the boy remained, lying where he had fallen, quite still. Three old women came out of one of the houses, and with some difficulty, lifted him and carried him in. The eagle and the man on the cross stood guard for a little while over the empty village; then, as though they had seen enough, slowly sank down through their openings, out of sight, into the underworld.

Lenina was still sobbing. "Too awful," she kept repeating, and all Bernard's comforts were useless. "Too awful! That blood!" She shuddered. "Oh, I wish I had my soma."

There was the sound of feet in the inner room.

Lenina did not move, but sat with her face in her hands, unseeing, alone. Only Bernard turned around.

The dress of the young man who now stepped out onto the terrace was Indian, but his braided hair was straw-colored, his eyes a pale blue, and his skin white, but tanned.

"Hello. Good morning," said the stranger, in perfect but strange English. "You're civilized, aren't you? You come from the Other Place, outside the Reservation?"

"Who on earth … ?" Bernard began, surprised.

The young man sighed and shook his head. "A very sad man." And, pointing to the bloodstains in the center of the square, "Do you see that awful spot?" he asked, his voice full of emotion. "A gram is better than a damn," said Lenina automatically, hiding her face. "I wish I had my soma!"

"I should have been there," the young man continued. "Why wouldn't they let me be the sacrifice? I would have gone around ten times–twelve, fifteen. Palowhtiwa only got to seven. They could have had twice as much blood from me. The oceans turning red." He threw out his arms dramatically; then, sadly, let them drop again. "But they wouldn't let me. They didn't like me because of my skin. It's always been like that. Always." Tears welled up in his eyes; he was embarrassed and turned away.

Surprise made Lenina forget about wanting soma. She lowered her hands and, for the first time, looked at the stranger. "Do you mean you wanted to be hit with that whip?"

Still turned away, the young man nodded. "For the good of the village–to make the rain come and the corn grow. And to please Pookong and Jesus. And to show that I can take pain without crying. Yes," and his voice suddenly became strong, he turned, standing up straight, his chin raised proudly, "to show that I'm a man … Oh!" He gasped and stopped talking, staring. He had seen, for the first time in his life, a girl whose cheeks weren't chocolate or tan, whose hair was auburn and always wavy, and whose expression (so new to him!) was one of kind interest. Lenina was smiling at him; he's a nice-looking boy, she thought, and has a really beautiful body. The blood rushed to the young man's face; he looked down, then up again for a moment, only to find her still smiling at him. He was so overwhelmed that he had to turn away and pretend to be looking very closely at something on the other side of the square.

Bernard's questions changed the subject. Who? How? When? From where? Keeping his eyes on Bernard's face (because he wanted so much to see Lenina smiling that he couldn't look at her), the young man tried to explain. Linda and he–Linda was his mother (the word made Lenina uncomfortable)–were strangers in the Reservation. Linda had come from the Other Place long ago, before he was born, with a man who was his father. (Bernard perked up his ears.) She had gone walking alone in those mountains to the North, had fallen down a steep place and hurt her head. ("Go on, go on," said Bernard excitedly.) Some hunters from Malpais had found her and brought her to the village. As for the man who was his father, Linda had never seen him again. His name was Tomakin. (Yes, "Thomas" was the D.H.C.'s first name.) He must have flown away, back to the Other Place, without her–a bad, unkind, unnatural man.

"And so I was born in Malpais," he finished. "In Malpais." And he shook his head.

The filth of that little house on the edge of the village!

A space of dust and trash separated it from the village. Two starving dogs were digging disgustingly in the garbage at its door. Inside, when they entered, the dim light smelled bad and buzzed with flies.

"Linda!" the young man called.

From the inner room a rather rough female voice said, "Coming."

They waited. In bowls on the floor were the remains of a meal, maybe several meals.

The door opened. A very fat blonde Native American woman stepped out and stood looking at the strangers staring in disbelief, her mouth open. Lenina noticed with disgust that two of her front teeth were missing. And the color of the ones that were left… She shuddered. It was worse than the old man. So fat. And all the lines in her face, the flabbiness, the wrinkles. And the sagging cheeks, with those purplish spots. And the red veins on her nose, the bloodshot eyes. And that neck–that neck; and the blanket she wore over her head–ragged and filthy. And under the brown sack-shaped tunic those huge breasts, the bulge of the stomach, the hips. Oh, much worse than the old man, much worse! And suddenly the creature burst out talking quickly, rushed at her with outstretched arms and–Ford! Ford! it was too disgusting, in another moment she'd be sick–pressed her against the bulge, the bosom, and began to kiss her. Ford! to kiss, slobberingly, and smelled too horrible, obviously never had a bath, and simply reeked of that awful stuff that was put into Delta and Epsilon bottles (no, it wasn't true about Bernard), positively stank of alcohol. She broke away as quickly as she could.

A blubbered and distorted face confronted her; the creature was crying.

"Oh, my dear, my dear." The flood of words flowed with sobs. "If you knew how glad–after all these years! A civilized face. Yes, and civilized clothes. Because I thought I should never see a piece of real acetate silk again." She touched the sleeve of Lenina's shirt. The nails were black. "And those adorable viscose velveteen shorts! Do you know, dear, I've still got my old clothes, the ones I came in, put away in a box. I'll show them you afterwards. Though, of course, the acetate has all gone into holes. But such a lovely white bandolier–though I must say your green morocco is even lovelier. Not that it did me much good, that bandolier." Her tears began to flow again. "I suppose John told you. What I had to suffer–and not a gram of soma to be had. Only a drink of mescal every now and then, when Popé used to bring it. Popé is a boy I used to know. But it makes you feel so bad afterwards. the mescal does, and you're sick with the peyotl; besides it always made that awful feeling of being ashamed much worse the next day. And I was so ashamed. Just think of it: me, a Beta–having a baby: put yourself in my place." (The mere suggestion made Lenina shudder.) "Though it wasn't my fault, I swear; because I still don't know how it happened, seeing that I did all the Malthusian Drill–you know, by numbers, One, two, three, four, always, I swear it; but all the same it happened, and of course there wasn't anything like an Abortion Center here. Is it still down in Chelsea, by the way?" she asked. Lenina nodded. "And still floodlighted on Tuesdays and Fridays?" Lenina nodded again. "That lovely pink glass tower!" Poor Linda lifted her face and with closed eyes happily imagined the bright remembered image. "And the river at night," she whispered. Great tears slowly came out from behind her tight-shut eyelids. "And flying back in the evening from Stoke Poges. And then a hot bath and vibro-vacuum massage… But there." She drew a deep breath, shook her head, opened her eyes again, sniffed once or twice, then blew her nose on her fingers and wiped them on the skirt of her tunic. "Oh, I'm so sorry," she said in response to Lenina's involuntary grimace of disgust. "I oughtn't to have done that. I'm sorry. But what are you to do when there aren't any handkerchiefs? I remember how it used to upset me, all that dirt, and nothing being sterile. I had an awful cut on my head when they first brought me here. You can't imagine what they used to put on it. Filth, just filth. 'Civilization is Sterilization,' I used to say to them. And 'Streptocock-Gee to Banbury-T, to see a fine bathroom and W.C.' as though they were children. But of course they didn't understand. How should they? And in the end I suppose I got used to it. And anyhow, how can you keep things clean when there isn't hot water laid on? And look at these clothes. This awful wool isn't like acetate. It lasts and lasts. And you're supposed to mend it if it gets torn. But I'm a Beta; I worked in the Fertilizing Room; nobody ever taught me to do anything like that. It wasn't my business. Besides, it never used to be right to mend clothes. Throw them away when they've got holes in them and buy new. 'The more stiches, the less riches.' Isn't that right? Mending's anti-social. But it's all different here. It's like living with lunatics. Everything they do is mad." She looked round; saw John and Bernard had left them and were walking up and down in the dust and garbage outside the house; but, nonetheless quietly lowering her voice, and leaning, while Lenina stiffened and shrank, so close that the blown reek of embryo-poison stirred the hair on her cheek. "For instance," she hoarsely whispered, "take the way they have one another here. Mad, I tell you, absolutely mad. Everybody belongs to every one else–don't they? don't they?" she insisted, tugging at Lenina's sleeve. Lenina nodded her averted head, let out the breath she had been holding and managed to draw another one, relatively untainted. "Well, here," the other went on, "nobody's supposed to belong to more than one person. And if you have people in the ordinary way, the others think you're wicked and anti-social. They hate and despise you. Once a lot of women came and made a scene because their men came to see me. Well, why not? And then they rushed at me… No, it was too awful. I can't tell you about it." Linda covered her face with her hands and shuddered. "They're so hateful, the women here. Mad, mad and cruel. And of course they don't know anything about Malthusian Drill, or bottles, or decanting, or anything of that sort. So they're having children all the time–like dogs. It's too revolting. And to think that I… Oh, Ford, Ford, Ford! And yet John was a great comfort to me. I don't know what I should have done without him. Even though he did get so upset whenever a man… Quite as a tiny boy, even. Once (but that was when he was bigger) he tried to kill poor Waihusiwa–or was it Popé?–just because I used to have them sometimes. Because I never could make him understand that that was what civilized people ought to do. Being mad's infectious I believe. Anyhow, John seems to have caught it from the Indians. Because, of course, he was with them a lot. Even though they always were so awful to him and wouldn't let him do all the things the other boys did. Which was a good thing in a way, because it made it easier for me to condition him a little. Though you've no idea how difficult that is. There's so much one doesn't know; it wasn't my business to know. I mean, when a child asks you how a helicopter works or who made the world–well, what are you to answer if you're a Beta and have always worked in the Fertilizing Room? What are you to answer?"

Chapter Eight

OUTSIDE, in the dust and among the garbage (there were four dogs now), Bernard and John were walking slowly up and down.

"So hard for me to realize," Bernard was saying, "to understand. As though we were living on different planets, in different centuries. A mother, and all this dirt, and gods, and old age, and disease…" He shook his head. "It's almost unbelievable. I shall never understand, unless you explain."

"Explain what?"

"This." He pointed to the pueblo. "That." And it was the little house outside the village. "Everything. All your life."

"But what can I say?"

"From the start. As far back as you can remember."

"As far back as I can remember." John frowned. There was a long silence.

It was very hot. They had eaten a lot of tortillas and sweet corn. Linda said, "Come and lie down, Baby." They lay down together in the big bed. "Sing," and Linda sang. Sang "Streptocock-Gee to Banbury-T" and "Bye Baby Banting, soon you'll need decanting." Her voice got softer and softer…

There was a loud noise, and he woke with a start. A man was saying something to Linda, and Linda was laughing. She had pulled the blanket up to her chin, but the man pulled it down again. His hair was like two black ropes, and round his arm was a lovely silver bracelet with blue stones in it. He liked the bracelet; but all the same, he was frightened; he hid his face against Linda's body. Linda put her hand on him and he felt safer. In the other words he did not understand so well, she said to the man, "Not with John here." The man looked at him, then again at Linda, and said a few words in a soft voice. Linda said, "No." But the man bent over the bed towards him and his face was huge, terrible; the black ropes of hair touched the blanket. "No," Linda said again, and he felt her hand squeezing him more tightly. "No, no!" But the man took hold of one of his arms, and it hurt. He screamed. The man put up his other hand and lifted him up. Linda was still holding him, still saying, "No, no." The man said something short and angry, and suddenly her hands were gone. "Linda, Linda." He kicked and wriggled; but the man carried him across to the door, opened it, put him down on the floor in the middle of the other room, and went away, shutting the door behind him. He got up, he ran to the door. Standing on tiptoe he could just reach the big wooden latch. He lifted it and pushed; but the door wouldn't open. "Linda," he shouted. She didn't answer.

He remembered a huge room, rather dark; and there were big wooden things with strings fastened to them, and lots of women standing round them–making blankets, Linda said. Linda told him to sit in the corner with the other children, while she went and helped the women. He played with the little boys for a long time. Suddenly people started talking very loud, and there were the women pushing Linda away, and Linda was crying. She went to the door and he ran after her. He asked her why they were angry. "Because I broke something," she said. And then she got angry too. "How should I know how to do their awful weaving?" she said. "Awful savages." He asked her what savages were. When they got back to their house, Popé was waiting at the door, and he came in with them. He had a big gourd full of stuff that looked like water; only it wasn't water, but something with a bad smell that burnt your mouth and made you cough. Linda drank some and Popé drank some, and then Linda laughed a lot and talked very loud; and then she and Popé went into the other room. When Popé went away, he went into the room. Linda was in bed and so fast asleep that he couldn't wake her.

Popé used to come often. He said the stuff in the gourd was called mescal; but Linda said it ought to be called soma; only it made you feel ill afterwards. He hated Popé. He hated them all–all the men who came to see Linda. One afternoon, when he had been playing with the other children–it was cold, he remembered, and there was snow on the mountains–he came back to the house and heard angry voices in the bedroom. They were women's voices, and they said words he didn't understand, but he knew they were dreadful words. Then suddenly, crash! something was upset; he heard people moving about quickly, and there was another crash and then a noise like hitting a mule, only not so bony; then Linda screamed. "Oh, don't, don't, don't!" she said. He ran in. There were three women in dark blankets. Linda was on the bed. One of the women was holding her wrists. Another was lying across her legs, so that she couldn't kick. The third was hitting her with a whip. Once, twice, three times; and each time Linda screamed. Crying, he tugged at the edge of the woman's blanket. "Please, please." With her free hand she held him away. The whip came down again, and again Linda screamed. He caught hold of the woman's enormous brown hand between his own and bit it with all his might. She cried out, pulled her hand free, and gave him such a push that he fell down. While he was lying on the ground she hit him three times with the whip. It hurt more than anything he had ever felt–like fire. The whip whistled again, fell. But this time it was Linda who screamed.

"But why did they want to hurt you, Linda?" he asked that night. He was crying, because the red marks of the whip on his back still hurt so terribly. But he was also crying because people were so awful and unfair, and because he was only a little boy and couldn't do anything against them. Linda was crying too. She was grown up, but she wasn't big enough to fight against three of them. It wasn't fair for her either. "Why did they want to hurt you, Linda?"

"I don't know. How should I know?" It was difficult to hear what she said, because she was lying on her stomach and her face was in the pillow. "They say those men are their men," she went on; and she did not seem to be talking to him at all; she seemed to be talking with someone inside herself. A long talk which she didn't understand; and in the end she started crying louder than ever.

"Oh, don't cry, Linda. Don't cry."

He held her close. He put his arm around her neck. Linda cried out. "Oh, be careful. My shoulder! Oh!" and she pushed him away, hard. His head hit the wall. "Little idiot!" she shouted; and then, suddenly, she started to slap him. Slap, slap …

"Linda," he cried out. "Oh, mother, don't!"

"I'm not your mother. I won't be your mother."

"But, Linda … Oh!" She slapped him on the cheek.

"Turned into a wild thing," she shouted. "Having young ones like an animal … If it hadn't been for you, I might have gone to the Inspector, I might have gotten away. But not with a baby. That would have been too shameful."

He saw that she was going to hit him again, and lifted his arm to protect his face. "Oh, don't, Linda, please don't."

"Little beast!" She pulled down his arm; his face was open.

"Don't, Linda." He shut his eyes, waiting for the hit.

But she didn't hit him. After a little while, he opened his eyes again and saw that she was looking at him. He tried to smile at her. Suddenly she put her arms around him and kissed him again and again.

Sometimes, for days, Linda didn't get up at all. She lay in bed and was sad. Or else she drank the stuff that Popé brought and laughed a lot and went to sleep. Sometimes she threw up. Often she forgot to wash him, and there was nothing to eat except cold tortillas. He remembered the first time she found those little bugs in his hair, how she screamed and screamed.

The happiest times were when she told him about the Other Place. "And you really can fly, whenever you want?"

"Whenever you want." And she would tell him about the lovely music that came from a box, and all the fun games you could play, and the delicious things to eat and drink, and the light that appeared when you pressed a small thing on the wall, and the pictures that you could hear and feel and smell, as well as see, and another box for making nice smells, and the pink and green and blue and silver houses as tall as mountains, and everyone happy and no one ever sad or angry, and everyone belonging to everyone else, and the boxes where you could see and hear what was happening on the other side of the world, and babies in lovely clean bottles–everything so clean, and no bad smells, no dirt at all–and people never lonely, but living together and being so cheerful and happy, like the summer dances here in Malpais, but much happier, and the happiness being there every day, every day. … He listened for hours. And sometimes, when he and the other children were tired from playing too much, one of the old men of the village would talk to them, in those different words, of the great Transformer of the World, and of the long fight between Right Hand and Left Hand, between Wet and Dry; of Awonawilona, who made a great fog by thinking in the night, and then made the whole world out of the fog; of Earth Mother and Sky Father; of Ahaiyuta and Marsailema, the twins of War and Chance; of Jesus and Pookong; of Mary and Etsanatlehi, the woman who makes herself young again; of the Black Stone at Laguna and the Great Eagle and Our Lady of Acoma. Strange stories, all the more wonderful to him for being told in the different words and so not fully understood. Lying in bed, he would think of Heaven and London and Our Lady of Acoma and the rows and rows of babies in clean bottles and Jesus flying up and Linda flying up and the great Director of World Hatcheries and Awonawilona.

Lots of men came to see Linda. The boys began to point at him. In the strange different words they said that Linda was bad; they called her names he did not understand, but that he knew were bad names. One day they sang a song about her, again and again. He threw stones at them. They threw back; a sharp stone cut his cheek. The blood wouldn't stop; he was covered with blood.

Linda taught him to read. With a piece of charcoal she drew pictures on the wall–an animal sitting down, a baby inside a bottle; then she wrote letters. THE CAT IS ON THE MAT. THE TOT IS IN THE POT. He learned quickly and easily. When he knew how to read all the words she wrote on the wall, Linda opened her big wooden box and pulled out from under those funny little red trousers she never wore a thin little book. He had often seen it before. "When you're bigger," she had said, "you can read it." Well, now he was big enough. He was proud. "I'm afraid you won't find it very interesting," she said. "But it's the only thing I have." She sighed. "If only you could see the lovely reading machines we used to have in London!" He began reading. The Chemical and Bacteriological Conditioning of the Embryo. Practical Instructions for Beta Embryo-Store Workers. It took him fifteen minutes to read the title alone. He threw the book on the floor. "Awful, awful book!" he said, and began to cry. The boys still sang their horrible song about Linda. Sometimes, too, they laughed at him for being so ragged. When he tore his clothes, Linda did not know how to fix them. In the Other Place, she told him, people threw away clothes with holes in them and got new ones. "Rags, rags!" the boys used to shout at him. "But I can read," he said to himself, "and they can't. They don't even know what reading is." It was quite easy, if he thought hard enough about the reading, to pretend that he didn't mind when they made fun of him. He asked Linda to give him the book again.

The more the boys pointed and sang, the harder he read. Soon he could read all the words quite well. Even the longest. But what did they mean? He asked Linda; but even when she could answer it didn't seem to make it very clear, And usually she couldn't answer at all.

"What are chemicals?" he would ask.

"Oh, stuff like magnesium salts, and alcohol for keeping the Deltas and Epsilons small and slow, and calcium carbonate for bones, and all that sort of thing."

"But how do you make chemicals, Linda? Where do they come from?"

"Well, I don't know. You get them out of bottles. And when the bottles are empty, you send to the Chemical Store for more. It's the Chemical Store people who make them, I suppose. Or else they send to the factory for them. I don't know. I never did any chemistry. My job was always with the embryos. It was the same with everything else he asked about. Linda never seemed to know. The old men of the village had much more definite answers.

"The seed of men and all creatures, the seed of the sun and the seed of earth and the seed of the sky–Awonawilona made them all out of the Fog of Increase. Now the world has four wombs; and he laid the seeds in the lowest of the four wombs. And gradually the seeds began to grow …"

One day (John later figured it was soon after his twelfth birthday) he came home and found a book he had never seen before on the bedroom floor. It was a thick book and looked very old. Mice had eaten the binding; some pages were loose and wrinkled. He picked it up and looked at the title page: The Complete Works of William Shakespeare.

Linda was lying on the bed, drinking that horrible stinking mescal from a cup. "Popé brought it," she said. Her voice was thick and hoarse, like someone else's. "It was in one of the chests in the Antelope Kiva. They say it's been there for hundreds of years. I think it's true, because it seemed full of nonsense when I looked at it. Uncivilized. But it'll be good for you to practice reading." She took a last sip, put the cup on the floor by the bed, turned over, hiccoughed a few times, and fell asleep.

He opened the book randomly.

Nay, but to live In the rank sweat of an enseamed bed, Stew'd in corruption, honeying and making love Over the nasty sty …

The strange words went through his mind; they rumbled like thunder talking; like the drums at the summer dances, if the drums could speak; like the men singing the Corn Song, beautiful, so beautiful you cried; like old Mitsima saying magic over his feathers and carved sticks and bits of bone and stone–kiathla tsilu silokwe silokwe silokwe. Kiai silu silu, tsithl–but better than Mitsima's magic, because it meant more, because it talked to him, talked wonderfully and only partly understandably, a terrible beautiful magic, about Linda; about Linda lying there snoring, with the empty cup on the floor by the bed; about Linda and Popé, Linda and Popé. He hated Popé more and more. A man can smile and smile and be evil. Cruel, untrustworthy, lustful, unkind villain. What did the words really mean? He only partly knew. But their magic was strong and kept rumbling in his head, and somehow it was like he had never really hated Popé before; never really hated him because he couldn't say how much he hated him. But now he had these words, these words like drums and singing and magic. These words and the strange story they came from (he couldn't understand it, but it was wonderful all the same)–they gave him a reason for hating Popé; and made his hatred more real; they even made Popé himself more real.

One day, when he came in from playing, the door to the inner room was open, and he saw them lying together on the bed, asleep–white Linda and Popé almost black beside her, with one arm under her shoulders and the other dark hand on her breast, and one of his long braids across her throat, like a black snake trying to strangle her. Popé's gourd and a cup were on the floor near the bed. Linda was snoring.

His heart seemed to have disappeared, leaving a hole. He was empty. Empty, and cold, and a little sick, and dizzy. He leaned against the wall to steady himself. Cruel, untrustworthy, lustful… Like drums, like the men singing for the corn, like magic, the words repeated in his head. He went from cold to suddenly hot. His cheeks burned, the room swam and darkened. He ground his teeth. "I'll kill him, I'll kill him, I'll kill him," he kept saying. And suddenly there were more words.

When he is drunk asleep, or in his rage Or in the incestuous pleasure of his bed …

The magic was on his side, the magic explained and gave orders. He stepped back into the outer room. "When he is drunk asleep…" The meat knife was on the floor near the fireplace. He picked it up and tiptoed to the door again. "When he is drunk asleep, drunk asleep…" He ran across the room and stabbed–oh, the blood!–stabbed again, as Popé woke up, lifted his hand to stab once more, but his wrist was caught, held, and–oh, oh!–twisted. He couldn't move, he was trapped, and Popé's small black eyes were very close, staring into his. He looked away. There were two cuts on Popé's left shoulder. "Oh, look at the blood!" Linda was crying. "Look at the blood!" She couldn't stand the sight of blood. Popé lifted his other hand–to hit him, he thought. He braced himself. But the hand just took him under the chin and turned his face, so he had to look into Popé's eyes again. For a long time, for hours. And suddenly–he couldn't help it–he began to cry. Popé laughed. "Go," he said, in the other Indian words. "Go, my brave Ahaiyuta." He ran to the other room to hide his tears.

"You are fifteen," said old Mitsima, in the Indian words. "Now I can teach you to work the clay."

They squatted by the river and worked together.

"First," said Mitsima, taking wet clay, "we make a little moon." He squeezed the clay into a disk and bent up the edges, making a shallow cup.

Slowly and clumsily, he copied the old man's gentle moves.

"A moon, a cup, now a snake." Mitsima rolled clay into a long rope, circled it, and pressed it to the cup's rim. "Then another snake. And another. And another." Round by round, Mitsima built the pot's sides. It narrowed, bulged, then narrowed again at the neck. Mitsima squeezed, patted, stroked, and scraped. Finally, it stood there, like the water pots of Malpais, but white instead of black, and still soft. His own crooked pot sat next to Mitsima's. He had to laugh looking at them. "The next will be better," he said, wetting more clay.

To shape, to form, to feel his fingers improve gave him great joy. "A, B, C, Vitamin D," he sang. "The fat's in the liver, the cod's in the sea." Mitsima sang too–a song about killing a bear. They worked all day, and he felt happy and focused.

"Next winter," said old Mitsima, "I'll teach you to make a bow."

He stood outside the house for a long time, until the ceremonies finished. The door opened, and they came out. Kothlu came first, hand outstretched and tightly closed, as if holding a jewel. Kiakimé followed, her hand also clenched. They walked silently, followed by the brothers, sisters, cousins, and all the old people.

They walked out of the pueblo, across the mesa. At the cliff's edge, they stopped, facing the morning sun. Kothlu opened his hand. Cornmeal lay white on his palm. He breathed on it, spoke softly, then threw it like white dust towards the sun. Kiakimé did the same. Then Khakimé's father held up a feathered prayer stick, prayed, then threw the stick after the cornmeal.

"It is done," said old Mitsima loudly. "They are married."

"Well," said Linda, as they turned away, "it seems like a lot for so little. In civilized countries, when a boy likes a girl, he just… Where are you going, John?"

He paid no attention to her calling, but ran on, away, away, anywhere to be by himself.

"It is finished." Old Mitsima's words repeated themselves in his mind. Finished, finished … In silence and from far away, but fiercely, desperately, hopelessly, he had loved Kiakimé. And now it was finished. He was sixteen.

At the full moon, in the Antelope Kiva, secrets would be told, secrets would be done and carried out. They would go down, boys, into the kiva and come out again, men. The boys were all afraid and also impatient. And at last it was the day. The sun went down, the moon rose. He went with the others. Men were standing, dark, at the entrance to the kiva; the ladder went down into the red lighted depths. Already the first boys had begun to climb down. Suddenly, one of the men stepped forward, caught him by the arm, and pulled him out of the ranks. He broke free and dodged back into his place among the others. This time the man struck him, pulled his hair. "Not for you, white-hair!" "Not for the son of the she-dog," said one of the other men. The boys laughed. "Go!" And as he still lingered on the edges of the group, "Go!" the men shouted again. One of them bent down, took a stone, threw it. "Go, go, go!" There was a shower of stones. Bleeding, he ran away into the darkness. From the red-lit kiva came the sound of singing. The last of the boys had climbed down the ladder. He was all alone.

All alone, outside the pueblo, on the bare plain of the mesa. The rock was like bleached bones in the moonlight. Down in the valley, the coyotes were howling at the moon. The bruises hurt him, the cuts were still bleeding; but it was not from pain that he sobbed; it was because he was all alone, because he had been driven out, alone, into this skeleton world of rocks and moonlight. At the edge of the cliff he sat down. The moon was behind him; he looked down into the black shadow of the mesa, into the black shadow of death. He had only to take one step, one little jump. … He held out his right hand in the moonlight. From the cut on his wrist the blood was still oozing. Every few seconds a drop fell, dark, almost colorless in the dead light. Drop, drop, drop. Tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow …

He had discovered Time and Death and God.

"Alone, always alone," the young man was saying.

The words awoke a sad echo in Bernard's mind. Alone, alone … "So am I," he said, in a rush of openness. "Terribly alone."

"Are you?" John looked surprised. "I thought that in the Other Place … I mean, Linda always said that nobody was ever alone there."

Bernard blushed uncomfortably. "You see," he said, mumbling and with eyes turned away, "I'm rather different from most people, I suppose. If one happens to be made different …"

"Yes, that's just it." The young man nodded. "If one's different, one's bound to be lonely. They're awful to one. Do you know, they shut me out of absolutely everything? When the other boys were sent out to spend the night on the mountains–you know, when you have to dream what your sacred animal is–they wouldn't let me go with the others; they wouldn't tell me any of the secrets. I did it by myself, though," he added. "Didn't eat anything for five days and then went out one night alone into those mountains there." He pointed.

With a slight air of superiority, Bernard smiled. "And did you dream of anything?" he asked.

The other nodded. "But I can't tell you what." He paused; then, in a low voice, "Once," he continued, "I did something no one else did: I stood against a rock in the middle of the day, in summer, with my arms out, like Jesus on the Cross."

"What on earth for?"

"I wanted to know what it felt like to be crucified. Hanging there in the sun…"

"But why?"

"Why? Well…" He hesitated. "Because I felt I should. If Jesus could handle it. And if you've done something wrong… Besides, I was unhappy; that was another reason."

"That seems a strange way to fix your unhappiness," said Bernard. But he then thought that it made some sense. Better than taking soma…

"I fainted after a while," said the young man. "Fell on my face. See the mark where I cut myself?" He lifted his thick yellow hair from his forehead. The scar was visible, pale and wrinkled, on his right temple.

Bernard looked, then quickly looked away, with a slight shudder. His upbringing made him more disgusted than sympathetic. The idea of illness or wounds was not only awful to him, but also repulsive and sickening. Like filth, or deformity, or old age. He quickly changed the subject.

"Would you like to come back to London with us?" he asked, starting a plan he had been secretly forming ever since he realized, in the little house, who the young savage's "father" must be. "Would you like that?"

The young man's face lit up. "Do you really mean it?"

"Of course; if I can get permission, that is." "Linda too?"

"Well…" He hesitated, unsure. That awful creature! No, it was impossible. Unless, unless… It suddenly struck Bernard that her very awfulness might be a great advantage. "But of course!" he cried, covering up his first hesitations with too much loud friendliness.

The young man took a deep breath. "To think it's really happening—what I've dreamt of all my life. Do you remember what Miranda says?"

"Who's Miranda?"

But the young man clearly hadn't heard the question. "O wonder!" he was saying; and his eyes shone, his face was bright red. "How many good people are here! How beautiful people are!" The flush suddenly grew stronger; he was thinking of Lenina, of an angel in bottle-green fabric, glowing with youth and skin cream, plump, kindly smiling. His voice wavered. "O brave new world," he began, then suddenly stopped; the blood had drained from his cheeks; he was as pale as paper.

"Are you married to her?" he asked.

"Am I what?"

"Married. You know—for always. They say 'for always' in the Indian language; it can't be broken."

"Ford, no!" Bernard couldn't help laughing.

John laughed too, but for a different reason–he laughed from pure joy.

"O brave new world," he repeated. "O brave new world that has such people in it. Let's begin right away."

"You talk in a very strange way sometimes," said Bernard, looking at the young man with confused surprise. "And shouldn't you wait until you actually see this new world?"
