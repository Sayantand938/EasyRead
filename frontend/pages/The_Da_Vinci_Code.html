FACT

The Priory of Sion—a secret group in Europe started in 1099—is a real group. In 1975, the National Library in Paris found old papers called Les Dossiers Secrets. These papers listed many members of the Priory of Sion, like Sir Isaac Newton, Botticelli, Victor Hugo, and Leonardo da Vinci.

Opus Dei, a very religious Catholic group connected to the Vatican, has been in the news lately. People have said they use mind control, force people, and do a harmful practice called "hurting the body." Opus Dei has just finished building a $47 million main office at 243 Lexington Avenue in New York City.

All the descriptions of art, buildings, papers, and secret ceremonies in this story are correct.

Prologue

Louvre Museum, Paris 10:46 P.M.

Jacques Saunière, a famous museum boss, stumbled through the tall archway of the museum's big hall. He reached for the closest painting, a Caravaggio. He grabbed the gold frame and pulled the painting towards him until it came off the wall. Saunière, who was seventy-six, fell to the ground under the painting.

Just as he expected, a loud iron gate fell nearby, blocking the entrance to the room. The wooden floor shook. In the distance, an alarm started to go off.

The museum boss lay there for a moment, trying to catch his breath and think. I'm still alive. He crawled out from under the painting and looked around the big room for a place to hide. A voice spoke, very close and scary. "Don't move."

The curator stopped moving, on his hands and knees, and slowly turned his head.

Just fifteen feet away, outside the closed gate, the huge shape of the person who was attacking him stared through the iron bars. He was wide and tall, with very pale skin and thin white hair. His eyes were pink with dark red centers. The albino took out a gun from his coat and pointed it through the bars, right at the curator. "You shouldn't have run." His accent was hard to understand. "Now tell me where it is."

"I already told you," the curator said, his voice shaking, as he knelt on the floor of the gallery, unable to defend himself. "I don't know what you're talking about!"

"You're lying." The man looked at him, not moving at all except for the shine in his pale eyes.

"You and your group have something that doesn't belong to you."

The curator felt a rush of fear. How could he know that?

"Tonight, the real owners will get it back. Tell me where it's hidden, and you'll live." The man pointed his gun at the curator's head. "Is it a secret you're willing to die for?" Saunière couldn't breathe.

The man tilted his head, looking down the barrel of his gun.

Saunière held up his hands to protect himself. "Wait," he said slowly. "I'll tell you what you need to know." The curator chose his next words carefully. The lie he told was one he had practiced many times... each time hoping he would never have to use it.

When the curator finished speaking, his attacker smiled in a pleased way. "Yes. That's exactly what the others told me."

Saunière jumped back. The others?

"I found them, too," the big man said mockingly. "All three of them. They said the same thing you just did."

It can't be! The curator's real identity, and the identities of his three helpers, was almost as important as the old secret they kept safe. Saunière now understood his helpers, following the rules, had told the same lie before they died. It was part of the plan.

The attacker pointed his gun again. "When you die, I will be the only one who knows the truth."

The truth. Suddenly, the curator understood how bad things really were. If I die, the truth will be lost forever. Without thinking, he tried to move to a safe place.

The gun fired, and the curator felt a burning pain as the bullet hit his stomach. He fell forward, fighting the pain. Slowly, Saunière turned over and looked back at his attacker through the bars.

The man was now aiming at Saunière's head.

Saunière closed his eyes, his thoughts full of fear and sadness. The sound of an empty gun echoed in the hallway.

The curator's eyes opened quickly.

The man looked down at his gun, seeming almost amused. He reached for another clip of bullets, but then changed his mind, smiling calmly at Saunière's stomach. "My job here is done." The curator looked down and saw the bullet hole in his white shirt. It was surrounded by a small circle of blood just below his chest. My stomach. The bullet had missed his heart, almost cruelly. As someone who had fought in the Algerian War, the curator had seen this terrible, slow death before. He would live for fifteen minutes as his stomach acids leaked into his chest, slowly poisoning him.

"Pain is good, sir," the man said.

Then he disappeared.

Now alone, Jacques Saunière looked again at the iron gate. He was stuck, and the doors wouldn't open for at least twenty minutes. By the time someone found him, he would be dead. But the fear he felt now was much stronger than the fear of dying. I have to pass on the secret.

He stood up, remembering his three friends who had been killed. He thought about all the people who came before them... about the important job they all had. A never-ending line of knowledge.

Now, suddenly, even with all the safety measures... even with all the backup plans... Jacques Saunière was the only one left, the only person who knew one of the most important secrets ever kept. Shaking, he stood up.

I have to find a way....

He was stuck inside the Grand Gallery, and there was only one person in the world he could give the secret to. Saunière looked up at the walls of his fancy prison. The world's most famous paintings seemed to smile at him like old friends. He felt pain, but he gathered all his strength and focus. He knew that the desperate task he had to do would take every last second of his life.

CHAPTER 1

Robert Langdon woke up slowly.

A phone was ringing in the dark—a strange, annoying ring. He reached for the lamp next to the bed and turned it on. Looking around, he saw a fancy bedroom from the Renaissance period with old-fashioned furniture, walls with painted designs, and a huge four-poster bed made of dark wood. Where am I?

The fancy robe hanging on the bedpost had the letters: HOTEL RITZ PARIS. Slowly, he started to remember things.

Langdon picked up the phone. "Hello?"

"Mr. Langdon?" a man said. "I hope I didn't wake you?" Feeling confused, Langdon looked at the clock. It was 12:32 A.M. He had only been asleep for an hour, but he felt terrible.

"This is the hotel worker, sir. I'm sorry to bother you, but you have a visitor. He says it's very important."

Langdon still felt confused. A visitor? He now saw a crumpled paper on the table next to his bed.

THE AMERICAN UNIVERSITY OF PARIS

is happy to present

AN EVENING WITH ROBERT LANGDON

PROFESSOR OF RELIGIOUS SYMBOLS,

HARVARD UNIVERSITY

Langdon sighed. Tonight's talk—a slide show about old, non-Christian symbols hidden in the stones of Chartres Cathedral—had probably bothered some traditional people in the audience. It was likely that a religious expert had followed him home to argue.

"I'm sorry," Langdon said, "but I'm very tired and—"

"But, sir," the concierge insisted, speaking in a low, urgent voice. "Your guest is a very important man."

Langdon knew this was likely true. His books about religious art and symbols used by cults had made him a well-known figure in the art world, even though he didn't want to be. Last year, Langdon became much more famous after he was involved in a big event at the Vatican that everyone heard about. Since then, many important historians and art fans had been coming to his door, and it seemed like it would never stop.

"If you would be so kind," Langdon said, trying to be polite, "could you get the man's name and number, and tell him I'll try to call him before I leave Paris on Tuesday? Thank you." He hung up before the concierge could argue.

Sitting up now, Langdon looked at his bedside Guest Information Book, which said on the cover: SLEEP LIKE A BABY IN THE CITY OF LIGHTS. SLEEP AT THE PARIS RITZ. He turned and looked tiredly into the full-length mirror across the room. The man looking back at him was a stranger—messy and tired.

You need a vacation, Robert.

The past year had been very hard on him, and he didn't like seeing the proof in the mirror. His usually bright blue eyes looked tired and unclear tonight. Dark stubble covered his strong jaw and chin. The gray hairs around his temples were spreading further into his thick, black hair. Even though his female coworkers said the gray made him look more attractive, Langdon knew it wasn't true.

If Boston Magazine could see me now.

Last month, Langdon was embarrassed when Boston Magazine named him one of the ten most interesting people in the city. This was an honor that his coworkers at Harvard made fun of him for. Tonight, three thousand miles from home, this award came back to bother him at the lecture he gave.

"Ladies and gentlemen..." the host said to a packed room at the American University of Paris's Pavilion Dauphine, "Our guest tonight doesn't need to be introduced. He has written many books: The Meaning of Secret Groups, The Art of the Illuminati, The Lost Language of Symbols, and when I say he wrote the book on Religious Symbols, I mean that he really did. Many of you use his books in class."

The students in the audience nodded eagerly.

"I was going to introduce him by talking about his impressive work history. However..." She looked playfully at Langdon, who was sitting on stage. "Someone in the audience just gave me a much more, let's say... interesting introduction."

She held up a copy of Boston Magazine.

Langdon felt embarrassed. Where did she get that?

The host started reading parts of the silly article, and Langdon felt himself sinking lower in his chair. Thirty seconds later, the crowd was smiling, and the woman didn't seem like she was going to stop. "And Mr. Langdon's refusal to talk about his strange role in last year's Vatican meeting definitely makes him more interesting." The host teased the crowd. "Would you like to hear more?"

The crowd clapped.

Someone stop her, Langdon begged as she looked at the article again.

"Even though Professor Langdon might not be as good-looking as some of our younger winners, this man in his forties has a lot of charm as a scholar. His interesting way of being is made stronger by his deep voice, which his female students say is like 'chocolate for the ears.'"

The hall filled with laughter.

Langdon gave a forced, awkward smile. He knew what was coming next—some silly line about

"Harrison Ford in Harris tweed"—and because he thought it was finally okay to wear his Harris tweed and Burberry turtleneck again, he decided to do something.

"Thank you, Monique," Langdon said, standing up too early and moving her away from the stage. "Boston Magazine is clearly good at making up stories." He turned to the audience with an embarrassed sigh. "And if I find out who gave them that article, I'll have them sent out of the country."

The crowd laughed.

"Well, everyone, as you know, I'm here tonight to talk about the power of symbols..."

The ringing of Langdon's hotel phone broke the silence again.

He groaned, not believing it, and answered the phone. "Yes?"

Like he thought, it was the hotel worker. "Mr. Langdon, I'm sorry again. I'm calling to tell you that your guest is coming to your room now. I thought I should let you know." Langdon was now fully awake. "You sent someone to my room?"

"I'm sorry, sir, but a man like this... I can't just stop him."

"Who is he?"

But the hotel worker had hung up.

Right away, someone banged hard on Langdon's door.

Feeling unsure, Langdon got out of bed, his toes sinking into the soft carpet. He put on the hotel robe and went to the door. "Who is it?"

"Mr. Langdon? I need to talk to you." The man's English had an accent, and he spoke in a loud, commanding way. "My name is Lieutenant Jerome Collet. I'm with the Central Judicial Police." Langdon stopped. The Judicial Police? The DCPJ was like the FBI in the U.S. Langdon kept the chain on the door and opened it a little. The man looking back at him had a pale, thin face. He was very skinny and wore a blue uniform that looked official.

"Can I come in?" the officer asked.

Langdon paused, feeling unsure as the stranger's pale eyes looked him over. "What's this about?"

"My captain needs your help with a personal issue."

"Now?" Langdon asked, surprised. "It's past midnight."

"Are you supposed to meet with the Louvre's curator tonight?" Langdon felt a sudden worry. He and the respected curator, Jacques Saunière, were supposed to have drinks after Langdon's talk, but Saunière didn't come. "Yes. How did you know?"

"We saw your name in his schedule."

"I hope everything is okay?"

The agent sighed sadly and pushed a photo through the small opening in the door. When Langdon saw it, he froze.

"This photo was taken less than an hour ago. Inside the Louvre." As Langdon looked at the strange picture, his first feeling of disgust and shock turned into anger. "Who would do this!"

"We were hoping you could help us figure that out, since you know about symbols and were going to meet with him."

Langdon stared at the picture, his fear now mixed with horror. The image was awful and very strange, and it felt like he had seen something like it before. A little over a year ago, Langdon got a photo of a dead body and a similar request for help. Twenty-four hours later, he almost died in Vatican City. This photo was different, but something about the situation felt strangely familiar.

The agent looked at his watch. "My captain is waiting, sir." Langdon barely heard him. He was still staring at the picture. "This symbol here, and the way his body is so weird..."

"In place?" the agent asked.

Langdon agreed, feeling cold as he looked up. "I can't believe anyone would do this to a person." The agent looked serious. "You don't get it, Mr. Langdon. What you see in this picture..." He stopped. "Mr. Saunière did that to himself."

CHAPTER 2

A mile away, the large albino man named Silas walked slowly through the front gate of the fancy brownstone house on Rue La Bruyère. The belt with spikes that he wore around his leg hurt his skin, but he felt happy that he was serving God. Pain is good.

His red eyes looked around the lobby as he went into the house. It was empty. He walked up the stairs quietly, not wanting to wake up any of the other people who lived there. His bedroom door was open; they were not allowed to lock doors here. He went inside and closed the door.

The room was simple—wooden floors, a plain dresser, and a mat on the floor in the corner that he used as a bed. He was staying here for the week, but he had been lucky to have a similar safe place in New York City for many years.

God has given me a place to live and a reason for my life.

Tonight, Silas felt like he had finally started to pay back what he owed. He quickly went to the dresser, found the cell phone hidden in the bottom drawer, and made a call.

"Yes?" a man's voice answered.

"Teacher, I am back," Silas said.

"Speak," the voice ordered, sounding happy to hear from him.

"All four are gone. The three leaders... and the main leader himself." There was a short pause, like they were praying. "Then I guess you have the information?"

"All four agreed. They did this on their own."

"And you believed them?"

"They agreed too much for it to be a lucky guess."

An excited breath. "Great. I was worried that the brotherhood's need for secrecy might win."

"The fear of death makes people do things."

"So, my student, tell me what I need to know."

Silas knew the information he got from his victims would be a shock. "Teacher, all four said that the clef de voûte... the famous keystone is real." He heard a quick breath over the phone and could feel the Teacher's excitement. "The keystone. Just like we thought."

According to old stories, the brotherhood made a map of stone—a clef de voûte... or keystone —a stone tablet with carvings that showed where the brotherhood's biggest secret was hidden... information so important that keeping it safe was why the brotherhood existed.

"When we have the keystone," the Teacher said, "we will be almost there."

"We are closer than you think. The keystone is here in Paris."

"Paris? Amazing. It's almost too simple."

Silas told about what happened earlier that night... how each of his four victims, just before dying, had tried hard to save their lives by telling their secret. They all told Silas the same thing—that the keystone was cleverly hidden in a specific place inside one of Paris's old churches—the Eglise de Saint-Sulpice.

"Inside a church," the Teacher said loudly. "They make fun of us!"

"Like they have for hundreds of years."

The Teacher stopped talking, as if enjoying the victory of the moment. Then, he spoke.

"You have done a great thing for God. We have waited hundreds of years for this. You must get the stone for me. Right away. Tonight. You know what's at risk."

Silas knew the risks were huge, but what the Teacher was asking seemed impossible. "But the church is like a fort. Especially at night. How will I get in?" With the sure voice of someone very powerful, the Teacher explained what to do.

When Silas finished the call, he felt excited.

One hour, he thought, happy that the Teacher had given him time to do what he needed to do before going into a church. I must clean my soul of today's sins. The sins he did today were for a good reason. They were acts of war against God's enemies, which had been done for hundreds of years. Forgiveness was certain.

Even so, Silas knew that forgiveness needed a sacrifice.

He pulled down his window shades, took off all his clothes, and knelt in the middle of his room. He looked down at the belt with sharp metal points that was fastened around his leg. All true followers of The Way wore this—a leather strap with sharp metal pieces that cut into the skin. It was meant to constantly remind them of Christ's suffering. The pain from the belt also helped to fight against sinful desires.

Even though Silas had already worn his belt for more than the required two hours today, he knew that today was special. He grabbed the buckle and tightened it another notch, making the metal points dig deeper into his skin. He breathed out slowly, enjoying the feeling of pain that he believed cleansed him. "Pain is good," Silas whispered, repeating the important saying of Father Josemaría Escrivá—the Teacher of all Teachers. Even though Escrivá had died in 1975, his teachings were still alive. His words were still spoken by thousands of loyal followers around the world as they knelt on the floor and did the holy practice of "hurting the body." Silas then looked at a thick rope with knots that was neatly coiled on the floor next to him. It was called "The Discipline." The knots were covered in dried blood. Wanting the cleansing feeling of his own pain, Silas said a quick prayer. Then, holding one end of the rope, he closed his eyes and swung it hard over his shoulder, feeling the knots hit his back. He swung it over his shoulder again, hitting his skin. He hit himself again and again.

"I punish my body."

Finally, he felt the blood start to flow.

CHAPTER 3

The cold April air rushed through the open window of the Citroën ZX as it drove quickly south past the Opera House and crossed Place Vendôme. In the passenger seat, Robert Langdon felt the city rush by as he tried to clear his head. His quick shower and shave had made him look okay, but it didn't help his worry. The scary picture of the curator's body stayed in his mind.

Jacques Saunière is dead.

Langdon felt very sad about the curator's death. Even though Saunière was known for being private, his dedication to the arts made him easy to respect. His books about the secret codes in the paintings of Poussin and Teniers were some of Langdon's favorite books to use in class. He had been really looking forward to tonight's meeting, and he was disappointed when the curator didn't show up. Again, the picture of the curator's body flashed in his mind. Did Jacques Saunière do that to himself?

Langdon turned and looked out the window, trying to get the picture out of his head. Outside, the city was just starting to quiet down—street sellers moving carts of sugared almonds, waiters taking bags of trash to the curb, a couple of late-night lovers cuddling to stay warm in a breeze that smelled like jasmine flowers. The Citroën moved through the chaos easily, its loud siren cutting through the traffic like a knife.

"The captain was happy to find out you were still in Paris tonight," the agent said, speaking for the first time since they left the hotel. "A lucky coincidence." Langdon didn't feel lucky at all, and he didn't really believe in coincidences. He had spent his life studying the hidden connections between different symbols and ideas, and he saw the world as a network of deeply connected histories and events. The connections might be hidden, he often told his symbol classes at Harvard, but they are always there, just below the surface.

"I guess," Langdon said, "that the American University of Paris told you where I was staying?" The driver shook his head. "Interpol."

Interpol, Langdon thought. Of course. He had forgotten that the simple request of all European hotels to see a passport at check-in was more than just a nice thing to do—it was the law. Every night, all over Europe, Interpol could find out exactly who was sleeping where. Finding Langdon at the Ritz probably took only five seconds. As the Citroën sped south across the city, the bright shape of the Eiffel Tower appeared, reaching up into the sky in the distance to the right. Seeing it, Langdon thought of Vittoria, remembering their fun promise a year ago that every six months they would meet again at a different romantic place in the world. The Eiffel Tower, Langdon thought, would have been on their list. Sadly, he last kissed Vittoria in a loud airport in Rome more than a year ago.

"Did you mount her?" the agent asked, looking over.

Langdon looked up, sure he had heard wrong. "I'm sorry, what?"

"She is lovely, right?" The agent pointed through the windshield at the Eiffel Tower. "Have you been up it?"

Langdon rolled his eyes. "No, I haven't climbed the tower."

"It's the symbol of France. I think it's perfect."

Langdon nodded, not really paying attention. People who study symbols often said that France—a country known for being macho, having many womanizers, and having short, insecure leaders like Napoleon and Pepin the Short—couldn't have picked a better national symbol than a giant, tall object. When they got to the intersection at Rue de Rivoli, the traffic light was red, but the Citroën didn't slow down. The agent sped the car through the intersection and onto a tree-filled part of Rue Castiglione, which was the north entrance to the famous Tuileries Gardens—Paris's version of Central Park. Most tourists thought the name Jardins des Tuileries had to do with the many tulips that grew there, but "Tuileries" actually referred to something much less romantic. This park used to be a huge, dirty hole where builders dug up clay to make the city's famous red roof tiles—or "tuiles." As they drove into the empty park, the agent reached under the dashboard and turned off the loud siren. Langdon sighed, enjoying the sudden quiet. Outside the car, the pale headlights shone on the gravel path, and the tires made a soft, repeating sound. Langdon had always thought of the Tuileries as a special place. These were the gardens where Claude Monet experimented with shapes and colors, and where the Impressionist art movement began. But tonight, the place felt strangely scary. The Citroën turned left, heading west down the park's main road. It went around a round pond, and then the driver went across a deserted road into a wide open area. Langdon could now see the end of the Tuileries Gardens, marked by a large stone archway. Arc du Carrousel.

Even though wild parties used to be held at the Arc du Carrousel, art lovers respected this place for a different reason. From the open area at the end of the Tuileries, you could see four of the best art museums in the world... one in each direction. Out the right window, south across the Seine River and Quai Voltaire, Langdon could see the brightly lit front of the old train station—now the famous Musée d'Orsay. Looking left, he could see the top of the very modern Pompidou Center, which held the Museum of Modern Art. Behind him to the west, Langdon knew the old obelisk of Ramses stood above the trees, marking the Musée du Jeu de Paume.

But it was straight ahead, to the east, through the archway, that Langdon could now see the huge Renaissance palace that had become the most famous art museum in the world. Musée du Louvre.

Langdon felt a familiar sense of wonder as he tried to take in the whole size of the building. Across a very large open space, the impressive front of the Louvre rose like a fortress against the Paris sky. Shaped like a giant horseshoe, the Louvre was the longest building in Europe, longer than three Eiffel Towers placed end to end. Even the huge open area between the museum buildings couldn't take away from the impressive size of the front of the building. Langdon had once walked all the way around the Louvre, a very long three-mile walk.

Even though it would take a visitor about five days to really see all 65,300 pieces of art in this building, most tourists chose a shorter visit that Langdon called "Louvre Lite"—a quick run through the museum to see the three most famous things: the Mona Lisa, Venus de Milo, and Winged Victory. Art Buchwald had once joked that he had seen all three masterpieces in five minutes and fifty-six seconds.

The driver took out a small walkie-talkie and spoke quickly in French. "Mr. Langdon has arrived. Two minutes."

A reply came back, but it was hard to understand.

The agent put away the device and turned to Langdon. "You will meet the captain at the main entrance."

The driver ignored the signs that said cars weren't allowed on the plaza. He sped up the engine and drove the Citroën over the curb. The Louvre's main entrance was now in sight, standing out in the distance. It was surrounded by seven triangle-shaped pools with lit-up fountains. It was the Pyramid.

The new entrance to the Paris Louvre had become almost as well-known as the museum itself. The glass pyramid, which was modern and caused a lot of debate, was designed by I. M. Pei, an American architect born in China. Some traditional people still hated it, saying it ruined the feel of the old courtyard. Someone once said that architecture was like frozen music, and these critics said the pyramid was like nails scratching on a chalkboard. But people who liked new things thought the seventy-one-foot-tall clear pyramid was amazing. They said it mixed old and new styles, connecting the past and the future, and helping the Louvre move into the next century.

"Do you like our pyramid?" the agent asked.

Langdon looked unhappy. It seemed like the French loved to ask Americans this question. It was a tricky question, of course. If you said you liked the pyramid, you seemed like an American with bad taste. If you said you didn't like it, you were insulting the French.

"Mitterrand was a brave man," Langdon answered, trying to be neutral. The former French president who had ordered the pyramid was said to have been obsessed with Egypt. He had filled Paris with Egyptian things like obelisks, art, and other objects. François Mitterrand loved Egyptian culture so much that the French still called him the Sphinx.

"What is the captain's name?" Langdon asked, changing the subject.

"Bezu Fache," the driver said, as they got closer to the pyramid's main entrance. "We call him the Bull." Langdon looked at him, wondering if every French person had a secret animal nickname.

"You call your captain the Bull?"

The man raised his eyebrows. "Your French is better than you let on, Mr. Langdon." My French is terrible, Langdon thought, but I know a lot about zodiac symbols. Taurus was always the bull. Astrology was a common symbol all over the world.

The driver stopped the car and pointed between two water fountains to a big door in the side of the pyramid. "That's the entrance. Good luck, sir."

"You're not coming with me?"

"I'm supposed to leave you here. I have other things to do." Langdon sighed and got out of the car. This is your problem.

The driver started the engine and drove away quickly.

As Langdon stood alone and watched the car's lights disappear, he thought he could easily change his mind, leave the courtyard, get a taxi, and go home to sleep. But he had a feeling that would be a bad idea.

As he walked toward the misty fountains, Langdon felt like he was crossing an invisible line into a different world. The strange, dreamlike feeling of the evening was coming back. Just twenty minutes ago, he was asleep in his hotel room. Now, he was standing in front of a see-through pyramid built by the Sphinx, waiting for a policeman called the Bull. I feel like I'm in a weird painting, he thought.

Langdon walked to the main entrance—a huge spinning door. The entrance area inside was dimly lit and empty.

Should I knock?

Langdon wondered if any of the famous Egypt experts from Harvard had ever knocked on a pyramid's front door and expected someone to answer. He lifted his hand to hit the glass, but then a person appeared from the darkness below, walking up the curved stairs. The man was short and dark, looking like an early human, wearing a dark suit that was tight on his broad shoulders. He walked with clear confidence on his short, strong legs. He was talking on his phone but finished the call as he reached Langdon. He signaled for Langdon to come in.

"I am Bezu Fache," he said as Langdon went through the spinning door. "Captain of the Central Police." His voice was deep and rough... like a storm coming.

Langdon put out his hand to shake. "Robert Langdon."

Fache's big hand grabbed Langdon's with a very strong grip.

"I saw the picture," Langdon said. "Your agent said Jacques Saunière himself did—"

"Mr. Langdon," Fache said, looking directly at him with his dark eyes. "What you see in the picture is just the start of what Saunière did."

CHAPTER 4

Captain Bezu Fache walked like an angry bull, with his wide shoulders back and his chin tucked into his chest. His dark hair was slicked back, making his pointed hairline look like an arrow that came before him like the front of a warship. As he walked, his dark eyes seemed to burn the ground, showing a clear anger that matched his reputation for being very strict.

Langdon followed the captain down the famous marble stairs into the area below the glass pyramid. As they went down, they passed two armed police guards with machine guns. The message was clear: No one goes in or out tonight without Captain Fache's permission.

Going below ground, Langdon felt a growing fear. Fache's presence was not friendly, and the Louvre itself felt like a tomb at this time. The stairs, like a dark movie theater aisle, were lit by small lights in each step. Langdon could hear his own footsteps echoing off the glass above. When he looked up, he could see the faint mist from the fountains fading away outside the clear roof.

"Do you like it?" Fache asked, nodding up with his wide chin. Langdon sighed, too tired to play games. "Yes, your pyramid is amazing." Fache grunted. "It's like a scar on Paris."

That's one strike. Langdon felt that his host was hard to please. He wondered if Fache knew that this pyramid, built because President Mitterrand wanted it, had exactly 666 pieces of glass—a strange request that was always talked about by people who believed that 666 was the number of Satan.

Langdon decided not to mention it.
