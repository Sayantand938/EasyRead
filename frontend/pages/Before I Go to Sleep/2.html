Part One: Today

The bedroom is strange. Unfamiliar. I don’t know where I am, or how I got here. I don’t know how I’m going to get home.

I spent the night here. I was woken by a woman’s voice. At first, I thought she was in bed with me, but then I realized she was reading the news and I was hearing a radio alarm. When I opened my eyes, I found myself here, in this room I don’t recognize.

My eyes adjust, and I look around in the dim light. A dressing gown hangs on the back of the wardrobe door. It looks like it belongs to a woman, but someone much older than me. Some dark-colored trousers are folded neatly over the back of a chair at the dressing table, but I can't see much else. The alarm clock looks complicated, but I find a button and manage to turn it off.

Just then, I hear a sudden intake of breath behind me and realize I'm not alone. I turn around. I see a lot of skin and dark hair, with some white in it. A man. His left arm is outside the covers, and there is a gold band on his ring finger. I try not to groan. So this one is not only old and grey, I think, but also married. Not only have I slept with a married man, but I have done so in what I guess is his home, in the bed he usually shares with his wife. I lie back to calm down. I should be ashamed.

I wonder where the wife is. Do I need to worry about her coming back at any moment? I imagine her standing on the other side of the room, screaming, calling me a slut. A monster. A bunch of snakes. I wonder how I will defend myself if she appears. But the guy in the bed doesn't seem worried. He has turned over and is snoring.

I lie as still as I can. Usually, I can remember how I get into situations like this, but not today. There must have been a party, or a trip to a bar or a club. I must have been pretty drunk. Drunk enough that I don’t remember anything at all. Drunk enough to have gone home with a man with a wedding ring and hairs on his back.

I fold back the covers as gently as I can and sit on the edge of the bed. First, I need to use the bathroom. I ignore the slippers at my feet. After all, sleeping with the husband is one thing, but I could never wear another woman’s shoes. I creep barefoot onto the landing. I am aware that I am naked, and afraid of choosing the wrong door, of running into a lodger, a teenage son. Relieved, I see the bathroom door is partly open and go in, locking it behind me.

I sit, use the toilet, then flush it and turn to wash my hands. I reach for the soap, but something is wrong. At first, I can’t figure out what it is, but then I see it. The hand holding the soap does not look like mine. The skin is wrinkled, the nails are unpolished and bitten short, and like the man in the bed I just left, the ring finger has a plain, gold wedding ring.

I stare for a moment, then wiggle my fingers. The fingers of the hand holding the soap move too. I gasp, and the soap falls into the sink with a thud. I look up at the mirror.

The face I see looking back at me is not my own. The hair has no volume and is cut much shorter than I wear it. The skin on the cheeks and under the chin sags. The lips are thin, the mouth turned down. I cry out, a wordless gasp that would turn into a shriek of shock if I let it, and then notice the eyes. The skin around them is lined, yes, but despite everything else I can see that they are mine. The person in the mirror is me, but I am twenty years too old. Twenty-five. More.

This isn’t possible. Beginning to shake, I grip the edge of the sink. Another scream starts to rise in my chest and this one erupts as a strangled gasp. I step back, away from the mirror, and it is then that I see them. Photographs. Taped to the wall, to the mirror itself. Pictures, mixed with yellow pieces of gummed paper, felt-tip notes, damp and curling.

I choose one at random. Christine, it says, and an arrow points to a photograph of me – this new me, this old me – in which I am sitting on a bench on a quayside, next to a man. The name seems familiar, but only slightly, as if I am having to try to believe that it is mine. In the photograph we are both smiling at the camera, holding hands. He is handsome, attractive, and when I look closely I can see that it is the same man I slept with, the one I left in the bed. The word Ben is written beneath it, and next to it Your husband.

I gasp, and rip it off the wall. No, I think. No! It can’t be … I scan the rest of the pictures. They are all of me, and him. In one I am wearing an ugly dress and unwrapping a present. In another both of us wear matching weatherproof jackets and stand in front of a waterfall as a small dog sniffs at our feet. Next to it is a picture of me sitting beside him, sipping a glass of orange juice, wearing the dressing gown I have seen in the bedroom next door.

I step back further, until I feel cold tiles against my back. It is then I get the glimmer that I associate with memory. As my mind tries to settle on it, it flutters away, like ashes caught in a breeze. I realize that in my life there is a then, a before, though before what I cannot say, and there is a now. There is nothing between the two but a long, silent emptiness that has led me here, to me and him, in this house.

I go back into the bedroom. I still have the picture in my hand – the one of me and the man I had woken up with – and I hold it in front of me.

‘What’s going on?’ I say. I am screaming; tears run down my face. The man is sitting up in bed, his eyes half closed. ‘Who are you?’

‘I’m your husband,’ he says. His face is sleepy, without a trace of annoyance. He does not look at my naked body. ‘We’ve been married for years.’

‘What do you mean?’ I say. I want to run, but there is nowhere to go. ‘“Married for years”? What do you mean?’

He stands up. ‘Here,’ he says, and passes me the dressing gown, waiting while I put it on. He is wearing pyjama trousers that are too big for him, a white vest. He reminds me of my father.

‘We got married in nineteen eighty-five,’ he says. ‘Twenty-two years ago. You—’

‘What—?’ I feel the blood drain from my face, the room begin to spin. A clock ticks, somewhere in the house, and it sounds as loud as a hammer. ‘But—’ He takes a step towards me. ‘How—?’

‘Christine, you’re forty-seven now,’ he says. I look at him, this stranger who is smiling at me. I don’t want to believe him, don’t even want to hear what he’s saying, but he carries on. ‘You had an accident,’ he says. ‘A bad accident. You had head injuries. You have problems remembering things.’

‘What things?’ I say, meaning, Surely not the last twenty-five years? ‘What things?’

He steps towards me again, coming to me as if I am a frightened animal. ‘Everything,’ he says. ‘Sometimes starting from when you were in your early twenties. Sometimes even earlier than that.’

My mind spins, going around and around with dates and ages. I don’t want to ask, but know that I must. ‘When … when was my accident?’

He looks at me, and his face shows both compassion and fear.

‘When you were twenty-nine …’

I close my eyes. Even as my mind tries to refuse this information I know, somewhere, that it is true. I hear myself start to cry again, and as I do so this man, this Ben, comes over to where I stand in the doorway. I feel him next to me, do not move as he puts his arms around my waist, do not resist as he pulls me to him. He holds me. Together we rock gently, and I realize the motion feels familiar. It makes me feel better.

‘I love you, Christine,’ he says, and though I know I should say I love him too, I don’t. I say nothing. How can I love him? He is a stranger. Nothing makes sense. I want to know so many things. How I got here, how I survive. But I don’t know how to ask.

‘I’m scared,’ I say.

‘I know,’ he replies. ‘I know. But don’t worry, Chris. I’ll look after you. I’ll always look after you. You’ll be fine. Trust me.’

He says he will show me the house. I feel calmer. I have put on underwear and an old T-shirt that he gave me, then put the robe over my shoulders. We go out to the landing. ‘You’ve seen the bathroom,’ he says, opening the door next to it. ‘This is the office.’

There is a glass desk with what I think is a computer, though it looks very small, almost like a toy. Next to it is a metal filing cabinet, and a wall planner above it. Everything is neat and tidy. ‘I work in here sometimes,’ he says, closing the door. We cross the landing and he opens another door. A bed, a dressing table, more wardrobes. It looks almost the same as the room where I woke up. ‘Sometimes you sleep in here,’ he says, ‘when you want to. But usually you don’t like waking up alone. You panic when you don’t know where you are.’ I nod. I feel like someone looking to rent a new apartment. A possible housemate. ‘Let’s go downstairs.’

I follow him down. He shows me a living room – a brown sofa and chairs, a flat screen on the wall which he says is a television – and a dining room and kitchen. None of it is familiar. I feel nothing, not even when I see a framed photograph of us on a sideboard. ‘There’s a garden out back,’ he says and I look through the glass door from the kitchen. It is just starting to get light, the night sky turning dark blue, and I can see the shape of a large tree, and a shed at the end of the small garden, but not much else. I realize I don’t even know where we are.

‘Where are we?’ I say.

He stands behind me. I can see us both in the glass. Me. My husband. Middle-aged.

‘North London,’ he replies. ‘Crouch End.’

I step back. Panic starts to rise. ‘Jesus,’ I say. ‘I don’t even know where I live …’

He takes my hand. ‘Don’t worry. You’ll be fine.’ I turn to face him, waiting for him to tell me how I will be fine, but he doesn't. ‘Shall I make your coffee?’

For a moment I feel angry at him, but then say, ‘Yes. Yes, please.’ He fills a kettle. ‘Black, please,’ I say. ‘No sugar.’

‘I know,’ he says, smiling at me. ‘Want some toast?’

I say yes. He must know so much about me, yet this still feels like the morning after a one-night stand: breakfast with a stranger in his house, planning how soon I can leave and go back home.

But that’s the difference. Apparently, this is my home.

‘I think I need to sit down,’ I say.

He looks up at me. ‘Go sit down in the living room,’ he says. ‘I’ll bring this in a minute.’

I leave the kitchen.

A few moments later Ben follows me in. He gives me a book. ‘This is a scrapbook,’ he says. ‘It might help.’ I take it from him. It is bound in plastic that looks like worn leather but doesn't, and has a red ribbon tied around it in a messy bow. ‘I’ll be back in a minute,’ he says, and leaves the room.

I sit on the sofa. The scrapbook feels heavy on my lap. Looking at it feels like snooping. I remind myself that whatever is inside is about me, and was given to me by my husband.

I untie the bow and open it to a random page. A picture of me and Ben, looking much younger.

I slam it shut. I run my hands around the cover, fan the pages. I must do this every day.

I can’t imagine it. I am sure there has been a terrible mistake, but there can’t have been. The proof is there – in the mirror upstairs, in the lines on the hands that touch the book in front of me. I am not the person I thought I was when I woke up this morning.

But who was that? I think. When was I that person, who woke up in a stranger’s bed and only thought of escaping? I close my eyes. I feel like I am floating. Unattached. In danger of getting lost.

I need to ground myself. I close my eyes and try to focus on something solid. I find nothing. So many years of my life, I think. Gone.

This book will tell me who I am, but I don’t want to open it. Not yet. I want to sit here for a while, with the whole past a blank. In limbo, balanced between what could be and what is. I am scared to find out about my past. What I have done, and what I have not.

Ben comes back in and sets a tray in front of me. Toast, two cups of coffee, a jug of milk. ‘You OK?’ he says. I nod.

He sits beside me. He has shaved and put on trousers, a shirt, and a tie. He doesn’t look like my father anymore. Now he looks like he works in a bank or an office. Not bad, though, I think, then push the thought away.

‘Is every day like this?’ I say.

He puts a piece of toast on a plate and spreads butter on it. ‘Pretty much,’ he says. ‘Do you want some?’ I shake my head, and he takes a bite. ‘You seem to remember things when you’re awake,’ he says. ‘But when you sleep, you forget most of it. Is your coffee OK?’

I tell him it’s fine, and he takes the book from my hands. ‘This is like a scrapbook,’ he says, opening it. ‘We had a fire a few years ago, so we lost many old photos and things, but there are still some bits and pieces here.’ He points to the first page. ‘This is your degree certificate,’ he says. ‘And here’s a photo of you on your graduation day.’ I look where he points; I am smiling, squinting in the sun, wearing a black gown and a felt hat with a gold tassel. Behind me is a man in a suit and tie, his head turned away from the camera.

‘That’s you?’ I say.

He smiles. ‘No. I didn’t graduate at the same time as you. I was still studying then. Chemistry.’

I look up at him. ‘When did we get married?’ I say.

He turns to face me, taking my hand in his. I am surprised by how rough his skin is. I guess I'm used to the softness of youth. ‘The year after you got your Ph.D. We’d been dating for a few years by then, but you – we – we both wanted to wait until you finished your studies.’

That makes sense, I think, though it seems oddly practical of me. I wonder if I even wanted to marry him.

As if reading my mind, he says, ‘We were very much in love,’ and then adds, ‘we still are.’

I can’t think of anything to say. I smile. He takes a drink of his coffee before looking back at the book in his lap. He turns some more pages.

‘You studied English,’ he says. ‘Then you had a few jobs after you graduated. Just odd things. Secretarial work. Sales. I’m not sure you knew what you wanted to do. I got a BSc and did teacher training. It was hard for a few years, but then I was promoted, and we ended up here.’

I look around the living room. It is stylish and comfortable, but plainly middle class. A framed picture of a woodland scene hangs on the wall above the fireplace, and china figures sit next to the clock on the mantelpiece. I wonder if I helped pick the decorations.

Ben continues. ‘I teach in a secondary school nearby. I’m head of the department now.’ He says it without any pride.

‘And me?’ I say, even though I already know the only answer. He squeezes my hand.

‘You had to quit working after your accident. You don’t do anything.’ He must notice my disappointment. ‘You don’t need to. I make enough money. We manage. We’re OK.’

I close my eyes and put my hand to my forehead. This feels like too much, and I want him to be quiet. I feel like I can only handle so much, and if he keeps adding more, I will eventually explode.

What do I do all day? I want to ask, but I am afraid of the answer, so I say nothing.

He finishes his toast and takes the tray to the kitchen. When he comes back, he is wearing an overcoat.

‘I have to go to work,’ he says. I feel myself tighten up.

‘Don’t worry,’ he says. ‘You’ll be fine. I’ll call you. I promise. Remember, today is just like any other day. You’ll be fine.’

‘But—’ I start to say.

‘I have to go,’ he says. ‘I’m sorry. I’ll show you some things you might need, before I leave.’

In the kitchen he shows me which things are in which cupboard, points out some leftovers in the fridge that I can have for lunch and a wipe-clean board screwed to the wall, next to a black marker pen tied to a piece of string. ‘I sometimes leave messages here for you,’ he says. I see that he has written the word Friday on it in neat, even capitals, and beneath it the words Laundry? Walk? (Take phone!) TV? Under the word Lunch he has noted that there is some leftover salmon in the fridge and added the word Salad? Finally he has written that he should be home by six. ‘You also have a diary,’ he says. ‘In your bag. It has important phone numbers in the back of it, and our address, in case you get lost. And there’s a mobile phone—’

‘A what?’ I say.

‘A phone,’ he says. ‘It’s cordless. You can use it anywhere. Outside the house, anywhere. It’ll be in your handbag. Make sure you take it with you if you go out.’

‘I will,’ I say.

‘Right,’ he says. We go into the hall and he picks up a battered leather satchel by the door. ‘I’ll be off, then.’

‘OK,’ I say. I am not sure what else to say. I feel like a child kept out of school, left alone at home while her parents go to work. Don’t touch anything, I imagine him saying. Don’t forget to take your medicine.

He comes over to where I stand. He kisses me, on the cheek. I don’t stop him, but neither do I kiss him back. He turns towards the front door, and is about to open it when he stops.

‘Oh!’ he says, looking back at me. ‘I almost forgot!’ His voice sounds suddenly forced, the enthusiasm not real. He is trying too hard to make it seem natural; it is obvious he has been building up to what he is about to say for some time.

In the end it is not as bad as I feared. ‘We’re going away this evening,’ he says. ‘Just for the weekend. It’s our anniversary, so I thought I’d book something. Is that OK?’

I nod. ‘That sounds nice,’ I say.

He smiles, looking relieved. ‘Something to look forward to, right? A bit of sea air? It’ll be good for us.’ He turns back to the door and opens it. ‘I’ll call you later,’ he says. ‘See how you’re doing.’

‘Yes,’ I say. ‘Please do.’

‘I love you, Christine,’ he says. ‘Never forget that.’

He closes the door behind him, and I turn around. I go back into the house.

Later, mid-morning. I sit in an armchair. The dishes are done and stacked neatly on the drainer, and the laundry is in the machine. I have been keeping busy.

But now I feel empty. What Ben said is true. I have no memory. Nothing. There is not one thing in this house that I remember seeing before. Not a single photograph – either around the mirror or in the scrapbook in front of me – makes me remember when it was taken. I can't recall a moment with Ben, other than those since we met this morning. My mind feels completely empty.

I close my eyes and try to focus on something. Anything. Yesterday. Last Christmas. Any Christmas. My wedding. There is nothing.

I stand up. I move through the house, from room to room. Slowly. Drifting, like a ghost, letting my hand brush against the walls, the tables, the backs of the furniture, but not really touching any of it. How did I end up like this? I think. I look at the carpets, the patterned rugs, the china figures on the mantelpiece, and the decorative plates arranged on the display racks in the dining room. I try to tell myself that this is mine. All mine. My home, my husband, my life. But these things do not belong to me. They are not part of me. In the bedroom, I open the wardrobe door and see a row of clothes I don’t recognize, hanging neatly, like empty versions of a woman I have never met. A woman whose home I am wandering through, whose soap and shampoo I have used, whose dressing gown I have taken off, and whose slippers I am wearing. She is hidden from me, like a ghost, distant and untouchable. This morning, I had chosen my underwear feeling guilty, searching through the pairs of underwear, rolled up with tights and stockings, as if I was afraid of being caught. I held my breath as I found underwear in silk and lace at the back of the drawer, items bought to be seen as well as worn. I put the unused ones back exactly as I had found them. I chose a pale-blue pair that seemed to have a matching bra and slipped them both on, before pulling a heavy pair of tights over the top, and then trousers and a blouse.

I sat down at the dressing table to look closely at my face in the mirror, approaching my reflection carefully. I traced the lines on my forehead, the folds of skin under my eyes. I smiled and looked at my teeth, and at the wrinkles around my mouth, the crow’s feet that had appeared. I noticed the blotches on my skin, a discoloration on my forehead that looked like a bruise that hadn’t completely faded. I found some make-up, and put a little on. A light powder, a touch of blusher. I pictured a woman – my mother, I realize now – doing the same, calling it her warpaint, and this morning, as I blotted my lipstick on a tissue and recapped the mascara, the word felt right. I felt I was going into some kind of battle, or that some battle was coming to me.

Sending me off to school. Putting on her make-up. I tried to think of my mother doing something else. Anything. Nothing came. I saw only an empty space, big gaps between small islands of memory, years of emptiness.

Now, in the kitchen, I open cupboards: bags of pasta, packets of a rice called arborio, tins of kidney beans. I don’t know these foods. I remember eating cheese on toast, boil-in-the-bag fish, corned-beef sandwiches. I pull out a tin of chickpeas, a sachet of something called couscous. I don’t know what these things are, or how to cook them. How then do I survive, as a wife?

I look up at the wipe-clean board that Ben had shown me before he left. It is a dirty grey color, words have been written on it and wiped out, replaced, changed, each leaving a faint mark. I wonder what I would find if I could go back and read the layers, if it were possible to explore my past that way, but realize that, even if it were possible, it would be pointless. I am sure that all I would find are messages and lists, groceries to buy, tasks to do.

Is that really my life? I think. Is that all I am? I take the pen and add another note to the board. Pack bag for tonight? it says. Not much of a reminder, but my own.

I hear a noise. A tune, coming from my bag. I open it and empty its contents onto the sofa. My purse, some tissues, pens, a lipstick. A powder compact, a receipt for two coffees. A diary, just a couple of inches square and with a floral design on the front and a pencil in its spine.

I find something that I guess must be the phone that Ben described – it is small, plastic, with a keypad that makes it look like a toy. It is ringing, the screen flashing. I press what I hope is the right button.

‘Hello?’ I say. The voice that replies is not Ben’s.

‘Hi,’ it says. ‘Christine? Is that Christine Lucas?’

I don’t want to answer. My surname seems as strange as my first name had. I feel as though any solid ground I had reached has disappeared again, replaced by quicksand.

‘Christine? Are you there?’

Who could it be? Who knows where I am, or who I am? It could be anyone. I feel panic rising in me. My finger is close to the button that will end the call.

‘Christine? It’s me. Dr. Nash. Please answer.’

The name means nothing to me, but I still say, ‘Who is this?’

The voice changes its tone. Is it relief? ‘It’s Dr. Nash,’ he says. ‘Your doctor?’

Another flash of panic. ‘My doctor?’ I say. I’m not ill, I want to add, but I don’t even know this. I feel my mind starting to spin.

‘Yes,’ he says. ‘But don’t worry. We’ve just been working on your memory. Nothing is wrong.’

I notice the tense he used. Have been. So this is someone else I don't remember.

‘What kind of work?’ I say.

‘I’ve been trying to help you, to make things better,’ he says. ‘Trying to figure out exactly what caused your memory problems, and if there’s anything we can do about them.’

It makes sense, but another thought occurs to me. Why hadn't Ben mentioned this doctor before he left this morning?

‘How?’ I say. ‘What have we been doing?’

‘We’ve been meeting over the last few weeks. About twice a week, more or less.’

It seems impossible. Another person I see regularly who has made no impression on me at all.

But I’ve never met you before, I want to say. You could be anyone.

The same could be said of the man I woke up with this morning, and he turned out to be my husband.

‘I don’t remember,’ I say instead.

His voice softens. ‘Don’t worry. I know.’ If he’s telling the truth, he must understand that as well as anyone. He explains that our next appointment is today.

‘Today?’ I say. I think back to what Ben told me this morning, to the list of tasks written on the board in the kitchen. ‘But my husband hasn’t mentioned anything to me.’ I realize it is the first time I have called the man I woke up with my husband.

There is a pause, and then Dr. Nash says, ‘I’m not sure Ben knows you’re meeting me.’

I notice that he knows my husband’s name, but I say, ‘That’s ridiculous! How can he not? He would have told me!’

There is a sigh. ‘You’ll have to trust me,’ he says. ‘I can explain everything when we meet. We’re really making progress.’

When we meet. How can we do that? The thought of going out without Ben, without him even knowing where I am or who I am with, scares me.

‘I’m sorry,’ I say. ‘I can’t.’

‘Christine,’ he says, ‘it’s important. If you look in your diary, you’ll see what I’m saying is true. Do you have it? It should be in your bag.’

I pick up the floral book from where it had fallen on the sofa and feel shocked to see the year printed on the front in gold letters. Two thousand and seven. Twenty years later than it should be.

‘Yes.’

‘Look at today’s date,’ he says. ‘November thirtieth. You should see our appointment.’

I don’t understand how it can be November – December tomorrow – but I still skim through the pages, thin as tissue, to today’s date. There, tucked between the pages, is a piece of paper, and on it, written in handwriting I don’t recognize, are the words November 30th – seeing Dr. Nash. Beneath them are the words Don’t tell Ben. I wonder if Ben has read them, whether he looks through my things.

I decide there is no reason he would. The other days are blank. No birthdays, no nights out, no parties. Does this really describe my life?

‘OK,’ I say. He explains that he will come and pick me up, that he knows where I live and will be there in an hour.

‘But my husband—’ I say.

‘It’s OK. We’ll be back long before he gets in from work. I promise. Trust me.’

The clock on the mantelpiece chimes and I glance at it. It is old-fashioned, a large dial in a wooden case, edged with Roman numerals. It reads eleven thirty. Next to it sits a silver key for winding it, something that I suppose Ben must remember to do every evening. It looks old enough to be an antique, and I wonder how we came to own such a clock. Perhaps it has no history, or none with us at least, but is simply something we saw once, in a shop or on a market stall, and one of us liked it. Probably Ben, I think. I realize I don’t like it.

I’ll see him just this once, I think. And then, tonight, when he gets home, I will tell Ben. I can’t believe I’m keeping something like this from him. Not when I rely so completely on him.

But there is an odd familiarity to Dr. Nash’s voice. Unlike Ben, he does not seem entirely foreign to me. I realize I almost find it easier to believe that I have met him before than I do my husband.

We’re making progress, he’d said. I need to know what kind of progress he means.

‘OK,’ I say. ‘Come.’

When he arrives Dr. Nash suggests we go for a cup of coffee. ‘Are you thirsty?’ he says. ‘I don’t think there’s much point in driving all the way to the office. I mostly wanted to talk to you today, anyway.’

I nod, and say yes. I was in the bedroom when he arrived and watched him park his car and lock it, saw him fix his hair, smooth his jacket, pick up his briefcase. Not him, I thought as he nodded to the workmen who were unloading tools from a van, but then he walked up the path to our house. He looked young – too young to be a doctor – and, though I don’t know what I had been expecting him to be wearing, it was not the sports jacket and grey corduroy trousers that he had on.

‘There’s a park at the end of the street,’ he says. ‘I think it has a café. We could go there?’

We walk together. The cold is strong and I pull my scarf tight around my neck. I am glad I have in my bag the mobile phone that Ben has given me. Glad too that Dr Nash has not insisted we drive somewhere. There is some part of me that trusts this man, but another, larger part tells me he could be anyone. A stranger.

I am an adult, but a damaged one. It would be easy for this man to take me somewhere, though I don’t know what he would want to do. I am as vulnerable as a child.

We reach the main road that separates the end of the street from the park opposite, and wait to cross. The silence between us feels heavy. I had planned to wait until we were sitting down before asking him, but find myself speaking. ‘What sort of doctor are you?’ I am saying. ‘What do you do? How did you find me?’

He looks over at me. ‘I’m a neuropsychologist,’ he says. He is smiling. I wonder if I ask him the same question every time we meet. ‘I focus on patients with brain disorders, and I'm interested in some of the newer brain scanning methods. For a long time I’ve been especially interested in studying how memory works. I heard about you from articles on the subject, and found you. It wasn’t too hard.’

A car rounds the bend further up the road and heads towards us. ‘The articles?’

‘Yes. There have been a couple of studies written about you. I contacted the place where you were being treated before you came to live at home.’

‘Why? Why did you want to find me?’

He smiles. ‘Because I thought I could help you. I’ve been working with patients with these kinds of problems for a while. I believe they can be helped; however, they need more help than the usual one hour per week. I had some ideas about how real improvements could be made and wanted to try some of them out.’ He pauses. ‘Plus I’ve been writing a paper on your case. The best one, you might say.’ He begins to laugh, but stops when I do not join in. He clears his throat. ‘Your case is unusual. I believe we can learn a lot more about how memory works than we already know.’

The car passes and we cross the road. I feel myself getting anxious, tense. Brain disorders. Researching. Tracked you down. I try to breathe, to relax, but I can't. There are two of me now, in the same body: one is a forty-seven-year-old woman, calm, polite, aware of what behavior is appropriate and what is not – and the other is in her twenties, and screaming. I can’t decide which one is me, but the only noise I hear is the distant traffic and the shouts of children from the park, so I guess it must be the first.

On the other side I stop and say, ‘Look, what’s going on? I woke up this morning in a place I’ve never seen but that’s apparently my home, lying next to a man I’ve never met who tells me I’ve been married to him for years. And you seem to know more about me than I know about myself.’

He nods slowly. ‘You have amnesia,’ he says, putting his hand on my arm. ‘You’ve had amnesia for a long time. You can’t hold onto new memories, so you’ve forgotten much of what’s happened to you in your adult life. Every day you wake up as if you are a young woman. Some days you wake as if you are a child.’

Somehow it seems worse, coming from him, a doctor. ‘So it’s true?’

‘I’m afraid so. Yes. The man at home is your husband. Ben. You’ve been married to him for years, since long before your amnesia began.’ I nod. ‘Shall we go on?’

I say yes, and we walk into the park. A path circles its edge, and there is a children’s playground nearby, next to a hut where I see people coming out with trays of snacks. We head there, and I sit at one of the chipped Formica tables while Dr. Nash orders our drinks.

When he returns he is carrying two plastic cups filled with strong coffee, mine black, his white. He adds sugar from the bowl on the table but doesn't offer me any, and that, more than anything, convinces me we have met before. He looks up and asks me how I hurt my forehead.

‘What—?’ I say at first, but then I remember the bruise I saw this morning. My makeup has clearly not covered it. ‘That?’ I say. ‘I’m not sure. It’s nothing, really. It doesn’t hurt.’

He doesn’t answer. He stirs his coffee.

‘So my husband looks after me at home?’ I say.

He looks up. ‘Yes, though it wasn’t always the case. At first, your condition was so bad that you needed constant care. It's only recently that Ben felt he could care for you alone.’

So, how I feel now is better, then. I'm glad I don't remember when things were worse.

‘He must love me a lot,’ I say, more to myself than to Nash.

He nods. There is a pause. We both sip our drinks. ‘Yes. I think he must.’

I smile and look down at my hands holding the hot drink, at the gold wedding band, at the short nails, at my legs, crossed neatly. I don’t recognize my own body.

‘Why doesn’t my husband know I’m seeing you?’ I say.

He sighs and closes his eyes. ‘I’ll be honest,’ he says, clasping his hands together and leaning forward in his seat. ‘At first, I asked you not to tell Ben that you were seeing me.’

A jolt of fear goes through me, almost like an echo. Yet he doesn’t seem untrustworthy.

‘Go on,’ I say. I want to believe he can help me.

‘Many people – doctors, psychiatrists, psychologists, and so on – have approached you and Ben in the past, wanting to work with you. But he has always been very unwilling to let you see these professionals. He has made it very clear that you have had a lot of treatment before, and he thinks it has only upset you. Of course, he wanted to protect you – and himself – from any more upset.’

Of course; he doesn’t want to raise my hopes. ‘So you convinced me to come and see you without him knowing?’

‘Yes. I did talk to Ben first. We spoke on the phone. I even asked him to meet me so I could explain what I could offer, but he refused. So I contacted you directly.’

Another jolt of fear, as if from nowhere. ‘How?’ I say.

He looked down at his drink. ‘I went to see you. I waited until you came out of the house and then introduced myself.’

‘And I agreed to see you? Just like that?’

‘Not at first. No. I had to persuade you to trust me. I suggested we meet once, just for one session. Without Ben’s knowledge if that was what it took. I said I would explain why I wanted you to come and see me, and what I thought I could offer you.’

‘And I agreed …’

He looks up. ‘Yes,’ he says. ‘I told you that after that first visit, it was up to you whether you told Ben or not, but if you decided not to, I would ring you to make sure you remembered our appointments, and so on.’

‘And I chose not to.’

‘Yes. That’s right. You’ve spoken about wanting to wait until we were making progress before telling him. You felt that was better.’

‘And are we?’

‘What?’

‘Making progress?’

He drinks more coffee and puts his cup back on the table. ‘I think so, yes. It's hard to measure progress exactly. But you seem to have remembered a lot of memories over the last few weeks – many for the first time, as far as we know. And you're more aware of certain truths, where you weren't before. For example, you sometimes wake up and remember that you’re married now. And …’ He pauses.

‘And?’ I say.

‘And, well, you’re becoming more independent, I think.’

‘Independence?’

‘Yes. You don’t depend on Ben as much as you did. Or me.’

That’s it, I think. That's the progress he's talking about. Independence. Maybe he means I can go to the shops or a library without someone watching me, though I’m not even sure that’s true right now. Either way, I haven’t made enough progress to brag about it to my husband. Not even enough to always wake up remembering I have one.

‘But that’s it?’

‘It’s important,’ he says. ‘Don’t underestimate it, Christine.’

I don’t say anything. I take a sip of my drink and look around the café. It is almost empty. There are voices from a small kitchen at the back, the occasional rattle as the water in a container boils, the noise of children playing in the distance. It is hard to believe that this place is so close to my home and yet I don't remember ever being here before.

‘You say we’ve been meeting for a few weeks,’ I say to Dr Nash. ‘So what have we been doing?’

‘Do you remember anything of our previous sessions? Anything at all?’

‘No,’ I say. ‘Nothing. As far as I know, I am meeting you for the first time today.’

‘Forgive me for asking,’ he says. ‘As I said, you sometimes have flashes of memory. It seems you know more on some days than on others.’

‘I don’t understand,’ I say. ‘I don’t remember ever meeting you before, or what happened yesterday, or the day before, or last year. Yet I can remember some things from years ago. My childhood. My mother. I remember being at university, just. I don’t understand how these old memories could have survived when everything else has been wiped clean.’

He nods as I speak. I don’t doubt he has heard it before. Possibly I ask the same thing every week. Possibly we have exactly the same conversation.

‘Memory is complex,’ he says. ‘People have a short-term memory that can store facts and information for about a minute, and also a long-term memory. Here we can store huge amounts of information, and keep it for a very long time. We now know that these two functions seem to be controlled by different parts of the brain, with some connections between them. There is also a part of the brain which seems to take short-term memories and code them as long-term memories for recall much later.’

He speaks easily, quickly, as if he is sure of himself. I would have been like that once, I suppose; sure of myself.

‘There are two main types of amnesia,’ he says. ‘Most often, the person affected cannot remember past events, with recent events being the most affected. So, for example, if the person has a car accident, they may not remember the accident, or the days or weeks before it, but can remember everything up to, say, six months before the accident.’

I nod. ‘And the other?’

‘The other is rarer,’ he says. ‘Sometimes people cannot move memories from short-term to long-term storage. People with this condition live in the moment, able to remember only the very recent past, and only for a short time.’

He stops talking, as if waiting for me to say something. It is as if we each know our lines and have practiced this conversation often.

‘I have both?’ I say. ‘I have lost the memories I had, and I can’t make new ones?’

He clears his throat. ‘Yes, unfortunately. It’s not common, but it’s possible. However, the pattern of your amnesia is unusual. Usually, you don’t have any memory of anything that happened since early childhood. But you seem to process new memories in a way I have never seen before. If I left this room now and returned in two minutes, most people with anterograde amnesia would not remember meeting me, especially not today. But you seem to remember whole blocks of time – up to twenty-four hours – which you then lose. That’s not typical. Honestly, it doesn’t make sense, based on how we think memory works. It suggests you can move things from short-term to long-term storage. I don’t understand why you can’t keep them.’

I may be living a broken life, but at least it is broken into large enough pieces that I can still be somewhat independent. I guess that means I am lucky.

‘Why?’ I say. ‘What caused it?’

He doesn’t say anything. The room goes quiet. The air feels still and heavy. When he speaks, his words seem to echo. ‘Many things can cause memory problems,’ he says. ‘Either long-term or short-term. Disease, trauma, drug use. The specific type of problem depends on which part of the brain has been affected.’

‘Yes,’ I say. ‘But what caused mine?’

He looks at me for a moment. ‘What has Ben told you?’

I think back to our conversation in the bedroom. An accident, he had said. A bad accident.

‘He didn’t really tell me anything,’ I say. ‘Nothing specific, anyway. He just said I’d had an accident.’

‘Yes,’ he says, reaching for his bag that sits under the table. ‘Your amnesia was caused by trauma. That’s true, at least partly.’ He opens his bag and takes out a book. At first I wonder if he is going to look at his notes, but instead he passes it across the table to me. ‘Look. I want you to have this,’ he says. ‘It will explain everything. Better than I can. About what has caused your condition, especially. But other things as well.’

I take it from him. It is brown, bound in leather, its pages held closed by an elastic band. I take that off and open it randomly. The paper is heavy and faintly lined, with a red margin, and the pages filled with dense handwriting. ‘What is it?’ I say.

‘It’s a journal,’ he says. ‘One that you’ve been keeping over the past few weeks.’

I am shocked. ‘A journal?’ I wonder why he has it.

‘Yes. A record of what we’ve been doing recently. I asked you to keep it. We’ve been doing a lot of work trying to find out exactly how your memory works. I thought it might be helpful for you to keep a record of what we’ve been doing.’

I look at the book in front of me. ‘So I’ve written this?’

‘Yes. I told you to write whatever you like in it. Many people with amnesia have tried similar things, but usually it’s not as helpful as you might think because they have such a small window of memory. But as there are some things that you can remember for the whole day, I didn’t see why you shouldn’t jot down some notes in a book every evening. I thought it might help you to keep a thread of memory from one day to the next. Plus I felt that memory might be like a muscle, something that can be strengthened through exercise.’

‘And you’ve been reading it as we’ve been going along?’

‘No,’ he says. ‘You’ve been writing it in private.’

‘But how—?’ I start to say, and then say, ‘Ben’s been reminding me to write in it?’

He shakes his head. ‘I suggested that you keep it secret,’ he says. ‘You’ve been hiding it at home. I’ve been calling you to tell you where it’s hidden.’

‘Every day?’

‘Yes. More or less.’

‘Not Ben?’

He pauses, then says, ‘No. Ben hasn’t read it.’

I wonder why not, what it might contain that I don't want my husband to see. What secrets might I have? Secrets I don’t even know myself.

‘But you’ve read it?’

‘You left it with me a few days ago,’ he says. ‘You said you wanted me to read it. That it was time.’

I look at the book. I am excited. A journal. A link back to a past that's lost, even if it's recent.

‘Have you read it all?’

‘Yes,’ he says. ‘Most of it. I think I’ve read everything important, anyway.’ He pauses and looks away from me, scratching the back of his neck. Embarrassed, I think. I wonder if he is telling me the truth, what the book contains. He finishes his coffee, and says, ‘I didn’t make you let me see it. I want you to know that.’

I nod, and finish my drink in silence, flipping through the pages of the book as I do so. On the inside of the front cover is a list of dates. ‘What are these?’ I say.

‘They’re the dates we’ve been meeting,’ he says. ‘And the ones we had planned. We’ve been arranging them as we go. I’ve been calling to remind you, asking you to look in your journal.’

I think of the yellow note in my diary today. ‘But today?’

‘Today I had your journal,’ he says. ‘So we wrote a note instead.’

I nod, and look through the rest of the book. It is filled with handwriting that's hard to read and that I don’t recognize. Page after page. Days and days of work.

I wonder how I found the time, but then I think of the board in the kitchen and the answer is clear; I have had nothing else to do.

I put it back on the table. A young man in jeans and a T-shirt comes in and glances at us, before ordering a drink and sitting at a table with the newspaper. He doesn’t look at me again, and the twenty-year-old me is upset. I feel invisible.

‘Shall we go?’ I say.

We walk back the way we came. The sky has clouded over and a thin mist hangs in the air. The ground feels soggy underfoot; it feels like walking on quicksand. On the playground I see a roundabout, turning slowly even though no one is riding it.

‘We don’t usually meet here?’ I say, when we reach the road. ‘In the café, I mean?’

‘No. We usually meet in my office. We do exercises. Tests and things.’

‘So why here today?’

‘I just wanted to give you your book back,’ he says. ‘I was worried about you not having it.’

‘I’ve come to rely on it?’ I say.

‘In a way, yes.’

We cross the road, and walk back down to the house I share with Ben. I can see Dr Nash’s car, still parked where he left it, the tiny garden outside our window, the short path and neat flower beds. I still can’t believe this is the place where I live.

‘Do you want to come in?’ I say. ‘Another drink?’

He shakes his head. ‘No. No, I won’t, thanks. I have to leave. Julie and I have plans tonight.’

He stands for a moment, looking at me. I notice his hair, cut short and neatly parted, and how his shirt has a vertical stripe that clashes with the horizontal one on his sweater. I realize he is only a few years older than I thought I was this morning. ‘Julie is your wife?’

He smiles and shakes his head. ‘No, my girlfriend. Actually, my fiancée. We got engaged. I keep forgetting.’

I smile back at him. I should remember these details, I guess. The little things. Maybe these little things are what I've been writing down in my book, these small hooks on which a whole life is hung.

‘Congratulations,’ I say, and he thanks me.

I feel like I should ask more questions, should show more interest, but there is little point. I will have forgotten anything he tells me by the time I wake up tomorrow. Today is all I have. ‘I should get back anyway,’ I say. ‘We’re going away this weekend. To the coast. I need to pack later …’

He smiles. ‘Goodbye, Christine,’ he says. He turns to leave, but then looks back at me. ‘Your journal has my numbers in it,’ he says. ‘At the front. Call me if you want to see me again. To continue your treatment, I mean. OK?’

‘If?’ I say. I remember my journal, the appointments we had scheduled between now and the end of the year. ‘I thought we had more sessions booked?’

‘You’ll understand when you read your book,’ he says. ‘It will all make sense. I promise.’

‘OK,’ I say. I realize I trust him, and I am glad. Glad that I don’t only have my husband to depend on.

‘It’s up to you, Christine. Call me, whenever you like.’

‘I will,’ I say, and then he waves and gets into his car and, checking over his shoulder, he pulls out into the road and is gone.

I make a cup of coffee and carry it into the living room. From outside I hear the sound of whistling, interrupted by heavy drilling and an occasional burst of short laughter, but even that fades to a gentle buzz as I sit in the armchair. The sun shines weakly through the net curtains and I feel its faint warmth on my arms and thighs. I take the journal out of my bag.

I feel nervous. I do not know what this book will contain. What shocks and surprises. What mysteries. I see the scrapbook on the coffee table. In that book is a version of my past, but one chosen by Ben. Does the book I hold contain another? I open it.

The first page is blank. I have written my name in black ink across its center. Christine Lucas. It’s a wonder I haven’t written Private! beneath it. Or Keep out!

Something has been added. Something unexpected, terrifying. More terrifying than anything else I have seen today. There, beneath my name, in blue ink and capital letters, are three words.

DON’T TRUST BEN.

There is nothing I can do but turn the page.

I begin to read my history.
