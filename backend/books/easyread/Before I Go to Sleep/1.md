## Part One

### Today

The bedroom feels strange. It's not familiar. I don't know where I am or how I got here. I don't know how to go home.

I slept here last night. A woman's voice woke me up. At first, I thought she was in the bed with me. But then I realized it was the news on the radio alarm. When I opened my eyes, I was in this room I don't know.

My eyes get used to the dark. I look around. There's a robe on the closet door. It looks like it belongs to an older woman. Some dark pants are folded on a chair at the table. I can't see much else. The alarm clock looks hard to use, but I find a button and turn it off.

Then, I hear someone breathe heavily behind me. I'm not alone. I turn around. I see a lot of skin and dark hair with some white in it. It's a man. His left arm is out of the blanket. He has a gold ring on his finger. I don't like this. I think, "He's old, with grey hair, and married." I slept with a married man in his house, in his wife's bed. I lie down. I should feel bad.

I wonder where his wife is. Will she come home soon? I imagine her yelling at me and calling me names. I wonder how I will protect myself if she comes. But the man in the bed doesn't seem worried. He turned over and is snoring.

I stay as still as I can. I usually remember how I end up in situations like this. But not today. Maybe there was a party, or I went to a bar or club. I must have been very drunk. So drunk that I don't remember anything. Drunk enough to go home with a married man with hair on his back.

I lift the blanket carefully and sit on the bed. First, I need to use the bathroom. I don't wear the slippers on the floor. Sleeping with the husband is one thing, but I won't wear his wife's shoes. I walk quietly to the hallway without shoes. I feel naked. I'm afraid of opening the wrong door and finding someone else. I'm happy to see the bathroom door open a little. I go in and lock the door.

I sit down, use the toilet, and flush it. Then I turn to wash my hands. I reach for the soap, but something is wrong. I don't know what it is at first. Then I see it. The hand holding the soap is not mine. The skin has wrinkles. The nails are short and not painted. And like the man in the bed, the finger has a simple gold wedding ring.

I look in the mirror for a bit, then move my fingers. The fingers on the hand with the soap also move. I am surprised, and drop the soap in the sink. I look up at the mirror again.

The face I see is not mine. The hair is flat and short. My cheeks and chin look old. My lips are thin and sad. I shout without words, like I am trying not to scream. Then I see the eyes. They have lines around them, but they are my eyes. The person in the mirror is me, but I look 20 or 25 years older. Maybe more.

This can't be real. I start to shake and grab the sink. I want to scream, but I hold it in. I move away from the mirror. Then I see them: pictures. They are taped to the wall and the mirror. There are also yellow notes with writing on them. They look old and wet.

I pick a picture. It says "Christine," with an arrow pointing to a photo of me. It's the older me. I am sitting on a bench next to a man. I know the name "Christine," but it feels far away. It's like I have to try to remember it. In the picture, we are both smiling and holding hands. He is good-looking. I see it's the same man I slept with. The word "Ben" is under the picture, and "Your husband" is next to it.

I am shocked and tear the picture off the wall. "No!" I think. "This can't be true." I look at the other pictures. They are all of me and him. In one, I am wearing an ugly dress and opening a gift. In another, we are wearing matching jackets and standing by a waterfall. A small dog is smelling our feet. Next to that, I am sitting next to him, drinking orange juice, and wearing the robe I saw in the next room.

I move back until I touch the cold wall. Then I start to remember something. But it's like ashes in the wind, gone quickly. I know there was a "before" in my life, but before what, I don't know. And there is a "now." But there is nothing between them, just a long empty time that brought me here, to this house with him.

I go back to the bedroom. I still have the picture of me and the man. I hold it up.

"What is happening?" I say. I am yelling and crying. The man is sitting up in bed, sleepy. "Who are you?"

"I'm your husband," he says. He sounds sleepy, but not angry. He doesn't look at my naked body. "We have been married for years."

I asked, "What do you mean?" I wanted to run away, but I couldn't. "Married for years? What do you mean?"

He stood up. He gave me a robe and waited for me to put it on. He was wearing pajama pants that were too big and a white shirt. He looked like my dad.

He said, "We got married in 1985. That was 22 years ago. You—"

"What?" I felt like all the blood was leaving my face. The room started to spin. I heard a clock ticking in the house. It was very loud. "But—" He walked closer to me. "How?"

He said, "Christine, you are 47 years old now." I looked at him. He was a stranger, but he was smiling at me. I didn't want to believe him. I didn't even want to hear what he was saying. But he kept talking. "You had an accident," he said. "A bad accident. You hurt your head. You have trouble remembering things."

I asked, "What things?" I meant, surely I remember the last 25 years? "What things?"

He walked closer to me again, like I was a scared animal. "Everything," he said. "Sometimes you forget things from when you were in your early twenties. Sometimes you forget things from even before that."

My mind was spinning. I was thinking about dates and ages. I didn't want to ask, but I knew I had to. "When... when was my accident?"

He looked at me. His face showed both sadness and fear.

"When you were 29..."

I close my eyes. My mind doesn't want to believe what I know is true. I start to cry. The man, Ben, comes to me. He puts his arms around me. I don't move. He holds me close. We move back and forth gently. It feels familiar and makes me feel better.

"I love you, Christine," he says. I should say I love him too, but I don't. I say nothing. How can I love him? He is a stranger. Nothing makes sense. I want to know many things, like how I got here and how I live. But I don't know how to ask.

"I'm scared," I say.

"I know," he says. "I know. Don't worry, Chris. I will take care of you. I will always take care of you. You will be okay. Trust me."

He says he will show me the house. I feel calmer. I have on underwear and an old T-shirt he gave me. I also have a robe on. We go to the hallway. "You've seen the bathroom," he says. He opens the door next to it. "This is the office."

There is a glass table with a computer. It looks very small, like a toy. Next to it is a grey file cabinet. Above it is a calendar on the wall. Everything is clean and organized. "I work here sometimes," he says, and closes the door. We walk to another door. He opens it. There is a bed, a table for makeup, and closets. It looks like the room I woke up in. "Sometimes you sleep here," he says, "if you want to. But you don't like waking up alone. You get scared when you don't know where you are." I nod. I feel like someone looking at a new apartment. Like a possible roommate. "Let's go downstairs."

I follow him. He shows me a living room with a brown sofa and chairs, and a TV on the wall. Then a dining room and a kitchen. Nothing looks familiar. I don't feel anything, even when I see a picture of us on a table. "There's a garden outside," he says. I look through the glass door in the kitchen. It is almost morning. The sky is turning blue. I can see a big tree and a small building in the garden. I don't even know where in the world we are.

"Where are we?" I say.

He is behind me. I can see us in the glass. Me. My husband. Middle-aged.

He says, "North London. The Crouch End area."

I move back. I start to feel scared. "Oh no," I say. "I don't even know my address."

He holds my hand. "Don't worry. You will be okay." I look at him, waiting for him to tell me how I will be okay. But he doesn't say. "Should I make your coffee?" he asks.

For a second, I feel angry at him. But then I say, "Yes, please." He starts to fill the kettle with water. "Black coffee, please," I say. "No sugar."

"I know," he says, and smiles at me. "Do you want toast?"

I say yes. He seems to know a lot about me. But it feels like waking up with someone new after a date. Like eating breakfast with a stranger and thinking about how soon I can leave and go home.

But things are different. This is supposed to be my home.

"I need to sit down," I say.

He looks at me. "Go sit in the living room," he says. "I'll bring the coffee and toast soon."

I leave the kitchen.

Soon after, Ben comes in. He gives me a book. He says it's a scrapbook and might help me. I take the book. It looks like old leather, but it's plastic. A red ribbon is tied around it. Ben says he will be back soon and leaves.

I sit down with the book. It feels heavy. Looking at it feels like I am looking at something private. But I remember it's about me and Ben gave it to me.

I open the book. There is a picture of me and Ben when we were younger.

I quickly close the book. I touch the sides and move the pages. I must do this every day, I think.

I can't believe this is happening. I think there must be a mistake, but there isn't. I see the proof in the mirror and in my hands. I am not the same person I was this morning.

But who was I this morning? When was I the person who wanted to run away? I close my eyes. I feel like I am floating and might get lost.

I need to feel safe. I close my eyes and try to think of something real. But I can't. So many years of my life are missing.

This book will tell me who I am. But I don't want to open it yet. I want to sit here with no past. I am between what might be and what is real. I am scared to find out about my past, what I did and didn't do.

Ben comes back and puts a tray of food in front of me. There is toast, coffee, and milk. He asks if I am okay. I say yes.

He sits next to me. He has shaved and is wearing nice clothes. He doesn't look like my father anymore. He looks like he works in an office. I think he looks good, but I don't want to think that.

"Is every day like this?" I ask.

He puts toast on a plate and puts butter on it. "Yes, almost every day," he says. "Do you want some?" I shake my head. He eats some toast. "You remember things when you are awake," he says. "But when you sleep, you forget most of it. Is your coffee good?"

I say the coffee is good. He takes the book from me. "This is like a memory book," he says, opening it. "We had a fire a few years ago, so we lost many old pictures. But we still have some things here." He points to the first page. "This is your college degree," he says. "And this is a picture of you on graduation day." I look at the picture. I am smiling in the sun. I am wearing a black robe and a hat with a gold string. A man in a suit is standing behind me, but his head is turned away.

"Is that you?" I ask.

He smiles. "No. I finished college later than you. I was still studying. I studied chemistry."

I look at him. "When did we get married?" I ask.

He turns to me and holds my hand. His skin feels rough. I am surprised because I am used to young, soft skin. "We got married the year after you finished your Ph.D. We had been going out for a few years. But you wanted to wait until you finished your studies."

That makes sense to me. But it feels like I was very practical. I wonder if I really wanted to marry him.

Like he knows what I am thinking, he says, "We loved each other very much." Then he adds, "We still do."

I don't know what to say. I smile. He drinks some coffee and looks at the book again. He turns the pages.

He said, "You studied English. Then you had some small jobs after you finished school. Just different things, like office work and sales. I don't think you knew what job you wanted. I got a science degree and became a teacher. It was hard at first, but then I got a better job, and now we live here."

I look around the room. It is nice and comfortable. It looks like a normal middle-class home. There is a picture of a forest on the wall above the fireplace. There are small china dolls next to the clock. I wonder if I helped pick out these things for the house.

Ben keeps talking. "I teach at a school close by. Now I am in charge of my department." He doesn't sound proud when he says this.

"And what about me?" I ask. I already know what he will say. He holds my hand tightly.

"You had to stop working after your accident. You don't work at all." He can tell that I am sad. "You don't need to work. I make enough money. We are doing okay. We are fine."

I close my eyes and touch my forehead. This is too much to hear, and I want him to stop talking. I feel like I can only understand so much at once. If he keeps telling me more, I will break down.

What do I do all day? I want to ask, but I am afraid to know the answer, so I don't say anything.

He finishes his toast and takes the dishes to the kitchen. When he comes back, he has his coat on.

"I have to go to work," he says. I feel nervous.

"Don't worry," he says. "You will be okay. I will call you. I promise. Remember, today is just like every other day. You'll be fine."

"But," I start to say.

"I have to leave," he says. "I'm sorry. I'll show you some things you might need before I go."

In the kitchen, he shows me where things are kept. He points to food in the fridge I can eat for lunch. He shows me a board on the wall with a pen attached. "I sometimes write messages for you here," he says. I see he wrote "Friday" on it. Under that, he wrote "Laundry? Walk? (Take phone!) TV?" Under "Lunch," he wrote that there is salmon in the fridge and added "Salad?" He also wrote that he will be home by six. "You also have a book," he says. "It's in your bag. It has important phone numbers and our address in case you get lost. And there's a mobile phone."

"A what?" I ask.

"A phone," he says. "It has no wires. You can use it anywhere, even outside. It will be in your bag. Take it with you when you go out."

"I will," I say.

"Okay," he says. We go to the hall, and he picks up a bag. "I'm leaving now."

"Okay," I say. I don't know what else to say. I feel like a child who can't go to school and is left home alone while her parents work. I imagine him saying, "Don't touch anything. Don't forget your medicine."

He comes to me and kisses my cheek. I don't stop him, but I don't kiss him back. He turns to the door but stops before opening it.

"Oh!" he says, looking back. "I almost forgot!" His voice sounds fake. He is trying too hard to sound happy. It is clear he has been wanting to say something for a while.

It's not as bad as I thought. He says, "We are leaving tonight. Just for the weekend. It's our anniversary, so I made a reservation. Is that okay?"

I nod my head. "That sounds good," I say.

He smiles and seems happy. "It's something fun to look forward to. Some fresh air from the sea. It will be good for us." He turns and opens the door. "I will call you later," he says. "I will check on you."

"Yes," I say. "Please do."

"I love you, Christine," he says. "Always remember that."

He closes the door and leaves. I turn around and go back inside the house.

Later, in the morning. I am sitting in a chair. The dishes are washed and put away. The laundry is in the washing machine. I have been doing things to keep busy.

But now I feel empty. What Ben said is true. I can't remember anything. There is nothing in this house that I remember seeing before. Not one picture makes me remember when it was taken. I can't remember any time with Ben, except for today. My mind is completely empty.

I close my eyes and try to think about something. Anything. Yesterday. Last Christmas. Any Christmas. My wedding. But I can't remember anything.

I stand up and walk around the house, from room to room. I go slowly. I feel like a ghost, touching the walls, tables, and furniture, but not really touching them. How did I get here? I think.

I look at the carpets, rugs, and small statues in the house. I try to tell myself that this is all mine. My home, my husband, my life. But these things don't feel like they belong to me. They are not a part of who I am.

In the bedroom, I open the closet and see clothes I don't know. They are hanging neatly, like empty versions of a woman I don't know. I feel like I'm walking through her house, using her things. She is like a ghost, far away and untouchable.

This morning, I felt guilty when I picked out underwear. I looked through the clothes like I was afraid of being caught. I found fancy underwear in the back of the drawer. They were bought to be seen, not just worn. I put them back the way I found them. Then, I chose plain underwear and put them on, with tights, pants, and a shirt.

I sat at the table and looked at my face in the mirror. I was careful as I looked at my reflection. I touched the lines on my face. I smiled and looked at my teeth and wrinkles. I saw spots on my skin, like a bruise that didn't go away. I put on a little make-up, like my mother used to do. She called it "warpaint." This morning, that word felt right. I felt like I was going into a fight.

I remember my mother putting on make-up to send me to school. I try to remember other things about her, but I can't. I only see empty spaces between small memories.

Now, in the kitchen, I open the cabinets. I see pasta, rice, and beans that I don't know. I remember eating simple foods like cheese toast and fish. I find food called chickpeas and couscous. I don't know what they are or how to cook them. How can I be a wife if I don't know these things?

I look at the board that Ben showed me. It's gray and dirty. Words have been written and erased, leaving marks. I wonder if I could read the old words and learn about my past. But even if I could, it wouldn't matter. I would only find lists of things to buy and do.

Is that really my life? I think. Is that all I am? I write a note on the board: "Pack bag for tonight?" It's a small reminder, but it's mine.

I hear a noise. It's a tune from my bag. I open the bag and take everything out. I see my wallet, tissues, pens, lipstick, make-up, a coffee receipt, and a small diary.

I find the phone that Ben talked about. It's small and plastic, with a keypad that looks like a toy. It's ringing, and the screen is flashing. I press a button.

"Hello?" I say. The voice on the phone is not Ben's.

"Hi," it says. "Christine? Is that Christine Lucas?"

I don't want to talk. My last name sounds as weird as my first name did before. It feels like I'm losing my grip on things, like I'm sinking in sand.

"Christine? Are you there?"

Who is this? Who knows where I am or who I am? It could be anyone. I feel scared. My finger is close to the button to hang up.

"Christine? It's me, Dr. Nash. Please answer."

I don't know that name, but I still ask, "Who is this?"

The voice sounds different now. Is he relieved? "It's Dr. Nash," he says. "Your doctor?"

I feel even more scared. "My doctor?" I ask. I want to say I'm not sick, but I don't even know that. My head starts to spin.

"Yes," he says. "But don't worry. We were just working on your memory. Nothing is wrong."

I notice he said "were." So this is someone I don't remember.

"What kind of work?" I ask.

"I'm trying to help you feel better," he says. "I want to understand why you can't remember things and see if we can fix it."

That makes sense, but I wonder something else. Why didn't Ben tell me about this doctor before he left?

"How?" I ask. "What have we done?"

"We've been meeting for a few weeks," he says. "Maybe twice a week."

This seems strange. Another person I see often, but I don't remember them at all.

I want to say, "I've never met you. You could be anyone."

But I could say the same thing about the man I woke up with, and he's my husband.

"I don't remember," I say instead.

His voice becomes gentle. "It's okay. I know." If he's telling the truth, he must understand my memory problem. He says we have another meeting today.

"Today?" I ask. I think about what Ben said and the list in the kitchen. "But my husband didn't tell me anything about this." I realize this is the first time I've called the man I woke up with my husband.

Dr. Nash stops talking for a moment. Then he says, "I don't think Ben knows you are meeting me."

I see that he knows my husband's name. I say, "That's not true! Of course he knows! He would have told me!"

Dr. Nash sighs. He says, "You need to believe me. I can explain everything when we meet. We are getting better."

He wants to meet. But how? I am scared to go out without Ben. I don't want him to not know where I am or who I am with.

"I'm sorry," I say. "I can't do it."

"Christine," he says, "this is important. Look in your diary. You will see I am telling the truth. Do you have your diary? It should be in your bag."

I pick up my diary. It has flowers on it. It fell on the sofa. I am shocked to see the year. It says 2007. That is twenty years before now.

"Yes, I have it."

"Look at today's date," he says. "November 30th. You should see our meeting."

I don't understand how it can be November. Tomorrow is December. But I look through the pages to today's date. The pages are very thin. I see a piece of paper. It has writing on it that I don't know. It says "November 30th - seeing Dr. Nash." Below that, it says "Don't tell Ben." I wonder if Ben has seen this. Does he look at my things?

I think he has no reason to come. The other days are just empty. No birthdays, no fun nights, no parties. Is this really my life?

"Okay," I say. He says he will come get me. He knows where I live and will be here in one hour.

"But my husband..." I say.

"It's okay. We will be back before he comes home from work. I promise. Trust me."

The clock makes a sound. I look at it. It is an old clock with big numbers. It says eleven thirty. Next to it is a key to wind it. Ben must wind it every night. The clock looks very old. I wonder how we got it. Maybe it has no story. Or maybe we just saw it in a store and liked it. Ben probably liked it. I don't like it.

I will see him just one time, I think. Then, tonight, I will tell Ben. I can't believe I am hiding this from him. I trust him so much.

But Dr. Nash's voice sounds familiar. He does not seem like a stranger, like Ben does. It is easier to believe I know him than my own husband.

"We're getting better," he said. I need to know what he means by "better."

"Okay," I say. "Come."

Dr. Nash comes and asks if I want coffee. He asks, "Are you thirsty?" He says it's not important to go to the office. He just wants to talk to me today.

I say yes. I saw him arrive. I watched him park, fix his hair, and grab his briefcase. I thought he wasn't the doctor at first. He looked young. He wore a sports jacket and pants. I didn't expect that.

He says there's a park with a cafe nearby. He asks if we can go there.

We walk together. It's cold, so I wrap my scarf tight. I'm happy I have my phone in my bag. I'm also happy we're walking, not driving. I trust him a little, but I also feel like he could be anyone. A stranger.

I am grown up, but I am hurt inside. This man could easily take me somewhere. I don't know why he would want to. I feel as weak as a child.

We get to the road to the park and wait to cross. The quiet between us is heavy. I wanted to wait to ask him, but I speak. "What kind of doctor are you?" I ask. "What do you do? How did you find me?"

He looks at me and smiles. "I study the brain," he says. "I help people with brain problems. I'm interested in how the brain works. I study memory. I read about you and found you. It wasn't hard."

A car drives by. "You read about me?" I ask.

"Yes. There were stories written about you. I contacted the place where you lived before you came home."

"Why? Why did you want to find me?"

He smiles and says, "I wanted to help you. I have worked with people who have similar problems. I think they can get better, but they need more help than just one hour a week. I had some ideas to make them better and wanted to try them out."

He stops talking. "Also, I am writing a paper about your case. It will be very important." He starts to laugh but stops when I don't laugh with him. He clears his throat. "Your case is special. I think we can learn more about how memory works than we know now."

A car goes by, and we walk across the street. I feel worried and tight. Brain problems. Research. He found me. I try to breathe and relax, but I can't. I feel like two people in one body: one is a calm, polite 47-year-old woman who knows how to act. The other is a young woman in her 20s, and she is screaming. I don't know which one is me. I only hear cars and children playing in the park, so I think I am the first woman.

On the other side of the road, I stop and say, "What is happening? I woke up this morning in a strange place that is supposed to be my home. I was next to a man I don't know, but he says we have been married for years. You seem to know more about me than I do."

He nods slowly. "You have amnesia," he says, and touches my arm. "You have had amnesia for a long time. You can't remember new things, so you have forgotten much of your adult life. Every day you wake up like a young woman. Sometimes you wake up like a child."

It sounds worse coming from a doctor. "So it's true?"

"I'm afraid so. Yes. The man at home is your husband, Ben. You have been married for years, since before you lost your memory." I nod. "Shall we continue walking?"

I say yes, and we go into the park. There is a path around the park, and a playground for children next to a small building. I see people getting food from the building. We go there, and I sit at a table. Dr. Nash orders drinks.

He comes back with two plastic cups of strong coffee. Mine is black, and his has milk. He adds sugar to his coffee but doesn't offer me any. That makes me think we have met before. He looks up and asks how I hurt my forehead.

"What?" I say. Then I remember the bruise I saw this morning. My makeup didn't hide it. "That? I don't know. It's nothing. It doesn't hurt."

He doesn't answer. He stirs his coffee.

"So, my husband takes care of me at home?" I ask.

He looks up. "Yes, but not always. At first, you were very sick. You needed someone to take care of you all the time. It's only been recently that Ben felt he could take care of you by himself."

So, I am doing better now. I am happy I don't remember when I was worse.

"He must love me a lot," I say quietly.

He nods. We both drink. "Yes. I think he does."

I smile and look down. I see my hands holding the drink, my wedding ring, my short nails, and my legs. I don't know my own body.

"Why doesn't my husband know I'm seeing you?" I ask.

He sighs and closes his eyes. "I will be honest," he says. He puts his hands together and leans forward. "At first, I asked you not to tell Ben you were seeing me."

I feel a sudden fear. But he looks like I can trust him.

"Please continue," I say. I want to believe he can help me.

Many people, like doctors and therapists, wanted to help you and Ben. But Ben didn't want you to see them. He said you had a lot of help before, but it didn't work. It only made you sad. He didn't want you to be sad again.

You said, "So you asked me to see you without Ben knowing?"

He said, "Yes. I talked to Ben first on the phone. I asked to meet him to explain how I could help. But he said no. So, I contacted you directly."

You felt scared. You asked, "How did you find me?"

He looked down and said, "I went to your house. I waited for you to come outside, and then I talked to you."

You asked, "And I agreed to see you that easily?"

He said, "Not at first. I had to make you believe you could trust me. I asked you to meet me just once, without Ben knowing if you wanted. I said I would tell you why I wanted to help you."

You said, "And I agreed..."

He looked up and said, "Yes. I said that after the first visit, it was your choice to tell Ben or not. If you didn't tell him, I would call you to remind you about our meetings."

You said, "And I didn't tell him."

"Yes, that's right. You said you wanted to wait until we were getting better before telling him. You thought that was best."

"Are we getting better?"

"What?"

"Are we making progress?"

He drinks more coffee and puts the cup down. "Yes, I think so. It's hard to measure exactly. But you remember more things now than before. Some of these memories are new, as far as we know. You also understand some things better now. For example, sometimes you wake up and remember you are married. And..." He stops.

"And?" I ask.

"And, you are becoming more independent, I think."

"Independent?"

"Yes. You don't need Ben or me as much as you used to."

That's the progress he means, I think. Being independent. Maybe he means I can go to the shops or library alone. But I'm not even sure I can do that yet. I haven't made enough progress to tell my husband. I don't even always remember I have a husband when I wake up.

"Is that all?"

"It is important," he says. "Don't think it is not important, Christine."

I don't talk. I drink some of my drink and look around the café. There are not many people here. I hear voices from the kitchen in the back. I hear the sound of water boiling. I hear kids playing far away. It's hard to think this place is so close to my house. I don't remember ever being here before.

"You said we have met for a few weeks," I say to Dr. Nash. "What have we done?"

"Do you remember anything from when we met before? Anything at all?"

"No," I say. "Nothing. I think I am meeting you for the first time today."

"Sorry for asking," he says. "Like I said, you remember things sometimes. Some days you seem to know more than other days."

"I don't get it," I say. "I don't remember meeting you before. I don't remember what happened yesterday, or the day before, or last year. But I remember some things from years ago. When I was a kid. My mom. I remember being in college a little bit. I don't understand how I can remember old things, but I don't remember anything else."

He nods as I talk. I think he has heard this before. Maybe I ask the same thing every week. Maybe we always have the same talk.

"Memory is hard to understand," he says. "People have a short-term memory. It can keep things for about a minute. We also have a long-term memory. It can keep many things for a long time. We now know that these two types of memory are controlled by different parts of the brain. But they are connected. There is also a part of the brain that takes short-term memories and makes them into long-term memories so we can remember them later."

He talks easily and fast, like he knows what he is saying. I think I used to be like that, sure of myself.

He says, "There are two main kinds of amnesia." Amnesia means you can't remember things. "The most common kind makes it hard to remember the past. It's harder to remember things that happened recently. For example, if someone has a car accident, they might not remember the accident. They might also not remember the days or weeks before it. But they can remember things from six months before the accident."

I nod my head. "What about the other kind?"

He says, "The other kind is rare. It means you can't move memories from short-term to long-term memory. People with this kind of amnesia live in the present. They can only remember what just happened, and only for a short time."

He stops talking, like he wants me to say something. It's like we know what we are supposed to say.

"Do I have both kinds?" I ask. "Do I forget old memories and have trouble making new ones?"

He clears his throat. "Yes, that's right. It's not common, but it can happen. But your amnesia is strange. You don't remember anything since you were a child. But you make new memories in a way I've never seen before. If I left the room and came back in two minutes, most people with this kind of amnesia wouldn't remember me. But you seem to remember big chunks of time, like a day. Then you forget it. That's not normal. It doesn't make sense, because it seems like you can move memories to long-term storage. I don't know why you can't keep them."

My life is broken, but at least the pieces are big enough for me to take care of myself. I guess I am lucky.

"Why?" I ask. "What caused this?"

He doesn't answer. The room is quiet. The air feels heavy. When he talks, his voice sounds like it's bouncing off the walls. "Many things can hurt your memory," he says. "It can be long-term or short-term memory. It could be a disease, an accident, or drugs. The kind of memory problem depends on which part of the brain is hurt."

"Yes," I say. "But why did I lose my memory?"

He looks at me. "What did Ben tell you?"

I remember our talk in the bedroom. Ben said it was an accident. A bad accident.

"He didn't really say much," I say. "He just said I had an accident."

"Yes," he says. He gets his bag from under the table. "Your memory loss is from something bad that happened. That's true, at least a little." He opens his bag and takes out a book. I think he will look at his notes. But he gives the book to me. "Here. I want you to have this," he says. "It will tell you everything. It will explain why you lost your memory better than I can. It will also tell you other things."

I take the book. It is brown and made of leather. A rubber band keeps it closed. I take off the rubber band and open the book. The paper is thick and has lines on it. The lines are light. There is a red line on the side of the page. The pages are full of writing. "What is this?" I say.

"It's a diary," he says. "You have been writing in it for the last few weeks."

I am surprised. "A diary?" I wonder why he has it.

"Yes. It has what we have been doing lately. I asked you to write in it. We have been working hard to learn how your memory works. I thought it would help you to write down what we did."

I look at the book. "So, I wrote this?"

"Yes, I told you to write anything you want in the book. Many people with memory problems have tried this. It often doesn't help much because they forget things quickly. But you can remember some things all day. So, I thought you could write notes in the book every night. This might help you remember things from one day to the next. Also, I thought your memory might be like a muscle. You can make it stronger by using it."

"And you have been reading my book as I write in it?"

"No," he says. "You write in it alone."

"But how? Ben reminds me to write in it?"

He shakes his head. "I told you to keep it a secret," he says. "You hide it at home. I call you and tell you where it is."

"Every day?"

"Yes, almost every day."

"Not Ben?"

He stops for a moment, then says, "No. Ben has not read it."

I wonder why Ben hasn't read it. What is in it that I don't want my husband to see? What secrets do I have? Secrets I don't even know.

"Did you read it?"

"You gave it to me a few days ago," he says. "You wanted me to read it. You said it was time."

I look at the book. I feel happy. It's a journal. It connects me to the past, even though it was not long ago.

"Did you read everything?"

"Yes," he says. "Most of it. I think I read the important parts." He stops and looks away, rubbing his neck. I think he feels shy. I wonder if he is telling me the truth about what's in the book. He finishes his coffee and says, "I didn't make you show it to me. I want you to know that."

I nod and finish my drink quietly. I look through the pages of the book. On the first page, there is a list of dates. "What are these?" I ask.

"Those are the dates we met," he says. "And the dates we planned to meet. We made plans as we went along. I called to remind you and asked you to check your journal."

I remember the yellow note in my diary today. "But what about today?"

"I had your journal today," he says. "So we wrote a note instead."

I nod and look through the rest of the book. It is full of writing that I don't know. Page after page. It looks like a lot of work.

I don't know how I had time for this. But then I remember the board in the kitchen. I had nothing else to do, so I had time.

I put it back on the table. A young man comes in. He wears jeans and a T-shirt. He looks at us quickly, then orders a drink. He sits at a table and reads the newspaper. He doesn't look at me again. I feel sad, like nobody can see me. I feel invisible.

'Let's go,' I say.

We walk back the same way. The sky is cloudy now. There is a light mist in the air. The ground is wet and soft. It feels like walking on sand that is sinking. I see a merry-go-round in the playground. It is turning slowly, but nobody is on it.

'We don't usually meet here, do we?' I ask. 'I mean, in the café?'

'No. We usually meet in my office. We do exercises and tests there.'

'So why did we meet here today?'

'I just wanted to give you your book back,' he says. 'I was worried you needed it.'

'I need it now?' I ask.

'Yes, in a way.'

We walk across the street and go back to my house. Ben and I live there together. I see Dr. Nash's car. It is still parked there. I see our small garden, the path, and the flowers. I still cannot believe I live here.

"Do you want to come inside?" I ask. "Do you want another drink?"

He shakes his head. "No, thank you. I need to leave. Julie and I have plans tonight."

He looks at me for a moment. I see his short hair and his striped shirt. It doesn't match his sweater. I realize he is not as old as I thought he was this morning. "Is Julie your wife?" I ask.

He smiles and shakes his head. "No, my girlfriend. We are engaged to be married. I forget sometimes."

I smile back. I should remember these details. Maybe these small things are what I write in my book. Maybe these little things make up a life.

"Congratulations," I say. He thanks me.

I feel like I should ask more questions. I should be more interested. But it doesn't matter. I will forget everything he says tomorrow. Today is all I have. "I should go back," I say. "We are going to the beach this weekend. I need to pack."

He smiles. "Goodbye, Christine," he says. He starts to leave, but then looks back. "My phone numbers are in your journal," he says. "Call me if you want to see me again. To continue your treatment. Okay?"

"If?" I ask. I remember my journal. I remember the appointments we had planned. "I thought we had more appointments?"

"You'll understand when you read your book," he says. "It will all be clear. I promise you."

"Okay," I say. I know I believe him, and I am happy about that. I'm happy I can trust someone besides my husband.

"It's your choice, Christine. Call me whenever you want."

"I will," I say. He waves goodbye, gets in his car, and drives away. He looks back as he leaves.

I make coffee and go to the living room. Outside, I hear whistling, loud drilling, and people laughing. But the sounds become quieter as I sit in the chair. The sun is not very bright, but I feel it warm my arms and legs. I take the book out of my bag.

I feel worried. I don't know what is in this book. What bad things or surprises are in it? What secrets will I find? I see the scrapbook on the table. Ben chose what went into that book about my past. Will this book show a different past? I open it.

The first page is empty. I wrote my name in the middle: Christine Lucas. I almost wrote "Private!" or "Keep out!" under it!

But something else is written there. Something scary and surprising. It's the scariest thing I've seen today. Under my name, in blue letters, are three words:

DON'T TRUST BEN.

I have to turn the page.

I start reading about my past.
